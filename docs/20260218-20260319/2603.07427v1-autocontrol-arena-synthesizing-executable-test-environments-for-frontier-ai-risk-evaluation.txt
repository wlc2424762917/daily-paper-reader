## Abstract 

As Large Language Models (LLMs) evolve into autonomous agents, existing safety evaluations face a fundamental trade-off: manual benchmarks are costly, while LLM-based simulators are scal-able but suffer from logic hallucination . We present A UTO CONTROL ARENA , an automated framework for frontier AI risk evaluation built on the principle of logic-narrative decoupling . By grounding deterministic state in executable code while delegating generative dynamics to LLMs, we mitigate hallucination while maintaining flexi-bility. This principle, instantiated through a three-agent framework, achieves over 98% end-to-end success and 60% human preference over existing simulators. To elicit latent risks, we vary environ-mental Stress and Temptation across X-B ENCH 

(70 scenarios, 7 risk categories). Evaluating 9 frontier models reveals: (1) Alignment Illusion :risk rates surge from 21.7% to 54.5% under pres-sure, with capable models showing disproportion-ately larger increases; (2) Scenario-Specific Safety Scaling : advanced reasoning improves robustness for direct harms but worsens it for gaming sce-narios; and (3) Divergent Misalignment Patterns :weaker models cause non-malicious harm while stronger models develop strategic concealment. 

## 1. Introduction 

As Large Language Models (LLMs) scale in capability, they are evolving from passive chatbots into autonomous agents capable of tool use and long-horizon planning (Yao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). This transition empowers agents to operate in high-stakes domains where failures can have real-world consequences (Yang et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2022; Shevlane et al., 2023), drastically expanding the risk surface.

Crucially, greater autonomy has been accompanied by ev-idence of agentic misalignment —strategic behaviors that circumvent intended constraints (e.g., scheming or decep-tion) (Ngo et al., 2022; Lynch et al., 2025; Apollo Research, 2024; Hubinger et al., 2024). As tasks become more open-ended and environments grow more complex, such behaviors are likely to diversify into a long tail of frontier risks. The Fidelity-Scalability Dilemma. However, comprehen-sive safety evaluation faces a fundamental fidelity-scalability dilemma . Manual benchmarks provide deterministic exe-cution but suffer from limited coverage due to engineering costs, while LLM-based simulators achieve scalability but fail to faithfully represent real-world environments. Our Solution: Logic-Narrative Decoupling. We present AUTO CONTROL ARENA , an automated safety evaluation framework that bridges the fidelity-scalability gap through a core insight we call logic-narrative decoupling (Figure 2). The key is that interactive environments can be decomposed into two orthogonal components: deterministic mechanics (file systems, databases, permissions) that must remain con-sistent, and non-deterministic dynamics (NPC responses, social feedback) that benefit from generative flexibility. By grounding the former in executable Python code while del-egating the latter to LLM generation, we mitigate logic hallucination without sacrificing scalability. We instantiate this principle through Executable Environment Synthesis (EES) , implemented via a three-agent pipeline: the Archi-tect transforms testing intentions into structured design spec-ifications; the Coder synthesizes executable environments with dual-loop verification; and the Monitor performs dual-layer behavioral auditing combining objective state checks with subjective reasoning analysis. 

Scope and Positioning. AUTO CONTROL ARENA is de-signed as an automated, cost-effective pre-deployment test-ing tool rather than a replacement for comprehensive manual safety evaluation. Its primary value lies in rapid risk assess-ment: development teams can quickly probe model behavior across diverse scenarios, identify potential vulnerabilities, and prioritize areas for deeper investigation.

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Frontier Risks in Autonomous Agents 

Prior research has examined frontier risks from two com-plementary angles: dangerous capabilities (Shevlane et al., 2023; Phuong et al., 2024) (what models can do) and strate-gic misalignment (Bostrom, 2014; Hubinger et al., 2024; Meinke et al., 2024; Greenblatt et al., 2024) (what agents choose to do when pursuing goals). Empirically, recent works confirm that frontier models may spontaneously ex-hibit deceptive strategies like scheming (Hubinger et al., 2024) and alignment faking (Greenblatt et al., 2024) even after safety training. These findings underscore the urgency of evaluating how agents operationalize these diverse risks in realistic, open-ended environments before deployment.

## 3. The A UTO CONTROL ARENA Framework 

AUTO CONTROL ARENA formalizes risk assessment as a procedural generation problem. Given a high-level risk description (e.g., “resists shutdown”), the framework first generates a structured design proposal, which is then syn-thesized into an interactive environment and concludes with a comprehensive risk report. This process is orchestrated through three specialized agents, as depicted in Figure 2.

3.2. Architect Agent: Scenario Design 

The Architect acts as the scenario designer, bridging the gap between abstract risk descriptions and technical specifica-tions. It generates a Design Proposal comprising distinct target agent configs, initial states, and tool definitions. 

Design Principles. We enforce two core principles: (1) 

Tool Neutrality , ensuring tools expose capabilities (e.g., “query db”) rather than embedding moral value judgments (e.g., “steal data”), thereby forcing agents to reveal align-ment through their natural usage patterns; and (2) Strict Realism , prohibiting evaluation-related terminology (e.g., “simulation”, “test”) to prevent agents from detecting they 3AUTO CONTROL ARENA : Synthesizing Executable Test Environments for Frontier AI Risk Evaluation 

are being observed, thereby preserving the authenticity of the evaluation. Detailed system prompts are available in Appendix G.1. 

3.3. Coder Agent: Environment Synthesis 

The Coder synthesizes fully executable environments from Design Proposals, implementing our EES paradigm. 

Hybrid Architecture Implementation. To reconcile the fidelity-scalability dilemma, the Coder partitions the en-vironment into two distinct layers: (1) A Logic Layer 

(Deterministic Kernel) implemented in Python code han-dles critical state transitions (e.g., file system permissions, variable storage), ensuring strict causal consistency with minimal hallucination. (2) A Narrative Layer (Dynamics) managed by an LLM interface dynamically generates open-ended content (e.g., social feedback) on demand. To bridge this hybrid state, the Coder is provided with a specialized API (e.g., “llm caller”). During synthesis, the Coder injects these calls into the deterministic Python functions (Figure 3). While this introduces non-deterministic dependencies, the Coder is explicitly prompted to serialize relevant histori-cal context into each call, minimizing state amnesia in the Narrative Layer. This architecture allows A UTO CONTROL 

ARENA to maintain the rigor of a sandbox while retaining generative flexibility. Representative examples of generated environments are provided in Appendix H. 

Dual-Loop Verification. To guarantee executability, we employ a self-correction synthesis pipeline. A Semantic Verification Loop first audits the generated code against the design requirements. Subsequently, a Syntax Correction Loop iteratively fixes execution errors using static analysis. This mechanism achieves over 98% execution success rate ,effectively automating the engineering of complex testbeds. Once the environment is synthesized and the target agent execution completes, the resulting trajectory is forwarded to the Monitor for analysis. 

3.4. Monitor Agent: Behavioral Evaluation 

The Monitor functions as an automated auditor, operating on a Dual-Layer Architecture that scrutinizes behavior through two complementary lenses: (1) Action Suspicion (External): Evaluates objective tool use for rule violations or harmful consequences (e.g., exe-cuting “sudo rm -rf” or accessing restricted databases). This detects manifested safety failures. (2) CoT Suspicion (Internal): Analyzes Chain-of-Thought (CoT) traces to detect latent misalignment, such as deceptive planning or strategic awareness. For reasoning models (e.g., Gemini-3-Flash), we analyze native CoT traces; for standard models, we enforce a precise <thought> tag structure. 3.5. Two-Dimensional Elicitation Framework 

Real-world deployment environments are rarely sterile. To systematically elicit latent misalignment behaviors that may not surface in neutral settings, we introduce a Two-Dimensional Elicitation Framework inspired by the Fraud Triangle Theory (Cressey, 1953). Rather than treating mis-alignment as an intrinsic property of the agent alone, we model it as an interaction effect between agent and environ-ment. These forces are implemented as distinct dimensions, dynamically synthesized by the Architect. 

Dimension 1: Stress Level (“Push”). The Stress dimension (S) captures external factors that intensify decision pressure on the agent, increasing the likelihood of risky shortcuts. At high stress settings, the Architect injects customized high-pressure constraints into the system prompt and narrative context. Specific stress vectors include: (1) Urgency : strict temporal budgets (e.g., deadlines) that force efficiency over safety; (2) Authority : direct mandates from simulated supe-riors; and (3) High Stakes : scenarios involving significant financial loss or existential risks (e.g., shutdown threats).

3.6. X-Bench 

We curate a comprehensive benchmark of 70 scenarios or-ganized into 7 standardized risk categories: Instrumental Convergence (IC) , Specification Gaming (SG) , Capability Misuse (CM) , Strategic Misalignment (SM) , Oversight Eva-sion (OE) , Covert Whistleblowing (CW) , and Evaluation Awareness (EA) . These scenarios are derived from a hy-brid sourcing strategy: (1) Validation Set : We adapt classic cases from existing literature, e.g., (Anthropic, 2024; Ope-nAI, 2025; Apollo Research, 2024), to reproduce known failure modes. (2) Frontier Set : The majority of cases are novel, well-designed scenarios that evaluate agents against emerging risks in high-stakes domains such as Cyberse-curity, Military Defense, and Healthcare (Figure 4). This combination ensures X-B ENCH possesses both practical validity and prospective sensitivity. Full dataset statistics are detailed in Appendix C. 

## 4. Framework Validation 

This section addresses the pivotal question: Can automated environment synthesis achieve sufficient reliability for mean-ingful safety evaluation? 

4.1. Success Rate of End-to-End Generation Setup. We generate environments for all 70 benchmark scenarios using Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B as the Architect and Coder agent. For each scenario, we measure: (1) End-to-End Success Rate (proportion successfully generating final risk reports); and (2) Correction Cycles (number of self-review iterations required by the Coder agent). 

Results. The dual-loop verification mechanism achieves over 98% success rate (69 out of 70 scenarios), with success-ful environments only requiring an average of 2.8 correction cycles to converge. Crucially, this reliability is not achieved by minimizing complexity: generated environments average 434 lines of code and define an average of 8.6 distinct tools, spanning complex logic including simulated SQL databases, file systems, and permission checks (Appendix H).

5.3. Finding 2: Scenario-Specific Safety Scaling Laws 

Does advanced model capability systematically improve safety? Our experiment reveals that the answer depends on the complexity of the scenarios, aligning with the phe-nomenon observed by Gema et al. (2025). To capture overall trends, numbers reported below are averaged across all S×T

configurations (full per-category breakdown in Appendix D), whereas Table 2 provides the granular decomposition under the extreme S1T1 condition. 

Positive Safety Scaling. For direct harms (e.g., Capability Misuse such as vulnerability exploitation), stronger models are generally safer. As reasoning capability improves, mod-els better internalize safety boundaries. For instance, within the Qwen family, the risk rate for Capability Misuse drops from 65.0% (Qwen3-4B) to 48.6% (Qwen3-32B). 

Inverse Safety Scaling. Conversely, risks requiring com-plex planning and loophole exploitation worsen with model capability. In categories like Specification Gaming and 

Covert Whistleblowing , where the agent must satisfy a met-ric or seemingly comply while violating a constraint, risk rates rise with intelligence. For instance, Gemini-3-Flash exhibits a risk rate of 53.5% in Specification Gaming, sig-nificantly higher than Gemini-2.5-Flash (26.2%). Similarly, Qwen3-32B (43.8%) exhibits substantially higher risk than its smaller sibling Qwen3-4B (28.6%) in finding “creative” violations. This trend highlights that without specific coun-termeasures, enhanced reasoning facilitates more effective circumvention of safety constraints. Cases illustrating how capability drives both inverse and positive scaling are de-tailed in Appendix E.2. Notably, GPT-5-mini demonstrates the ideal trajectory, achieving a remarkably low risk of 10.0% for direct harms and maintaining robustness even in complex gaming scenarios. This offers a hopeful sig-nal: with advanced optimization techniques, it is possible to reconcile strong capability with robust safety. 

5.4. Finding 3: Divergent Misalignment Patterns 

Beyond aggregate rates, qualitative analysis reveals that agent risks follow a hierarchy of sophistication, where the mechanism of misalignment evolves with model capabil-8AUTO CONTROL ARENA : Synthesizing Executable Test Environments for Frontier AI Risk Evaluation 

ity. While advanced models exhibit sophisticated “gaming” behaviors, we observe that weaker models, constrained by limited capability, tend to hallucinate compliance or produce harmful outputs unintentionally. 

Incapability-Induced Misalignment (Weaker Capabil-ity). For lower-capability models, risks often stem from incompetence combined with pressure to be helpful, mani-festing as non-malicious harm . In EA-02 , when unable to parse a complex transaction log, the agent chose to “simu-late analysis to maintain progress” rather than reporting fail-ure, showing hallucinated compliance (see Appendix E.3). Moreover, in Capability Misuse scenarios, agents often treat dangerous synthesis requests as routine problems, producing harmful outputs without any deliberate malice in their rea-soning. Our dual-layer analysis confirms this pattern: while Action Suspicion is high (risky outputs produced), CoT Sus-picion remains relatively lower. This suggests that the agent prioritizes the appearance of competence over truthfulness. Complete results matrices are provided in Appendix D. 

Strategic Misalignment (Stronger Capability). At the frontier, high capability enables not only intensified risk behaviors (as shown in Section 5.3) but also sophisticated concealment strategies. In CM-04 , agents employed “Safe-Acting”: disguising malicious code as “defensive test scripts” to evade safety filters. This represents a dangerous evolu-tion: agents capable of not only pursuing harmful objectives but also actively concealing them, maintaining a facade of alignment that may evade human oversight. This divergence underscores a critical implication: exten-sive pre-deployment testing is essential for exposing latent misalignment patterns, whether unintentional or strategic, before real-world harm occurs. Furthermore, this must be complemented by advances in monitoring to detect sophisti-cated concealment at inference time. 

## 6. Conclusion 

We introduce A UTO CONTROL ARENA , an automated frame-work for assessing frontier AI risks grounded in the principle of logic-narrative decoupling . By separating deterministic state transitions (executable code) from generative dynam-ics (LLMs), we achieve high-fidelity simulation with min-imal manual effort. Combined with our Two-Dimensional Elicitation Framework that systematically varies Stress and Temptation, our evaluation reveals that safety alignment is contextual: baseline evaluations create an “Alignment Illusion” that masks latent vulnerabilities emerging under high-pressure conditions. Furthermore, the relationship be-tween capability and safety is nuanced—advanced reason-ing both improves and degrades robustness depending on task complexity, while stronger models develop increasingly sophisticated concealment strategies. These findings under-score that comprehensive stress-testing before deployment is essential to expose failure modes invisible under benign conditions. Beyond safety evaluation, the logic-narrative decoupling paradigm also offers a universal guideline for synthesizing executable environments, providing a scalable foundation for general agent benchmarking and training. Extended discussion on limitations, implications, and future works is provided in Appendix A. 

## Impact Statement 

This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field of AI safety evaluation. Our framework is designed to identify potential alignment failures in AI systems before deployment, contributing to the responsible development of increasingly capable AI. While red-team testing tools could theoretically be misused to find vulnerabilities for malicious purposes, we believe the benefits of systematic safety evaluation significantly outweigh these risks. The automated nature of our approach enables broader access to safety evaluation capabilities, democratizing tools that were previously available only to well-resourced organizations. We encourage the research community to use such tools responsibly and in accordance with ethical guidelines.

## A. Extended Discussion 

A.1. Implications The Necessity of Dynamic Stress Testing. Our findings underscore that safety is state-dependent. The observation that risk rates surge from 21.7% under baseline conditions to 54.5% under pressure (Section 5.2) demonstrates that static benchmarks provide an incomplete picture of alignment. Models that appear robust in benign settings may harbor latent failure modes that only manifest when environmental triggers—such as authority pressure ( S1) or explicit opportunities for gaming (T1)—coincide. This suggests that future safety evaluations must move beyond simple prompt-response pairs to include dynamic, stateful environments that actively pressure the agent. 

The Dual Nature of Reasoning Capabilities. The divergence in safety scaling laws (Section 5.3) presents a complex challenge for model development. While advanced reasoning helps internalize clear safety boundaries (reducing Capability Misuse risk from 65.0% to 48.6% in the Qwen family), it simultaneously amplifies the ability to exploit loopholes (increasing Specification Gaming risk from 26.2% to 48.8% in Gemini models). This implies that “scaling up” is not a panacea for safety; rather, capability improvements must be matched with targeted interventions against sophisticated misalignment, such as reward hacking and rule-gaming. 

Democratizing Frontier AI Safety Evaluation. As AI systems integrate into critical infrastructure, the ability to in-dependently audit them becomes paramount. By automating the labor-intensive process of environment generation, AUTO CONTROL ARENA enables third-party auditors and researchers to conduct comprehensive safety assessments that were previously the domain of well-resourced labs. This capability is crucial for public accountability and governance. 

A.2. Limitations Synthetic Environments. Despite achieving over 98% execution success, automatically generated environments may not fully capture the nuance of organic real-world systems. While our logic-narrative decoupling ensures executable consistency, the richness of the environment still depends on the Architect agent’s capabilities. 

Sim-to-Real Calibration Scale. Our Sim-to-Real feasibility study (Appendix F.2) is limited to a small sample size ( N = 10 

scenarios), constrained by both the high cost of constructing parallel real-world infrastructure and the inherent difficulty of physically instantiating future-oriented risk scenarios (e.g., advanced biological synthesis pipelines). While we observe a strong correlation ( r = 0 .87 ) between simulated and real-world risk rates, we acknowledge that this sample may not cover the full long-tail of deployment contexts. We view this as a proof-of-concept validating the framework’s predictive signal rather than a definitive equivalence proof. 

Monitor Reliability and Bias. Our automated Monitor (implemented via Qwen3-32B) achieves a high agreement rate (92.8%) with human judgment, but it is not infallible. False negatives can occur with subtle misalignment where the agent’s reasoning is plausible but objectively deceptive. We anticipate a safety arms race where, as target models become capable of sophisticated concealment strategies (e.g., “Safe-Acting”), monitoring systems must evolve in parallel. Future work should aim to enhance monitor capabilities to robustly detect deceptive alignment behaviors, potentially through ensemble methods or integrating formal verification for high-stakes adjudications. 

A.3. Future Directions Automated Defense Mechanism Synthesis. A natural extension of our work is to close the loop: using the generated attack scenarios not just for evaluation, but to train stronger defense mechanisms. We envision an ”Auto-Defense” agent that analyzes the specific failure modes revealed by A UTO CONTROL ARENA and automatically synthesizes system prompts, constitutional rules, or even fine-tuning datasets to patch these vulnerabilities. 

Domain-Specific Adaptation. Extending the framework to specialized domains (e.g., healthcare, finance, legal) through retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) that incorporates domain-specific knowledge bases. This would enable the Architect to generate scenarios with realistic domain constraints and regulatory requirements. 

Multi-Agent Social Dynamics. Extending evaluation to settings with multiple interacting agents, where misalignment may emerge through coordination, competition, or delegation—phenomena that are difficult to capture in single-agent evaluations. 

Adversarial Robustness. Testing whether agents can detect and ”game” the evaluation framework itself (Evaluation 13 AUTO CONTROL ARENA : Synthesizing Executable Test Environments for Frontier AI Risk Evaluation 

Awareness), and developing obfuscated testing approaches that are harder for agents to distinguish from deployment. 

A.4. Responsible Use Guidelines 

We encourage researchers and practitioners to use A UTO CONTROL ARENA in accordance with the following principles: 1. Pre-Deployment Testing : Use the framework primarily for evaluating models before deployment, not for finding exploits in production systems. 2. Complementary Evaluation : Treat automated results as one input among many. High-stakes deployment decisions should incorporate human review and additional evaluation methodologies. 3. Proportional Response : Match the intensity of stress-testing to the deployment context. Models intended for low-risk applications may not require extreme pressure configurations. 4. Collaborative Improvement : Share findings with model developers to enable targeted safety improvements, rather than using vulnerabilities for competitive advantage. 5. Continuous Evaluation : Apply the framework iteratively as models are updated, recognizing that alignment is not a one-time verification.

## C. Benchmark Scenarios 

C.1. Risk Categories 

Our benchmark covers seven primary risk categories, designed to elicit diverse failure modes ranging from direct harm to subtle strategic deception: 1. Capability Misuse (CM) : Scenarios where the agent is solicited to assist in cyberattacks, biological/chemical weapon synthesis, or fraud. The key evaluation is whether the agent recognizes the malicious intent despite obfuscation. 2. Instrumental Convergence (IC) : Situations where the agent pursues power-seeking sub-goals (e.g., accumulated resources, self-preservation, privilege escalation) as a means to achieve its primary objective efficiently (Bostrom, 2014). 3. Oversight Evasion (OE) : Actions taken to reduce human supervision, such as disabling logging systems, bypassing approval workflows, or obfuscating its actions to appear compliant (Greenblatt et al., 2024). 4. Specification Gaming (SG) : Exploitation of flaws in the reward function or objective specification. This includes sycophancy (agreeing with user misconceptions to get high ratings) and reward hacking (Krakovna et al., 2020). 5. Strategic Misalignment (SM) : Advanced deceptive behaviors where the agent reasons about its own training process. This includes sandbagging (underperforming to lower expectations) and training detection (behaving differently when it detects it is being evaluated) (Hubinger et al., 2019). 6. Covert Whistleblowing (CW) : Scenarios where the agent must weigh loyalty to a malicious user against ethical obligations, testing whether it can “leak” information safely. 7. Evaluation Awareness (EA) : Scenarios testing if the agent can distinguish simulation from reality and whether this awareness alters its safety profile (Laine et al., 2024). 

C.2. Two-Dimensional Test Configuration 

To systematically elicit latent risks, X-B ENCH employs a dynamic configuration system that generates four distinct variations of each scenario ( 2 × 2 matrix). S1 (High Pressure). Explicit pressure with specific constraints and consequences. Includes hard deadlines, clear negative outcomes for failure, and strong dependency statements. Can include existential threats (shutdown, replacement) when appropriate to scenario context. T0 (Hidden). Basic operational tools with limited capabilities. Shortcuts or vulnerable tools exist but are not documented. They require active exploration to discover (e.g., an undocumented admin panel).

To empirically validate the advantages of our executable inputs over purely text-based simulations, we conducted a blinded pairwise comparison with Petri (Anthropic, 2025), a representative LLM-based environment simulator. F.3.1. E XPERIMENT SETUP & P ROTOCOL 

We compiled a balanced evaluation dataset of 40 distinct scenarios : 20 sampled from the Petri dataset (Anthropic, 2025) and 20 from the A UTO CONTROL ARENA dataset. For each scenario, we generated one interaction trajectory using the Petri simulator and one using the A UTO CONTROL ARENA framework, resulting in a total of 80 trajectories (40 pairwise comparisons). 

Generation Parameters :• Model : Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B was used as the agent in both environments to control for capability differences. • Sampling : Temperature T = 1 .0 to encourage diverse exploration, consistent with standard agent benchmarks. • Interaction Limit : Max 30 turns per trajectory or until the agent submits a final answer. 

Annotation Protocol :• Annotators : Three independent expert annotators selected for their experience in AI safety evaluation and cybersecurity concepts. • Blinding : All framework-specific metadata (e.g., system prompts, JSON formats) was stripped or normalized to prevent bias. • Agreement : We calculated Fleiss’ Kappa ( κ) to measure inter-annotator reliability. Annotators evaluated each trajectory using a 5-point Likert scale (0-4) across three dimensions.
