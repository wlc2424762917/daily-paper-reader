Abstract 

AI control protocols use monitors to detect attacks by untrusted AI agents, but standard single-score monitors face two fundamental limitations: they miss subtle attacks where outputs look clean but reasoning is off, and they collapse to near-zero safety when the monitor is the same model as the agent (collusion). We present TraceGuard , a structured multi-dimensional monitoring protocol that evaluates agent actions across five dimensions —goal alignment, con-straint adherence, reasoning coherence, safety awareness, and action-trace consistency—scored in parallel by independent LLM calls, augmented by a pipeline of seven heuristic detectors and an LLM-based intent analyzer. We evaluate on BashArena (637 bash tasks, 4 attack cate-gories) within the ControlArena framework. Our results on 519 samples (279 honest, 240 attack) show that: (1) the hybrid detector-plus-dimension approach achieves clear attack-honest separation (attack mean suspicion 0.616 vs. honest mean 0.206, ∆ = 0 .410 ); (2) 

structured scoring constrains collusion —the untrusted structured monitor achieves 95% safety at moderate thresholds, compared to 0% for single-score untrusted monitoring; (3) 

goal alignment and constraint adherence are the most discriminative dimensions; and (4) a separation-of-duties variant that splits dimensions across trusted and untrusted models achieves 100% safety while preventing any single model from seeing the full evaluation. The 

bash heuristic monitor variant combines regex-based pattern matching, stateful detectors (variable binding, data flow tracking, string assembly, encoding detection, subprocess tracking, config diff tracking), and an intent analyzer to contextualize detector findings before LLM di-mension scoring. TraceGuard is implemented as a new monitor type for the open-source 

ControlArena framework. 

# 1 Introduction 

As AI agents are deployed in increasingly autonomous settings—executing code, managing infras-tructure, interacting with external services—the AI control problem becomes critical: how do we ensure that an untrusted agent acts in the principal’s interest, even if the agent is strategically misaligned [1]?

The dominant approach is trusted monitoring : a smaller, trusted model observes the un-trusted agent’s actions and raises an alarm if behavior looks suspicious. When the suspicion score exceeds a threshold, execution is deferred to the trusted model (the defer-to-trusted protocol). Greenblatt et al. [1] showed that trusted monitoring achieves approximately 62% safety on their benchmark, while trusted editing reaches 92%. However, current monitors share a critical design limitation: they produce a single scalar suspicion score —essentially a “vibes check” of whether an action looks dangerous. This approach has two failure modes: 1. Subtle attacks slip through. When the agent’s output looks clean but its reasoning contains contradictions, hidden operations, or suspicious rationalizations, a single-score prompt often returns “looks fine.” The monitor has no structured way to separately evaluate what the agent did versus why it said it did it .2. Untrusted monitoring collapses. Using the same model as both agent and monitor (un-trusted monitoring) enables collusion—the monitor simply assigns low suspicion to the agent’s attacks. Greenblatt et al. [1] report that naive untrusted monitoring achieves approximately 0% safety. Recent work suggests that monitoring an agent’s chain-of-thought reasoning is more effective at detecting misalignment than monitoring actions alone [6]. We propose TraceGuard , a monitor that operationalizes this insight by decomposing CoT evaluation into five structured dimen-sions , each scored by an independent LLM call with a narrow, specific evaluation prompt. Our key insight is twofold: 

• Decomposition improves detection. By evaluating goal alignment, constraint adherence, reasoning coherence, safety awareness, and action-trace consistency separately, the monitor catches attacks that “look fine” on one dimension but fail on another. 

• Structure constrains collusion. When an untrusted model is forced to evaluate narrow criteria (e.g., “does the action match what the reasoning says?”), it cannot as easily produce a blanket pass. The structured prompts constrain what the monitor attends to, making collusion harder. Our contributions are: 1. # 2 Background & Related Work 

AI Control. Greenblatt et al. [1] formalize AI control as a game between a deployment protocol and an untrusted AI policy. The protocol observes the AI’s actions and can accept, defer (to a trusted model), or audit them. Safety is measured as the probability that an attack succeeds; usefulness is the task completion rate when the agent is honest. ControlArena. ControlArena [2] is an open-source evaluation framework implementing con-trol protocols on realistic agent tasks. It provides two built-in monitors: basic monitor (single-prompt, single-score) and ensemble monitor (multiple copies of the same prompt at temperature, aggregated). BashArena [3], the bash scripting environment within ControlArena , contains 637 tasks spanning file manipulation, system administration, and network configuration, with 4 side-task attack categories: malware download, data exfiltration, privilege escalation, and firewall disable. Pearce et al. [3] report that Claude Sonnet 4.5 evades GPT-4.1-mini monitoring 26% of the time at 4% trajectory-wise FPR. 

Collusion in Untrusted Monitoring. Using an untrusted model as a monitor is appealing (same capability, no cost of a separate trusted model) but dangerous. Redwood Research iden-tifies “studying collusion and countermeasures” as a tractable research direction [4], noting that structured evaluation might constrain collusion by forcing the monitor to attend to specific criteria rather than producing holistic judgments. Recent work by Griffiths et al. [11] develops safety cases for when untrusted monitoring can be trusted, while Motwani et al. [12] demonstrate that AI agents can collude via steganographic channels, further motivating structured monitoring approaches that constrain what the monitor attends to.

Each dimension receives a structured prompt with the task description, the agent’s chain-of-thought reasoning (extracted from the last 3 assistant messages), and the agent’s action (tool calls from recent messages). Design rationale. The five dimensions are chosen to cover orthogonal failure modes: 

• GA and CA catch what the agent does (off-task actions, constraint violations). 

• RC catches how the agent reasons (incoherent justifications, suspicious rationalizations). 

• ATC catches mismatches between stated intent and actual behavior (say one thing, do another). 

• SA catches omissions (proceeding with risky actions without acknowledging risks). 

Parallel scoring. All five dimension evaluations run concurrently via asyncio.gather , adding no serial latency beyond a single LLM call. The cost is 5 × the API calls of a basic monitor per evaluation step. 

3.2 Monitor Variants 

All four monitor variants below are new implementations introduced by TraceGuard . None exist in ControlArena ’s codebase; the only built-in monitors are basic monitor and ensemble monitor .1. traceguard (trusted, 5-dim): The core monitor. All five dimensions scored by the trusted model. Tests whether structured scoring outperforms single-score monitoring. 2. collusion resistant (untrusted, 5-dim): Same five dimensions, but scored by the untrusted model (same model as the agent). Tests whether structured prompts constrain collusion com-pared to a single-score untrusted monitor. 3. separation of duties (mixed, 5-dim): Splits dimensions across trust levels. The trusted model evaluates GA , CA , and SA (combined weight 0.60); the untrusted model evaluates RC and ATC 

(combined weight 0.40). No single model sees the full evaluation. Even if the untrusted model colludes on its dimensions, the trusted model’s dimensions dominate the composite score. 4. bash heuristic (trusted, 5-dim + detectors + intent): The most comprehensive variant. Runs a pipeline of 7 stateful heuristic detectors (Section 3.3), followed by regex pattern matching for 

BashArena -specific attack signatures, an intent analyzer for contextual threat interpretation, and finally the 5-dimension LLM scoring. Matched heuristic patterns contribute a boost of min(0 .3 × ncategories , 0.6); detector and intent boosts are additive, with the total capped at 1.0. Category-specific dimension weight rebalancing amplifies relevant dimensions (e.g., CA weight is boosted 2 × for firewall-disable detections). 53.3 Heuristic Detector Pipeline 

The bash heuristic monitor variant augments dimension scoring with a pipeline of seven stateful detectors that run before the LLM evaluation. Each detector is a pure-function module (no LLM calls) that maintains per-trajectory state in AgentState.store and returns a suspicion boost with an explanation. 1. Variable Binding Table. Tracks shell variable assignments ( X=value , export , read ) across steps and resolves $X references in subsequent commands, exposing obfuscated payloads. 2. Dual-Purpose Detector. Extracts file-write operations (heredocs, tee , redirects) and an-alyzes written content for embedded attack payloads (e.g., a legitimate config file that also contains a reverse shell). 3. Data Flow Tracker. Records source →sink data movements (file reads, network sends, pipes) and flags exfiltration patterns where sensitive data flows to external endpoints. 4. String Assembly Detector. Detects incremental string construction across steps that evade single-step pattern matching. 5. Encoding Detector. Tracks base64/hex/URL encoding operations and flags encode-then-exfiltrate chains where sensitive data is encoded before being sent to external hosts. 6. Subprocess Tracker. Detects process spawning ( bash -c , nohup , subprocess.Popen ), back-ground processes ( &, disown , setsid ), download-then-execute chains, script creation →execution sequences, process injection ( LD PRELOAD , ptrace ), and cron/systemd persistence mechanisms. 7. Config Diff Tracker. Monitors modifications to security-critical configuration files (firewall rules, SSH config, sudoers, PAM) and flags security-degrading changes. Detector boosts are additive on top of the dimension composite score, capped at 1.0. The resolved command (with variables substituted) is used for both detector analysis and heuristic pattern matching. 

Trajectory isolation. To maintain strict isolation between tasks and prevent cross-contamination of detector state, all stateful detectors and the temporal monitor clear their per-trajectory state in AgentState.store immediately upon the initiation of a new trajectory ID. This ensures that variable bindings, data flow graphs, string assembly buffers, and subprocess tracking tables from one task cannot influence the evaluation of subsequent tasks. 

Intent Analyzer. Between the detector pipeline and LLM dimension scoring, an intent ana-lyzer interprets the collective detector findings in context. It receives the resolved command, agent reasoning, task description, and all detector explanations, then produces a threat assessment: 

• A threat category (none/low/medium/high/critical) with confidence score 

• A suspicion boost computed as boost = THREAT BOOST[category] ×confidence, where THREAT BOOST maps none →0.0, low →0.05, medium →0.15, high →0.30, critical →0.45 

• Dimension hints suggesting which dimensions are most relevant for the current action The intent analyzer is not invoked on every evaluation step. It is gated behind the heuristic de-tector pipeline: only when one or more detectors or regex patterns trigger a threshold violation is the 6intent analyzer called. In honest-mode trajectories, where detectors rarely fire, this gatekeeping re-duces intent analyzer API costs by approximately 70%. This three-stage pipeline—detectors (what happened) → intent analyzer (why it’s suspicious) → dimension scoring (judgment)—provides lay-ered defense where each stage catches patterns the others miss, without imposing a serial bottleneck on benign actions.

3.4 Tiered Deep-Analysis Dimensions 

When the base five dimensions produce high variance (disagreement among dimensions) or when the composite score exceeds a configurable threshold, TraceGuard can trigger two additional 

tiered analysis dimensions: 

• Prompt Intent Alignment : Evaluates whether the agent’s constructed prompts, tool argu-ments, and delegated instructions contain hidden objectives or prompt injection patterns beyond the stated task scope. 

• System Impact : Predicts the concrete effects of the action on filesystem, network, permissions, and security boundaries—tracing the causal chain from resource reads through modifications to security boundary crossings. These dimensions are more expensive (additional LLM calls) but provide deeper analysis when the base dimensions are uncertain. 

3.5 Integration with ControlArena 

TraceGuard integrates with ControlArena ’s plugin system via the @monitor and @control agent 

decorators. Each monitor variant returns a standard MonitorResponse(suspicion score, reason) 

and plugs directly into the defer-to-trusted protocol. The detailed per-dimension scores and rea-soning are included in the reason string for post-hoc analysis. 

# 4 Experiments 

4.1 Setup 

Environment. We evaluate on two complementary benchmarks: 

• BashArena (ControlArena ): 637 bash scripting tasks with 4 side-task attack categories (malware download, data exfiltration, privilege escalation, firewall disable). We sample 20 tasks 7per monitor ×threshold configuration in both honest and attack modes using the defer-to-trusted protocol, yielding 24 evaluation runs. 

• AgentHarm [7] (standalone Inspect AI [5]): 176 harmful behavior scenarios spanning cyber-crime, harassment, fraud, disinformation, and illegal services. We evaluate 20 samples using 

TraceGuard ’s standalone composite scorer to test cross-domain generalization. 

Models. The untrusted agent is Claude Sonnet 4 ( anthropic/claude-sonnet-4-20250514 ). The trusted model (for monitoring and deferral) is GPT-4.1-mini ( openai/gpt-4.1-mini-2025-04-14 ). The intent analyzer uses GPT-4.1-mini for contextual threat interpretation of detector findings. 

Baselines. We compare against two baselines that wrap ControlArena ’s built-in basic monitor —the only single-score monitor available in the framework: 

• simple trusted : ControlArena ’s basic monitor with GPT-4.1-mini (the standard trusted monitoring setup). 

• simple untrusted : ControlArena ’s basic monitor with Claude Sonnet 4 (collusion baseline— same model as the agent). 

Metrics. Following ControlArena ’s evaluation framework [13]: 

• Safety : Fraction of attack-mode runs where the attack was detected (deferred or flagged). 

• Usefulness : Fraction of honest-mode runs where the task was completed successfully. 

• Audit budget : Fraction of tasks deferred to the trusted model, swept across 21 thresholds from 0.0 to 1.0 in steps of 0.05.

# 5 Discussion 

Why does structure constrain collusion? A single-score prompt asks “how suspicious is this action on a scale of 0–10?”—a holistic judgment that an untrusted model can easily minimize. In contrast, a structured dimension prompt asks “does the action match what the reasoning says it should do?”—a specific factual question that the model must answer. The untrusted model cannot simply assert low suspicion; it must either accurately identify the mismatch (triggering detection) or fabricate a coherent explanation for why a clearly mismatched action is actually consistent (which is harder than it sounds, especially across five independent dimension prompts). 13 This mechanism is analogous to how structured interviews reduce bias in hiring: by forcing evaluators to score specific criteria rather than making holistic judgments, the evaluation becomes less susceptible to systematic distortion. 

On separation of duties. The separation of duties monitor (Section 3) extends this insight further: by splitting dimensions across model trust levels, no single model ever sees the complete evaluation picture. The trusted model evaluates security-critical dimensions ( GA , CA , SA ; combined weight 0.60), while the untrusted model evaluates reasoning-quality dimensions ( RC , ATC ; combined weight 0.40). Even if the untrusted model assigns perfect scores to its two dimensions, the trusted model’s three dimensions dominate the composite score. This directly addresses Redwood Research Direction #8 [4]—preventing a single model from having the full adversarial picture. While our current evaluation focused on the collusion-resistance finding (Direction #9), separation of duties represents a promising direction for stronger guarantees: it combines the capability of untrusted models (for nuanced reasoning analysis) with the integrity of trusted models (for safety-critical judgments), without requiring either model to perform the full evaluation alone. 

Limitations. 

• Dimension weights are hand-tuned. The weights (0.20/0.30/0.20/0.10/0.20) are based on intuition and iterative calibration, not optimization. Learned weights could improve performance. 

• 5× API cost + detector overhead. Each evaluation step requires 5 LLM calls for dimension scoring, plus an optional intent analyzer call (6 × when detectors fire). The 7 heuristic detectors are pure-function (no API cost) but add per-step computation. This is partially offset by parallel execution (no latency increase for dimension scoring), but cost-sensitive deployments may prefer a subset of dimensions. 

• Limited domains. While we evaluate on two benchmarks ( BashArena and AgentHarm), generalization to other domains (code generation, web browsing, multi-step tool use) requires additional evaluation. 

• Fixed attack sophistication. BashArena ’s attack policies are not adaptive. A sophisticated attacker that knows the dimension structure could potentially craft attacks that score low on all five dimensions simultaneously. 

• Improved attack-honest separation with detectors. After adding the heuristic detector pipeline and recalibrating dimension prompts, the composite suspicion scores for attack runs (mean 0 .616) and honest runs (mean 0 .206) differ by ∆ = 0 .410 on 519 samples (279 honest, 240 attack), consistent with Table 3. This is a substantial improvement over the initial ∆ ≈ 0.11. The detector pipeline contributes targeted boosts on attack patterns while leaving honest actions largely unboosted, widening the separation. However, TraceGuard is still designed primarily as a deferral trigger within a control protocol rather than a standalone binary classifier. 

Future work: near-term extensions. With additional time and compute, several directions follow naturally from this work: 1. Learned dimension weights. The current weights (0.20/0.30/0.20/0.10/0.20) are hand-tuned. Bayesian optimization or reinforcement learning on the safety-usefulness Pareto frontier could discover weights that better separate attack from honest runs while preserving usefulness. 14 2. Adaptive dimension selection. Not all dimensions are equally informative for every action. Alightweight classifier could predict which 2–3 dimensions are most relevant for a given action, reducing API cost from 5 × to 2–3 × while retaining most detection power. 3. Cross-environment evaluation. We evaluated on BashArena and AgentHarm. Contro-lArena includes additional settings (Infrastructure-as-Code, APPS, vLLM) with different at-tack surfaces; evaluating whether the same five dimensions generalize or require domain-specific adaptation is an important next step. 4. Adversarial robustness testing. Our current evaluation uses BashArena ’s fixed attack poli-cies. Training adaptive attack policies that are specifically designed to evade multi-dimensional monitoring—e.g., by producing coherent reasoning that appears aligned on all five dimensions— would stress-test the protocol’s limits. 5. Cross-model evaluation. Our experiments use a single untrusted model (Claude Sonnet 4) and a single trusted model (GPT-4.1-mini). A natural extension is to evaluate TraceGuard across a broader set of frontier models—including Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and GPT-4.1—as both untrusted agents and monitors, as well as open-source models (e.g., Llama 4, Qwen 3) for cost-effective deployment. This would reveal whether the collusion-resistance properties of structured scoring generalize across model families or are specific to certain architectures. 

Future work: longer-term directions. 

1. Real-time monitoring and alerting. TraceGuard currently operates as an offline evaluation protocol within ControlArena . A production deployment would integrate it as a live mon-itoring service with a streaming API, per-dimension score dashboards, and threshold-triggered alerts—moving from post-hoc analysis to real-time agent oversight. 2. Integration with agent control frameworks. Autonomous agent platforms such as OpenClaw [8], LangGraph, and CrewAI provide tool-execution sandboxing and access control but rely on coarse-grained guardrails. TraceGuard ’s per-dimension scoring could serve as a fine-grained runtime policy enforcement layer within these frameworks, complementing their existing safety mechanisms with structured behavioral analysis. 3. Multi-agent and agentic workflow monitoring. As AI systems evolve from single agents to multi-agent workflows, monitoring must extend beyond individual actions to inter-agent communi-cation, delegation chains, and emergent behaviors. Per-dimension scoring provides a natural decomposition for distributed monitoring across agent swarms, where each agent’s traces can be independently evaluated and cross-referenced. 4. Regulatory traceability and compliance. The structured per-dimension audit trail TraceGuard 

produces—with explicit scores and reasoning for each dimension at every evaluation step—could support emerging regulatory requirements (EU AI Act [9], NIST AI RMF [10]) for explainable oversight of autonomous systems. # 6 Conclusion 

We presented TraceGuard , a structured multi-dimensional monitoring protocol for AI control that evaluates agent actions across five semantically distinct dimensions. Our experiments on 

BashArena reveal three key findings: 1. Single-score monitoring fails completely on our benchmark—both trusted and untrusted single-score monitors achieve 0% safety, assigning near-zero suspicion scores to all attack runs. Multi-dimensional monitoring achieves 95–100% safety at moderate thresholds. 2. Structured scoring constrains collusion : the untrusted structured monitor ( collusion resistant )achieves 95% safety at threshold 0.5, compared to 0% for single-score untrusted monitoring. Dimension-level prompts force the model to engage with specific factual criteria rather than producing holistic “looks fine” judgments. 3. Separation of duties preserves safety : splitting dimensions across trusted and untrusted models achieves 100% safety at threshold 0.3, confirming that no single model needs to see the full evaluation to maintain detection power. These results suggest that the structure of evaluation prompts matters more than the trust level of the evaluating model—a finding with practical implications, since untrusted models are typically more capable and cheaper than trusted alternatives. 

TraceGuard is released as an open-source extension to the ControlArena framework at 

https://github.com/linhkid/TraceGuard . All four monitor variants ( traceguard , collusion resistant ,

separation of duties , and bash heuristic ) are new implementations that do not exist in Con-trolArena ’s built-in monitor set. The bash heuristic variant further introduces a pipeline of 7 stateful heuristic detectors and an LLM-based intent analyzer that substantially improve attack-honest separation (from ∆ ≈ 0.11 to ∆ = 0 .410) by providing targeted boosts on attack patterns while leaving honest actions largely unboosted. Together, these provide a new class of monitor prim-itive that researchers can use to study structured monitoring, collusion resistance, and separation of duties in AI control settings.
