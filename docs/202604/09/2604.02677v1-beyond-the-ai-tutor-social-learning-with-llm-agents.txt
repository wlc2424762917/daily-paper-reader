## Abstract 

Most AI-based educational tools today adopt a one-on-one tutoring paradigm, pairing a single LLM with a single learner. Yet decades of learning science research suggest that multi-party interaction— through peer modeling, co-construction, and exposure to diverse perspectives—can produce learning benefits that dyadic tutoring alone cannot. In this paper, we investigate whether multi-agent LLM configurations can enhance learning outcomes beyond what a single LLM tutor provides. We present two controlled experi-ments spanning distinct learning contexts. In a convergent problem-solving study ( 𝑁 = 315 ), participants tackle SAT-level math prob-lems in a 2 ×2 design that varies the presence of an LLM tutor and LLM peers, each making different kinds of errors (conceptual vs. arithmetic); participants who interacted with both a tutor and peers achieved the highest unassisted test accuracy. In a divergent com-position study ( 𝑁 = 247 ), participants write argumentative and creative essays with either no AI assistance, a single LLM (Claude or ChatGPT), or both Claude and ChatGPT together; while both LLM conditions improved essay quality, only the two-agent condition avoided the idea-level homogeneity that single-model assistance was found to produce. Together, these studies offer one of the first controlled investigations of multi-agent LLM learning envi-ronments, probing whether the move from one-on-one AI tutoring toward richer agent configurations can unlock the collaborative and observational benefits long documented in human social learning research.

## 1 Introduction 

Learning has never been solely a one-on-one act. From early child-hood through higher education, understanding is shaped not by any single instructor but by the interplay of many, such as the class-room debate, the peer who makes the same mistake you almost made, the online thread where clarity emerges from disagreement [ 6, 55 , 89 ]. Even Sesame Street was designed around the insight that children learn different things from different characters [29]. AI-assisted education has largely been organized around a dif-ferent image. From early intelligent tutoring systems [ 3] to today’s LLM-powered platforms [ 36 ], the field has organized itself around a single aspiration of Bloom’s one-on-one human tutor, replicated at scale [ 12 ]. That goal has driven remarkable progress, and recent evidence confirms that LLM tutors can produce measurable learn-ing gains [ 45 , 54 ]. But it has also imposed a constraint that was never inherent to the technology. Nothing about LLMs requires one-on-one interaction. Orchestration frameworks (e.g., [ 53 , 95 ]) make it trivial to instantiate multiple persona-rich agents, thereby lowering the barrier to multi-agent learning environments. Meanwhile, students are already living in a multi-agent world. They consult ChatGPT, then Claude, then Gemini, sometimes within the same study session. The question is no longer whether learners will encounter multiple AI agents; it is whether we understand what happens when they do. The answer could go either way. Multiple agents could unlock peer modeling, productive conflict, and co-construction, as learning science has long shown to be effective [ 20 ,25 , 37 ]. Or they could overwhelm, distract, and fragment attention, undermining the very learning they are meant to support. We do not yet know. In this paper, we investigate whether multi-agent LLM config-urations can produce learning benefits that single-agent tutoring cannot. In particular, whether deploying multiple LLM agents, as peers alongside a tutor or as role-specialized collaborators, offers advantages that a single assistant cannot. Learning science has long shown that peer interaction does something qualitatively dif-ferent from expert instruction. Peers struggle, surface common misconceptions, and support self-efficacy in ways that authorita-tive feedback alone cannot [ 6, 20 , 93 ]. We investigate whether such dynamics translate to LLM agents, across two experiments span-ning the convergent-divergent spectrum (problems with a single correct answer to open-ended composition tasks) [ 32 ]. In the first, participants solve SAT-level math problems in a 2 ×2 design that varies the presence of an LLM tutor and error-prone LLM peers, asking whether expert guidance and peer observation jointly im-prove learning beyond either alone. In the second, participants write argumentative and creative essays with no AI assistance, a single LLM, or two role-specialized, distinct LLMs (GPT-5.2 and Claude Opus 4.6) simultaneously, asking whether multi-agent col-laboration improves writing quality and mitigates the idea-level homogenization that single-model assistance has been shown to produce [63].

Across both experiments, we find that multi-agent configura-tions can offer genuine benefits, but not uniformly or always where one might expect. In the math study, adding LLM peers to a tutor-mediated environment yielded the highest test accuracy among conditions, suggesting that peer observation has independent learn-ing value even when an expert is present. In the writing study, single-LLM assistance improved essay quality but homogenized ideas across participants; the two-agent Duo condition matched that quality improvement while restoring ideational diversity to baseline levels. The finding that homogenization is not an inherent cost of LLM assistance but a function of how agents are deployed has direct implications for the design and evaluation of LLM tools in writing classrooms, and multiple different frontier LLMs have positive externalities for society. Across both studies, peer-like configurations supported learner self-efficacy more than single au-thoritative agents did, even when they produced lower objective performance. This shows promise for the many learners whose primary barrier is motivation rather than knowledge. Together, these results suggest that the move from one-on-one AI tutoring toward richer multi-agent configurations is not merely a technical possibility but a pedagogically meaningful one, and that the design choices governing agent roles, error profiles, and interaction structure shape learning outcomes in ways the field has only begun to examine. 

## 2 Related Work 

We build our work on four interconnected bodies of research: the finding that people learn effectively from multiple sources (peers, characters, and online communities), AI tutoring systems, theories that explain why encountering diverse perspectives, errors, and conflict drives deeper understanding, and the distinction between convergent and divergent learning tasks. 

## 2.1 Learning from Multiple Sources: People, Characters, and Platforms 

A broad body of work underscores that learning is fundamentally a multi-source process. Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development [17 , 89 ] and Bandura’s Social Learning Theory [ 6 , 7] both emphasize that learners benefit from observing and interacting with multiple others, not just a single expert. In traditional classrooms, students who explain, discuss, or debate with peers deepen their understand-ing [ 20 , 70 ] and develop critical-thinking and self-regulation skills [25 ]. The teacher’s feedback is only one part of this ecology [ 11 , 40 ]; students also learn by watching peers attempt, fail, and recover—a dynamic that fosters observational learning [ 6] and co-construction of knowledge through group discussion [ 64 ]. Research on peer mod-eling and scaffolding further shows that these interactions enhance self-efficacy and prompt learners to articulate their reasoning more deeply [20, 93]. The principle that multiple voices aid learning extends well beyond classrooms. Educational television programs like Sesame Street deliberately employed distinct characters with different per-sonas, speech styles, and knowledge gaps so that children could learn different things from different characters [ 29 , 56 ]. Parasocial interaction theory [ 34 ] explains how learners form relationships with media characters and learn from them even without direct interaction. More recently, informal learning has migrated to so-cial platforms (e.g., Reddit, Stack Overflow, Discord study groups), where learners watch others argue, debug, and explain, gaining understanding from the exchange itself rather than from any single authoritative answer [ 55 ]. In these settings, the discussion among multiple voices serves as the learning resource [13]. Despite this rich tradition, we know surprisingly little about what happens when those multiple voices are LLM agents rather than people. Students increasingly consult ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and other LLMs (sometimes within the same study session), yet the learning effects of observing or participating in multi-agent AI interactions remain largely unexamined. 

## 2.2 AI Agents for Teaching 

The dominant paradigm in AI-assisted education has been one-on-one tutoring. Bloom’s “two-sigma problem” [ 12 ] framed the aspirational goal as replicating individual human tutoring at scale, which the field largely followed. From pioneering ITS like Scholar [15 ], through model-tracing and knowledge-tracing approaches in the Cognitive Tutor family [ 3 , 43 ] that proved nearly as effective as expert human tutors [ 87 ], to conversational systems like AutoTutor that prompted students to articulate and refine their reasoning [ 62 ]. Some researchers explored agents beyond one-on-one instruc-tion: embodied peers like Sam fostered learning through co-creation in joint storytelling [ 16 ]; facilitator agents prompted quieter stu-dents and moderated small-group discussion [ 18 , 48 , 82 ]; and learning-by-teaching systems like Betty’s Brain and SimStudent cast the AI as a tutee that students taught and corrected [ 52 , 57 ]. However, these agents required handcrafted dialogue trees, making them brittle, domain-specific, and difficult to scale. Today, LLMs have removed this barrier. Platforms such as KhanMigo 1, LearnLM [ 36 ], ChatGPT Edu 2, and Claude for Education 3 deliver personalized tutoring to millions, with early evidence of measurable learning gains [ 45 , 54 , 65 ] and reduced teacher workload [ 47 , 58 , 91 ]. Or-chestration frameworks such as AutoGen [ 95 ], Camel [ 53 ], and the OpenAI Agents SDK 4 now make it trivial to instantiate multiple persona-rich agents. Yet these tools remain locked in the one-on-one mold, optimizing feedback accuracy while overlooking group-interaction patterns that decades of learning science have shown to be effective [ 19 ]. Evidence also accumulates that always-on single-LLM help can encourage answer-seeking over understanding [ 8, 49 ], and lead to disengagement when Socratic agents compete with tools that simply give answers [ 83 ]. The technical barrier has fallen, but the pedagogical design space for multi-agent LLM learning environ-ments remains underexplored.

Kapur’s work on productive failure demonstrates that learners who struggle with problems before receiving instruction outper-form those who receive instruction first, because the initial struggle activates prior knowledge and reveals gaps [ 37 , 38 ]. Piaget’s foun-dational insight on cognitive disequilibrium holds that learning occurs when existing mental models fail to account for new infor-mation, creating a state the mind works to resolve [ 69 ]; D’Mello and Graesser [ 27 ] show that the resulting confusion, when managed, is a productive affective state that correlates with deeper learning. Relatedly, research on learning from errors shows that analyzing why a wrong answer is wrong is often more instructive than study-ing correct solutions alone [ 77 , 86 ]: explaining others’ mistakes deepens understanding more than receiving correct explanations [20 ], and even in LLM-mediated contexts, erroneous explanations with subsequent correction produce stronger gains than correctness feedback alone [ 45 ]. Finally, Bjork’s desirable difficulties framework [ 10 ] identifies conditions that make learning harder in the short term, interleaving, spacing, and generation, but improve long-term retention. Taken together, these theories suggest that multi-agent config-urations might lead to deeper learning than the cognitively less demanding single-tutor experience. However, this prediction has not been tested in LLM-mediated learning environments, and it remains unclear how much conflict is productive rather than over-whelming, and whether the optimal balance differs across task types. 

## 2.4 Convergent and Divergent Learning Tasks 

Learning encompasses a wide spectrum of activities, from solving a math problem with a single correct answer to brainstorming ideas for an essay with many valid possibilities. Guilford’s [ 32 ]foundational distinction between convergent thinking (arriving at one correct solution) and divergent thinking (generating multiple possible solutions) captures this spectrum and has been widely adopted in creativity research [ 21 , 72 , 73 ] and educational design [74 ]. Because the role of collaboration differs fundamentally across these task types, any investigation of multi-agent learning must account for both. Yet existing work has not systematically compared how multi-agent LLM configurations affect learning across this spectrum— where convergent tasks may benefit from diagnostic diversity among peers [ 78 , 93 ] and divergent tasks may benefit from role specializa-tion [ 33 , 94 ]. ## 3 Experiment-1: Math Learning with LLM Tutor and Peers 

Drawing on the theoretical foundations outlined in Sections 2.3 and 2.4, we designed a pre-registered 5 controlled experiment to investigate how different configurations of LLM agents affect learn-ers’ math problem-solving. In traditional classrooms, expert guid-ance (teacher feedback) and peer interaction serve distinct yet complementary functions: the teacher corrects and explains, while peers surface alternative strategies and common misconceptions. We translate this dynamic into an LLM-mediated context using a

## 3.1 Experimental Design 

3.1.1 Design of LLM Agents. All agents were powered by GPT-5.2 with distinct system prompts (provided in the Appendix). We designed three agents, each playing a different role in the learning interaction: 

Bob (Tutor). Bob acts as a supportive math tutor who guides the discussion and provides feedback on the participant’s answers. Rather than supplying solutions directly, Bob responds with clarifi-cations, hints, and encouragement (consistent with Socratic tutor-ing practices shown to promote deeper reasoning [62, 87]). 

Alice (Arithmetic-Error Peer). Alice has a strong concep-tual understanding of the material but frequently makes arithmetic mistakes (mixing up numbers or making calculation errors). She 

> 5https://aspredicted.org/rq8uz9.pdf

represents a common student profile where the underlying reason-ing is sound, but execution falters. 

Charlie (Conceptual-Error Peer). Charlie computes things accurately but sometimes misunderstands the underlying concepts, applying formulas incorrectly despite doing the math correctly. He represents the complementary error profile: procedurally fluent but conceptually fragile. We designed the two peers to reflect the two most common categories of student mistakes in mathematics: conceptual and procedural [ 77 , 86 ]. Exposing learners to both error types creates opportunities for the kind of diagnostic reasoning and schema conflict that learning-from-errors research predicts should deepen understanding [20, 45] (see Section 2.3). 

3.1.2 Interaction Design. Participants interacted with agents through a free-form text chat interface. The conversation dynamics during the lesson varied by condition: 

• Control: No chat interface or feedback. 

• Tutor Only: A chat window opened with Bob, who pro-vided feedback on the participant’s answer and guided them through the problem with follow-up questions. 

• Peers Only: A chat window opened with Alice and Charlie. One randomly selected peer presented its solution first; the other highlighted the first peer’s error and offered an alter-native. The peers also posed questions to each other and to the participant. 

• Tutor + Peers: A chat window opened with all three agents. Bob provided feedback to all learners, Alice, Charlie, and the participant, and alternated between selecting a peer and the participant to answer follow-up questions. Figure 2 shows example interactions from the experiment. In all agent conditions, participants could intervene in the chat at any time to ask questions or share their reasoning. was present, Bob responded to participant queries; otherwise, a randomly selected peer responded. Participants were introduced to the agents at the start of the session with brief descriptions of each agent’s strengths and tendencies (e.g., “Alice sometimes makes arithmetic mistakes but has strong conceptual understanding”), so that learners could calibrate their trust and critically evaluate each agent’s contributions. This simulates the mental models that stu-dents naturally form about their peers over time through repeated interaction. 

3.1.3 Problem Domain. We used five SAT-level math problems drawn from prior controlled studies on math teaching with crowd-workers [ 45 , 79 ], covering probability of independent events, solv-ing linear equations, computing average speed for a round trip, finding a missing value given a group mean, and solving a system of two linear equations. Each problem requires both conceptual understanding and procedural execution, making them well-suited to the peer agents’ complementary error profiles. For each problem, we created an isomorphic variant that shares the same structure and solution strategy but differs in surface values (e.g., a round-trip average speed problem uses 30 and 50 mph in one variant and 40 and 60 mph in the other; see Appendix for the full set). This allows us to use one variant during the lesson and another during the test, thereby controlling for item-specific memorization while preserving conceptual equivalence. 

3.1.4 Experimental Procedure. After providing informed consent, participants were introduced to the task and to the agents assigned to their condition (where applicable). Each participant was then assigned two problem categories (sampled from the pool of five) and solved them one at a time in a lesson phase: for each problem, the participant first viewed the question, submitted their own answer, and then entered a 5-minute lesson where they interacted with agents (or moved on directly to the next question in the Control condition) through free-form text chat. After the lesson phase, participants played Tetris for 1 minute as a distractor to create a brief retention interval. They then completed a test phase, solving the isomorphic variants of the same two problem categories without any agent assistance or feedback. Performance on this test is our primary outcome measure, indicating whether participants learned the underlying concepts rather than simply following the agent’s guidance in the moment. Finally, participants completed a brief survey on their perceptions of the task difficulty, confidence, how much they felt they learned, and their experience with the agents. 

3.1.5 Participants. We recruited 614 participants from Prolific, fil-tering for workers with an approval rating of at least 99% and a minimum of 100 completed tasks on the platform. All participants were based in the United States and reported English fluency. The task took approximately 14 minutes to complete, and participants were compensated at a rate of $8.17 per hour (Prolific minimum). Following our preregistered exclusion criteria, only 315 partici-pants passed our attention and authenticity checks—a substantial attrition rate that likely reflects the growing prevalence of auto-mated agents on crowdsourcing platforms [ 88 ]. This is notably fewer than the approximately 600 valid responses our simulation-based power analysis (informed by pilot data) estimated would be

Agent ratings were broadly stable across the Peers-only and Tutor + Peers conditions, suggesting that the tutor’s presence did not substantially alter participants’ perceptions of the peers.

## 4.1 Experimental Design 

4.1.1 Task. We selected argumentative and creative writing prompts from the New York Times Student Opinion series [ 51 ], choosing topics that are sufficiently open to allow diverse responses while re-maining accessible regardless of background (e.g., “In your opinion, what are the most important things students should learn at school?” 

for argumentative; see Appendix for full prompt set). We assem-bled a pool of 3 prompts for each essay type, with each participant randomly assigned one prompt from each type. Participants spent at least 5 minutes per essay. 

4.1.2 Design of LLM Agents. In the Single condition, participants have access to a single AI writing assistant, randomly assigned to either Claude (Opus 4.6) or ChatGPT (GPT-5.2). The agent provides concise feedback (no more than 50 words per message) on the participant’s writing as it develops, offering suggestions rather than solutions. This condition reflects the current status quo for how many students use LLMs for writing support: a single chatbot as a general-purpose assistant. In the Duo condition, participants have access to both Claude and ChatGPT simultaneously, each assigned a complementary role specialization. For creative essays, one agent emphasizes imagina-tion and voice (originality, expressiveness, elaboration) while the other emphasizes craft and structure (narrative coherence, literary quality, pacing). For argumentative essays, one agent takes a di-alectical approach (counterarguments, persuasiveness, argument

clarity) while the other focuses on logic and evidence (reasoning quality, organization, formal tone). Which model receives which role is randomized across participants, allowing us to disentan-gle the effect of role specialization from model-specific tendencies. When responding after the other agent, each is instructed to ac-knowledge and build on the previous feedback before pivoting to its own lens, creating a dialogic rather than fragmented feedback expe-rience. This design is motivated by transactive memory theory [ 94 ], which predicts that groups perform best when members specialize, and by writing pedagogy that distinguishes generative response to ideas from evaluative critique of craft [ 28 , 59 ]. Full system prompts are provided in the Appendix. In both LLM conditions, agents respond to participants’ writing, and participants can also initiate discussion or ask questions at any time via free-form text chat. All LLM agents have access to whatever is written in the essay box at each point. 

4.1.3 Experimental Procedure. After providing informed consent, participants were introduced to the task and to the AI agents as-signed to their condition (where applicable). Each participant then completed two writing tasks: an argumentative and a creative es-say, with the order randomized across participants. For each task, participants were instructed to write a short essay of approximately 200–300 words in response to a prompt, with a 5-minute time limit. Participants in the Single and Duo conditions could inter-act with their assigned agents throughout the writing process via free-form text chat. After both essays, participants completed a post-experiment survey.

For argumentative essays, we assess argument clarity [ 4 , 85 ], strength of evidence and reasoning [ 60 , 90 ], logical structure and organization [ 44 ], integration of counterarguments [61], persuasiveness [68], and language and formality [22]. Given the scale of the dataset, we use an LLM (GPT-5.2) to annotate essays on these dimensions. To validate this approach, two independent human raters scored a subset of 83 essays (49 creative, 34 argumentative) on the same rubric, achieving strong inter-rater agreement (Pearson

Human–GPT agreement on composite scores was also strong (Spearman 𝜌 = .77 for creative essays, 𝜌 = .73 for argumen-tative), indicating that the LLM judge reliably preserves relative quality differences across essays. Our secondary outcome is idea-level diversity among partici-pants within each condition, following the established methodology of prior work [ 5 , 63 ]. For each essay, we use an LLM (gpt-5-mini) to extract 3-5 atomic key points (distinct claims for argumentative es-says, distinct narrative elements or themes for creative essays). We embed each key point using a sentence transformer (SBERT) and compute the mean pairwise cosine similarity among all key points within each (condition × prompt) cell. To account for unequal cell sizes, we use a bootstrap procedure where for each cell, we sample 

𝑛 = 15 essays with replacement, compute the mean pairwise sim-ilarity among their key points, and repeat for 𝑀 = 1,000 rounds, yielding a mean and 95% confidence interval per cell. We aggre-gate across prompts within each condition to produce an overall similarity score. To test pairwise differences between conditions, we use a permutation test: for each pair of conditions, we pool all essays from both conditions, randomly shuffle the condition labels, recompute the difference in mean bootstrapped similarity between the two shuffled groups, and repeat for 𝑀 = 1,000 rounds to construct a null distribution; the 𝑝 -value is the proportion of permuted differences that equal or exceed the observed difference in magnitude. Higher similarity indicates greater homogenization of ideas across participants. We also collect participants’ self-reported writing self-efficacy (confidence in producing high-quality writing independently on similar future tasks), perceived helpfulness of writing support, whether the support stimulated ideas they would not have gener-ated on their own, and experienced cognitive load during writing. 

4.1.5 Participants. We report on the findings from these participants.

## 5 Discussion 

Taken together, our two experiments offer some of the first con-trolled evidence that multi-agent LLM configurations and peers can affect learning and composition in ways that a single-agent paradigm may not capture. 

## 5.1 Key Findings 

The two experiments converge on a central insight that the benefits of multi-agent LLM configurations are real but task-contingent, and their effects differ meaningfully across convergent and divergent learning contexts. In the convergent math domain, adding agents, whether peers, a tutor, or both, produced monotonically increasing learning gains on the unassisted test, with the Tutor + Peers condition achieving the highest accuracy. This aligns with predictions from productive failure and schema conflict theories [ 20 , 37 ] that learners who encountered both an authoritative guide and error-prone peers (one making arithmetic mistakes, the other making conceptual ones) were exposed to the diagnostic diversity required for deeper understanding. Importantly, peers contributed meaningfully even when a tutor was absent, suggesting that observational learning from imperfect agents carries independent value, consistent with Bandura’s peer modeling account [6]. In the divergent writing domain, the multi-agent benefit ap-peared not in quality, as both LLM conditions outperformed Control to a similar extent, but in ideational diversity. Single-LLM assistance significantly homogenized ideas across participants relative to the no-AI baseline, replicating prior findings [ 2 , 63 ], while the Duo condition was indistinguishable from Control on this measure. In educational terms, this distinction matters as writing tasks are not merely assessed for quality but are assigned precisely to develop a student’s capacity for independent thought, original argumenta-tion, and creative voice [ 9 , 31 ]. A classroom where every student’s essay converges on the same ideas, even high-quality ones, repre-sents a failure of that pedagogical goal, and raises concerns about whether LLM-assisted writing is cultivating intellectual authorship or gradually displacing it [ 49 ]. Our results suggest that this out-come is not inevitable. The homogenization concern associated with widespread AI use in writing [ 46 , 63 ] is not an inherent prop-erty of AI assistance, but a product of how agents are deployed. Role-specialized, architecturally distinct collaborators can preserve the ideational diversity that educators value, while single-model assistance, by funneling students toward a shared representational space, may quietly erode it. A second cross-experiment pattern is around how agents are perceived. In the math study, participants differentiated sharply among agents. The tutor received the highest ratings across all di-mensions, and between the two peers, Alice (arithmetic errors) was rated more harshly than Charlie (conceptual errors), despite both being equally error-prone. This asymmetry suggests that learners may calibrate trust based on the observability of mistakes rather than their severity (arithmetic errors are immediately verifiable, whereas conceptual ones are not [ 50 ]), with direct implications for the design of pedagogical peer agents. Crucially, the tutor’s ratings were stable whether it appeared alone or alongside peers, indicating that multi-agent configurations do not undermine perceptions of authoritative agents. In the writing study, the pattern inverted, as participants did not differentiate between Claude and ChatGPT on most dimensions in the given deployment setting, and perceived helpfulness declined when both agents were present together rela-tive to either operating alone. This suggests that, in writing contexts, perceived support quality may be driven primarily by task struc-ture and interaction design rather than by model identity, and that adding agents can dilute rather than multiply perceived value if the interaction architecture is not carefully managed. Moreover, multi-agent and peer-like configurations appear to support learner self-efficacy in ways that single authoritative agents do not. In the math study, participants in the Peers Only condition rated the test as less difficult and reported higher confidence than their objective performance warranted, suggesting that watching peers struggle, and succeeding relative to them, is motivating in a way that expert instruction alone is not, consistent with social comparison and peer modeling accounts [ 6 , 75 ]. In the writing study, participants in the Duo condition reported higher confidence in independent writing than those in the Single condition, even though both conditions produced essays of similar quality. Taken together, these patterns suggest a design trade-off that the field has not yet grappled with: conditions that maximize short-term learning gains (Tutor Only in math, Single in writing) are not the same conditions that maximize self-efficacy and perceived competence, and neither is necessarily best for learners whose primary barrier is motivation or disengagement rather than knowledge. There is, in short, no single optimal agent configuration. The right design depends on Social Learning with LLM Agents Working Paper, March 2026, Toronto, Canada 

what a learner needs most, and adaptive, learner-aware systems may be required to navigate this space effectively [26]. Finally, both experiments offer a consistent reminder that no AI is also a valid choice. Participants in the Control condition re-ported the least confusion in math and the least overwhelm during writing. The cognitive costs of multi-agent interaction are real, and the desirable-difficulties logic that makes confusion productive in the short term does not guarantee that learners will always benefit from it [ 10 ], particularly in time-constrained or high-stakes con-texts where cognitive overload can suppress rather than deepen engagement. 

## 5.2 Broader Implications 

Our findings arrive at a moment of rapid expansion in multi-agent AI systems. From simulated societies [ 66 ] and agentic economies [ 71 ] to orchestration frameworks built for automated task comple-tion [ 53 , 95 ], and, most recently, platforms like Moltbook 7, where hundreds of thousands of AI agents interact autonomously while millions of humans watch [ 35 ]. The momentum of agents inter-acting with one another is enormous, yet their effects on human cognition, thinking, and learning remain underexplored. This mat-ters because learning from multiple interacting voices is not new. People have long learned informally by watching others argue, de-bug, and explain, on Reddit, Stack Overflow, and Discord [ 55 ], and products like NotebookLM suggest that people find AI-generated dialogue genuinely engaging and educational [26]. The orchestration frameworks proliferating for agent-agent pro-ductivity can and should be repurposed for learning, but the design priorities must shift. The objective is no longer task-completion efficiency but the configuration of agent roles, error profiles, and interaction structures that best support human understanding [ 92 ]. Our math study shows that the specific design of agents, the errors they make, and how those errors are made legible to learners drive the learning benefit, not merely their presence. LLM peers may help address the engagement and attrition prob-lems that have historically plagued educational technology. The viral fascination with Moltbook and NotebookLM demonstrates that people are drawn to watching agents interact; our data adds that peer-like configurations can boost learner self-efficacy in ways that single authoritative agents do not, even when objective per-formance is comparable. If multi-agent setups can sustain motiva-tion where single tutor tools have struggled, they may prove more durable in practice. While we draw inspiration from human social learning theories, multi-agent AI learning is fundamentally different, and the rules of the game may not transfer wholesale. Our writing study revealed that the diversity benefit of two LLMs operated architecturally, via different models sampling from different regions of idea space, rather than experientially, through the learner perceiving genuinely distinct perspectives. The self-efficacy boosts in our math study may rest on manufactured social comparison, agents that “struggle” on command, rather than the authentic relative standing that makes human peer learning motivating over time. And Moltbook itself offers a vivid reminder of the gap that an unstructured agent inter-action produced spectacle and noise, not co-construction, precisely

These asymmetries warrant caution against overanthropomor-phizing multi-agent learning systems [ 24 , 76 ]. The temptation is to optimize for surface similarity to human peer learning (e.g., agents that disagree naturally and feel like classmates) when the actual mechanisms may differ in ways that matter for long-term calibra-tion, trust, and learning transfer [ 23 , 41 , 42 ]. At the same time, multi-agent LLMs offer genuine design affordances without human analogs, such as error profiles that can be precisely engineered, peers that can be personalized at scale, and roles that can be dis-entangled from identity through randomization. The field needs design principles informed by, but not naively imported from, the social learning theories that motivate this work. 

## 5.3 Limitations & Future Work 

Several limitations of the present work should inform the interpre-tation and extension of the findings. Both experiments recruited crowdworkers from Prolific rather than students in authentic educational settings. This is a common tradeoff in controlled learning research. Crowdsourced samples en-able precise experimental control and rapid iteration, but they intro-duce a meaningful confound: participants are primarily motivated by compensation rather than learning, which likely compresses the variance in engagement and effort that would be observed in a real classroom [ 67 ]. That we observed learning gains and behav-ioral differences despite this motivational mismatch is arguably encouraging, but the effect sizes reported here should be treated as conservative lower bounds rather than as predictions for field deployments. Experiment-1 lost nearly half of the recruited par-ticipants to attention and authenticity checks, likely reflecting the growing prevalence of automated bots on crowdsourcing platforms [88 ], and left us underpowered relative to our preregistered targets. Future work should account for this explicitly in recruitment design, and field studies with student populations in authentic learning contexts remain an essential next step. The design space we explore here is also deliberately narrow. We varied the agent count and role while holding many other factors constant: the number of agents (two peers, one tutor), the instanti-ated error types (arithmetic vs. conceptual), the role specializations assigned (dialectical vs. evidentiary in writing), and the interaction structure (free-form text chat). Each of these dimensions is itself a design variable with a large unexplored range. Different numbers of agents, role combinations, orchestration structures, and modalities could produce meaningfully different outcomes. Our experiments are a first step into this space rather than a definitive map. The tasks were also deliberately time-bounded (five-minute lessons and five-minute writing sessions), which may not reflect the dynamics of longer, more sustained learning interactions. As LLMs’ context windows expand and agentic workflows become more com-mon in educational settings, the relevant unit of interaction is increasingly a multi-hour project or a semester-long collaboration rather than a single session. Whether the peer-modeling and di-versity benefits we observed persist, accumulate, or attenuate over longer horizons is an open question. Similarly, our problem domains Working Paper, March 2026, Toronto, Canada Kumar et al. 

(SAT-level math problems and NYT writing prompts) were cho-sen for experimental tractability but may not generalize to the full range of academic tasks students encounter. Extending this work to open-ended problem-solving, scientific reasoning, or domain-specific writing contexts would strengthen the generalizability of these findings. Finally, the time and scope constraints of this work precluded deeper analysis of the interaction logs themselves, such as the content, sequencing, and quality of exchanges between participants and agents, that likely mediate the learning outcomes we observed. 

## 6 Conclusion 

For decades, AI in education has aspired to replicate the one-on-one human tutor, and LLMs have brought that vision closer to reality than ever before. But much of what we learn comes from the interplay of multiple voices. In this paper, we have taken a first step toward understanding whether that richer ecology can be instantiated with LLM agents. Across two controlled experiments, we find that multi-agent configurations offer genuine benefits, such as deeper learning in convergent problem-solving and preserved ideational diversity in divergent writing, but that those benefits are task-contingent, entail real cognitive costs, and depend critically on how agents are designed and deployed. We are entering a world in which learners will increasingly encounter not just one AI but many agents that interact with each other and collectively shape what students think, write, and understand at scale. The effects of this shift on human learning are only beginning to be studied, and we hope this work, along with the interfaces, datasets, and protocols we release alongside it, helps lay a foundation for that inquiry.
