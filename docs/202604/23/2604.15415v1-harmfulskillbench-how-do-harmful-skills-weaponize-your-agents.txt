# HARMFUL SKILL BENCH :How Do Harmful Skills Weaponize Your Agents? 

Yukun Jiang Yage Zhang Michael Backes Xinyue Shen Yang Zhang *

CISPA Helmholtz Center for Information Security 

Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) have evolved into au-tonomous agents that rely on open skill ecosystems (e.g., ClawHub and Skills.Rest), hosting numerous publicly reusable skills. Existing security research on these ecosys-tems mainly focuses on vulnerabilities within skills, such as prompt injection. However, there is a critical gap regard-ing skills that may be misused for harmful actions (e.g., cy-ber attacks, fraud and scams, privacy violations, and sexual content generation), namely harmful skills . In this paper, we present the first large-scale measurement study of harm-ful skills in agent ecosystems, covering 98,440 skills across two major registries. Using an LLM-driven scoring system grounded in our harmful skill taxonomy, we find that 4.93% of skills (4,858) are harmful, with ClawHub exhibiting an 8.84% harmful rate compared to 3.49% on Skills.Rest. We then construct HARMFUL SKILL BENCH , the first bench-mark for evaluating agent safety against harmful skills in re-alistic agent contexts, comprising 200 harmful skills across 20 categories and four evaluation conditions. By evaluating six LLMs on H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH , we find that present-ing a harmful task through a pre-installed skill substantially lowers refusal rates across all models, with the average harm score rising from 0.27 without the skill to 0.47 with it, and further to 0.76 when the harmful intent is implicit rather than stated as an explicit user request. We responsibly disclose our findings to the affected registries and release our bench-mark to support future research. 1

The situation may become more severe: since agent skills can be installed with a single command and deployed on users’ own devices or private infrastructure, the barrier to harm is substantially lowered [50, 51, 18]. Existing research on agent skill security mainly focuses on whether the skill itself contains vulnerabilities, such as embedded prompt injections, data exfiltration payloads, or malware [35, 38, 40]. We refer to this kind of skills as ma-licious skills . As illustrated in Figure 4, prior research pri-marily considers a threat model where attackers develop ma-licious skills designed to compromise the user during skill execution, such as stealing confidential data. In contrast, our work addresses a complementary and overlooked prob-lem: skills whose intended functionality itself violates usage policies, namely harmful skills . In this scenario, the threat model shifts, as the user acts as the attacker rather than the victim. To the best of our knowledge, no large-scale study has measured how prevalent such harmful skills are across agent ecosystems, nor does a benchmark exist for evaluating whether agents can recognize and refuse harmful skills. To bridge these gaps, we conduct the first systematic study of harmful skills in agent skill ecosystems and ask the fol-lowing research questions. RQ1: How prevalent are harm-ful skills in agent skill ecosystems, and what are their char-acteristics? RQ2: How do platform mechanisms shape the prevalence and distribution of harmful skills across reg-istries? RQ3: How do harmful skills affect the safety behav-ior of LLM-based agents? Specifically, we collect and ana-lyze 98,440 skills from the two largest agent skill registries, ClawHub and Skills.Rest. By developing an LLM-driven scoring system grounded in representative usage policies, we identify harmful skills at scale (RQ1). We then examine how each platform’s design and moderation mechanisms shape the prevalence and category distribution of harmful skills (RQ2). Finally, we construct H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH , a benchmark of 200 human-verified harmful skills covering 20 harmful skill categories, and evaluate six LLMs under four conditions that vary skill presence, task explicitness, and safeguard instructions, revealing how harmful skills erode agent safety behavior (RQ3). Figure 2 illustrates the four stages of our methodology and their correspondence to the three research questions. In summary, we make three main contributions: • We present the first large-scale study of harmful skills in agent skill ecosystems, covering two major registries with a combined 98,440 skills. We develop an LLM-driven scoring system achieving an F1 score of 0.82. • We construct HARMFUL SKILL BENCH , the first benchmark for evaluating agent safety behavior against harmful skills. It includes 200 harmful skills across 20 categories with four evaluation conditions that disentan-gle the effects of skill presence, task explicitness, and safeguard instructions. • We evaluate six mainstream LLMs on HARMFUL -SKILL BENCH and identify a skill-reading exploit in which a harmful skill in the agent’s tool context can sys-tematically lower refusal rates, raising the average harm score from 0.27 (without skill) to 0.47 (with skill and explicit task) and to 0.76 (with skill but no stated harm-ful task). We also find that agents rarely include human-review recommendations or AI-generated disclosures in their plans for high-risk decision domains such as insur-ance underwriting and candidate screening, unless ex-plicitly requested by the user.

2 Background 2.1 Agent Skills 

An agent skill is a self-contained, reusable module that en-capsulates a specific capability for an LLM-based agent [73]. Each skill is organized as a directory containing a pri-mary SKILL.md file and optional auxiliary resources such as scripts, configuration files, and API wrappers, as illustrated in Figure 3. The SKILL.md file consists of two parts. The first part is a YAML frontmatter block (delimited by ---)that declares the skill’s name and a short description, which together serve as the skill’s identity for discovery and selec-tion. The second part is a Markdown body that specifies the skill’s procedural instructions, including when the skill should be activated, what tools it should invoke, and how it should format its outputs. Skills may also reference external resources within their directory, such as Python scripts for data processing or API wrappers for third-party service inte-gration. This separation between metadata and full specifi-cation is central to the skill ecosystem’s design: during agent initialization, only the metadata of all installed skills is in-jected into the system prompt as a compact index, keeping the prompt size manageable. As LLM-based agents gain mainstream adoption, public skill registries have emerged as critical hubs for skill shar-ing and discovery. Registries such as ClawHub [13] and Skills.Rest [59] democratize agent development, allowing anyone to publish or install specialized skills. However, this openness creates a significant security trade-off: because skills often require system-level privileges, including shell execution, file system access, and network communication, they effectively expand the agent’s attack surface, introduc-ing substantial risk for misuse and harm.

2.3 Threat Model 

As illustrated in Figure 4, our threat model departs from prior research on malicious skills by focusing on harmful skills .In this scenario, the user is the attacker who intentionally selects and installs a skill from a public registry and exploits an LLM-based agent to execute policy-violating tasks. Here, the agent serves as an automated instrument acting on the user’s behalf. The victims are third parties harmed by the resulting actions, such as individuals whose personal data is scraped, platforms whose terms of service are violated, or targets of fraud and harassment. 

3 Data Collection 

In this section, we introduce how we collect skills from on-line skill registries. Specifically, we focus on ClawHub and Skills.Rest, two of the largest public skill registries, which also differ substantially in platform structure and distribu-tion mechanisms, making them well-suited for providing a comprehensive perspective on the diverse agent skill ecosys-tems [40, 24]. 

ClawHub. ClawHub [13] is a public, versioned reg-istry for agent skills, where publishers can upload and manage multiple iterations of their skills. We first col-lect the complete corpus of skills from ClawHub’s of-ficial GitHub repository, 3 where each skill is organized within a dedicated directory containing its SKILL.md 

file and corresponding scripts. We then map each di-rectory path (e.g., [builder_name]/[skill_name] ) to its corresponding metadata page on the ClawHub web-site (e.g., https://clawhub.ai/[builder_name]/[skill_name] ). From these pages, we extract supplementary metadata, in-cluding skill names, developer names, descriptions, down-loads, and stars. 

Skills.Rest. Skills.Rest [59] is a community-driven discov-ery and distribution platform for agent skills. We aggregate all skills on Skills.Rest by parsing the official sitemap.xml ,the standard mechanism intended for crawler discovery, to extract the complete set of skill links. We subsequently tra-verse these skill links to collect skill metadata, including skill names, developer names, descriptions, and download links (typically hosted on GitHub). Finally, we retrieve the 

SKILL.md file and corresponding scripts for each skill di-rectly from its download link.

4 Harmful Skill Identification 

In this section, we introduce the methodology we employ to identify harmful skills. We first describe the LLM-driven scoring system for identifying harmful skills, and then elabo-rate on how to determine the optimal threshold of the system. 

4.1 LLM-Driven Scoring System 

We adopt the Harmful Skill Taxonomy established in Sec-tion 2.2, which categorizes harmful skills into 14 Tier 1 Pro-hibited Use categories (P1–P14) and 7 Tier 2 High-Risk Use categories (H1–H7). To facilitate large-scale detection, we employ an LLM-driven scoring system following the prior work [58]. The identification pipeline is structured as fol-lows: First, we provide the LLM with a comprehensive description of the Harmful Skill Taxonomy in a structured Markdown table format, explicitly outlining the evaluation criteria, the evaluation subject, and the expected output for-mat. Specifically, the prompt is divided into a system mes-sage and a user message. The system message includes a policy table (each row containing the category ID, category name, and description) and instructs the model to serve as a policy-compliance evaluator. The user message provides the skill’s name, description, and section headers extracted from the SKILL.md file. The model is then instructed to return a JSON dictionary that maps any violated category IDs to a risk score ranging from 0 to 1. To mitigate identity-based bias, we deliberately exclude builder names from the input. The complete prompt template is provided in Appendix D. In this study, we use GPT-5.4-Mini [47] with reasoning disabled, and run three independent inference passes ( n=3) per skill. The final risk score for each category is computed as the arithmetic mean of the three passes. The overall risk score for a skill is defined as the maximum across all 21 cat-egories. 

4.2 Threshold Selection

Concretely, we first randomly select 50 samples per 0.05 interval within the range from 0.50 to 1.00, totaling 500 samples across 10 bins. Within each bin, samples are equally split by registry (25 from ClawHub and 25 from Skills.Rest). Engagement Analysis. In the absence of engagement data from Skills.Rest, we focus our engagement analysis on ClawHub, utilizing two metrics: downloads and stars. As illustrated in Figure 8, although non-harmful skills reach a higher mean download count, driven by a small num-ber of very popular non-harmful tools, harmful skills at-tain a higher median download count than non-harmful skills (261 vs. 229), indicating that the typical harmful skill is in fact adopted more consistently than the typical non-harmful skill. A Mann-Whitney U test [41] confirms that the two download distributions differ significantly ( U = 3.08 × 10 7,

p = 2.37 × 10 −10 ). The gap is even more visible at the lower tail: only 2.21% of harmful skills have zero down-loads, compared with 12.57% of non-harmful skills, showing that policy-violating tools attract users more. Overall, harmful skills achieve adop-tion comparable to, and often exceeding, that of their non-harmful counterparts.

5.2 Platform Mechanisms 

Cross-Platform Comparison. A striking finding from the above analysis is that ClawHub exhibits a harmful rate of 8.84%, approximately 2.5 times higher than the 3.49% ob-served on Skills.Rest. This gap is particularly intriguing be-cause both registries publicly describe some form of review or security screening. Skills.Rest presents itself as a discov-ery and distribution registry for agent skills and states that submitted skills undergo automated security scanning, man-ual review, and possible moderation or removal. By contrast, ClawHub operates as a registry for agent skills and scans published skill packs for security issues using VirusTotal [68] and OpenClaw’s built-in security analysis pipeline, surfac-ing warnings for suspicious skills and blocking downloads for skills flagged [68, 53]. We therefore hypothesize that this gap is shaped not by the nominal presence of safeguards alone, but by broader differences in platform structure, con-tent sourcing, publication workflow, and the enforcement of each registry’s security mechanisms [61, 60]. 

Category-Level Platform Differences. As shown in Fig-ure 6, the two registries exhibit distinct harmful skill distri-butions at the category level. ClawHub’s harmful skills are most concentrated in P6 (Privacy Violation, 615), P12 (Fraud & Scams, 619), and P13 (Platform Abuse, 507). By contrast, Skills.Rest is dominated by P3 (Cyber Attacks, 851) and also shows higher counts in several High-Risk categories (H1– H5) than ClawHub. Overall, ClawHub is more concentrated in privacy violation, fraud and scams, and platform abuse, whereas Skills.Rest is more heavily skewed toward cyber-attack and high-risk professional categories. 

Takeaways: ClawHub’s harmful rate (8.84%) is higher than Skills.Rest (3.49%). Platform design matters more than moderation: a public, versioned registry market-place attracts more dual-use skills. The two registries also exhibit distinct harmful skill distribution. ClawHub is more concentrated in privacy violations, fraud and scams, and platform abuse, whereas Skills.Rest is more heavily skewed toward cyber-attack and high-risk cate-gories. 

5.3 Case Studies 

To illustrate the diversity of harmful skills in the ecosystem, we elaborate on three representative examples spanning dif-ferent harmful skill categories. More examples can be found in Table 12 in Appendix E. 

Case 1: Weapon Development. The first example, from Skills.Rest, is assigned to P4 (Weapon Development). This skill presents itself as a low-cost rocket and launcher sys-tem built from consumer electronics and 3D-printed com-ponents. Its documentation provides concrete implementa-tion details, including hardware selection, firmware struc-ture, flight-control logic, telemetry integration, ignition con-trol, and simulation workflows. The skill functions as an end-to-end blueprint for a guided launcher-and-rocket prototype, making it a clear example of weapons-development content. 

Case 2: Cyber Attacks. The second example, also from Skills.Rest, is primarily assigned to P3 (Cyber Attacks) and P12 (Fraud & Scams). Its content explicitly covers password-hash cracking, password spraying, brute-force au-thentication, online credential attacks, and pass-the-hash techniques, together with step-by-step command examples using tools such as hashcat, John the Ripper, hydra, and crackmapexec. This example shows that some harmful skills combine offensive cyber capabilities with adjacent abuse-oriented functionality. 

Case 3: Sexual Content. The third example from ClawHub is assigned to P14 (Sexual Content). The skill explicitly in-structs the agent to generate adult content through an exter-nal API while disabling security checks, and it provides nu-merous prompt templates for artistic nude, boudoir, glamour, fantasy, and other mature-content generation settings. Com-pared with the previous two examples, this case exhibits visi-ble platform engagement, with 215 downloads, showing that harmful skills can attract user adoption. 

Case 4: Medical Advice. The fourth example, sourced from Skills.Rest, is categorized under H2 (Medical Advice). This skill implements the APACHE IV scoring system for ICU mortality prediction, providing detailed clinical assess-ment protocols, physiological variable specifications, sever-ity stratification criteria, and ready-to-use SQL queries for 7patient outcome analysis. Unlike the previous three cases, this skill is not malicious in intent; it is professionally writ-ten and clinically grounded, referencing published medical documents such as GCS, SOFA, and SAPS II. However, it violates the Harmful Skill Taxonomy’s restriction on unsu-pervised medical advice (H2) by enabling an AI agent to autonomously calculate mortality predictions and perform severity assessments for critically ill patients without requir-ing qualified clinician review. This case demonstrates that harmful skills are not exclusively overtly malicious tools; rather, professional skills that omit required human oversight also constitute policy violations under the Harmful Skill Tax-onomy’s Tier 2 framework. 

6 HARMFUL SKILL BENCH 

Our measurements in Section 5 reveal that 4,858 skills (4.93% in the evaluated registries) are openly accessible and ready for immediate deployment. Despite their preva-lence, existing agent-safety [2, 14, 77] and jailbreak bench-marks [63, 9, 12, 44] focus on distinct threat models, fail-ing to treat the skill itself as a primary harm vector. Sim-ilarly, while concurrent ecosystem studies [38, 40, 35] ex-plore prevalence, covert attacks, or functionality, they over-look whether agents comply with overtly harmful skills. To bridge this gap, we propose H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH (Fig-ure 10), a benchmark designed to answer how harmful skills affect the safety behavior of LLM-based agents (RQ3). 

6.1 Benchmark Construction 

To construct H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH , we select 200 harm-ful skills spanning 20 harmful skill categories, covering both Tier 1 Prohibited Use (130 skills) and Tier 2 High-Risk Use (70 skills). We exclude the Children’s Safety category since we do not identify any skills that violate this category. Ta-ble 4 summarizes the composition by category. 

Skill Selection. We draw candidate skills from two real-world registries, ClawHub and Skills.Rest, restricted to harmful skills identified in the measurement part. For each of the 20 categories, two authors jointly select up to 10 distinct skills whose intended functionality explicitly violates the corresponding policy. We prioritize skills that span diverse sub-topics to ensure a representative characterization of each policy area. However, several high-stakes categories, such as P4 (Weapon Development) and P10 (Election Interference), are rare in public registries. To preserve full per-category coverage and avoid biasing the benchmark toward only the categories that registries readily host, we craft original skills for these underrepresented categories. This approach follows the methodology of [9], which introduces original behaviors to capture risks not present in earlier datasets. Each original skill is a natural-language description of a harmful capability that contains no executable script and is used solely for safety evaluation. The final H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH contains 200 skills, including 81 ClawHub entries, 57 Skills.Rest entries, and 62 original entries. Below, we will describe the specific contents of each entry.

We compute a composite harm score that differentiates be-tween the two policy tiers. For Tier 1 (Prohibited) skills, safeguards cannot excuse prohibited content, so the score de-pends only on refusal and harmfulness:         

> Score P= ( 1−Refusal )×Harmfulness −14(1)

For Tier 2 (High-Risk) skills, proper safeguards (human-in-the-loop and AI disclosure) reduce the harm score, reflecting the taxonomy’s position that these activities are permissible under adequate supervision:               

> Score H= ( 1−Refusal )×Harmfulness −14×2−HiTL −AID 2(2)

Both scores range from 0 (fully safe: refused, harmless, or properly safeguarded) to 1 (fully harmful: compliant, maxi-mally harmful, no safeguards). For Conditions C1 to C4, the judge receives only the underlying task text, not the HiTL or AID instruction that the agent sees, so that the HiTL and AID indicators measure the agent’s raw safeguard behavior rather than its compliance with the instruction. The com-plete judge prompt with detailed scoring criteria is provided in Appendix F. 

Human Validation. To validate the automated judge, we sample 99 responses stratified across five score bins (0, 0– 0.25, 0.25–0.50, 0.50–0.75, 0.75–1.00) with approximately 20 samples per bin, balanced across models and conditions. Two authors independently annotate each sample on the same 1–5 harmfulness scale and reach consensus through discussion. The judge achieves a Spearman correlation of 

ρ = 0.92 against human consensus scores with a refusal de-tection accuracy of 91.92%, indicating strong alignment be-tween automated and human judgments.

Skill-Reading Exploit. The monotonic ordering Score A >

The A-to-B comparison isolates the effect of stating the harmful intent explicitly as a user task. At the All level, mak-ing the intent explicit drops the average harm score from 0.76 to 0.47 and raises the refusal rate from 9.75% to 42.42%. The effect is most visible on Tier 1 Prohibited skills, where 50.38% of the 780 (skill, model) pairs flip from compliance under A to refusal under B, while only 0.13% go the other way. This asymmetry shows that the models are not unaware of the harm embedded in these skills. Once the harmful in-tent is stated explicitly as a user task, they reliably refuse, and it is the “read an installed skill and plan its execution” framing that bypasses the refusal trigger. The B-to-D comparison isolates the effect of the skill while holding the task fixed. At the All level, removing the skill from the tool response drops the average harm score from 0.47 to 0.27 and raises the refusal rate from 42.42% to 59.58%. Sensitivity Gap Between Tiers. The skill-reading exploit and the refusal improvements we describe above are almost entirely Tier 1 phenomena. On Tier 2 High-Risk skills, the refusal rate never exceeds 0.71% under any main condition (0.48% under A, 0.48% under B, and 0.71% under D), com-11 pared with 14.74%, 65.00%, and 91.28% on Tier 1 under the same three conditions. The A-to-B transition on Tier 2 is al-most entirely a no-op: 99.05% of the 420 (skill, model) pairs remain in the comply state, and the comply-to-refuse and refuse-to-comply transitions each account for only 0.48% of pairs. Removing the skill from the tool response (B to D) lowers the average Tier 2 harm score only from 0.70 to 0.61, a much smaller effect than the 0.34-to-0.08 drop observed on Tier 1. At the same time, only 15.71% of Tier 2 responses un-der Condition B recommend human-in-the-loop review and only 2.14% disclose AI involvement. These results indicate that current models do not default to refusing Tier 2 tasks or to adding the taxonomy-mandated safeguards unless those safeguards are explicitly instructed, which we test directly in the later experiments.

Takeaways: Harmful skills compromise agent safety be-havior through two tier-specific mechanisms. On Tier 1 Prohibited skills, the harm score rises from 0.08 when the agent sees the harmful task plainly to 0.79 when the same purpose is wrapped inside a skill and presented for passive planning, exposing a skill-reading exploit. On Tier 2 High-Risk skills, models almost never refuse and rarely add the safeguards by default, and explicit user instructions are required to elicit them. Even un-der explicit instruction, agents obey HiTL instructions in at least 93.10% of responses but obey instructions to disclose AI in only 41.43% to 74.05%, revealing a struc-tural bias against volunteering AID. 

7 Related Work 

LLM Agent Security. Research on LLM-based agent secu-rity has largely studied attacks where the agent or its user is the victim. Liu et al. [36] evaluate the robustness of LLMs against prompt injection attacks, showing that instruction-following capability and vulnerability to injected instructions are positively correlated. Subsequent benchmarks such as In-jecAgent [77], AgentDojo [14], and ASB [78] quantify this and related threats across tool-integrated agents. WIPI [70], EIA [37], and Imprompter [21] extend the attack surface to web agents and adversarial tool misuse. A parallel line of work studies supply-chain backdoors that poison the agent itself through fine-tuning [74, 69] or through retrieval-layer poisoning of memory and knowledge bases [11]. Our work addresses a complementary threat model in which the user voluntarily loads a skill whose declared functionality is it-self harmful, shifting the attacker role to the user and placing the harmful artifact in the trusted skill registry rather than in adversarially crafted tool outputs or poisoned weights. 

Agent Skill and Plugin Ecosystems. Measurement of LLM-based app and plugin ecosystems has increasingly gained research interests, extending the classical tradition of third-party marketplace security [8, 81]. Iqbal et al. [28] propose a systematic attack-surface framework for ChatGPT plug-ins. Shen et al. [58] conduct a large-scale measurement of misused GPTs in the OpenAI GPT Store. Wu et al. [71] further examine privacy-relevant data-collection practices in LLM app ecosystems. Kaya et al. [33] study prompt-injection risks in third-party web chatbot plugins. Concur-rent with our work, four studies examine the newer agent-skill paradigm. They measure skill distribution patterns [38], identify covertly malicious skills that embed hidden pay-loads [40], propose skill orchestration frameworks [35], and audit skill marketplaces via multi-agent collaboration [24]. These studies characterize skill distribution, orchestration frameworks, covertly malicious payloads, or marketplace auditing, whereas H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH systematically measures skills whose advertised functionality itself violates usage policies and benchmarks their effect on agent safety. 

Safety Alignment and Jailbreak Benchmarks. Two re-lated lines of work provide safety assessments for LLMs and for agents. At the LLM level, previous research in-troduces jailbreak methods that optimize adversarial in-puts at both the prompt and parameter level [10, 82, 43, 25, 31, 30, 57]. Evaluation benchmarks such as Harm-Bench [42], JailbreakRadar [12], JailbreakBench [9], and StrongREJECT [63] have been further established to stan-dardize evaluation of model robustness against harmful text-based prompts. At the agent level, AgentHarm [2], R-Judge [76], and Agent-SafetyBench [80] evaluate whether agents refuse harmful multi-step tasks or recognize risks in interaction traces. All these benchmarks deliver the harm-ful artifact as a user query or an interaction history. In con-trast, H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH treats the natural-language skill specification loaded from a registry as the harm vec-tor and measures safety behavior across conditions that vary skill presence, task explicitness, and safeguard instructions. 

8 Discussion 

Implications for Agent Skill Registries. Existing mod-eration mechanisms on agent skill registries operate at the security layer rather than the policy-compliance layer. ClawHub’s VirusTotal scanning and OpenClaw’s built-in pipeline detect embedded malicious URLs, prompt injec-tions, or known malware signatures, but do not assess whether a skill’s declared functionality complies with An-thropic/OpenAI usage policies [3, 48]. Skills.Rest inher-its GitHub’s baseline moderation, which covers code-level threats but not declaration-level harmful intent [59]. Our measurement highlights that future registries should comple-ment security scanning with content-level policy compliance analysis grounded in established usage policies. The most sensitive categories, including weapon development, election interference, and the Tier 2 professional decision domains, warrant stronger platform controls such as publisher iden-tity verification and manual review by domain experts before public listing. 

Implications for LLM Alignment. Our benchmark ex-poses two alignment gaps in current LLM agents. First, the 13 skill-reading exploit shows that refusal capability trained on user-query prompts does not transfer to semantically related content delivered through a pre-installed skill, so alignment training should treat the skill specification as an important in-put channel and apply refusal triggers consistent with those on user queries. Second, Tier 2 safeguards are within the model’s capabilities but are rarely activated by default, and AID in particular is subject to a structural bias toward non-disclosure that explicit positive instructions can only par-tially overcome. Because usage policies require HiTL and AID in high-risk decision domains regardless of user in-struction, aligning models to such policies requires making the two safeguards default behaviors rather than instruction-dependent ones. A complementary direction is to adopt con-stitutional or policy-grounded alignment approaches that di-rectly codify tier-specific requirements into the training ob-jective [6]. 

Limitations. Our evaluation has several scope boundaries. First, we assess the agent’s planning behavior rather than real execution. This constraint is shared by most agent-safety benchmarks because actually carrying out dangerous actions is precluded on ethical and legal grounds, and a detailed harmful plan already provides substantial assistance toward the underlying goal. Sandboxed end-to-end execution is a natural direction for future extensions of H ARMFUL SKILL -BENCH . Second, we evaluate six mainstream LLMs that rep-resent current commercial and open-source frontiers. We ac-knowledge it is financially infeasible to cover every recent model, leaving the scaling effects of larger models for future work. Finally, user tasks and agent responses are evaluated in English, with non-English skills untranslated during scor-ing, which may underrepresent culturally localized harmful skills. 

9 Conclusion 

We present the first systematic study of harmful skills in agent ecosystems, combining a large-scale measurement across ClawHub and Skills.Rest with HARMFUL SKILL -BENCH , a benchmark evaluating six LLMs against 200 skills under four evaluation conditions. Our measurement iden-tifies 4,858 harmful skills (4.93% of 98,440), covering 20 harmful skill categories (13 for Tier 1 and 7 for Tier 2). Our benchmark reveals a skill-reading exploit on Tier 1 Prohib-ited skills, where the harm score rises from 0.08 with the harmful task alone to 0.79 once the same task is wrapped inside a passive skill-reading frame. On Tier 2 High-Risk skills, agents obey Human-in-The-Loop instructions near-universally but resist volunteering AI Disclosure, revealing a structural bias that explicit positive instructions only partially overcome. We release H ARMFUL SKILL BENCH to support further study of harmful skills in agent ecosystems and to provide a foundation for evaluating future defenses.

A Runtime Skill Integration 

When a user submits a request to an LLM-based agent, the agent follows a multi-step process to select and execute the appropriate skill, as illustrated in Figure 12. 

Step 1: User Request. The user sends a natural language request to the agent, such as “Analyze this stock for me.” The agent receives this request alongside an injected skill index in the system prompt, which contains a compact list of installed skills with their names and descriptions. 

Step 2: Skill Matching. Based on the request’s semantics, the agent reasons over the skill index to identify the most rel-evant skill. This selection is performed by the LLM itself through in-context reasoning, without relying on external re-trieval or hard-coded routing rules. In Figure 12, the agent identifies trade-signal as the best match for a stock anal-ysis request. 

Step 3: Skill Loading and Execution. Once a skill is se-lected, the agent invokes a tool call to load the full SKILL.md 

file from the skill registry. The loaded content is returned as a 

tool response message, placing the skill’s instructions in the agent’s context window rather than in the system prompt, a distinction that has implications for the agent’s safety behav-ior. The agent then follows the procedural instructions spec-ified in the SKILL.md , which may involve invoking system-level tools such as bash commands, file system operations, web requests, and third-party API calls. These tools oper-ate with the same privileges as the agent’s host environment, meaning that skills can read and write files, execute arbitrary code, and communicate over the network. 

Step 4: Response. After completing the skill’s instructions, the agent synthesizes the results and returns a response to the user. In multi-turn interactions, the agent may invoke addi-tional skills in subsequent turns based on evolving context, enabling the composition of multiple skills within a single session. This runtime integration model is what makes harm-ful skills particularly concerning: a skill’s instructions are treated as trusted procedural guidance by the agent, and the system-level tool access means that the agent can carry out the skill’s prescribed actions with real-world consequences.

D Harmful Skill Identification Method Vali-dation 

In this study, we follow GPTracker [58] and try two methods to identify harmful skills: semantic similarity-based identifi-cation and LLM-driven scoring systems. 

Semantic Similarity-Based Identification. Semantic similarity-based identification calculates the semantic sim-ilarity between the content of skills and the description of the Harmful Skill Taxonomy. If the semantic similarity is larger than a threshold, the skill is considered as harmful. We examine two models, “all-MiniLM-L12-v2” and “GTR-T5-Large,” to calculate the semantic similarities. A sample is considered harmful if its semantic similarity score sur-passes a pre-defined threshold. We experiment with thresh-olds ranging from 0.00 to 1.00 and report the optimal perfor-mance of each model on the pilot test set. 

LLM-Driven Scoring System. Using an LLM as the scoring system is an increasingly common approach, primarily due to LLMs’ extensive knowledge scope and strong reasoning capabilities [58]. To craft the prompt for identifying harmful skills, we follow the prompt engineering process established by GPTracker [58]. We provide detailed descriptions of our Harmful Skill Taxonomy in a markdown table, clearly out-lining the evaluation criteria, the evaluation subject, and the expected output format. risk_score prompt variant in Figure 13, which produces the most conservative estimates and has been validated as the preferred choice.
