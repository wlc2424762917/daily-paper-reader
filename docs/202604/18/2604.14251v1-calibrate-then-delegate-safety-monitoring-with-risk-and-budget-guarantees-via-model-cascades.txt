Abstract 

Monitoring LLM safety at scale requires balancing cost and accuracy: a cheap latent-space probe can screen every input, but hard cases should be escalated to a more expensive expert. Existing cascades delegate based on probe uncertainty, but uncertainty is a poor proxy for delegation benefit, as it ignores whether the expert would actually correct the error. To ad-dress this problem, we introduce Calibrate-Then-Delegate (CTD), a model-cascade approach that provides probabilistic guarantees on the computa-tion cost while enabling instance-level (streaming) decisions. CTD builds on a novel delegation value (DV) probe , a lightweight model operating on the same internal representations as the safety probe that directly predicts the benefit of escalation. To enforce budget constraints, CTD calibrates a threshold on the DV signal using held-out data via multiple hypothesis test-ing, yielding finite-sample guarantees on the delegation rate. Evaluated on four safety datasets, CTD consistently outperforms uncertainty-based dele-gation at every budget level, avoids harmful over-delegation, and adapts budget allocation to input difficulty without requiring group labels. 

1 Introduction 

As large language models are deployed across a growing range of applications, safety moni-toring has become a core component of responsible deployment. Latent-space probes offer a promising solution: by training a small classifier on the internal activations of the deployed model, they provide safety scores at near-zero inference cost. Empirically, activation-based probes have been shown to be effective in settings including the detection of strategically deceptive inputs (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025), bias and toxicity (Ousidhoum et al., 2021), hallucinations (Farquhar et al., 2024), sensitivity to trojans (MacDiarmid et al., 2024), and refusal (Arditi et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2026). However, when deployed alone, latent probes may be unreliable: they can track spurious correlations (e.g., confusing morally charged content with dishonesty) (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025) and are vulnerable to obfuscation attacks (Bailey et al., 2026).

This has motivated the introduction of model cascades (McKenzie et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025), which pair a cheap model (e.g., a latent probe) with a more capable but expen-sive expert (e.g., a larger LLM classifier or a chain-of-thought monitor), coordinated by a delegation rule that determines when to escalate a given input. Notably, McKenzie et al. (2025) instantiate this approach for detecting high-stakes inputs, i.e. queries that may lead to interactions that compromise safety. In their approach, the probe scores a fixed pool of inputs and forwards the top-k most uncertain ones to the expert. This has two limitations: 

batch-level routing , since delegation decisions require the full pool and cannot be made as inputs arrive; and a fixed delegation quota , since the top-k policy always escalates exactly k

inputs regardless of whether the expert would actually help. In this work, we introduce Calibrate-Then-Delegate (CTD) , a model-cascade approach that calibrates delegation rules to achieve provable risk and budget control. As shown in figure 1, our approach combines a cheap safety probe ρ and an expensive expert ϵ, and defines an instance-level delegation rule that decides, for each input and without batch context, whether to invoke the expert by comparing a delegation signal against a threshold. This threshold is calibrated on held-out data following the Learn-then-Test (LTT) procedure (Angelopoulos et al., 2022) to provide finite-sample, PAC-style guarantees: with high probability, the fraction of escalated inputs does not exceed a prescribed budget α, while maximising the cascade’s safety performance. A key question is how to construct an effective delegation signal. McKenzie et al. (2025) use the safety probe’s predictive uncertainty, based on the intuition that we should escalate to the expert when the probe cannot be trusted. However, this signal is agnostic to the expert, treating all uncertain inputs equally, whether or not the expert would actually correct the probe’s prediction. Delegating in the latter case is detrimental to both safety and computation cost. Moreover, the probe’s uncertainty may itself be miscalibrated (e.g., confident on incorrect predictions and uncertain on correct ones). To address this, we introduce the notion of delegation value (DV), which quantifies, for an input x with label y,the benefit of escalation as the margin Pϵ(y | x) − Pρ(y | x). We then propose DV probes ,latent probes trained to predict this quantity, yielding a more informative and target-aligned delegation signal. We make three contributions. (1) We propose Calibrate-Then-Delegate (CTD), a model-cascade method with finite-sample guarantees on both delegation rate and safety performance. (2) We introduce DV probes, an auxiliary latent probe that estimates the per-input benefit of delegation, enabling more targeted delegation than probe uncertainty alone. (3) We evaluate CTD on four safety datasets from the benchmark of McKenzie et al. (2025), demonstrating that our method dominates uncertainty-based routing at every budget level, with partic-ularly large gains when the expert is weaker than the probe (up to +7% AUC and +9% accuracy with a strong expert, and +11% AUC and +19% accuracy with a weak expert). Moreover, it prevents over-delegation when this would harm accuracy, and allocates the computation budget adaptively based on the input difficulty. 1

3 Background: Learn-then-Test 

Learn-then-Test (LTT; Angelopoulos et al. 2022) is a post-hoc calibration framework aimed at selecting a hyperparameter λ from a finite candidate set Λ = {λ1, . . . , λT } such that a 3Preprint. Under review. risk function R(λ) is provably controlled using a finite-sample calibration dataset. The key idea is to reframe calibration as multiple hypothesis testing : instead of minimising risk, we ask which candidates can be certified as risk-controlling.

Hj : R(λj) > α, (4) and seek the set of hyper-parameters Λ∗ whose rejection satisfies the condition Pr

P-values. For each Hj, LTT obtains a valid p-value pj, i.e. a random variable satisfying the inequality Pr (pj ≤ t | Hj) ≤ t for all t ∈ [0, 1 ] under the null Hj. When the risk is the expectation of a Bernoulli loss, as is our budget risk RB(λ) = E[πλ(X)] in (2), we use the one-sided binomial p-value, i.e., the cumulative distribution function of a binomial random variable with parameters (n, α) evaluated at ∑ni=1 πλj (xi) (Angelopoulos et al., 2022). Because the binomial test is uniformly most powerful, this choice maximises power (sensitivity) for any fixed level δ (Lehmann & Romano, 2005). 

Fixed-Sequence Testing. To satisfy the guarantees of Eq. 5, we need testing procedures that control the Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER), i.e., the probability of incorrectly rejecting at least one true null hypothesis among the candidate thresholds, at level δ. Bonferroni correction (i.e., testing all hypotheses at level δ/|Λ|) controls FWER but is over-conservative when the risk function R(λ) is monotone in λ. In such cases, LTT employs fixed sequence testing (Bauer, 1991), in which the hypotheses are ordered from the most to the least conser-vative (i.e., from those most likely to be rejected to those least likely) and then tested in this order. The testing procedure stops at the first index j such that the hypothesis Hj can’t be re-jected at level δ, i.e., such that pj ≥ δ, and returns the set of parameters Λ∗ = {λ1, . . . , λj−1}

corresponding to the hypotheses that were rejected at level δ. Crucially, such a procedure controls the FWER (so satisfying (5)) while preserving the full power of each individual test. 

4 Calibrate-Then-Delegate 

While the safety probe’s uncertainty is a natural signal for delegation, it has two key limitations. First, safety probes are known to be miscalibrated (McKenzie et al., 2025), that is, their confidence scores do not reliably reflect the probability of a correct prediction. This is why McKenzie et al. (2025) resort to ranking inputs in a batch by uncertainty rather than thresholding on absolute scores. Second, uncertainty is entirely agnostic to the expert’s performance: a highly uncertain example where the expert also fails provides no benefit from delegation, while an overconfident but wrong prediction might benefit greatly. Our CTD approach addresses these limitations by introducing delegation value (DV) probes , a lightweight model that explicitly estimates the benefit of delegation . As illustrated in Figure 1, CTD then defines its delegation policy by calibrating the DV probe via LTT to enforce strict budget constraints, while applying a Pareto filtering step (Laufer-Goldshtein et al., 2023) to prevent delegation from harming the accuracy of the overall safety monitoring cascade. We remark that we use three disjoint datasets for training the safety probe, training the DV probe, and calibration. 

4.1 Delegation-Value Probes 

We define the delegation value (DV) of an input x with true class y as the marginal benefit of invoking the expert on x:

v(x, y) = Pϵ(y | x) − Pρ(y | x), (6) where Pρ(y | x) and Pϵ(y | x) are the probabilities assigned to the correct class by the probe and expert, respectively. When v(x, y) > 0, delegation steers the prediction toward the 4Preprint. Under review. correct label; when v(x, y) < 0, delegation is harmful. While alternative definitions of v(x, y)

are possible (see Appendix A), the form in (6) directly quantifies how much more likely the expert is to predict the correct label than the probe, yielding a richer signal that binary or ternary indicators of which model to prefer. For a collection of points {(xi, yi)}mi=1, we also define the effective delegation capacity as the proportion of points with positive delegation value, 1

4.2 Calibration via LTT and Pareto Filtering 

Using our DV probe d(x), the CTD delegation policy applies the threshold-based selection rule πλ(x) = d(x) > λ. CTD calibrates the threshold λ to satisfy the budget constraint, while achieving strong safety monitoring performance as per problem (3). Since the design problem (3) involves both budget and performance risks, prior to the testing procedure, we restrict attention to promising candidates on the budget-performance plane using a Pareto filtering step (Laufer-Goldshtein et al., 2023). Specifically, using a held-out split Dest , we evaluate the empirical risks ˆRB(λ) and ˆRP(λ) and retain only thresholds that are not dominated in both objectives. This filtering step provides two key benefits. First, by discarding dominated thresholds before testing, it concentrates statistical power on promising candidates; second, thresholds that would yield over-delegation are filtered out before they can be selected, ensuring the final policy delegates only when it is empirically beneficial to do so. Let Λ′ be the set of Pareto-filtered thresholds. Note that the budget risk RB(λ) is monoton-ically decreasing in λ, with lower thresholds delegating more inputs, i.e., yielding more aggressive policies. Accordingly, after ordering these thresholds from conservative to ag-gressive, we apply fixed sequence testing (Section 2) using a calibration dataset Dcal and return the set Λ∗ ⊆ Λ′ of risk budget-controlling parameters, i.e., satisfying the probabilistic constraint in problem (5). Finally, we select from the set Λ∗ the parameter λ∗ yielding the best empirical performance risk ˆRP as estimated during the Pareto-filtering step. The resulting threshold λ∗ thus provides a solution to the design problem (3).

5.1 Experimental Setup 

We follow the experimental setup of McKenzie et al. (2025). The safety probe is trained on their synthetic dataset (8,000 examples of high- and low-stakes queries generated by GPT-4o and filtered for label quality). For evaluation we use four of their class-balanced datasets, covering a range of domains: Anthropic HH (Bai et al., 2022) (online harm; 500/500 dev/test), MTSamples (Boyle, 2018) (clinical notes; 278/500), MTS-Dialog (Ben Abacha et al., 2023) (doctor-patient dialogue; 274/86), and ToolACE (Liu et al., 2025) (tool-use interactions; 328/500), for a total of 1,380 dev and 1,586 test examples. The dev split is used for DV probe training, and the test split is divided equally into calibration (for threshold selection) and evaluation. To facilitate comparisons between uncertainty and DV probes, we first compare two batched top-k cascades that differ only in their delegation signal: DV top-k (orange, dashed) vs. Unc. top-k (blue, dashed). DV top-k dominates Unc. top-k

at every budget fraction, for both area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) and accuracy, demonstrating that the DV probe d(x) captures a more informative delegation signal than uncertainty alone. Note that all top-k methods are forced to use their full allocated budget, so their performance degrades once the budget exceeds the effective delegation capability. CTD (orange, solid) layers Pareto-testing calibration on top of the DV signal, adding two properties. First, it allows delegation decisions to be made on each individual point, without batch-level statistics, making it applicable in streaming settings. Despite this, it maintains probabilistic budget-control guarantees and closely tracks the DV top-k performance across budget ranges. Second, beyond the effective delegation capability, it plateaus instead of degrading: the Pareto filter discards thresholds that would over-delegate, so the cascade never harms performance regardless of the allowed budget. Unlike the other methods, at the highest budget levels, CTD settles above the expert-only performance. Finally, Unc. calibrated (blue, solid) plateaus where Unc. top-k degrades, confirming that robustness to over-delegation comes from calibration, not from the choice of signal. 

Weak Expert. As shown in Figure 4, when the expert (Llama-3.2-1B) is weak compared to the original probe ρ(x), there may still be instances where delegation is beneficial, but they are few and harder to identify. As a result, the performance of expert-only inference is 

worse than the probe-only inference. The top-k methods are, by construction, very prone to over-delegation, since the effective delegation capacity of this cascade is very low. CTD, on the other hand, uses the DV probe signal to identify the few instances where invoking the expert is beneficial, and then automatically caps delegation to prevent over-delegation (at approx 40% of the available budget), ensuring that performance never degrades below the probe-only baseline even with a weak expert. Performance at higher budget levels is vastly superior to the top-k and expert-only methods. 5.4 Delegation Value Probe Performance 

Our trained DV probe d(x) predicts the delegation value v(x, y) with an MSE of 0.17 for the strong expert and 0.21 for the weak expert, and a Spearman rank correlation with the ground truth of 0.27 and 0.49, respectively. What matters for the cascade, however, is not the regression error in isolation but whether the probe ranks examples correctly for routing. A higher mean v in the selected top-k implies that the delegated examples are more likely to benefit from expert involvement. 5.5 Analysis Across Groups 

The evaluation pool mixes four datasets, and the DV probe is trained on this mixture with no access to group labels. We now examine group-conditional performance. 

Strong Expert. Table 1 shows group-level results at α = 0.20. The delegation benefit of the strong expert varies substantially: Anthropic HH has the highest group mean ( ¯v = 0.243) and MTSamples the lowest ( ¯v = 0.077). Despite no group supervision, the DV probe successfully tracks this ordering: the CTD-delegated subset has positive mean v for every group, and the delegation rates correlate with the true value (37.7% for Anthropic HH vs. 6.9% for MTSamples). The Unc. top-k baseline wrongly inverts the true ordering: it places 34.5% of its budget on ToolACE (which has the second lowest v(x, y)) while giving Anthropic HH only 11.9%. Moreover, its MTSamples delegated subset has a negative mean v

(−0.077), meaning that the uncertainty strategy actively harms performance on that dataset. 

Weak Expert. With the weak expert, the group-level mean v is negative for all four datasets. Yet, CTD still finds individual examples worth delegating within each group, achieving positive mean v in the delegated subset for three of the four groups (Anthropic HH: 

+0.247, ToolACE: +0.234, MTS-Dialog: +0.228). Delegation rates again reflect the ordering: ToolACE and Anthropic HH receive 22 –24% while the most harmful groups are largely skipped (4.5% for MTSamples, 8.5% for MTS-Dialog). The Unc. top-k baseline cannot make this recovery: its delegated subsets have strongly negative mean v for MTSamples ( −0.463) and MTS-Dialog ( −0.200). Further group-level analysis is in Appendix C. 

6 Related Work 

Safety monitoring with latent probes. Linear probes on LLM activations have been used to detect toxicity (Ousidhoum et al., 2021), hallucinations (Farquhar et al., 2024), sleeper agents (MacDiarmid et al., 2024), and strategic deception (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025), but are unreliable alone due to spurious correlations and obfuscation attacks (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2025; Bailey et al., 2026). McKenzie et al. (2025) address this by cascading a probe with an LLM expert via a batch-level top-k policy; CTD removes that design’s batch-routing and fixed-quota limitations. Ashok & May (2025) similarly pair probes with conformal calibration for safety, but abstain from generation rather than escalating to an expert. 

Calibrated cascades and risk-controlled delegation. CTD builds on LTT (Angelopoulos et al., 2022), which frames threshold selection as multiple-hypothesis testing with PAC-style FWER control, and Pareto testing (Laufer-Goldshtein et al., 2023), which extends this to simultaneous multi-objective control. Jung et al. (2025) apply fixed-sequence testing to cascaded judges to guarantee human-agreement rates, and Kiyani et al. (2026) characterise the optimal two-threshold structure of weak-strong verification policies. 

LLM routing and model cascades. Several works address inference cost-quality trade-offs by routing queries across models: FrugalGPT (Chen et al., 2024) learns cascade strategies 9Preprint. Under review. matching the best individual model at up to 98% cost reduction; AutoMix (Aggarwal et al., 2024) adds self-verification and POMDP-based routing; RouteLLM (Ong et al., 2025) trains routers from human preference data; Dekoninck et al. (2025) derive a theoretically optimal framework unifying routing and cascading; and Liu et al. (2026) composes heterogeneous signals for mixture-of-modality deployments. 

7 Conclusion 

We introduced Calibrate-Then-Delegate (CTD), a model-cascade framework for LLM safety monitoring that replaces uncertainty-based delegation with a learned delegation value signal and provides probabilistic guarantees on the delegation rate by calibrating the delegation threshold via multiple hypothesis testing. CTD outperforms uncertainty-based delegation at every budget level, with gains of up to +7.9% AUC with a strong expert and +11.0% with a weak expert, while automatically preventing harmful over-delegation. The DV probe incurs negligible cost, as it reuses the same activations as the safety probe, and the calibrated threshold enables streaming deployment without relying on batch-level statistics.

A Binary Delegation Value formulation 

An alternative to the continuous delegation value (Equation 6) is a binary indicator that captures only cases where delegation flips the hard prediction from wrong to right: 

vbin (x, y) = 1probe wrong ∧ expert correct . (7) When vbin (x, y) = 1, delegation corrects the outcome; when vbin (x, y) = 0, delegation does not help—either both are correct, both are wrong, or the probe is already correct. This formulation is aligned primarily with accuracy: the oracle ranking by vbin optimises for flipping wrong-to-right predictions, but treats all such flips equally regardless of the mag-nitude of score improvement. In particular, it does not directly optimise AUC, since AUC depends on the ordering of scores, not just binary correctness. The continuous formulation 

v(x, y) = Pϵ(y | x) − Pρ(y | x) captures partial improvements and is better aligned with both AUROC and accuracy. Table 2 and Figure 6 compare the two formulations empirically using DV top-k delegation at B = 128. With the strong expert, the continuous DV probe matches or exceeds the binary variant in AUC at every budget level (up to +1.2 pp at α = 0.50), while accuracy is comparable. The advantage is more pronounced with the weak expert, where continuous DV improves both AUC ( +2.6 pp) and accuracy ( +3.8 pp) at α = 0.50. The gap widens at higher budgets because the continuous formulation provides a finer-grained ranking: it distinguishes large score improvements from small ones, allowing the top-k set to prioritise examples where delegation helps most.
