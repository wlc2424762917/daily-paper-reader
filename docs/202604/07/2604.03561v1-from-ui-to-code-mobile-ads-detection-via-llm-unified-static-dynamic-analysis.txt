## Abstract 

Mobile advertisements (ads) are essential to the app economy, yet detecting them is challenging because ad content is dynamically fetched from remote servers and rendered through diverse user interfaces (UIs), making ads difficult to locate and trigger at runtime. To address this challenge, we present A DWISE , a novel framework that formulates mobile ads detection as LLM-guided, ad-oriented UI exploration. A DWISE first performs static program analysis to identify UI widgets used to place ads, which we call ad widgets . It then uses a grounded LLM reasoning loop to navigate toward and trigger these widgets under three complementary do-main guidance signals: (1) WTG-based guidance , which provides global transition priors from a statically constructed window transition graph (WTG); (2) semantic guidance , which reasons over app functionality to prioritize user-likely interaction paths; and (3) structural guidance , which applies retrieval-augmented generation to match the current UI against recurring ad-heavy layouts from a knowledge base. By combining static program analysis with LLM-based reasoning over UI struc-ture, app semantics, and retrieved analogies, A DWISE enables more effective ads detection in complex mobile UIs. Experiments on 100 benchmark apps show that ADWISE outperforms state-of-the-art baselines by 25.60% in ad widget detection. In addition, A DWISE uncovers 34.34% more ad regulation violations across six categories, directly benefiting downstream ad regulation. 

## 1 Introduction 

Mobile advertisements, commonly known as mobile ads, are prevalent in over 57% of Google Play apps (Viennot et al., 2014), serving as a key source of engagement and revenue for developers (app, 2024; Google AdMob, 2024). While ads help monetize apps and promote app downloads, unregulated ad practices can degrade user experiences (Yan et al., 2023; Manager, 2024; Zhao et al., 2023b) and compromise app ecosystem integrity (Rastogi et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020; Xing et al., 2015). For instance, as shown in Figure 1, users may encounter disruptive ads that appear unexpectedly during loading, unskippable ads that trap users, or malvertising ads with deceptive content that leads to malware downloads. Although mobile platforms and ad libraries attempt to curb these behaviours through strict policies, vetting ad compliance at scale is non-trivial, and many apps that violate ad regulations manage to bypass review processes and reach a wide user base. Recognizing this problem, prior research has proposed dynamic UI exploration-based methods to detect mobile ads and check for regulation violations (Liu et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2024; Rastogi et al., 2016; Cai et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023b), but these efforts rely on a content-based detection workflow that is inherently inefficient and brittle. Specifically, they (1) navigate through the app using general-purpose UI exploration techniques and (2) check regulation violations by verifying each UI content against predefined rules. However, general-purpose explorations are optimized for broader code coverage and thus waste resources on UI exploration paths that do not lead to ads (Liu et al., 2022; Ran et al., 2023; 2024). Besides, relying solely on content ignores underlying code behaviors and fails to generalize when UI content is dynamic. For example, the ads in Figure 1 (b) and (c) will show different content after reloading the app, easily bypassing the content check. 1

Our Insights. A key to overcoming this challenge lies in steering UI exploration to pinpoint 

ad widgets (i.e., the UI components rendering ads), and rigorously examining both their code behaviors and content for signs of non-compliance . Fundamentally, these widgets serve as the central mechanism that determines where ads appear, how users interact with them, and what content is delivered upon triggering. To achieve this, we propose A DWISE , a novel approach that harnesses Large Language Models (LLMs) to unify static ad analysis and dynamic UI exploration for effective ad widget detection and mobile ad regulation . In particular, while static analysis is capable of identifying potential ad widgets (i.e., what to look for), it often generates infeasible paths to the UIs containing these widgets (i.e., 

where to find them) (Liu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2015a). Such imprecision renders a straightforward integration of static analysis and dynamic UI exploration ineffective, as it misguides exploration along infeasible paths. We bridge this gap by identifying two fundamental domain patterns in ad widget placement that current static analysis fails to capture: (1) Semantic Pattern (The “User” Perspective) . Ad widgets are often placed on exploration paths directly tied to the app’s specific purpose and functionality, which are frequently visited by the app’s target users (e.g., popup ads appearing within a music app’s playlist). (2) Structural Pattern (The “Developer” Perspective) . Conversely, many ad widget placements ignore specific app semantics and instead rely on generic, repetitive UI layouts common across the ecosystem (e.g., interstitial ads during loading states, or standard settings menus). Unfortunately, the varying functionalities and diverse UIs of apps make it challenging to establish consistent rules for finding ad widgets placed by these two patterns. Our key insight is that LLMs are uniquely positioned to address this duality due to their extensive training on real-world UIs and code. They can comprehend an app’s metadata from the app market to infer user intent (addressing the Semantic Pattern) and identify similar UI layouts from information provided by Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) to recognize developers’ conventions (address-ing the Structural Pattern). By leveraging this capability, ADWISE can effectively unify static ad analysis with dynamic exploration to pinpoint ad widgets. 

Our Approach. Based on the above insights, A DWISE is designed to have two phases: 

Phase 1: Static Ad Analysis. ADWISE first performs static analysis to identify candidate ad widgets from both UI attributes and code behaviors. Specifically, it conducts an over-approximate dataflow analysis to recover each widget’s view class, resource-id, and method invocations, and then applies inference rules to detect different types of ad widgets based on their ad-showing logic. A DWISE also constructs a static WTG over app UIs, locates candidate ad widgets in the corresponding windows, and derives shortest-path signals that indicate how these UIs may be reached. 

Phase 2: Ad-Oriented UI Exploration. ADWISE then casts UI exploration as a grounded LLM agent loop for targeted ad widgets detection. At each step, the agent first checks whether the current UI contains any candidate ad widgets recovered by static analysis, and directly triggers them using their resource-ids to collect the resulting ad content. If unexplored ad widgets remain, the agent selects 2Preprint. Under review. the next UI action under three complementary domain guidance signals: (1) WTG-based guidance ,which provides a global navigation prior derived from the static WTG; (2) semantic guidance , which summarizes the app’s intended functionality from market metadata so the agent can follow user-likely exploration paths; and (3) structural guidance , which retrieves similar ad UIs from a knowledge base so the agent can exploit recurring developer-side placement patterns. To reduce hallucination in this reasoning process, A DWISE further incorporates action reflection and UI state verification : after each step, the agent checks whether the intended transition actually occurred, records failed actions, and prunes invalid or non-interactable UI elements before the next decision. This design lets the LLM reason globally, act locally, and adapt online to infeasible transitions and noisy UI states. A DWISE 

repeats this loop until all reachable ad widgets are triggered or the exploration budget is exhausted. Extensive evaluation on 271 ad widgets shows that A DWISE outperforms six baseline methods, achieving at least 25.60% higher detection rate and running at least 8.68% faster. Meanwhile, ADWISE successfully identifies 133 regulation violations, exceeding the best-performing baseline by at least 34.34% . These results demonstrate A DWISE ’s effectiveness in mobile ad detection and its practical value for supporting ad regulation. More broadly, A DWISE shows how LLMs can serve as a reasoning layer that reconciles imprecise program-analysis outputs with dynamic UI exploration, enabling more effective task-oriented exploration in complex mobile environments. 

## 2 Preliminaries 

2.1 Preliminary Study on Ad Widgets Dataset. To better understand different types of ad widgets, we conduct a preliminary study on a dataset built from AndroZoo (Allix et al., 2016a), a well-known and frequently updated collection of Android applications. We choose apps from AndroZoo based on three criteria: (1) the apps must be released or updated after 2022; (2) they need to include ad libraries, indicating they have mobile ads; (3) their download numbers from the app market must vary, as apps with different levels of popularity may use ads differently. After filtering AndroZoo using all these criteria, we sample 100 apps, which span across 35 app categories, varying in popularity from 10, 000 to 100 million downloads and last updated after 2022, well representing the latest advertising practices. We then manually analyze each app to record the ad widgets. We first execute the apps on real devices, as ad libraries do not show ads on emulators to avoid fraud. We then explore the apps, navigate to all the ad UIs, and trigger the ad widgets. During this process, we develop a label tool to record the screenshots of ad UIs and the ad content and dump the hierarchies of ad UIs. Additionally, we decompile the apps using dex2jar (Github, 2024), and manually inspect the code of ad widgets. More details of our preliminary study can be found in Appendix A.

2.2 Definitions of Ad Widgets 

We first introduce the common definitions of UI layout and widgets in mobile apps. 

Definition 1 (UI Layout, Widget) A UI layout L(W ) is a rooted tree of widgets W , where each node w(v, id , I) ∈ W represents a widget, with v denoting the view class, id being a unique layout/resource identifier, and I = {i1, i2, ..., in} being a set of invocation events, where each invocation event irepresents a method call made on the widget object. w is uniquely identified using both L.id and w.id .

Based on the observations from the preliminary study, we formally define ad widgets. 

Definition 2 (Ad Widget) An ad widget wa is a widget used to place ads. We categorize ad widgets into three types according to their implementation characteristics: (1) adview widgets , which are specialized views created by ad libraries to display ads; (2) popup widgets , which invoke ad library APIs to show ads; and (3) native widgets , which redirect users to advertised content in either an app market or a website. 

2.3 Problem Definition 

Let an app contain a set of UI layouts L = {L1, L2, . . . , Lm}. Each UI layout L(W ) ∈ L is a rooted tree of widgets, following Definition 1. Our goal is to identify a subset of UI layouts La ⊆ L such that each L(W ) ∈ La contains ad widgets Wa ⊆ W , and to trigger them at runtime. Formally, for each 

wa(v, id , I) ∈ Wa, the goal is to execute at least one invocation event in I during app execution: 

∀L(W ) ∈ La, ∀wa(v, id , I) ∈ Wa, ∃i ∈ I such that i is executed at runtime. In other words, the task is to dynamically explore the app, reach the corresponding UI layout of each ad widget, and execute its invocation event(s). 

Why Pure Dynamic UI Exploration Fails? Without prior knowledge of which widget is an ad widget, a dynamic explorer must treat every widget in every reachable UI layout as a candidate and exhaustively try possible interactions. Since real-world apps often contain many UI layouts and widgets, this leads to severe UI and widget state explosion, making pure dynamic exploration inefficient and often ineffective. 

Our Key Idea. To address this challenge, A DWISE first performs static analysis to identify ad widgets and their associated UI layouts. Formally, A DWISE computes a set of ad-widget tuples 

A = {(L, wa(v, id , I)) },where each tuple specifies where an ad widget is located and how it can be triggered. At runtime, ADWISE then performs targeted dynamic UI exploration with two objectives: (1) navigate to the UI layout L containing the ad widget, and (2) trigger the widget using its identifier id and invocation events I.

3.2 Ad-Oriented UI Exploration 

We next describe how A DWISE uses static analysis results and ad widget placement strategies to guide LLM-based UI exploration toward ad UIs and to trigger ad widgets. 

3.2.1 Domain Guidance for UI Exploration 

ADWISE computes three types of domain guidance for the LLM. Example prompts for each guidance type are provided in Appendix Figure 12. 

WTG-Based Guidance. In principle, the static analysis results allow us to compute optimized transition paths in the WTG to reach ad UIs. However, as noted in prior work (Liu et al., 2022; 5Preprint. Under review. Rountev & Yan, 2014), statically constructed WTGs often contain infeasible UI transitions. In contrast, LLMs have shown strong capabilities in understanding UIs and code (Brown et al., 2020; Nam et al., 2024; Ran et al., 2024). Therefore, A DWISE provides WTG information to the LLM to improve its reasoning and planning during UI exploration. Since a WTG is represented as a graph while LLM input is a sequence of text tokens, A DWISE 

converts the WTG into textual guidance by applying Dijkstra’s algorithm (Dijkstra, 1959) to compute the shortest-path distance from the current UI to other reachable ad UIs. This shortest-path information helps the LLM optimize navigation, enabling more efficient and cost-effective exploration. 

Semantic Guidance. Developers earn revenue when ad-library ads (i.e., adview and popup ads) are viewed by users (cost per impression (AdMob, 2024b)) or clicked by users (cost per click (AdMob, 2024a)). To maximize revenue, developers tend to place ads in UIs that target users are most likely to visit, which are often closely tied to the app’s main functionality. Moreover, prior work (Wen et al., 2024; Ran et al., 2024) shows that providing explicit exploration guidance substantially improves the ability of LLMs to reach target UIs. Based on these observations, A DWISE generates semantic guidance that encourages the LLM to explore the app in a way that resembles target-user behavior. Specifically, we first crawl app metadata from the app market, including the app category, name, and description. A DWISE then prompts the LLM to summarize the app’s main functionality based on this metadata. For example, “ Listen to Taj’s songs and explore high-quality audio content. ” is the semantic guidance generated for the “Taj Music & Audio” app in Figure 3. In addition, our empirical study shows that native widgets often appear in menus and pop-up dialogs near app entry or exit. To encourage the LLM to consider such widgets, A DWISE appends the instruction: “Navigate through the app and click any buttons or links that lead to other apps or advertising content.” 

Structural Guidance. Certain ad widgets are placed in UIs that are not directly related to the app’s main functionality but are still designed to attract user engagement, such as the “MORE APPS” menu item in Figure 3. As a result, these placements are difficult to capture using semantic guidance alone. To address this challenge, we observe that ad widget placement strategies are often similar across apps with similar UI structures. For example, native widgets are frequently placed near or inside a top-level menu when such a menu exists. To capture such regularities, A DWISE adopts a retrieval-augmented prompting strategy that helps the LLM infer structural ad placements from similar ad UIs. Specifically, A DWISE builds a knowledge base of ad UIs by collecting their UI trees. When visiting a UI, A DWISE computes its embedding, compares it against the embeddings of UI trees in the knowledge base using cosine similarity, and retrieves the top-k most similar UI trees as structural guidance. Details of this process are elaborated in Appendix B.4. 

3.2.2 Grounded UI Reasoning 

While LLMs are effective at understanding UI content, they often hallucinate during planning and automation in complex mobile UIs (Wen et al., 2024; Ran et al., 2024). To address this challenge, ADWISE incorporates two techniques that ground the LLM’s reasoning in observable runtime evidence, drawing on established principles from the ReAct (Yao et al.) and self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023) paradigms. 

Action Reflection. After each exploration step, A DWISE prompts the LLM to compare the pre-action and post-action UI states and explicitly judge whether the intended navigation occurred. If the LLM determines that the UI remained unchanged or transitioned to an unexpected screen, it marks the previous action as failed and appends this outcome to a short-term action history maintained across steps. This reflective feedback serves two purposes: (1) it prevents the agent from re-selecting the same ineffective action in subsequent steps, breaking action repetition loops that are a well-documented failure mode in LLM-based mobile agents (Wen et al., 2024), and (2) it enables the LLM to adaptively revise its exploration strategy. For example, by choosing an alternative navigation path suggested by the WTG guidance when a direct path proves infeasible at runtime. Please refer to Appendix Figure 13 for the prompt template. 

UI State Verification. Before presenting the current UI to the LLM for action selection, A DWISE 

performs a verification pass that filters the raw UI hierarchy to retain only genuinely interactable elements. ## 4 Evaluation 

4.1 Evaluation Setup Dataset. We evaluate A DWISE on 271 ad widgets from the 100-app dataset that we constructed in Subsection 2.1. This dataset spans a wide range of app popularity levels and categories, and reflects the latest advertising practices. 

Metrics. We report the detection rate of ad widgets and per-widget detection latency (PWDL). Let 

Wt denote the set of widgets triggered during exploration. An ad widget wa is considered detected if there exists wt ∈ Wt such that either (1) a child of wa has the same resource-id as wt , or (2) wt is enclosed within the bounding box of wa. The detection rate is Detection Rate = Ndetected /Ntotal . We measure efficiency using PWDL: PWDL = ( Tlast − Tstart )/Ndetected , where Tstart and Tlast denote the exploration start time and the time when the last ad widget is detected, respectively. 

Downstream Ad Regulation. After triggering ad widgets and retrieving their content, A DWISE 

applies a set of detection rules to identify ad regulation violations (Appendix D). We consider three categories of violations based on platform policies, ad library documentation, and prior studies: 

placement (ad size, position, and visibility), interaction (improper triggering or display behavior), and 

content (harmful or policy-violating ad content). We report violations under these three categories in our evaluation. Detailed definitions and background are provided in Appendix D.1. 

Baselines. We compare A DWISE with baselines from two directions: ad detection (Ma et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2023) and guided UI exploration (Ran et al., 2024; Lai & Rubin, 2019). For text-based ad detection , we use AdGPE (Ma et al., 2024), which identifies ad content using common call-to-action keywords and slogans. For UI-based ad detection , we consider MadDroid (Liu et al., 2020), FraudDroid (Dong et al., 2018), and AdRambler (Zhao et al., 2023b), which detect ads through specific view classes (e.g., AdView , WebView , and ImageView ); since these tools are not open-sourced, we implement MadDroid based on the paper. For vision-based ad detection , we use DARPA (Cai et al., 2023), which applies an object detector to identify visual ad patterns. For static analysis-guided UI exploration , we use GoalExplorer (Lai & Rubin, 2019), which locates targets in the WTG and guides breadth-first exploration. For LLM-guided UI exploration , we use Guardian (Ran et al., 2024), which plans exploration with LLM-based guidance; we exclude Autodroid (Wen et al., 2024) because it is not fully open-sourced. We also include Android Monkey (Google, 2023) as a control group with random depth-first exploration. ## 5 Related Work 

Static Android UI Analysis. Existing tools such as Gator (Rountev & Yan, 2014; Yang et al., 2015a), Frontmatter (Kuznetsov et al., 2021), and IconIntent (Xiao et al., 2019) employ the Soot framework (Vall ´ee-Rai et al., 2010) to model the lifecycle of Android applications and analyze their callback functions. This process is used for associating UI widgets with corresponding view classes and event handlers. The scope of such static analysis has been expanded to construct WTGs, as presented by Gator’s following work (Yang et al., 2015c), StoryDroid (Chen et al., 2019), and Promal (Liu et al., 2022), which additionally incorporate analysis of inter-component communications. ADWISE is built upon Gator and IconIntent to effectively analyze widgets and construct WTGs for static ad analysis. 

Mobile Ads Detection. UI exploration is a major approach for ad content identification (Hu & Neamtiu, 2011; Rastogi et al., 2013; Nath et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2014; Hao et al., 2014). For example, MadDroid leveraged breadth-first search for UI exploration and rule-based HTTP hooking for ad content identification (Liu et al., 2020). Researchers also utilized computer vision techniques to detect button edges (Rastogi et al., 2016) or ad-related visual patterns (Cai et al., 2023) for identifying ad contents. However, the UI exploration techniques used by these approaches are for general-purpose testing, causing low effectiveness and efficiency. 

Guided UI Exploration. Prior research on guided Android UI exploration mainly utilized static analysis (Lai & Rubin, 2019) and machine learning techniques (Ran et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2020). More recently, LLMs (Ye et al., 2025) have been recognized as powerful tools for automating UI exploration. Existing work such as Guardian (Ran et al., 2024) and AutoDroid (Wen et al., 2024) has demonstrated the efficacy of LLMs in understanding mobile UI and planning exploration paths. Inspired by these developments, the design of A DWISE integrates a hybrid approach that synergizes both LLM capabilities and static analysis. This combination aims to enhance the effectiveness of guided UI exploration. 

Mobile Ads Analysis. Research on mobile ads analysis spans various directions. A line of studies investigated the performance and extra energy consumption of mobile ads (Vallina-Rodriguez et al., 2012; Khan et al., 2013; Mohan et al., 2013), while others proposed developer-side solutions to enhance mobile ad quality (Gui et al., 2015; Jin et al., 2019). Another key direction of research addressed privacy and security concerns, including privacy leakage (Grace et al., 2012; Enck et al., 2014; Hardt & Nath, 2012; Liu et al., 2015), mobile ad fraud (Liu et al., 2014; Dave et al., 2013), malicious advertising (Rastogi et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020), and ad promotion (Ma et al., 2024) using AI-driven methods (Zhao et al., 2023a; Ju et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2022; Ju et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2021). By detecting ad widgets, A DWISE supports various downstream analyses in these directions. 

## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we have proposed a novel approach for mobile ads detection named A DWISE that innovatively harnesses LLMs to unify static ad analysis and dynamic UI exploration with ad widget placement strategies and knowledge of ad UIs. Our evaluation on 271 ad widgets demonstrates the superior performance of A DWISE , achieving a remarkable 25.60% improvement in detection rate over state-of-the-art approaches. From the detected ads, A DWISE further identifies 133 instances of regulation violations, exceeding state-of-the-art approaches by 34.34% , highlighting its effectiveness in assisting ad regulation.

## B Technical Details of A DWISE 

B.1 Static Analysis for Ad Widget Identification 

This subsection introduces the static program analysis technique used for ad widget identification. ADWISE models a widget as w(v, id , I), where the view class v and the method invocations I are defined in an app’s code, and the resource-id id is separately specified in the app’s UI layout files. Given that a widget’s view class and resource-id are defined via different Android framework APIs, and its method invocations correspond to the set of method calls on the program variable that represents the widget, A DWISE employs a novel dataflow analysis combining application code and UI layout information to identify each widget’s view class v and associate v with its resource-id id 

and method invocations I. Existing approaches (Yang et al., 2015b; Xiao et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2015c) are restricted to detecting behaviors tied to built-in event handlers, overlooking many widget behaviors such as MenuItem clicks and Adaptor events. To overcome this limitation, A DWISE extends the analysis to all method invocations on widgets , enabling broader coverage of ad-related behaviors. Since this inevitably introduces unrelated behaviors that do not trigger ads, A DWISE applies inference rules derived from ad widget definitions to evaluate v and I, filtering out irrelevant cases and retaining only the qualified ad widgets. The resource-ids id are later used to guide ad-oriented UI exploration to look for the UIs that contain the ad widgets. Specifically, this xx consists of two phases: In the first phase, A DWISE associates each widget 

w(v, id , I) with its view class v, resource-id id , and method invocations I. Specifically, A DWISE first scans the application code for method calls findViewById(w.id) , which returns a view object bound to a variable x, establishing the mapping x → w.id .Then, A DWISE conducts a type analysis on x to resolve the type of the object referenced by x (e.g.,  

> (AdView) x

), thus creating the mapping x → v. Finally, A DWISE performs a reference analysis to find all aliases of x and collects every method call made on them, thus obtaining the mapping x → I. This process computes the complete attributes for each widget w(v, id , I).

B.2 Static Analysis for WTG Construction 

ADWISE constructs the WTG G in three steps. First, A DWISE identifies candidate windows Win 

by analyzing specific API invocations such as setContentView , Dialog.show , and MenuInflater.inflate 

. For fragments, A DWISE tracks FragmentTransaction.add/replace calls to recover dynamically attached UIs. Next, for each method containing navigation-related calls (e.g., startActivity and 

> FragmentTransaction

), A DWISE adds edges (Win s,Win t ) ∈ E between the source window Win s and the resolved target Win t . Finally, A DWISE computes ε(e) by linking each transition edge e ∈ E back to the widget w that invokes the transition in its method invocation w.I.

B.4 Retrieval-Augmented Structural Guidance 

The construction of structural guidance consists of three steps: • Data Collection: ADWISE builds a knowledge base of ad UIs by collecting UI trees from a variety of ad-related screens. For each ad UI, A DWISE converts its XML layout file into a UI tree, extracts text from each node, and concatenates the extracted text into a sequence representing the full UI tree. For each widget, A DWISE retains only informative attributes, including text , content description , resource-id , and clickable . In total, A DWISE collects 50 ad UIs for each ad widget type. • Text Embedding: ADWISE encodes these UI trees into vector representations using OpenAI’s 

text-embedding-ada-002 model. • Prompt Construction: When A DWISE visits a UI, it computes the embedding of the current UI, compares it with the embeddings of UI trees in the knowledge base using cosine similarity, and retrieves the top-k most similar UI trees. A DWISE then uses these retrieved UI trees to construct the structural guidance.

D.1 Ad Regulation Categorization 

Based on ad policies from Google and Apple, ad library documentation, and existing academic efforts, we categorize mobile ad regulations into three types: • Placement Regulation : It regulates the size, location, and visibility of ads (AdMob, 2024c;d; Meta, 2024; AppLovin, 2024). Common violations include oversized banners, ads that overlap content, or ads that are hidden behind or within interactive UI elements. • Interaction Regulation : It specifies how ads should behave when users interact with them (AdMob, 2024e; Apple, 2024; for Better Ads, 2024). For example, ads should not be displayed during app launch or exit, and full-screen ads must allow users to close them immediately (AdMob, 2024e; Apple, 2024; for Better Ads, 2024). • Content Regulation : It regulates the content of ads. Violations include harmful content and drive-by-download (Google, 2024c;b;d; Technologies, 2024). Table 4 summarizes the three categories of ad regulations, along with references from platforms, ad networks, and prior research. We observe that both major platforms (Google and Apple) and popular ad networks (e.g. Unity, Meta Ads, AppLovin) enforce requirements across all three categories. In contrast, existing academic approaches only address subsets of these regulations, leaving notable gaps between industrial requirements and research efforts. To the best of our knowledge, A DWISE is the first academic approach that supports all three types of regulation. 

D.2 Rule-Based Detection of Ad Regulation Violation 

After triggering ad widgets and retrieving their content, A DWISE applies a set of detection rules to identify violations of ad regulations. In this work, we focus on the most impactful types of violations across three ad regulation categories that negatively affect user experiences or pose risks to user privacy. We consider two types of Placement Violations :19 Preprint. Under review. 

Figure 11: The filed bug report for Meta Bug Bounty: Abuse Track • Oversized Ads. Ads that occupy more than 30% of the screen are considered oversized (Manager, 2024). A DWISE detects such ads by checking the Bounds attribute of adview and native widgets. • Overlapped Ads. Ads that obstruct or overlap with other interactive app content are considered violations (AdMob, 2024c). A DWISE detects such ads by finding overlaps between the bounds of ad widgets and other clickable or input widgets. We consider two types of Interaction Violations :• Disruptive Ads. These include popup ads shown immediately when the app loads or before the app exits. A DWISE detects such ads by checking whether there is an interstitial ads after loading or before exiting the app. • Unskippable Ads. Full-screen ads that cannot be closed promptly (for Better Ads, 2024; AdMob, 2024e). After triggering a popup widget, A DWISE checks whether it gets stuck in an ad library’s activity for two consecutive actions. The rationale is that if the ad is skippable, the LLM should be able to close it easily. We also consider two types of Content Violations :• Malvertising. Ads can be exploited by attackers to distribute malware (Ma et al., 2024) or direct users to malicious websites (Rastogi et al., 2016). Some ads redirect users to download pages without clear consent. A DWISE records the landing URL after the ad is triggered to detect such behavior. These apps and URLs are then scanned using VirusTotal to assess their maliciousness. We consider an app or URL malicious if it is flagged by at least three VirusTotal engines, following common practice. • Clickbait Ads. Ads that lure users to click using exaggerated claims or deceptive images. To detect these ads, A DWISE prompts a multimodal LLM to ask whether the ad content is related to clickbait. Note that although the above-mentioned violations do not cover all real-world cases, A DWISE can be easily tailored based on ad widgets and their attributes to support different policies and specifications. Conducting a large-scale evaluation of ad regulation violations in the wild has ethical challenges, as it will generate invalid impressions and clicks, which constitute ad fraud and disrupt the advertising ecosystem. Fortunately, we were selected for the Specialized Abuse Track of the Meta Bug Bounty Program (Meta, 2025), which authorizes us to test for vulnerabilities and abuse on their platform. This allowed us to evaluate the effectiveness of A DWISE in detecting ad regulation violations in a real-world setting without ethical concerns. Specifically, we applied AndroGuard (Desnos & Gueguen, 2018) to the AndroZoo dataset (Allix et al., 2016b) to identify apps that integrate the Meta Ads SDK, yielding a dataset of 2, 047 apps. Deploying A DWISE on this set, we successfully identified 383 instances of ad regulation violations, including 97 placement violations, 86 interaction violations, and 200 content violations. Given the substantial manual effort involved, we sampled 10 instances per regulation category and verified that each instance indeed violated the reported regulations. Subsequently, we reported the placement and interaction violations to Google Play (Help, 2023) and filed a bug report with the Meta Bug Bounty Program (Figure 11) to disclose the content violations. At the time of submission, the report is under review. Due to space limits, we provide the details of this study on our website (AdS, 2025). These results demonstrate A DWISE ’s capability to detect actionable regulation violations in the wild. 20 Preprint. Under review.
