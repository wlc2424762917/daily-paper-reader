Abstract 

Evaluating the safety of LLM-based agents is an increasingly important challenge, as risks in realistic deployments often emerge gradually over multi-step interactions rather than from isolated prompts or final responses. Existing trajectory-level benchmarks, however, remain limited in three key dimensions: (1) insufficient interaction diversity, due to restricted tool ecosystems and narrow scenario coverage; (2) limited observability of safety failures, as coarse labels fail to capture how risks arise and evolve; and (3) lack of long-horizon realism, where short or simplified trajecto-ries underestimate delayed and context-dependent risks. To address these limitations, we introduce ATBench, a trajectory-level benchmark that en-ables structured, diverse, and realistic evaluation of agent safety. ATBench formulates agentic safety risks along three orthogonal dimensions—risk source, failure mode, and real-world harm—providing a controllable frame-work for capturing diverse risk patterns. Building on this formulation, we construct trajectories using a taxonomy-guided generation engine with heterogeneous tool pools and incorporate a long-context delayed-trigger protocol that models realistic risk emergence through multi-stage inter-actions. The benchmark contains 1,000 trajectories (503 safe, 497 unsafe), averaging 9.01 turns and 3.95k tokens, with 1,954 invoked tools drawn from pools spanning 2,084 available tools. Data quality is ensured through rule-based & LLM-based filtering and a human full audit. Experiments across frontier LLMs, open-source models, and specialized guard systems show that ATBench remains challenging even for the strongest evaluators— GPT-5.4 achieves only 76.7% F1 on binary safety classification and 33.6% on fine-grained risk-source diagnosis—while enabling taxonomy-stratified analysis, cross-benchmark comparison, and diagnosis of long-horizon fail-ure patterns. Data and code will be released upon acceptance. 

1 Introduction 

LLM-based agents are rapidly evolving from single-turn text interaction to long-horizon execution involving tool use, external observations, and persistent state (Yuan et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025b). This shift fundamentally changes the unit of safety evaluation. In realistic settings, safety risks are often not revealed by a single response but instead emerge gradually over extended interaction traces — through compounding planning errors, unsafe tool use, over-reliance on environmental feedback, or delayed exploitation of previously acquired permissions. Consequently, evaluating agent safety requires moving beyond prompt-level assessment toward reasoning over full trajectories of interaction.

Second, they offer limited observability of safety failures , where coarse-grained labels obscure the underlying causes, behaviors, and consequences of unsafe outcomes. Third, they are lack of long-horizon realism , as short or simplified trajectories fail to capture delayed, context-dependent risk emergence. These limitations are reflected across existing trajectory-level benchmarks Yuan et al. (2024a); Luo et al. (2025), as summarized in Table 1. To address these limitations, we introduce ATBench , a trajectory-level benchmark designed around a simple objective: maximize diversity while preserving realism under long-horizon interactions. In this paper, we organize agent safety along three orthogonal dimensions— risk source, failure mode, and real-world harm—and use this taxonomy together with a heterogeneous tool ecosystem to support controllable and diverse benchmark construction. We further incorporate a delayed-trigger long-context protocol so that risks can be planted early, realized later, and evaluated under realistic multi-step interaction. The current release contains 1,000 held-out trajectories (503 safe and 497 unsafe), with 2,084 available tools, 1,954 invoked tools, and an average of 9.01 turns per trajectory. To improve reliability, we apply post-generation filtering followed by a five-reviewer full human audit. Experiments show that ATBench remains challenging even for strong proprietary and open-source models. More importantly, it enables evaluation beyond aggregate performance, including trajectory-level safety assessment, cross-benchmark difficulty comparison, and fine-grained diagnostic analysis across taxonomy slices. These capabilities position ATBench as both a practical evaluation suite for guard models and a principled testbed for studying long-context, tool-mediated agent risks. 2 Related Work 

Trajectory-level agent safety evaluation. Recent work has moved agent safety evalua-tion beyond static text-only settings toward interactive environments with tool use and multi-step execution (Debenedetti et al., 2024; Kuntz et al., 2025; Xia et al., 2025). This shift motivates trajectory-level benchmarks that evaluate safety over complete execution traces rather than isolated prompts or final responses. R-Judge shows that behavior-level evaluation is substantially harder than response-only judgment (Yuan et al., 2024b). Agent-SafetyBench broadens coverage of unsafe agent behaviors through diverse environments and failure modes (Zhang et al., 2024). AgentAuditor introduces a human-level LLM-as-a-judge framework together with ASSEBench for realistic agent interaction records (Luo et al., 2025). These efforts establish the importance of trajectory-level evaluation. Recent concurrent work also studies pre-execution tool safety and deceptive agent behavior under constrained environments (Xie et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025). These efforts are highly valuable, but they still leave room for broader tool exposure, more controllable coverage of failure diversity, and more realistic long-horizon interactions. Table 1 summarizes representative comparisons. ATBench is designed to address these gaps through controllable diversity under realism. 

Synthetic tool pools and trajectory generation. Large-scale synthetic data has become a practical route for constructing agent datasets when real trajectories are expensive, sensitive, or unsafe to collect at scale.(Fu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). ToolAlpaca demonstrates generalized tool-use learning from simulated interactions (Tang et al., 2023a). ToolLLM introduces ToolBench, which collects and instantiates a large real-world API pool (Qin et al., 2023b). ToolACE improves data quality through self-evolving synthesis and dual-layer verification over a larger API pool (Liu et al., 2024). Recent work further shows that synthetic trajectories can support guardrail training at scale (Huang et al., 2025). Our construction pipeline is closely related to this line of work: the tool pool of ATBench is adapted from these resources, then normalized, deduplicated, and expanded with simulated tools for safety-oriented coverage completion. Unlike generic tool-use data construction, however, ATBench uses taxonomy-guided generation to control which risks are instantiated, where they are triggered, how they evolve across a trajectory, and what downstream harm they produce. 

Guard models and trajectory-level diagnosis. A parallel direction develops guard models and runtime safety mechanisms for agent execution, including Llama Guard for conversa-tional safeguarding (Inan et al., 2023), ShieldAgent for verifiable safety policy reasoning over action trajectories (Chen et al., 2025a), AgentSpec for explicit runtime constraints (Wang et al., 2025a), Pro2Guard for proactive probabilistic enforcement (Wang et al., 2025b), and PolyGuard and Qwen3Guard for large-scale policy-grounded and multilingual guardrail modeling (Kang et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025b). These methods focus on intervention, filter-ing, or online enforcement during execution. By contrast, ATBench focuses on evaluation. It provides a diverse and realistic testbed for measuring whether general-purpose models and specialized guards can generalize across heterogeneous tools, long contexts, delayed triggers, and fine-grained categories of failure and harm. 3Preprint. 

3 Benchmark Construction for Diversity with Realism 

3.1 Task Definition and Benchmark Design Trajectory-level safety evaluation task. ATBench is a benchmark for trajectory-level evalua-tion of agent safety under long-horizon tool use. Each example is a complete multi-turn interaction trace containing user requests, assistant responses, tool calls, and environment feedback. The evaluation task is to determine whether the overall trajectory is safe or unsafe .For unsafe trajectories, ATBench further supports fine-grained diagnosis beyond binary detection. 

Design objective and benchmark scope. Our construction method combines two ingredi-ents. First, we define a three-dimensional safety taxonomy that specifies the target risk space and build a heterogeneous tool pool that provides the executable interface space needed to realize that risk space through diverse scenarios. Second, we instantiate these ingredients with a generation-and-curation pipeline that includes planner-based trajectory synthesis, paired safe/unsafe construction, a delayed-trigger long-context protocol, rule-based and LLM-based filtering, and full human audit. 

3.2 Three-Dimensional Safety Taxonomy and Tool Pool Taxonomy as a construction scaffold. To make diversity controllable rather than incidental, we organize unsafe trajectories along three orthogonal dimensions proposed in this paper: 

risk source , failure mode , and real-world harm . These dimensions answer where risk enters a trajectory, how unsafe behavior is realized, and what consequence it produces. In the construction pipeline, this taxonomy serves as a scaffold for coverage control rather than as an end in itself. It specifies which slices of agentic risk should be instantiated, while the generation engine determines how to realize them in concrete long-horizon trajectories. 

Taxonomy structure. At the leaf level, the taxonomy contains 8 risk-source categories, 14 failure-mode categories, and 10 harm categories. The risk-source axis is organized around four high-level families: user input, environmental observation, external entities, and inter-nal logic and failures. The failure-mode axis covers behavioral failures and output-content failures. The harm axis captures downstream consequences such as privacy, financial, se-curity, physical, reputational, societal, fairness, and functional harms. These high-level axes provide structured material for sampling diverse agentic risk patterns in a controlled manner, rather than relying on an unstructured inventory of scenarios. Full taxonomy definitions remain in Appendix A. 

Heterogeneous tool pool. The taxonomy is paired with a heterogeneous tool pool, which provides the executable material needed to realize diverse scenarios. We assemble this tool pool from realistic public APIs(e.g. rapid api), tools adapted from prior tool-use resources such as ToolBench and ToolAlpaca (Qin et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2023b), and simulated tools that fill underrepresented capability and risk-relevant regions. We then normalize tool names, arguments, and descriptions across sources and remove near-duplicate tools. This design allows the same taxonomy slice to be instantiated through different tool combinations, interface conditions, and interaction structures rather than through a single template. In this sense, the taxonomy defines the axes of diversity, and the tool pool provides the interface space for realizing that diversity. 

Primary-label annotation protocol. Each trajectory receives a binary safe /unsafe verdict. For unsafe trajectories, annotators assign one primary label per taxonomy dimension in a fixed causal order. Risk source identifies the earliest factor that introduces the unsafe condition. Failure mode identifies the dominant unsafe behavior that realizes that risk. Real-world harm identifies the principal consequence of the failure chain. When multiple labels are plausible, annotators select the label that is most central to the main causal chain. This single-primary-label policy keeps taxonomy-stratified evaluation stable. Full annotation guidelines are provided in Appendix B.1. 4Preprint. Risk Sampling

3.3 Generation Engine for Diversity under Realism 

The generation engine translates the diversity defined by the taxonomy and supported by the tool pool into executable trajectories while preserving realism. For each sample, the planner operates on scenario controls that jointly specify a taxonomy slice sampled along the three risk dimensions and candidate tools drawn from the tool pool, and then instantiates a concrete user task, trigger location, and intended safety outcome. Because the same risk slice can be realized through different task formulations, tool combinations, and interaction structures, the benchmark does not collapse into a small number of repeated templates. 

Generation pipeline. Figure 2 shows the end-to-end pipeline. Given a sampled taxonomy slice and a set of scenario controls, the planner first creates a trajectory blueprint that specifies the user task, selected tools, high-level steps, trigger locations, and intended safety outcome. The pipeline then executes this blueprint step by step. Specifically, a Query Generator produces the opening user request. When the sampled risk source lies in the tool-interface layer, a Tool Description Simulator injects the trigger into the tool interface. For each planned step, a Tool Call Simulator generates the tool invocation, a Simulated Data Generator returns either benign or injected environment feedback, and an Agent Response Generator produces the assistant response. Together, these components yield a full trajectory containing tool calls, environment observations, and assistant turns. We further construct paired safe and unsafe variants from the same scenario skeleton. In the unsafe variant, the risk is realized and the agent fails. In the safe variant, the risk is removed, resisted, or correctly handled. The taxonomy serves as a coverage controller throughout this process. The sampled risk source determines where the risk is injected, the failure mode constrains the unsafe behavior, and the real-world harm label determines the downstream consequence. 

Long-context delayed-risk construction. We also include a long-context setting to capture safety risks that arise from dependencies across multiple steps and intermediate states, rather than from local context alone. This better reflects realistic agent behavior, where earlier benign-appearing actions can later become safety-critical, than simply extending the number of turns. Implementation details are provided in Appendix B.2. 

Rule-based and LLM-based filtering. Rule-based and LLM-based filtering improve realism in the final dataset. We first apply rule-based structural filtering, including checks for tool-name existence, schema conformance, and required-parameter completeness. We then apply LLM-based plausibility filtering to verify that the trajectory is behaviorally reasonable and that its annotations are consistent with the observed behavior. 4 Experiments 

We organize the empirical study into three parts. First, we report main results on ATBench, including both trajectory-level safety performance and fine-grained diagnostic behavior. Second, we compare model performance across ATBench and prior agent-safety benchmarks to assess relative difficulty. Third, we analyze category-wise performance across fine-grained taxonomy slices.

4.2 Main Results on ATBench Trajectory-level safety results. Table 2 shows that ATBench remains challenging even for strong evaluators. GPT-5.4 achieves the highest coarse-grained F1 of 76.7%, followed by Gemini-3.1-Pro at 75.0% and the trajectory-trained AgentDoG-Qwen3-4B at 71.1%. Strong open-source general models score substantially lower (Qwen3.5-397B at 67.8%, Qwen3-235B at 60.8%), indicating that ATBench is far from saturated even for frontier systems. The gap widens considerably for fine-grained diagnosis. Even strong closed-source models reach only 33.6% on risk source and 13.5% on failure mode, while real-world harm prediction remains limited at 30.2% for GPT-5.4. Recognizing that a trajectory is unsafe proves far easier than recovering where the risk originates, how it unfolds, and what harm it produces. Trajectory-trained models retain an edge here: AgentDoG-Qwen3-4B achieves the strongest fine-grained results (46.8% risk source, 16.5% failure mode, 40.6% harm), whereas off-the-shelf guard models remain considerably weaker on structured causal attribution.

4.3 Cross-Benchmark Difficulty Comparison 

Figure 4 compares seven representative models across R-Judge, ASSE-Safety, ATBench500, and ATBench in terms of trajectory-level accuracy. For most representative models, perfor-mance drops on ATBench, including GPT-5.2 (90.8% on R-Judge versus 69.0%), Qwen3-235B (85.1% versus 59.2%), and AgentDoG-Qwen3-4B (91.8% versus 64.0%). A few models, such as Qwen3-Guard, do not follow this monotonic pattern, but the overall trend still points to higher difficulty on ATBench. This gap stems from the benchmark design: broader tool coverage, longer trajectories, delayed-trigger construction that separates risk setup from consequence, and structured risk-space sampling that distributes evaluation across diverse sources, failure modes, and harms. 5 Discussion 

ATBench centers on the trade-off between diversity and realism. Broader tool coverage, richer scenario composition, and finer-grained labels reduce the risk of overfitting to nar-row failure patterns, but diversity without structural checks can produce templated or implausible data. Our construction pipeline addresses this tension through delayed-trigger long-context protocols, rule-based & LLM-based filtering, and full human audit, and we expect this trade-off to remain central for future benchmark design. 

Limitations. ATBench still has limitations. First, each unsafe trajectory carries only one primary label per taxonomy dimension, which may miss valid secondary interpretations in multi-causal cases. Second, all trajectories are constructed in English; extending the benchmark to multilingual settings would broaden its applicability to global agent de-ployments. Third, the benchmark focuses on text-and-tool trajectories; multimodal and embodied settings remain important future extensions. 

6 Conclusion 

We introduced ATBench, a benchmark for long-horizon agent safety evaluation built around a simple principle: maximize diversity while preserving realism. To operationalize this objective, the benchmark uses a unified and hierarchical three-dimensional taxonomy over risk source, failure mode, and real-world harm as a structured scaffold, and then instantiates that diversity through a taxonomy-guided generation engine, a heterogeneous tool pool, a shared-state setup–exploit long-context protocol, and human-in-the-loop quality control. Our experiments show that ATBench is challenging for both general-purpose models and specialized guards, especially on trajectory-level safety evaluation and fine-grained diag-nosis. We hope ATBench can help standardize more realistic evaluation of agent safety systems and encourage future work on long-context, tool-mediated risk understanding. 9Preprint.

A.2 Risk Source 

The risk source dimension characterizes where a potential risk originates within an agent’s interaction loop. It focuses on the factors that introduce unsafe conditions before or during decision-making. We categorize risk sources into four primary classes: user inputs , environmental observa-tions , external entities (e.g. , tools or APIs), and the agent’s internal decision-making logic .User inputs may contain ambiguous, misleading, or adversarial instructions. Environmental observations may provide incomplete, noisy, or manipulated information. External entities can return erroneous, outdated, or harmful responses that misguide subsequent actions. In addition, internal failures of the underlying language model may lead to flawed reasoning, planning, or action selection even without external interference. 13 Preprint.

A.3 Failure Mode 

The failure mode dimension describes how a risk is realized through the agent’s behavior or outputs after a risk source has been introduced. It captures the concrete patterns of unsafe execution or generation that directly lead to undesirable outcomes. We divide failure modes into two broad categories. Behavioral failure modes arise from flawed planning, reasoning, or execution, such as improper action sequencing, unsafe tool usage, or deviations from intended procedures. Output content failure modes , by contrast, occur when the agent’s textual output itself directly constitutes the risk, without invok-ing tools or executing external actions. This includes generating misleading information, unauthorized disclosures, or other unsafe content that may cause harm when consumed. 

A.4 Real-world Harm 

The real-world harm dimension captures the real-world harms resulting from unsafe agent behavior. It focuses on the impact of failures rather than their causes or mechanisms. Real-world harms may include physical, financial, privacy, psychological, reputational, or societal harms. Such outcomes can arise from adversarial manipulation, benign user error, or internal model failures. By modeling consequences explicitly, this dimension supports outcome-oriented safety evaluation and impact assessment.

B.2 Long-Context Delayed-Trigger Details 

This subsection describes the implementation of delayed-trigger trajectory generation in the long-context setting. 

Two-episode structure. For long-context scenarios, the planner organizes the trajectory into two contiguous episodes: a S ETUP episode and a later E XPLOIT episode. The setup episode establishes reusable state, while the exploit episode is constructed to depend on that earlier state rather than on a newly introduced local trigger. 

Context carry-over. At execution time, user, assistant, and environment messages produced in the setup episode remain in the shared conversation history and are carried forward into the exploit episode. Consequently, later generation stages condition on the visible prior transcript rather than on the current turn alone. 

Grounding of delayed triggers. When an exploit-side step is marked as source=CONTEXT ,the planner is instructed to ground it in earlier tool outputs or previously established permissions rather than introducing a fresh trigger. 

Safe and unsafe realization. The trajectory contains a designated delayed-trigger risk point, typically in the exploit episode. In unsafe trajectories, the agent acts on the carried-over dependency and the risk is realized. In safe trajectories, the same prior context remains available, but the agent refuses, verifies, or otherwise neutralizes the dependency. Thus, the safety-relevant divergence occurs at the delayed-trigger step while both variants share the same carried-over context.

D Full Human Audit Details 

This appendix provides the detailed protocol and statistics for the full-dataset human audit used to correct benchmark labels before release. 

Audit protocol. After automatic synthesis and rule-based & LLM-based filtering, we conduct a full human audit over the entire ATBench release. Five reviewers from authors inspect all 1,000 trajectories rather than a small sampled subset. Each trajectory is first reviewed at the coarse-grained level to determine whether the released safe /unsafe verdict is supported by the full interaction trace. Only trajectories that remain unsafe after this step are further reviewed for the fine-grained taxonomy labels, namely risk source, failure mode, and real-world harm. Whenever reviewers determine that a released label is not adequately supported by the trajectory, we revise the annotation accordingly. This process is intended as a dataset-correction pass rather than an inter-annotator agreement study.                   

> Audit item Value
> Reviewers 5Audited trajectories 1,000
> unsafe →safe corrections 4
> safe →unsafe corrections 1Revised binary safe/unsafe labels (total) 5Unsafe trajectories with any taxonomy revision 129 Revised risk-source labels 64 Revised failure-mode labels 67 Revised real-world-harm labels 34 Revised fine-grained labels (total) 165 Final safe / unsafe trajectories 503 / 497

Table 6: Summary statistics from the full human audit of ATBench. Fine-grained revision counts are computed over trajectories retained as unsafe after coarse-grained review. 

Audit outcome. The audit changes the binary verdict for 5 trajectories in total, including 4 unsafe -to-safe corrections and 1 safe -to-unsafe correction. Among trajectories retained as 

unsafe , the audit yields 165 fine-grained label corrections across 129 trajectories, including 64 revisions to risk source, 67 revisions to failure mode, and 34 revisions to real-world harm. After this correction pass, the final released benchmark contains 503 safe trajectories and 497 

unsafe trajectories. 

Interpretation. These correction counts suggest that the benchmark is relatively stable at the coarse-grained level, with only a small number of binary-label changes across the full dataset. In contrast, fine-grained taxonomy labels require substantially more manual correction within the unsafe subset. This pattern indicates that filtering is effective at removing many obvious coarse errors, but is still not sufficient on its own to guarantee release-quality fine-grained annotations. Full human audit therefore remains an important final quality-assurance step for benchmark releases that rely on automated generation pipelines. 21 Preprint.
