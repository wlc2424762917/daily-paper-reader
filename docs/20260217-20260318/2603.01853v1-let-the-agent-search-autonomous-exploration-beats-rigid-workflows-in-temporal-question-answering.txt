Abstract 

Temporal Knowledge Graph Question Answer-ing (TKGQA) demands multi-hop reasoning under temporal constraints. Prior approaches based on Large Language Models (LLMs) typically rely on rigid, hand-crafted retrieval workflows or costly supervised fine-tuning. We show that simply granting an off-the-shelf LLM autonomy —letting it decide what to do next —already yields substantial gains even in a strict zero-shot setting. Building on this in-sight, we propose an Autonomous, Training-free Agent for Temporal Question Answering (AT2QA ) that iteratively interacts with the tem-poral knowledge graph (TKG) via a general search tool for dynamic retrieval. Experiments on MultiTQ demonstrate large improvements: AT2QA achieves 88.7% Hits@1 ( +10.7% over prior SOTA), including a +20.1% leap on chal-lenging multi-target queries, showing that agen-tic autonomy can decisively outperform fine-tuning for temporal question answering. Code and the full set of sampled trajectories are avail-able on [GitHub]. 

1 Introduction 

While traditional Knowledge Graphs (KGs) have long served as a fundamental infrastructure for question answering, real-world facts are inherently dynamic and time-dependent. To prevent models from relying on outdated knowledge, Temporal Knowledge Graphs (TKGs) capture this evolution by extending static facts into quadruples denoted as ⟨subject, relation, object, timestamp ⟩. Con-sequently, Temporal Knowledge Graph Question Answering (TKGQA) necessitates simultaneous reasoning over structural entities and precise tem-poral dynamics. Complex temporal queries entail executing multi-hop reasoning and satisfying com-binatorial and multi-granular temporal constraints, rendering TKGQA substantially more challenging than conventional static Knowledge Graph Ques-tion Answering (KGQA). In recent years, the field of TKGQA has wit-nessed a significant paradigm shift from tradi-tional task-specific architectures to Large Language Model (LLM) integration frameworks. Traditional methods primarily rely on embedding-based rep-resentation learning and semantic parsing-based graph querying. Specifically, embedding-based paradigms (Mavromatis et al., 2022; Chen et al., 1

2023b) attempt to project natural language ques-tions and TKG quadruples into continuous low-dimensional vector spaces, ranking the plausibility of candidate answers via scoring functions, as illus-trated in Figure 1(a). However, these approaches often act as opaque "black boxes" and struggle to deeply capture the complex semantic information of temporal constraints within queries (Chen et al., 2023c), rendering them inadequate for complicated temporal reasoning tasks. In contrast, LLMs, endowed with remarkable zero-shot reasoning and deep semantic parsing ca-pabilities, have demonstrated exceptional perfor-mance in handling complex natural language tasks (Yang et al., 2025; Peng et al., 2025). Consequently, recent research focus has rapidly pivoted toward ex-ploring the potential of LLMs in solving TKGQA (Qian et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c; Qian et al., 2025; Gong et al., 2025). These emerging methods typically follow human-designed, rigid workflows, attempting to inject dynamic graph knowledge into language models via static Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) or heavy fine-tuning procedures. Nevertheless, when confronting highly complex temporal reasoning demands in real-world scenar-ios, both the aforementioned traditional TKGQA methods and LLM-based TKGQA workflows re-veal four fundamental limitations: (1) Severe lack of self-correction capabili-ties. For multi-hop queries, embedding-based methods typically output the top-ranked can-didate without any mechanism to handle in-correct predictions(Figure 1(a)). Likewise, static LLM workflows lack autonomous self-correction, making them prone to hallucina-tions and cascading error propagation when an intermediate retrieval step fails (Figure 1(b)). (2) Prohibitive training cost and time consum-ing. Many recent LLM-based methods re-quire collecting task-specific data to fine-tune the backbone model, often via SFT and even large-scale RL (e.g., (Jiang et al., 2026; Gong et al., 2026)). Such training is expensive and time-consuming, which substantially limits the practicality and scalability of these ap-proaches. (3) Limited system interpretability Many ex-isting approaches do not produce an explicit, verifiable chain of supporting facts that links the question to the final prediction. Conse-quently, their decisions are difficult to audit: even correct outputs provide little insight into the underlying justification, while incorrect outputs offer limited visibility into where the reasoning deviates or which intermediate step fails. (4) Insufficient autonomy. Most existing meth-ods force LLMs to follow fixed, human-designed workflows (Hu et al., 2025a; Chen et al., 2024a) rather than letting them decide 

what to do next . This rigid execution con-strains the model’s ability to explore and adapt its reasoning process, limiting overall perfor-mance. Based on the above limitations, we ask a core question: Can we build a TKGQA method that is autonomous, training-free, interpretable with explicit evidence chains, and completely stimu-late the autonomy of LLMs—while substantially outperforming current SOTA? To this end, we propose AT2QA (Autonomous and Training-free 

Agent for Temporal Question Answering), a frame-work that unleashes LLM autonomy without any pa-rameter updates . AT2QA provides the LLM with a 

<search> tool to interact with the temporal knowl-edge graph environment, yielding a system-level self-correction mechanism that mitigates cascading errors in multi-hop temporal reasoning. The core highlights of this work are as follows: • Self-Correction: An evidence-driven verifi-cation and rollback mechanism that reduces error accumulation in multi-hop reasoning. • Training-Free: No fine-tuning or parameter updates are required; the cost is dominated by lightweight prompt experience mining and low-cost inference. • Interpretability: Explicit evidence chains are provided through retrieved facts and tool-call trajectories, enabling faithful auditing of both successes and failures. • A New Agentic Paradigm for TKGQA: We introduce an autonomy-first agent framework that replaces rigid, human-designed work-flows with LLM-directed exploration, effec-tively liberating the model’s planning and rea-soning capabilities in TKGQA. On the challenging multi-granularity temporal QA benchmark MultiTQ (Chen et al., 2023b), 2AT2QA achieves 88.7% Hits@1 , surpassing prior SOTA methods by a large margin and demonstrat-ing the promise of agentic reasoning over temporal knowledge graphs. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Traditional TKGQA 

Traditional Temporal knowledge graph question answering (TKGQA) primarily relied on represen-tation learning and logical parsing. Embedding-based methods (Saxena et al., 2021a; Mavroma-tis et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022, 2023b) and ap-proaches like TSQA (Shang et al., 2022), which for-mulate the task as temporal knowledge graph com-pletion, encode entities and temporal relations into low-dimensional spaces and rely on scoring func-tions to evaluate the plausibility of candidate facts. Although these methods laid a crucial foundation for the field, traditional embedding representations often act as opaque "black boxes" with weak sys-tem interpretability (Cai et al., 2023). In contrast, semantic parsing-based methods (Jia et al., 2018; Neelam et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024b) attempt to translate natural language ques-tions into logical query expressions, while Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Jia et al., 2021; Sharma et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a,b) have been intro-duced to capture the complex structural dependen-cies within the graphs. These traditional paradigms suffer from a common bottleneck: they typically demand prohibitive resources for specialized train-ing, making it exceedingly difficult to generalize to unseen TKGs. 

2.2 LLM-based TKGQA Workflows 

In recent years, the introduction of Large Language Models has profoundly driven a paradigm shift in TKGQA, rapidly steering the research focus toward leveraging the powerful in-context learn-ing and semantic parsing capabilities of LLMs for RAG or interactive querying over TKGs. Exist-ing LLM integration methods primarily focus on RAG and prompt engineering: ARI (Chen et al., 2024c) enhances the temporal adaptability of mod-els via time-aware training signals; TimeR 4 (Qian et al., 2024) and PoK (Qian et al., 2025) gener-ate more comprehensive reasoning plans by im-proving retrieval components; and TempAgent (Hu et al., 2025a) adapts the ReAct paradigm to the temporal domain by designing a toolkit with 10 specific temporal tools. RTQA (Gong et al., 2025) adopts a bottom-up decomposition strategy to solve sub-questions recursively, while MemoTime (Tan et al., 2025) utilizes closed-source APIs for reason-ing and stores solution paths as memories. Lack-ing a consistency verification mechanism, these methods frequently deviate from the correct rea-soning trajectory due to errors in preceding steps, ultimately yielding an erroneous answer.Recently, some works (e.g., TKG-Thinker (Jiang et al., 2026) and Temp-R1 (Gong et al., 2026)) have explored more autonomous agent architectures. Neverthe-less, these approaches either rely on complex, human-designed hard-coded paths, thereby depriv-ing LLMs of their intrinsic global planning and autonomous self-correction capabilities, or neces-sitate prohibitively expensive and time-consuming "reconstructive" training. 

3 Intuition 

Before presenting our methodology AT2QA, we detail two pivotal empirical observations that es-tablish the foundation of our framework. These findings challenge the prevailing assumption in TKGQA that high performance necessitates either complex supervised fine-tuning or rigid, human-crafted reasoning workflows. 

3.1 The Language Model is Smart Enough to Decide What to Do Next 

Recent research in TKGQA typically constrains LLMs within static decomposition frameworks or predefined reasoning paths(Gong et al., 2026; Jiang et al., 2026). Such approaches implicitly assume that LLMs lack the capability to independently nav-igate the intricate temporal constraints and struc-tural dependencies of knowledge graphs(Qian et al., 2025). However, our preliminary experiments sug-gest a contrary conclusion. We observe that when an off-the-shelf LLM is equipped with a generic search tool and granted the autonomy to determine when and what to re-trieve, it exhibits remarkable planning proficiency. As illustrated in Figure 2, even in a zero-shot set-ting without any parameter updates , AT2QA significantly outperforms current state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods that rely on extensive supervised training or rigid, human-crafted reasoning work-flows. 3.2 Eliciting Capabilities Instead of Fine-Tuning 

The second observation addresses the necessity of computational heavy-lifting. While Supervised Fine-Tuning is a standard paradigm to align models with TKG tasks, we hypothesize that the requisite reasoning patterns are already latent within the pre-trained weights of strong LLMs. To validate this, we conducted a Pass@k analysis on a randomly sampled subset of 3,000 questions from the MultiTQ benchmark. Specifically, the 

Pass@k metric evaluates whether at least one out of k independent generation attempts yields the correct answer(Yue et al., 2025). As depicted in Figure 3, AT2QA achieves an impressive accuracy of over 84% at Pass@1 in a zero-shot setting. Cru-cially, for the subset of "hard" queries where the model initially failed, we observed that repeated sampling (increasing k) rapidly closes the perfor-mance gap. At k = 10 , the model is able to gener-ate at least one correct reasoning path for nearly all queries. This finding carries a profound implication: the model inherently possesses the capability to solve even the most challenging temporal reason-ing problems. Empirical evidence proves that the correct reasoning trajectory already exists within the model’s latent space; the model effectively “knows” the solution but failed to assign the highest probability to the optimal path in a single infer-ence step. 4 Methodology 

In this section, we introduce AT2QA, a fully au-tonomous and training-free LLM agent framework capable of dynamic self-correction for Temporal Question Answering. AT2QA consists of two core components, as shown in Figure 4: a retrieval-augmented reasoning agent equipped with a struc-tured temporal search tool, and a trajectory op-timization strategy that mines effective few-shot demonstrations from the model’s self-generated experiences.

4.2 Tool-Augmented Reasoning Framework 

To bridge the gap between the LLM’s parametric knowledge and the structured data in G, we develop a Search tool that supports both semantic retrieval and symbolic filtering. 

Structured Temporal Retrieval. The Search 

tool accepts a query string q along with a set of structured constraints C = {ctime , c entity , c rel }.• Filtering: Before semantic matching, the tool filters G to obtain a candidate set Gsub ⊂G. Constraints act as a conjunctive filter: 

ctime specifies an inclusive time window 

[τstart , τ end ]; centity restricts involved entities (e.g., head/tail candidates); and crel filters for exact relation matches. This ensures that re-trieval is grounded in valid temporal and struc-tural contexts. • Semantic Ranking: We employ a dense retrieval approach. Each fact f ∈ Gsub 

and the query q are encoded into a shared embedding space. Facts are ranked based on the cosine similarity score s(f, q ) =cos( enc (f ), enc (q)) .• Hybrid Sorting: To handle temporal nu-ances, the tool supports two sorting modes: (1) Relevance-based , sorting solely by s(f, q ),and (2) Time-based , where the top-m relevant facts are re-ranked chronologically. This ex-poses the temporal evolution of facts to the agent. 

Reasoning Process. The LLM operates as an au-tonomous agent. At each step t, it generates a thought and a tool call action at = Search (qt, C t)

based on the history of previous actions and obser-vations. The iterative loop continues until the agent generates a special termination token or reaches the maximum step limit Tmax . This multi-turn inter-action allows the agent to perform Self-Correction :if retrieved evidence conflicts with the hypothe-sis, the agent can refine its constraints Ct+1 (e.g., expanding the time window) in subsequent turns. 

4.3 Training-Free Experience Mining 

To enhance the agent without any parameter up-dates, we adopt a Training-Free GRPO-style ex-perience selection scheme (Cai et al., 2025). In-5stead of optimizing model weights, we generate a pool of candidate reasoning traces and distill a small set of high-value “advantage experiences” that most effectively improve the LLM when used as few-shot demonstrations. 

Trajectory Sampling. Given a mini-batch of N

training questions and a group size g, we sample

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We evaluate AT2QA on MultiTQ 

(Chen et al., 2023b), a comprehensive and highly challenging benchmark explicitly designed for multi-granularity temporal question answering. MultiTQ requires models to handle complex tem-poral reasoning, including single-hop, multi-hop, composite, and multi-granularity temporal reason-ing, making it an ideal testbed for evaluating au-tonomous reasoning capabilities. Detailed statis-tics and category distributions are provided in Ap-pendix A. 

Baselines. To align with the paradigm shifts in TKGQA research, we reclassify existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods into two distinct categories for comparison: (1) TKG Embedding-based Methods : This includes EmbedKGQA (Jin et al., 2022), CronKGQA (Saxena et al., 2021b), and Mul-tiQA (Chen et al., 2023b). These traditional approaches map entities and timestamps into continuous vector spaces but inherently lack the semantic comprehension required for com-plex natural language queries. (2) LLM-based Static Workflows : This category encompasses both prompt-based pipelines (ARI(Chen et al., 2023a), Tem-pAgent(Hu et al., 2025b), MemoTime(Tan et al., 2025), RTQA(Gong et al., 2025)) and fine-tuned models (Search-R1(Jin et al., 2025), TimeR 4(Qian et al., 2024), PoK(Qian et al., 2025), Temp-R1(Gong et al., 2026)). Despite their use of LLMs, these methods rigidly force the model through predefined, human-crafted sub-steps or single-pass retrieval pipelines, severely limiting the model’s autonomy and susceptibility to error propagation. 

Evaluation Metric. Following standard practice in TKGQA, we report the Hits@1 (Exact Match) metric. A prediction is considered correct only if it exactly matches the ground-truth entities or timestamps. 

5.2 Implementation Details 

AT2QA is powered by deepseek-v3.2. To main-tain a balance between exploration and exploitation during the iterative reasoning process, we utilize the server default decoding parameters, specifically setting the generation temperature to 1.0. For the structured search tool, all dataset facts are embed-ded offline using the GLM-Embedding-3 model with a vector dimension of 256 . During inference, the tool performs exact nearest-neighbor search via brute-force cosine similarity over the structurally filtered candidates, returning a maximum of 10 

facts per call. The maximum number of interac-tion turns ( Tmax ) is strictly capped at 20 . For our training-free optimization, we utilize a fixed library of K = 3 demonstrations as the few-shot context. Running AT2QA on the huge MultiTQ benchmark (56 ,014 questions) costs only $150 under official 6API pricing, demonstrating low inference cost at scale.

This Self-Validation behavior demon-strates a high degree of meta-cognitive planning, sharply contrasting with naive RAG pipelines that stop after a single semantic match.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced AT2QA , a fully au-tonomous and training-free agentic framework for temporal question answering over temporal knowledge graphs. Departing from rigid, human-designed workflows and expensive fine-tuning pipelines, AT2QA equips an off-the-shelf LLM with a single global search interface and enables it to interact with TKG environment iteratively. The key to AT2QA’s robustness is granting the LLM autonomy . Rather than enforcing a rigid, hand-crafted pipeline, AT2QA allows the model to de-termine which actions are necessary next given the current evidence—whether to continue search-ing, adjust temporal/structural constraints, or refor-mulate queries. This autonomy naturally induces iterative verification and revision behaviors, en-abling the agent to recover from insufficient or contradictory evidence. Moreover, we proposed a training-free experience mining strategy that cu-rates a compact few-shot library from the model’s own successful trajectories, stabilizing tool-use be-haviors without gradient updates. Empirically, AT2QA establishes a new state of the art on the challenging MultiTQ benchmark, reaching 88.7% Hits@1 and delivering particularly large gains on multi-target, multi-hop queries. Be-yond raw accuracy, our trajectory analysis provides quantitative evidence that the improvements stem from genuine agentic behaviors, including self-validation and self-correction, rather than single-pass retrieval luck. Overall, our results suggest that for temporal reasoning over dynamic knowl-edge, unlocking model autonomy with a minimal tool interface can be more effective than imposing rigid pipelines or resorting to costly reconstructive training. 8Limitations 

Efficiency and scalability. Our implementation performs nearest-neighbor retrieval over a struc-turally filtered candidate set and allows up to 

Tmax = 20 interaction turns. While this design improves robustness, it increases latency and in-ference cost compared to single-pass RAG. Scal-ing to substantially larger graphs or tighter latency budgets may require more efficient indexing (e.g., ANN) and better turn-level early stopping policies. 

Autonomy can be inefficient and unstable. 

Granting the LLM full autonomy improves robust-ness, but it may also lead to extra exploration turns 

or occasional looping behaviors under ambiguous queries or strong distractors. As a result, latency and inference cost can be higher than single-pass RAG or fixed pipelines, and performance may be more sensitive to decoding randomness and stop-ping criteria.

A Dataset Details 

MultiTQ. Constructed from the ICEWS05-15 dataset (García-Durán et al., 2018), MultiT is the largest known multi-granularity dataset for tempo-ral knowledge graph question answering. It con-tains approximately 500K unique question-answer pairs and over 461K temporal facts represented as quadruples (subject, predicate, object, timestamp) .The dataset incorporates multiple temporal gran-ularities, including years, months, and days, with a temporal span exceeding 3,600 days. Questions are systematically categorized into Single (approx. 73%, focusing on simple factual lookup or single-hop reasoning) and Multiple (approx. 27%, requir-ing complex multi-hop reasoning with temporal ordering). These questions comprehensively cover 

Before/After temporal sequencing, First/Last com-parative reasoning, and various explicit and implicit temporal constraints. This high level of reasoning complexity and multi-granularity makes MultiTQ an ideal benchmark for evaluating AT2QA’s ca-pabilities in handling strict temporal boundaries, executing autonomous multi-turn searches, and per-forming rigorous consistency checks.
