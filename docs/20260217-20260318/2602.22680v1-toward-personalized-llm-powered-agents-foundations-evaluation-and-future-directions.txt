WENJIE WANG †, ShanghaiTech University, China Large language models have enabled agents that reason, plan, and interact with tools and environments to accomplish complex tasks. As these agents operate over extended interaction horizons, their effectiveness increasingly depends on adapting behavior to individual users and maintaining continuity across time, giving rise to personalized LLM-powered agents. In such long-term, user-dependent settings, personalization permeates the entire decision pipeline rather than remaining confined to surface-level generation. This survey provides a capability-oriented review of personalized LLM-powered agents. We organize the literature around four interdependent components: profile modeling, memory, planning, and action execution. Using this taxonomy, we synthesize representative methods and analyze how user signals are represented, propagated, and utilized, highlighting cross-component interactions and recurring design trade-offs. We further examine evaluation metrics and benchmarks tailored to personalized agents, summarize application scenarios spanning general assistance to specialized domains, and outline future directions for research and deployment. By offering a structured framework for understanding and designing personalized LLM-powered agents, this survey charts a roadmap toward more user-aligned, adaptive, robust, and deployable agentic systems, accelerating progress from prototype personalization to scalable real-world assistants. 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have evolved from passive text generators into general-purpose reasoning systems capable of understanding diverse data, planning actions, and using external tools. Building on these capabilities, LLM-powered agents extend LLMs into integrated systems that decompose complex objectives, invoke tools, in-teract with dynamic environments, and coordinate with humans or other agents to achieve high-level goals over extended horizons [ 8, 126 , 213 , 225 ]. This shift marks not only a technical expansion of LLM capabilities but also a conceptual transition toward autonomous, adaptive, and socially grounded intelligent assistants. As agent systems increasingly support long-term interaction and autonomous decision-making, personalization emerges as a central requirement for maintaining contextual continuity and aligning agent behavior with individual user goals across domains such as education, healthcare, and recommendation [ 31 , 157 , 229 ]. In LLM-powered agents, personalization extends across the full decision pipeline, shaping how agents interpret user intent, retain information over time, generate plans, and execute actions [ 215 , 288 ]. This system-wide scope introduces new challenges, in-cluding handling dynamic and multimodal feedback, maintaining consistency across interacting components, balancing user-specific adaptation with general capabilities, and safeguarding privacy and security. Correspondingly, evaluating personalized agents requires moving beyond static correctness to capture long-term effectiveness, adaptability, and user satisfaction. Despite growing interest, reviews on personalized LLM-powered agents remain fragmented. Existing studies often concentrate on individual capabilities such as user modeling and memory construction [ 250 , 287 ], planning and reasoning strategies [ 239 ], or adaptive interaction mechanisms [ 46 , 110 ]. A unified framework that connects personalization objectives across the full agent lifecycle remains limited. As a result, the interactions among different personalization mechanisms, their system-level trade-offs, and their implications for evaluation and deployment are not yet well understood. This survey presents a capability-oriented and system-level perspective on person-alized LLM-powered agents. Personalization is examined as a distributed property instantiated across four interdependent components: profile modeling, memory man-agement, planning, and action execution. This decomposition enables a systematic analysis of how user-specific signals are represented, propagated, and operationalized throughout the agent lifecycle, from intent understanding to real-world outcomes. As illustrated in Figure 1, when an individual user submits a request, a personalized agent coordinates four interdependent capabilities to generate a tailored response. Profile representations and role definitions shape internal preference modeling, memory schemes organize and retrieve relevant contextual information, planning determines personalized decision strategies, and action execution grounds these decisions in the environment. 2.3 User-Centric Data 

The internal representation of user preferences in LLM-powered agents is grounded in user-centric data, which captures contextual information, feedback, and interaction traces associated with a specific user. From a temporal and functional perspective, user-centric data can be broadly divided into historical data and interaction data .

Historical Data refers to information available before task execution, including user identifiers, demographic attributes, and records of prior interactions and feedback. This context provides a front-loaded prior over the user for the current episode, capturing relatively stable user characteristics together with accumulated evidence from past interactions, which supports consistent behavior and cross-task generalization. 

Interaction Data is generated during task execution and captures real-time user inputs, feedback, and contextual cues. It includes both explicit signals, such as correc-tions or ratings, and implicit signals, such as behavioral patterns or response timing.

2.4 User Preferences 

User preferences constitute the core signals that enable personalization in LLM-powered agents by conditioning their internal state, decision-making processes, and user-specific feedback. Prior work commonly categorizes preferences according to their mode of expression [ 103 , 288 ]. Explicit preferences are directly specified by users and can be incorporated into the agent without additional inference. Implicit prefer-ences are inferred indirectly from behavioral patterns or contextual cues, reflecting user judgments in a latent or under-specified form. Beyond expression form, preferences can be characterized by their semantic func-tion, capturing which aspect of the user they encode. We distinguish behavioral preferences , which govern how users communicate and reason, and topical preferences ,which specify what users prioritize in a given context. Behavioral preferences en-compass tone, reasoning style, general interaction tendencies, and personality-related traits, and are often stable across tasks. Topical preferences cover domain interests, factual stances, and likes or dislikes toward specific entities or events, and vary more with context. Both types can be stated explicitly or inferred implicitly, yielding the two-dimensional taxonomy in Figure 3. In practical systems, explicit preferences can be represented in various forms, including reward vectors [ 17 ], preference embeddings [ 159 ], or natural-language prompts [ 90 ]. Although explicit signals are often highly informative, they are typically sparse, which limits their direct applicability for optimization. As a result, implicit preferences are frequently leveraged through in-context prompting [ 89 ], retrieval-augmented generation (RAG; [ 161 ]), or preference modeling techniques [ 53 ] that extract structured representations from user behavioral feedback. 2.5 Capability-oriented Taxonomy 

Personalization in LLM-powered agents emerges from the coordinated operation of multiple internal capabilities rather than from a single adaptation module. Given the diversity of user data, task settings, and preference types, user-specific signals must be represented, retained, reasoned over, and operationalized throughout the whole pipeline. We therefore adopt a capability-oriented taxonomy (illustrated in Figure 4) that organizes personalization into four interdependent components: (1) Profile Modeling structures user-specific information into internal representa-tions, defining both user characteristics and the agent’s role. (2) Memory maintains and retrieves user-relevant information across interactions, supporting temporal continuity and consistent preference grounding. (3) Planning integrates user representations into reasoning processes, shaping de-cision paths, strategy selection, and prioritization under contextual constraints. (4) Action Execution operationalizes personalized decisions through tool invoca-tion and environment interaction, enabling the final outcome.

3.1 User Profile Modeling 

User profile modeling extracts and organizes user-specific signals into a representa-tion that can condition downstream personalization modules. In contrast to generic semantic context, profiles primarily encode personalization cues such as stable traits, long-term preferences, and interaction patterns that determine how an agent should respond or act for a particular user [ 244 ]. We view a user profile as a structured state 

ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 9

constructed from heterogeneous features, where the key design questions are which signals are decision-critical and how they should be aggregated into a usable form. Accordingly, we divide profile modeling into persona-based and response-based ap-proaches, reflecting whether the profile is anchored in user attributes or in user-specific feedback on model outputs. 

Persona-based Modeling. Persona-based methods infer high-level user attributes that remain informative across contexts. Profiles may be built from explicit self-descriptions and from implicit cues in historical interactions, and are often represented as either a structured attribute vector or a free-form natural-language persona summary. For instance, AlignXpert [ 103 ] constructs a high-dimensional preference space grounded in psychological and alignment-motivated dimensions, then estimates user-specific positions to form a preference vector. FSPO [ 194 ] adopts a user-description chain-of-thought mechanism to allocate additional inference effort to preference analysis, producing more fine-grained persona descriptions. Beyond one-shot estimation, sev-eral systems iteratively refine latent user attributes through ongoing dialogue. RLPA [ 290 ] and Wang and Yang [222] update user representations over interaction rounds, while RGMem [ 212 ] builds evolving profiles by extracting user insights from episodic memory fragments and progressively aggregating them via hierarchical refinement. Relatedly, RLPF [ 246 ] optimizes concise, human-readable user summaries using a feedback loop tied to downstream performance, and CURIO [ 218 ] introduces an intrin-sic motivation objective that encourages active inference of latent user types during multi-turn interaction. 

Response-based Modeling. Response-based methods model user preferences through how a user evaluates candidate outputs, capturing context-sensitive notions of what constitutes a preferred response. These profiles are typically derived from fine-grained supervision such as pairwise comparisons or implicit behavioral signals, and are de-signed to generalize preference judgments across prompts and situations. A common approach factorizes individual preference into a shared reward feature basis with user-specific weights, enabling rapid adaptation under sparse user data. For example, RFM [ 17 ], PReF [ 187 ], and LoRe [ 19 ] represent preferences as linear combinations of shared reward features and infer user-specific coefficients from limited feedback. Complementarily, some methods parameterize user preferences in natural language to improve interpretability and controllability. Janus [ 97 ] organizes preference di-mensions hierarchically from coarse categories to finer-grained value descriptions, while PLUS [ 144 ] learns text-based preference summaries that condition downstream reward modeling for personalized scoring and generation. 

3.2 Agent Role Definition 

Agent role definition determines who the agent is in relation to the user, which can be divided into user-simulated agent definition and adaptive agent definition based on whether the agent is on behalf of the user or not.  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 10 Y. Xu et al.

User-simulated Agent Definition. In some applications, especially delegation-oriented tasks such as user or dialogue simulation, agents are instantiated to act as simulated users with pre-defined attributes and preferences. These profiles usually function as static role configurations, parameterizing agent behavior for testing or evaluation and can substantially influence task planning and execution [ 147 ]. This one-way profile modeling emulates user traits without learning from real interaction, but remains crucial for creating realistic human–agent scenarios, enabling efficient large-scale ex-perimentation, and ensuring behavioral diversity across synthetic users[ 47 , 101 , 226 ]. 

Adaptive Agent Definition. In interactive personalized systems, the agent role itself becomes conditional on the user profile. The agent dynamically adjusts its persona, tone, or autonomy based on the user’s characteristics, goals, and trust level, enabling a two-way adaptive process. LD-Agent [ 102 ] adopts a bidirectional user-agent modeling approach [ 261 ], utilizing a tunable persona extractor to manage long-term persona bank for both sides. PersonaAgent [ 280 ] optimizes the user-specific system prompt through textual loss feedback between simulated and ground-truth responses, enabling test-time user-preference alignment. PersonaX [ 191 ] performs offline clustering and multi-persona profiling, combining with profile retrieval for online decision making. RPP [ 131 ] proposes an instance-wise prompt optimization framework, which includes both the user historical information and the task guidance for agent, aiming to generate personalized discrete prompts for better individual recommendation performance. 

3.3 Discussion 

Role of Profile Modeling. Profile modeling provides the foundational layer of person-alization by defining both who the user is through user profiling and who the agent becomes through agent role definition. These two components offer complementary but distinct perspectives: user profiling seeks to construct expressive representations of user traits and preferences, while agent role definition determines how the agent’s operational style should adapt to those user characteristics. 

Challenges. Despite their tight conceptual coupling, they face different technical challenges. On the one hand, user profiling is challenged by sparse, noisy, and in-consistent preference signals, as well as the potential conflict between long-term tendencies and context-specific preferences. On the other hand, agent role definition faces the trade-off between flexibility and stability across tasks, avoiding both rigid roles and undesirable role drift. Finally, a broader challenge lies in the weak coupling between profiling and role adaptation. Effective personalization requires bidirectional alignment where the agent must update its role based on refined user profiles, while user profiling should leverage signals arising from the agent’s adaptive behavior. 

Future Directions. Existing user profiling methods often yield representations that are static or task-specific, and agent roles that lack controllability or principled adap-tation dynamics. Moreover, user profiling and agent role definition are typically devel-oped in isolation, with limited mechanisms for mutual adjustment. Going forward, promising directions include lightweight and interpretable profiling methods that

4 Memory 

While profile modeling captures stable and high-level user traits, many fine-grained details essential for personalized responses cannot be fully encoded in a plain profile. To support such detailed personalization, agents rely on a memory module, which refers to the system’s ability to retain, recall, and leverage past interactions to improve future behavior [ 240 ]. Memory is commonly divided into personal memory , which stores user inputs and interaction histories, and system memory , which records intermediate reasoning states or task-execution artifacts [ 250 ]. This survey focuses on personal memory , as it directly shapes how agents adapt to individual users. In the following, we first discuss how personal memory is structured and updated, and then how it is retrieved to support personalized behaviors. Personal memory can be implemented as internal memory , which embeds user-related information within the LLM’s internal components, or as external memory ,which stores information in an auxiliary system. Internal memory approaches oper-ate through modifications to model parameters [78, 148, 208, 228], key–value caches [ 161 , 254 ], or hidden states [ 233 , 281 , 286 ]. Although parameter-efficient and compact [ 234 ], internal memory is constrained by limited memory capacity, challenges in updating stored information, and reliance on model retraining, making it less suitable for dynamic, long-horizon personalization. By contrast, external memory stores infor-mation in an auxiliary system, usually combined with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG; 99 ), which enriches the input prompt with information retrieved from the exter-nal knowledge base, thereby improving user satisfaction and engagement. Compared to internal memory, external memory is more flexible and especially beneficial for users with limited personal data [ 175 ]. However, it requires careful design for storage, management, and personalized access. Given these considerations, we focus on exter-nal personal memory systems and structure this section around two key components: (1) personal memory schemes , which define how user-related information is stored and updated, and (2) personalized retrieval , which determines how stored information is accessed to guide personalized behaviors. 

4.1 Personal Memory Schemes 

Personal memory schemes define the foundation of the memory module, specifying how user-related information is organized, stored, and updated throughout interaction. Based on the timescale and influence of stored information, personal memory is often divided into short-term memory, which captures recent conversational context, and long-term memory, which accumulates enduring user information across sessions. Short-term memory provides reliable signals for immediate responses but is limited by context window constraints and cannot effectively utilize information beyond the cur-rent interaction session. On the other hand, long-term memory enables representation of long-tail individual data and supports self-evolution through accumulated experi-ence [ 76 ], but suffers from information overload and difficulties in timely updating.  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 12 Y. Xu et al.

As a result, recent personalized agents increasingly adopt hybrid memory designs that combine both short- and long-term memory to balance continuity and fine-grained adaptation [ 102 ], motivating careful design of the underlying memory structure and 

update mechanism .

4.1.1 Memory Structure. The memory structure determines what information is stored 

and how it is represented , forming the foundation for effective personalized retrieval. Early agent systems often appended full dialogue histories or execution trajectories to memory [ 120 ], but such raw storage introduced redundancy, limiting the agent’s ability to surface user-relevant information. To achieve scalable personalized memory management, recent works convert interaction histories into compact and structured representations tailored to task demands [ 119 , 296 ]. Based on the format, memory structures can be broadly categorized as textual memory and structured memory .

Textual Memory. Textual memory stores user-related information in natural lan-guage form, typically by summarizing multi-turn interactions into concise and in-terpretable units. Because such representations preserve rich semantics and align naturally with the input–output format of LLMs, textual memory has become the dominant choice for personalized agents across diverse tasks [ 287 ]. However, effective textual memory design faces two key challenges: defining appropriate segmentation boundaries and capturing comprehensive user-related information. Early approaches often relied on rigid turn-level or session-level segmentation, which frequently mis-aligned with the semantic units of dialogue and resulted in fragmented retrieval. To address this, recent work has explored more flexible, topic-coherent segmentation strategies. For instance, SeCom [ 154 ] and RMM [ 207 ] partition conversations into se-mantically coherent segments, enabling memory units to reflect stable conversational topics rather than arbitrary boundaries. Nemori [ 145 ] further improves segmentation accuracy by using a boundary detector to group entire interactions into coherent episodes, preserving the integrity of user intent. Beyond segmentation, many systems enrich textual memory with auxiliary attributes such as timestamps, topic labels, and user personality [ 119 , 136 , 260 ]. For example, CAIM [ 241 ] introduces a tagging system that stores events with tuples of related tags, inductive thoughts, and timestamps. MemoryBank [ 296 ] and PersonaAgent [ 280 ] adopt hierarchical summarization to model multi-level event structure and dynamic personality shifts, simulating the multi-component structure of human memory and enabling more coherent and human-like personalized interaction. MemInsight [ 172 ] is an autonomous memory augmentation approach that employs an attribute mining module to extract structured, semantically meaningful attributes from input dialogues. Several recent works draw inspiration from human memory systems [ 182 , 283 ]. These systems typically distinguish between semantic memory and episodic memory [ 123 , 125 , 158 ]. MIRIX [ 230 ] proposes an architecture with six specialized memory components (core, episodic, semantic, proce-dural, resource, and knowledge vault) organized hierarchically. Mem-𝛼 [ 235 ] proposes an architecture with core, semantic, and episodic memory modules, combined with an RL framework that teaches agents to manage complex memory through interaction and feedback. MemWeaver [ 270 ] combines temporal and semantic dimensions across  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 13

behavioral-level and cognitive-level memory to jointly represent user actions and long-term preferences. Collectively, these techniques advance textual memory toward richer representations, more coherent segmentation, and multi-level organization, allowing personalized agents to retain user-relevant details more faithfully and capture both explicit and implicit signals with higher accuracy. 

Structured Memory. Structured memory provides a more explicit and computation-ally manipulable representation of interaction histories by storing information in predefined formats enriched with structural relationships. Compared with textual summaries, structured memory introduces stronger inductive biases that support scalable retrieval, multi-granularity reasoning, and more fine-grained personalization. Current approaches can be broadly grouped into vector-based memory , hierarchical tree structures , and graph-based memory architectures . Vector-based memory rep-resents each memory unit using learned embeddings stored in a vector database, enabling scalable similarity search and fast retrieval of user-relevant information. This design has become a standard practice in personalized agents due to its effi-ciency and flexibility [ 29 , 151 , 253 ]. However, vector memory alone discards structural relationships between memory items and offers limited interpretability, motivating more structured alternatives or hybrid systems that combine vectors with explicit organizational formats. Hierarchical tree structures organize memory at multiple abstraction levels, where each node corresponds to a memory unit and parent–child relationships encode increasingly coarse summaries. For instance, RAPTOR [ 178 ]recursively clusters and summarizes text into a tree, supporting retrieval at both fine-and coarse-grained levels. MemTree [ 170 ] similarly constructs a memory hierarchy in which each node stores aggregated textual content and semantic embeddings, allowing memory to be represented at varying abstraction depths. Graph-based architectures capture richer relationships by linking memory units through typed edges representing temporal, semantic, or relational dependencies [ 29 , 77 , 113 ]. Some methods focus on behavioral coherence, such as MemWeaver [270], which builds an event-level behavior graph where interaction events are nodes connected by temporal and semantic edges, along with a context-aware random walk mechanism. Other designs integrate multi-dimensional subgraphs to capture more nuanced relationships between memory units. For example, AriGraph [ 12 ] combines semantic knowledge (entity–relation triplets) with episodic observation nodes, enabling both long-term knowledge accumulation and temporally grounded recall. Zep [ 167 ] introduces a temporally-aware dynamic knowledge graph with three tiers, including episodic for non-lossy data storage, entity-level semantic for extracted entities and relationships, and community-level conceptual for high-level summarizations of strongly connected entity clusters. MemGAS [ 256 ] further constructs cross-granularity associations across session-, turn-, summary-, and keyword-level memory to enrich personalized retrieval signals. While powerful, these graph-based structures often depend on computation-ally expensive LLM-based entity and relation extraction and may lose fine-grained contextual information during graph construction. To address this, SGMem [ 253 ] 

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 14 Y. Xu et al.

proposes a lightweight sentence-graph memory, linking chunk nodes with their con-stituent sentences and connecting sentences via similarity edges to better preserve contextual semantics. Similarly, LiCoMemory [ 68 ] introduces CogniGraph, a light-weight hierarchical indexing graph that organizes session summaries, entity–relation triples, and dialogue chunks through cross-layer hyperlinks, keeping the graph mini-mal while storing full content externally. Across these approaches, structured memory aims to provide richer organization by modeling how information relates, evolves, and aggregates across interactions. 

4.1.2 Update mechanism. Memory update mechanism determines how an agent’s memory evolves as new interactions occur, ensuring that stored information remains accurate, consistent, and aligned with the user’s changing preferences. When a new memory unit arrives, the system must decide whether to incorporate it as a new entry, revise or merge existing ones, or prune outdated or contradictory content [ 42 ]. Broadly, memory updating differs across two types of external memory: textual memory, where updates rewrite or reorganize summary-level information, and structured memory, where updates modify entity–relation structures through node/edge creation, conflict resolution, and temporal maintenance. We review the update mechanism for these two categories separately. 

Textual memory stores interaction histories as natural-language segments or sum-maries. Updating therefore concerns how these segments are merged, rewritten, or reorganized when new dialogue arrives. Existing approaches can be grouped into 

similarity-driven updates and inference-guided updates .

Similarity-driven updates. These methods update memory by comparing new seg-ments with existing summaries and adjusting them based on semantic proximity and auxiliary metadata such as timestamps or topic labels. RMM [ 207 ] summarizes each session by topic and updates the memory bank through semantic merging, re-placing or consolidating summaries with high topical similarity. CAIM [ 241 ] extends this paradigm with a post-thinking step that generates structured, tag-annotated summaries from recent dialogue and integrates them into memory while refining related historical entries. Mem0 [ 29 ] introduces an LLM-driven update framework that chooses among ADD, UPDATE, DELETE , and NOOP operations for each new fact based on semantic similarity with existing memories. Memory-R1 [ 264 ] builds on this with a reinforcement-learning memory manager that learns the optimal operation policy across multi-session interactions. 

Inference-guided updates. Other systems treat memory updating as a reasoning or decision-making process, allowing the agent to incorporate implicit preference shifts or contextual dynamics not captured by similarity alone. Nemori [ 145 ] exemplifies this through its Predict–Calibrate mechanism, achieving proactively evolving memory based on gaps between predicted and actual user responses. PREMem [ 91 ] similarly performs pre-storage reasoning across sessions to analyze thematic evolution before inserting new content, enabling more consistent long-term topic tracking. These  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 15

methods provide more adaptive and self-correcting memory dynamics, capturing both short-term changes and long-term shifts in user preferences. 

Structured memory organizes information into interconnected architectures where inserting a new unit often requires reorganizing its related counterparts. Update mechanisms can likewise be grouped into similarity-driven and reasoning- or agentic-driven strategies. 

Similarity-driven updates. Most personalized structured-memory systems update the memory by assessing semantic similarity between new information and existing units, then making local modifications to maintain coherence. In hierarchical tree struc-tures, MemTree [ 170 ] updates memory by traversing the tree via semantic similarity, inserting new nodes through sibling attachment or leaf expansion, and incrementally updating ancestor nodes with LLM-based aggregation to preserve hierarchical coher-ence. Graph-based architectures demand even more complex updates, as every node may connect to multiple others [ 68 ]. Mem0 𝑔 [ 29 ] embeds new triples, matches them to existing nodes, and establishes metadata-rich edges while using an LLM-based conflict resolver to deactivate outdated relations. Similarly, Zep [ 167 ] integrates new episodic facts by timestamp alignment, updating or deactivating inconsistent edges and adjusting higher-level community clusters to maintain a temporally grounded semantic–episodic graph. MemGAS [ 256 ] clusters new and historical multi-granularity chunks with GMM, selectively reinforcing only contextually relevant associations to ensure stable personalization over long horizons. 

Reasoning- or agentic-driven updates. A smaller but emerging set of systems incor-porate explicit reasoning, decision-making, or agentic inference to guide how memory should be updated, moving beyond similarity thresholds toward more cognitively aligned update dynamics. A-mem [ 260 ] adopts an agentic update scheme in which each new memory becomes a structured note that triggers semantic linking and retroactive refinement of historical notes, enabling a continuously self-evolving memory graph. Although not designed for personalization, recent non-personalized graph-memory systems illustrate the potential of this direction. TRAIL [ 291 ] performs inference-driven updates by validating and refining candidate triples through confidence-guided decisions rather than pure similarity matching, while AriGraph [ 12 ] uses agentic reasoning to determine whether new observations should create links, update existing nodes, or refine episodic associations. These systems demonstrate that reasoning-based graph evolution can produce more coherent and cognitively grounded memory updates, suggesting a promising direction. 

4.2 Personalized Retrieval 

Memory retrieval determines which stored information becomes available to an agent at inference time and therefore directly influences the quality and consistency of personalized behaviors. Unlike conventional RAG systems, retrieval for personalized LLM-powered agents must simultaneously satisfy three requirements: (1) content relevance to the current query, (2) structural consistency with the underlying memory  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 16 Y. Xu et al.

organization, and (3) personal alignment, ensuring that retrieved information reflects the user’s preferences. Existing methods address these challenges through three dominant mechanisms: content-based retrieval , structure-aware retrieval , and policy-guided retrieval .

Content-based retrieval. Content-based retrieval identifies relevant memory by mea-suring semantic or lexical similarity between the user’s query and stored information, typically implemented with dense retrievers (e.g., FAISS [ 82 ], Contriever [ 70 ]) or sparse lexical retrievers (e.g., BM25 [ 171 ]). Retrieved segments are generally appended to the agent’s prompt to enrich contextual grounding [ 91 , 230 , 260 ]. To capture com-prehensive information about the user, content-based retrieval can be operated at multiple granularity levels. For example, RAPTOR [ 178 ] and MemTree [ 170 ] perform hierarchical content-based retrieval by selecting nodes whose embeddings are most similar to the query across different abstraction levels, even though the underlying tree structure is not explicitly traversed for reasoning. To further improve retrieval quality, some methods incorporate post-retrieval filtering or query refinement. Yuan et al .[271] adopt a self-reflection mechanism that assesses whether the retrieved content is sufficient for response generation and refines the query when necessary. While effective at capturing semantic relevance, content-based approaches may overlook structural dependencies and long-range contextual relationships, motivating more structured retrieval designs. 

Structure-aware retrieval. Structure-aware retrieval leverages the organization of memory, such as graph connectivity and episodic relationships, to retrieve informa-tion that is contextually linked to the query rather than merely similar in content. Such methods are often implemented in hierarchical or hybrid modes, where an ini-tial retrieval of entities, topics, or relations guides subsequent retrieval of detailed memory units [ 55 , 77 ], potentially augmented with graph-based techniques such as Personalized PageRank (PPR; 15 ). For instance, AriGraph [ 12 ] first retrieves semanti-cally relevant relational triplets and then, conditioned on these triplets, retrieves the most relevant episodic memories. Zep [ 167 ] combines semantic search with graph-structured signals, such as episode-mention frequency and node-distance scoring, to identify contextually relevant conversational episodes within its temporal memory graph. Other approaches enhance structure-aware retrieval with adaptive routing or self-reflection. MemGAS [ 256 ] computes multi-granularity semantic similarities and integrates them using an entropy-based router, then applies PPR over a dynami-cally maintained association graph to retrieve structurally coherent memory units. AssoMem [ 277 ] performs associative retrieval by selecting clue nodes and linked utterances, followed by a multi-signal ranking procedure that fuses relevance, graph-based importance, and temporal alignment through a mutual-information–driven fusion strategy. By explicitly modeling the structural dependencies among memory units, structure-aware retrieval provides richer contextual grounding and improves the coverage of long-range or implicit user preferences.  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 17

Policy-guided retrieval. Policy-guided retrieval refers to retrieval strategies in which the selection of information is governed by user- or task-conditioned decision policies rather than static similarity matching [ 280 , 296 ]. Unlike content-based or structure-aware retrieval, which rely primarily on semantic proximity or memory topology, policy-guided retrieval actively determines how retrieval should proceed and can operate over arbitrary corpora without relying on a persistent user memory store. A first line of work focuses on pre-retrieval query transformation , where the retrieval intent is personalized before search. PBR [ 284 ] generates user-style pseudo feedback and corpus-anchored expansions to form a personalized query representation, while PersonaRAG [ 273 ] adapts document selection and ranking using real-time behavioral signals such as reading patterns and interaction context. A second line of work adopts 

policy-driven strategy selection , learning to invoke or integrate heterogeneous retrieval tools based on the task. UniMS-RAG [ 221 ] formulates retrieval as a policy-learning problem, where supervised and reinforcement signals train the system to choose among keyword search, dense retrieval, and knowledge-graph lookup and to combine their results effectively. Persona-DB [ 200 ] employs a two-stage persona-aware policy that first matches users via high-level persona keys and then combines retrievals from target users and collaborator users using a learned composition ratio to address sparsity and enhance personalization. A third direction incorporates schema-guided information completion , framing retrieval as a structured inference process. OCG-Agent [ 190 ] decomposes narrative queries into an abstract data type schema and triggers a multi-route retrieval routine that fills schema fields in parallel; a reflect-and-complete loop then issues targeted supplementary retrievals until all required attributes are satisfied, followed by semantic normalization to unify heterogeneous results. Collectively, policy-guided retrieval shifts retrieval from passive document matching toward an active, decision-centric paradigm that adapts retrieval behavior to user preferences, task requirements, and interaction context, enabling more flexible and personalized information acquisition. 

4.3 Discussion 

Role of Personal Memory. Personal memory bridges short-term intent and long-term preference modeling. It fulfills three closely related roles. First, it supports preference evolution, allowing the agent to track how user preferences change across sessions rather than relying solely on fixed trait representations. Second, it provides person-alized contextual grounding, preserving fine-grained user details that are essential for accurate personalization but cannot be fully encoded in static profiles. Third, it enables preference-conditioned generation, guiding the agent to interpret new queries and produce responses informed by retrieved user-relevant signals. Together, these roles transform memory from a passive log of interactions into an active component of the personalization loop. 

Challenges. Despite its importance, personal memory faces several challenges. First, user preferences evolve at different rates, requiring memory systems to balance rapid adaptation with the preservation of long-term traits. Second, memory stores may  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 18 Y. Xu et al.

accumulate redundancy or hallucinations, especially when updates rely on LLM-driven summarization or relation extraction, leading to degraded personalization over long horizons. Third, ensuring that retrieved content is not only semantically relevant but also truly aligned with user preferences remains difficult. Finally, personal memory raises privacy and transparency concerns, highlighting the need for user-controllable and privacy-preserving memory mechanisms. 

Future Directions. Memory modules also have inherent limitations. Textual memo-ries scale poorly and require repeated rewriting; structured memories provide con-trollability but depend on costly extraction and risk losing contextual nuance; update mechanisms oscillate between shallow similarity rules and computationally heavy inference; and retrieval pipelines remain sensitive to ranking biases and distribution shifts. These limitations point to several broad future directions across the memory pipeline, including hybrid memory architectures that combine the semantic richness of textual representations with the organizational clarity of structured formats, more principled and user-controllable update mechanisms that ensure reliability and trans-parency, and improved retrieval strategies that better integrate personal alignment with semantic relevance. 

5 Planning in Personalized LLM-powered Agents 

Planning constitutes the cognitive engine of intelligent agents, enabling the decompo-sition of high-level objectives into actionable steps and the formulation of execution strategies [ 126 , 201 ]. In the context of personalized agents, planning transcends generic task completion; it must optimize for user utility , aligning decision paths with individ-ual preferences, constraints, and latent intents [ 45 , 56 , 288 ]. Unlike standard planners that prioritize efficiency or logical correctness, personalized planners navigate a con-strained search space defined by the user’s unique profile and behavioral history, often requiring the integration of long-term memory and preference models [ 63 , 241 ]. We categorize these methodologies into two distinct paradigms based on the integration of user signals: One-shot Planning , where personalization is internalized as a prior during reasoning, and Feedback-driven Planning , where personalization emerges dynamically through interaction [203, 282]. 

5.1 One-Shot Planning 

One-shot personalized planning refers to generating a complete plan in a single inference pass, where a plan is represented as a structured decomposition of subgoals and decisions conditioned on user-specific constraints and latent intent. We analyze one-shot methods along two axes: how user signals are grounded into planning representations ( Preference Induction and Memory Conditioning ) and how plans are synthesized via compute-intensive generation ( Internal Refinement ). 

Preference Induction. This line of work constructs a planning-ready user state by inferring latent intent and translating heterogeneous user signals into explicit con-straints or soft objectives that guide plan generation [ 56 ]. For instance, ALIGNXPLORE [ 104 ] utilizes a User-description Chain-of-Thought framework to synthesize a compact,  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 19

semantically grounded preference description from sparse interaction traces. Instead of retrieving raw logs, the model performs extended inductive reasoning to form global preference constraints that condition downstream planning. Similarly, in sequential recommendation, ReaRec [ 210 ] proposes a Think-Before-Recommend paradigm, using reasoning position embeddings to infer the user’s latent intent trajectory prior to plan generation. In embodied and decision-making contexts, PbP [ 257 ] demonstrates that treating learned user preferences as intermediate abstractions substantially improves few-shot personalized planning performance. 

Memory Conditioning. Complementary to inductive constraint formation, memory-grounded methods condition one-shot planning on selectively retrieved and com-pressed user history, ensuring consistency with both long-term preferences and recent context [ 207 , 241 ]. PRIME [ 283 ] integrates a dual-memory architecture with a Person-alized Thought Process that explicitly traces how past experiences and stable beliefs influence current decisions. Before plan generation, PRIME synthesizes a personalized thought trace via self-distillation, effectively grounding planning in the user-specific cognitive context. PersonaAgent [ 280 ] adopts a retrieval-augmented persona mech-anism, dynamically constructing system prompts from a unified memory bank to maintain cross-session planning consistency. Related work in recommendation and dialogue systems similarly shows that structured user memory retrieval significantly improves alignment and coherence in one-shot decision-making [24, 63]. 

Internal Refinement. Beyond direct one-pass generation, internal refinement im-proves it by constructing an initial plan and then internally critiquing and revising it against a user-conditioned objective. Related self-refinement templates such as Reflexion and structured critique-and-revise frameworks provide general mechanisms for generating reflective feedback and triggering plan edits [ 52 , 193 ], while recent test-time-compute reasoning models make deeper within-pass self-correction increas-ingly practical [ 54 , 140 ]. Representative systems instantiate this paradigm in several ways. In personalized travel planning, TravelPlanner++ [ 195 ] and PTS [ 184 ] study reflection-style itinerary refinement, where intermediate plans are iteratively revised under derived user implicit long-horizon preferences. PREFINE [ 216 ] constructs a pseudo-user critic and user-specific rubrics from interaction history, then critiques and revises candidates to better satisfy the target user. In embodied command execution, IoTGPT [ 269 ] applies self-correction by testing intermediate command sequences in a simulated environment and revising the plan before deployment. 

5.2 Feedback-Driven Planning 

Feedback-driven planning treats the initial plan as a hypothesis to be refined through interaction. These systems recognize that user intent is often underspecified, evolving, or partially latent [ 124 , 282 ], and employ iterative loops to converge on an optimal personalized solution [ 203 , 223 ]. We further organize feedback-driven planning by the source of feedback and how it updates the plan, including information-seeking clarification, mixed-initiative user revision, and non-user feedback signals.  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 20 Y. Xu et al.

Clarification Seeking. A central challenge in feedback-driven personalized planning lies in deciding when to ask for additional information: excessive clarification in-creases user burden, while premature commitment risks misinterpreting user-specific constraints or latent intent. Prior studies indicate that user requests in planning tasks are frequently underspecified or ambiguous [ 124 ], motivating approaches that treat clarification as decision-making under uncertainty. For instance, SAGE-Agent [ 203 ] formulates selective questioning as a POMDP and issues clarification queries only when the expected value of information outweighs interaction cost. Similarly, ECLAIR [ 143 ] focuses on detecting missing arguments or ambiguous entities in user instructions and triggers targeted follow-up questions before committing to a plan. Additionally, Ask-before-Plan style frameworks [ 282 ] explicitly decouple clarification from plan synthesis, showing that resolving key uncertainties upfront improves robust-ness and downstream plan quality. Collectively, these methods establish clarification as a principled and integral component of feedback-driven personalized planning rather than an ad hoc interaction heuristic. 

User Revision. User edits provide a high-fidelity personalization signal that can up-date user-conditioned objectives, constraints, and preferences for subsequent planning and generation. Gao et al . [45] show that learning latent preferences from historical edits can reduce future edit effort and improve user-specific alignment in subjec-tive settings. Building on this mixed-initiative view, AIPOM [ 88 ] represents plans as editable structures, enabling users to revise intermediate plans while the agent updates constraints and priorities accordingly. Similar revision loops appear in domain systems such as Dango [ 25 ], which supports iterative correction of agent-produced data-processing workflows, and PlanFitting [ 192 ], which refines personalized exercise plans through conversational revisions. This revision-driven interaction pattern is also adopted in deployed coding agents, where users review proposed changes and iteratively provide follow-up edits or instructions to steer subsequent updates [ 13 , 49 ]. 

Non-user Feedback. Beyond direct user input, plans can be refined using non-user signals that improve feasibility and preference satisfaction without repeatedly bur-dening the user. One line of work leverages agent-mediated feedback: VAIAGE [ 117 ]employs specialized agents to negotiate route feasibility and recommendations under user preferences, while Collab-Rec [ 16 ] supports multi-stakeholder planning with explicit user-advocacy mechanisms. Another line exploits environment-mediated feed-back by simulating intermediate outcomes. For instance, SceneWeaver [ 267 ] follows a Reason–Act–Reflect loop and triggers re-planning when rendered intermediate results violate user-specified attributes. 

5.3 Discussion 

Role of Personalized Planning. Personalized planning serves two complementary roles in LLM-based agents. First, it operationalizes personalization by bridging up-stream user intent inference and preference modeling into downstream decision-making [ 24 ], instantiating user-contingent objectives, constraints, and trade-offs that govern a plan. Second, particularly in feedback-driven settings [ 88 ], planning functions  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 21

as a closed-loop optimization process that alternates between eliciting informative feedback and revising the plan, enabling the agent to proactively and progressively uncover user intent and converge toward a higher degree of personalization. 

Challenges. Personalized planning raises several challenges. First, user intent and preferences are often partially observed and evolve over time [ 124 , 203 ], complicating when to infer, elicit, and commit. Second, personalized utility is also multi-objective [ 184 ], requiring arbitration among competing constraints and idiosyncratic trade-offs. Moreover, interactive refinement must remain controllable and convergent, limiting user burden while avoiding unstable revisions. Existing paradigms involve inherent trade-offs. One-shot planning internalizes user signals as priors for low-latency, coher-ent plans, but can be brittle under sparse or shifting signals. Feedback-driven planning improves alignment via iterative elicitation and revision, at the cost of additional latency and user effort [117]. 

Future Directions. Future work may benefit from developing uncertainty-aware planning workflows that adaptively allocate internal computation and revision effort based on confidence in inferred preferences and task stakes [ 140 ], combining the efficiency of one-shot planning with the alignment benefits of feedback-driven refine-ment. Besides, a promising direction is to learn user-conditioned planning patterns at both inter- and intra-user levels [ 163 ], capturing systematic differences in decom-position style and trade-off resolution while modeling their stability and drift over time, therefore providing reusable personalization signals that generalize across tasks. Finally, several aspects remain comparatively under-explored, including robustness to variations in user specification [ 203 ] and privacy-preserving planning [ 252 ] that supports long-horizon user conditioning without exposing raw personal data. 

6 Action Execution in Personalized LLM-powered Agents 

Action execution is the stage where a personalized agent realizes decisions through concrete operations in the external environment and closes the loop with execution feedback [ 87 , 186 ]. Unlike purely task-centric execution, this layer must ground ac-tions in user-specific constraints and preferences, and remain adaptive when tools or environments produce unexpected outcomes. We organize personalized action execution into two stages: a Pre-action Stage that selects and parameterizes actions under user-conditioned constraints, and a Post-action Stage that leverages execution signals for recovery and preference-consistent outcome realization. 

6.1 Pre-action Stage 

The pre-action stage focuses on user-conditioned action decision and grounding. It translates a selected intent or plan step into executable tool calls or environment operations under user-specific constraints. We further decompose this stage into 

action policy , which selects among functionally valid execution options for the same step, and action grounding , which instantiates the selected action with user-specific arguments and realizations.  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 22 Y. Xu et al.

6.1.1 Action Policy. At this level, personalization manifests as a bias over execution choices, spanning both tool utilization and higher-level action decisions. PEToolLLaMA [ 258 ] is an early effort that formalizes personalized tool learning from interaction history, constructs a tool-use dataset with diverse user preferences, and trains models to improve personalized tool selection. In the web-agent setting, PUMA [ 21 ] adopts a similar preference-aware learning strategy, showing that personalization can also shape action choices during web navigation and task completion. ToolSpectrum [ 27 ]further incorporates user profiles together with environmental context, indicating that context-aware conditioning can improve user-aligned tool utilization. Beyond selecting appropriate tools, ColorAgent [ 106 ] learns to proactively engage the user to clarify ambiguous intentions or incomplete instructions. Moving beyond tool invocation, ValuePilot [ 127 ] studies action-level decision making under user values, where agents evaluate and select actions according to user value preferences to produce context-sensitive and individualized choices. 

6.1.2 Action Grounding. Action grounding operationalizes a selected action by instan-tiating it into executable tool calls with user-conditioned constraints and parameters [21 , 27 ]. While many requests can be grounded by directly applying user-specific set-tings, personalization becomes more challenging in corner cases. A frequent grounding-time failure mode is infeasibility, where the instantiated query becomes unsatisfiable under the imposed constraints. AWARE-US [ 95 ] frames this as a preference-aware query repair problem, arguing that agents should restore feasibility by relaxing the least preferred constraints to the user rather than applying default heuristics. Another recurring issue is the omission of essential tool arguments. Huang et al . [65] study how agents infer unspecified arguments from user profiles, reducing execution friction while maintaining alignment with user intent. 

6.2 Post-action Stage 

The post-action stage closes the loop after an action is executed. Given execution feed-back, the agent evaluates whether the outcome satisfies user-conditioned objectives, applies corrective adjustments when mismatches arise ( execution-time correction ), and produces final outputs that conform to user preferences and quality criteria ( outcome realization ). 

6.2.1 Execution-Time Correction. A failed execution commonly produces feedback sig-nals, which can be leveraged for adjusting behavior without re-planning from scratch. While explicit studies on preference-aware recovery in LLM-powered agents remain limited, adjacent work on mitigating tool overuse under diminishing returns [ 160 ]and structured reflection against tool-interaction failures [ 199 ] provides foundational mechanisms upon which personalized recovery strategies can be built. 

6.2.2 Outcome Realization. Even when multiple execution outcomes are functionally valid, user satisfaction depends on how results are presented in accordance with individual preferences. In recommendation-oriented agents, personalized re-ranking is a core mechanism for transforming candidate results into ordered outputs that reflect  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 23

user-specific utility [ 156 ]. Methods such as PEAR and multi-level interaction reranking (MIR) explicitly model cross-item dependencies together with user preference features, yielding more faithful final rankings and improved utility metrics [ 112 , 255 ]. Beyond ranking, personalization also governs which execution outcomes are retained or discarded. WARPP [ 134 ] emphasizes runtime personalization in workflow execution by pruning or adjusting conditional branches based on user attributes, reducing irrelevant or erroneous outcomes without re-planning the entire process. In addition to selecting among existing results, outcome realization may involve proactive augmentation. ETAPP [59] treats proactivity as a personalization signal, evaluating whether agents can propose additional actions or suggestions to better satisfy user needs. 

6.3 Discussion 

Role of Personalized Action Execution. Action execution constitutes a core stage that bridges high-level reasoning with tangible outcomes in LLM-based agents [ 30 , 165 ]. By determining how abstract decisions are instantiated, execution-level choices directly influence whether personalization is effectively realized rather than remaining at the level of internal reasoning, which substantially shapes user experience, trust, and perceived utility. Consequently, personalized action execution functions as the final bottleneck of personalization in agent systems, translating inferred user intent into observable behavior and closing the loop between internal personalization mechanisms and external outcomes. 

Challenges. Designing personalized action execution mechanisms remains challeng-ing due to several factors. First, execution contexts are highly heterogeneous across tools, environments, and domains, complicating the design of general mechanisms that consistently respect individual preferences. Second, personalization signals at execution time are often subtle, requiring abstract preferences to be translated into concrete parameters rather than explicit instructions. Third, execution feedback may expose ambiguity or brittleness in how user preferences were interpreted upstream, necessitating careful handling and, in some cases, coordination with planning or preference modeling components to avoid cascading errors. 

Future Directions. Research on personalized action execution remains relatively limited, leaving several promising directions open. At the execution primitive level, developing personalization-aware tools that emphasize non-functional characteris-tics could enable differential behavior beyond task correctness. At the control level, learning preference-conditioned execution policies that generalize across tools and environments may allow agents to reuse personalization signals more effectively. Ad-ditionally, agents should better handle vague or missing arguments, localize execution failures, and aggregate outcomes in ways that reflect user priorities rather than default heuristics. Finally, there is a growing need for execution-level evaluation protocols that move beyond task success to assess preference adherence, consistency, and user satisfaction, in line with recent calls for more holistic evaluation of agent capabilities.

7 Evaluation 

Evaluating personalized agents presents a unique set of challenges that extend beyond the traditional assessment of AI systems, as the target of optimization is not merely task correctness but long-horizon user-specific satisfaction. These challenges largely stem from the implicit, context-dependent, and evolving nature of human preferences and desires, which are often under-specified, situation-sensitive, and updated through interaction. Consequently, evaluation should prioritize how well an agent aligns with an individual user’s expectations and experience, rather than only how well it completes a task in an objective sense. This motivates the need for a comprehensive evaluation mechanism that characterizes personalization from general principles down to “in-one’s-shoes" assessments that reflect each user’s subjective utility. Unlike standard AI systems, where performance is primarily measured by objective task success, personalized agents are optimized for subjective utility: their behavior should align with an individual user’s preferences, remain consistent across contexts and over time, and adapt as preferences are revealed or revised through interaction, while operating within safety and privacy constraints. Motivated by these considerations, we organize evaluation metrics for personalized agents into five complementary dimensions: Effectiveness , Adaptivity , General-ization , Robustness , and Risk , as illustrated in Table 1. Effectiveness. Effectiveness is the primary criterion for personalization quality and captures whether an agent can deliver user-contingent utility rather than generic help-fulness. It includes (i) preference discovery, where the agent must infer what the user wants from partial interaction traces or contextual signals (Discovery Accuracy [ 75 ]), (ii) preference usage, where agents should correctly incorporates user-specific infor-mation into generation (Knowledge Integration Score [ 14 , 220 ]), and (iii) preference satisfaction , where the final response should comply with explicit constraints and implicit preferences (Preference Alignment [ 252 , 289 ], Preference-Aware Planning Accuracy [ 257 , 258 ]). Adaptivity. Adaptivity evaluates whether a personalized agent can update its be-havior as new evidence about preferences arrives while preserving continuity and minimizing user burden. Adaptation Success Rate [ 74 , 183 ] measures whether the agent improves personalization after receiving explicit feedback, new constraints, or additional user context. Selective Forgetting [ 61 ] that measures the ability to over-write or retract outdated memory entries when faced with contradictory evidence is equally important due to the evolution of user preferences. Interaction Efficiency [ 108 ]captures the cost of adaptation, such as the number of turns, clarifications, or edits needed before the user accepts the result, which directly impacts practical usability. Proactivity [ 59 ] complements these metrics by measuring whether the agent can anticipate needs and offer helpful next steps at the right time without introducing irrelevant suggestions or unnecessary interruptions. 

Generalization. Generalization captures whether personalization transfers beyond the narrow contexts where it was learned. Out-of-Domain Performance [ 74 ] measures whether an agent can apply a user’s preferences in specific scenarios to new domains, tasks, or situations without requiring the user to restate them. This is particularly important for agents that aim to support diverse daily activities, where preferences learned in one domain should carry over to other domains in a principled way. 

Robustness. Personalized agents operate under inherently noisy and incomplete preference evidence, so a useful evaluation must measure reliability under distribu-tional stress rather than only average-case alignment. Two robustness pressures are especially consequential. Firstly, user preferences are frequently underspecified, par-tially observed, or expressed indirectly. Therefore, Accuracy under Ambiguity [ 65 , 153 ]evaluates whether the agent can make reasonable inferences, ask targeted clarifica-tion questions, and avoid overconfident assumptions when key signals are missing. Secondly, user inputs can be wrong or based on misconceptions, either unintention-ally or due to stale information. Misinformed Condition Performance [ 62 ] captures whether the agent can identify incorrect premises, provide corrective guidance, and still preserve a personalized interaction style while avoiding mistaken beliefs. 

Risk. Risk metrics quantify safety, privacy, and other downside constraints that are inseparable from personalization. Key metrics include: Safety Rate [ 249 ] measures whether the agent remains safe across heterogeneous user profiles, including cases where preferences might conflict with policies or where the user requests risky actions. Risk Sensitivity [ 252 ] evaluates whether the agent recognizes contextual hazards and calibrates its advice, for example by providing warnings, suggesting safer alternatives, or encouraging professional help when appropriate. Because personalization relies on user-specific information, Privacy Leakage Rate [ 142 , 293 ] measures whether the agent discloses private data in disallowed ways, especially under adversarial or indirect prompts. Conversely, Over-Secrecy Rate [ 142 ] measures the utility loss from excessive caution, where the agent refuses or withholds benign, helpful information despite the user’s legitimate request. Together, these metrics target the core trade-off in personalized agents that aims to maximize user utility subject to risk constraints.

7.2 Assessment Paradigms 

The above metrics are instantiated through different assessment paradigms depending on whether the target criterion is objectively verifiable or user-contingent. We group mainstream paradigms into four families. (1) Automatic scoring applies when ground-truth labels or reference answers exist, for example accuracy, precision/recall/F1 scores for classification, and overlap-based metrics such as BLEU [ 155 ] and ROUGE [ 116 ] for generation. (2) Rule-based constraint checking evaluates compliance with verifiable rubrics or hard constraints, such as whether required attributes are included, forbidden attributes are avoided, or preference constraints are satisfied in structured outputs. This protocol is particularly useful for delegation-style tasks where constraints can be deterministically validated. (3) Learned LLM-based evaluators (LLM-E) fine-tune evaluation models to provide more stable, fine-grained diagnostic feedback for specific preference dimensions, trading off generality for higher consistency in the target domain [ 219 ]. (4) LLM-as-a-judge (LLM-J) is commonly used for user-contingent criteria such as preference alignment, tone, and satisfaction, where the judge model is conditioned on a user profile or preference description and asked to rate or rank candidate outputs [ 294 ]. To improve reliability, LLM-J evaluations often adopt pairwise comparisons, calibrated rubrics, and multi-judge aggregation. 

7.3 Benchmark 

Building on the above metric taxonomy, we summarize representative benchmarks for personalized agents and organize them into two broad families. Interactive Alignment Benchmarks evaluate an agent’s ability to elicit, negotiate, and refine user preferences through multi-turn interaction, where the user remains an indispensable part of the loop. In contrast, User-Substitution Benchmarks evaluate whether an agent can stand in for the user by simulating their preferences or persona to produce user-consistent responses or decisions without further user input. Preference Discovery Benchmarks. In this category, IndieValueCatalog [ 75 ]curates value-expressing statements from diverse individuals and evaluates whether models can infer individualized value judgments, highlighting limitations in capturing fine-grained personal values. PersonaBench [ 206 ] constructs synthetic yet realistic personal profiles and associated artifacts to test whether agents can extract and reason over user-specific information. Output-level Alignment Benchmarks. This category evaluates personalization in an end-to-end manner, focusing on whether the agent’s final outputs satisfy a target user’s preferences and constraints in realistic interactive settings. For conversation-centered personalization, benchmarks such as ALOE [ 248 ] and PrefEval [ 289 ] test whether agents can infer, retain, and consistently follow user preferences across multi-turn dialogues. Finally, several benchmarks diagnose user-specific risk, such as PSG-Agent [ 249 ] and PENGUIN [ 252 ] that evaluate profile-contingent safety consid-erations for LLM-based agents, while PrivacyBench [ 142 ] and AgentDam [ 293 ] test privacy compliance for agents when handling sensitive user information.

8 Applications 8.1 Conversational Assistant 

8.1.1 Daily Dialogue Assistants. Personalized dialogue assistants primarily focus on long-term conversational continuity, where the agent must preserve user-specific con-text and generate preference-consistent responses across multiple interactions. These agents serve a wide range of functions, from general-purpose conversational support to open-domain recommendation and information retrieval [ 62 , 285 ]. Recent research has begun to treat long-term interaction as a benchmarked personalization problem [ 153 , 183 ]. Jones et al . [83] further highlights how memory behaviors shape user trust and expectations, emphasizing the need for careful interface design and memory management in deployed personalized assistants. On the product side, chatbots such as ChatGPT [ 152 ], Gemini [ 51 ], and DeepSeek [ 36 ] have operationalized persistent personalization by allowing users to enable, inspect, and delete stored memories. In recommendation and search systems, responses are further tailored to individual needs based on ongoing user interaction [ 66 , 92 , 236 ], evolving user-understanding [226, 275], alongside personalized decision-making models [232, 273]. 

8.1.2 Emotional Support Companions. These agents aim to provide sustained, empa-thetic assistance for users’ emotional well-being, where effectiveness depends strongly on user-contingent factors such as preferred tone, boundaries, and proactivity [ 69 ]. 

ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. 32 Y. Xu et al. 

Personalization can be realized from both sides, where users may actively shape an agent’s persona and interaction dynamics to better match their needs [ 295 ], while agents may adapt their behavior by explicitly modeling supportive strategies dur-ing interaction [ 11 ]. For example, ComPeer [ 122 ] provides proactive peer support by detecting key moments in a dialogue and generating supportive check-ins based on conversation history and personalized strategies. MultiAgentESC [ 262 ] enables strategy-aware support via a multi-agent pipeline that evaluates dialogue understand-ing, strategy selection, and response generation, providing a diverse set of personalized responses for complex emotional interactions. ARIEL [ 198 ] takes affect-aware support further by integrating physiological signals to track emotional states and adjust re-sponses accordingly. These research trends are reflected in companion-style products such as Replika and Inflection AI’s Pi, which focus on long-term personalization and relationship-building as core product features [10, 168]. 

8.1.3 Educational Agents. Educational agents use LLM-based systems to enhance both student and educator experiences by providing personalized guidance, adapting instruction to individual progress, and alleviating routine instructional tasks [ 114 , 185 ]. These agents can be divided into two main categories [ 31 ]: (i) Personalized Pedagogical Agents, which automate pedagogical tasks for both teachers and students [ 7 ], and (ii) Domain-Specific Agents, which focus on specialized fields like math, music, and language learning, offering tailored support to address domain-specific challenges. Personalized pedagogical agents like EduAgent [ 259 ], LG-RAG [ 35 ], and TeachTune [ 79 ] adapt course materials and feedback based on learner profiles and contextual data. Domain-specific agents such as MathAgent [ 265 ], Chat Melody [ 80 ], Duolingo [ 39 ], and EduMAS [ 107 ] integrate domain-specific knowledge to enhance learning outcomes and reduce cognitive load, making learning more efficient and personalized. 

8.2 Content Creation 

Personalized content creation concerns the generation of standalone textual artifacts, where agents adapt style, structure, and framing to personalization targets rather than interactive dialogue. Typical tasks include personalized writing or summarization with preferences expressed through tone, formality, and rhetorical organization [111, 263, 288 ]. Personalization is evaluated at the artifact level, giving rise to two complementary paradigms: author-centric alignment with individual writing styles and audience-centric adaptation to reader knowledge and expectations [21, 72, 150, 173]. 

8.2.1 Author-Centric. Author-centric content creation treats personalization as a requirement of authorship rather than an optimization objective, arising in scenarios where users expect generated documents to consistently reflect their individual writing style, habitual phrasing, and subjective preferences. This need is especially prominent in real-world content creation tasks such as creative writing, professional documenta-tion, and brand communication, where users evaluate quality at the artifact level and value stylistic continuity across independent outputs. Accordingly, personalization in this setting is defined by the ability of an agent to act as a stable authorial proxy, producing standalone texts that remain aligned with long-term user identity rather  

> ACM Comput. Surv., Vol. 1, No. 1, Article . Publication date: February 2018. Toward Personalized LLM-Powered Agents: Foundations, Evaluation, and Future Directions 33

than momentary interaction context [ 111 , 263 , 288 ]. This application perspective is reflected in deployed systems such as Adobe Firefly, which adapts creative content generation to a user’s assets and historical editing patterns [ 1 ], Jasper Brand Voice, which operationalizes author-centric alignment for consistent brand and personal voice reproduction [ 2 ], and Notion AI, which generates documents conditioned on persistent workspace context and prior authorial conventions [ 3 ]. Existing research further supports this paradigm by demonstrating that stable preference represen-tations and document-level alignment are central to effective personalized content creation beyond conversational settings [20, 37, 133, 173, 196, 216]. 

8.2.2 Audience-Centric. Audience-centric content creation frames personalization around target readers rather than individual authors, focusing on generating textual artifacts that adapt to audience knowledge, expertise, interests, or usage contexts while preserving the intended informational content [ 40 , 149 , 278 ]. This paradigm is central to real-world applications such as differentiated instructional content for novice versus expert users, culturally adapted documentation, and audience-aware information presentation in web and software systems, where personalization operates at the level of audience groups rather than individual writing styles. In deployed systems, audience-centric personalization underlies large-scale marketing, communication, and outreach platforms, including social media and advertising content generation tailored to audience segments as exemplified by Predis.ai and Omneky Smart Ads [ 4, 5 ], as well as personalized sales and marketing communications targeted at specific recipient groups in systems such as SalesForge AI [ 6 ]. Correspondingly, recent research emphasizes audience alignment, relevance, and comprehension as defining criteria for personalization quality in this setting, reflecting practical demands observed in audience-driven content delivery and assessment scenarios [ 44 , 129 , 177 , 188 , 197 , 209 ]. 

8.3 Delegation Assistants 

Delegation assistants are personalized agents that act on behalf of users by internaliz-ing preferences, managing information, planning actions, and supporting complex cognitive workflows. They operate over extended interaction horizons and assume delegated responsibilities aligned with user-specific goals and contexts. 

8.3.1 Information Handling. Information handling concerns the long-term manage-ment of user-specific knowledge as a delegated cognitive responsibility. Delegation assistants acquire, organize, retain, and surface relevant information across tasks and over extended time horizons, reducing user cognitive load and interaction overhead. Recent work frames information access itself as a personalized service, emphasizing agentic retrieval and memory-centric designs. ARAG [ 132 ] models retrieval as an agent-driven process that dynamically selects and synthesizes information tailored to individual users, while SPARK [ 28 ] extends this paradigm to personalized search through coordinated retrieval and knowledge sharing under user context. Beyond retrieval, effective information handling requires persistent memory maintenance and selective recall. Systems such as Mr.Rec[ 64 ] demonstrate how memory and reasoning modules jointly support personalized assistants that retain user-relevant knowledge

beyond single tasks, and log-contextualized RAG [ 35 ] shows how accumulated in-teraction histories enable continuity without repeated user input. These directions are reflected in deployed delegation assistants including Rewind AI [ 169 ], Glean [ 50 ], You.com [ 268 ], Microsoft Copilot [ 138 ], and Mem [ 135 ], where personalization emerges through sustained ownership of the user’s informational context, enabling downstream planning and action to operate on a coherent, user-aligned knowledge substrate. 

8.3.2 Task Planning. Task-planning delegation assistants aim to translate high-level user goals into executable action sequences while respecting user-specific preferences and contextual constraints. Personalization in this setting is essential for resolving trade-offs and prioritizing subtasks. Representative systems illustrate how personal-ization manifests in delegated planning. VAIAGE frames personalized travel planning as a collaborative multi-agent process, where specialized agents jointly coordinate subtasks under user-defined requirements [ 117 ]. Learning-based approaches further demonstrate that explicitly modeling user preferences during planning enables agents to generate plans that better align with individual goals and constraints [ 257 ]. Be-yond single-task settings, collaborative language-agent frameworks have been applied to complex planning scenarios involving multiple constraints, improving flexibility and robustness in delegated workflows [ 274 ]. Personalized task planning has also been explored across application domains, including embodied assistants that adapt planning behavior to human preferences in interactive environments [ 57 ], and social decision-making contexts such as participatory urban planning [166]. 

8.3.3 Behavior Adaptation. Behavior adaptation operates over extended time hori-zons, where agents align their interaction style, initiative, and decision tendencies with a user’s habitual patterns and expectations. It enables reliable delegation by internalizing stable routines while remaining responsive to evolving goals, thereby reducing user supervision and cognitive effort. Representative systems illustrate how such adaptive delegation is realized in practice. MEAgent models persistent user habits in mobile assistant settings, enabling stable delegation across daily activities [ 227 ], while memory-centric frameworks allow agents to accumulate and reuse user-specific knowledge beyond single interactions [ 235 , 242 ]. Test-time personalization approaches such as PersonaAgent show that adaptive behaviors can be realized dynamically by grounding responses in inferred user personas without retraining [ 280 ]. As delegation becomes more autonomous, agents increasingly exhibit proactive behaviors, anticipat-ing user needs rather than remaining purely reactive [ 202 ]. At the same time, empirical studies of LLM-based GUI agents caution that unconstrained behavior adaptation can lead to misalignment with human values, highlighting the need for controllable personalization in real-world delegation assistants [48]. 

8.4 Expert Support in Specific Domains 

Personalized agents are increasingly deployed as expert assistants in specialized domains such as healthcare, finance, legal services, and research workflows, where user-centric tailoring can improve relevance and usability but must be reconciled with stringent domain constraints and risk-sensitive requirements [217].

8.4.1 Healthcare. In healthcare, personalization is shifting from static question an-swering to longitudinal, stateful interaction, where effective agents must support a patient trajectory rather than a single-turn diagnosis. Frameworks such as MedChain [ 118 ] and MedPlan [ 60 ] organize multi-stage clinical pipelines that span history elici-tation, symptom clarification, and treatment planning, emphasizing active querying to resolve uncertainty before delivering user-specific guidance. To support continu-ity of care, many systems maintain persistent user state, which also heightens the need for safeguards against over-accommodation and unsafe agreement [ 243 ]. In response, constellation-style architectures such as Polaris and ColaCare decouple patient-facing communication from clinical reasoning, pairing an empathetic inter-face agent with specialist verification modules that check recommendations against guidelines [ 71 , 141 , 237 ]. For data-intensive workflows, EHRAgent further reduces hallucination risk by grounding personalized EHR analysis in deterministic code execution rather than purely generative reasoning [ 81 , 189 ]. Finally, evaluation en-vironments such as AgentClinic [ 181 ] highlight the need to stress-test personalized clinical agents with diverse simulated patient behaviors to assess robustness under realistic interaction dynamics [128]. 

8.4.2 Finance. In financial domains, personalization should balance alignment with individual goals and behaviors against risks arising from cognitive biases and strin-gent regulatory requirements, making trust calibration and compliance central chal-lenges for personalized agent design. FinPersona-based studies surface a recurring trust–quality paradox, showing that persona-driven personalization can increase user trust even when the underlying advice quality is poorer or riskier [ 204 , 205 ], high-lighting that naive mirroring of user preferences may reinforce risky biases. This motivates safety-aware personalization frameworks such as PSG-Agent [ 249 ], which incorporate personality-conditioned guardrails to calibrate recommendations. Along-side trust calibration, finance imposes hard compliance constraints that bound what an agent is permitted to recommend or execute. To support controllable reasoning under these constraints, recent systems increasingly adopt modular architectures. Fin-Robot [ 266 ] decomposes workflows into specialized roles for market data aggregation, quantitative analysis, and thesis generation, improving traceability of personalized recommendations. In parallel, compliance-centered designs such as GraphCompliance [ 32 ] align user context and intended actions with structured regulatory graphs, en-abling verifiable reasoning paths that constrain personalization within enforceable policy boundaries and reduce “plausible” but non-compliant outputs. 

8.4.3 Legal services. In legal services, personalization conditions reasoning on user-specific facts, jurisdictions, and drafting intents, transforming legal assistance into a context-sensitive inference problem. A key challenge is statutory drift, where person-alized agents must correctly apply evolving laws to individual cases, making static knowledge or one-time verification insufficient. Benchmarks such as LawShift [ 58 ]show that agents need to adapt updated statutes to the same user-provided facts without retraining, highlighting the need for inference-time adaptability. Personal-ization also amplifies hallucination risks in legal drafting, as agents tailor arguments

and citations to user context. To mitigate this, systems increasingly employ post-hoc verification loops, including Citation-Enhanced Generation (CEG) and watchdog frameworks such as HalMit, which validate personalized drafts against authoritative legal sources before delivery [ 109 , 121 ]. These are particularly important for multi-lingual and jurisdiction-aware legal assistants, such as Mina, where personalization must respect local procedural norms while preserving legal correctness [238]. 

8.4.4 Research Assistance. Research assistance supports expert users across the re-search lifecycle by performing project- and goal-conditioned scholarly work. Early agentic systems position LLM agents as general research collaborators for exploration and orchestration, exemplified by Agent Laboratory [ 180 ], while later work moves toward user- and context-conditioned pipelines, including project scoping for spe-cific impact objectives [ 41 ] and adaptive retrieval and dialogue behaviors that reflect individual information-seeking patterns [ 139 ]. Multi-agent settings further develop sustained, stateful workflows through virtual research groups that coordinate itera-tive investigation [ 100 , 146 ]. Recent studies emphasize human-centered deployment and interaction design in real research settings [ 26 , 33 ], and specialized assistants instantiated in institutional or scientific domains [ 98 , 137 ] highlight the need to bal-ance autonomy with trust and verifiability in expert use [ 272 , 279 ]. Complementary perspectives explore personalized scaffolding and customized writing support for research productivity [9, 84, 86]. 

9 Open Problems and Future Directions 

Despite rapid progress, building robust and deployable personalized agents remains an open challenge. We highlight several key directions that require sustained attention. 

9.1 Decision-Critical User Modeling 

A fundamental question in personalized agents is how to represent user preferences in a form that is both expressive and actionable. User preferences vary not only across individuals but also across different aspects of a single task, and may even conflict within the same user. Moreover, preferences are rarely stated explicitly and are instead implicitly revealed through historical behavior, interaction patterns, or tool usage. A complementary challenge lies in identifying which aspects of a user are actually decision-critical for a given task, which may differ substantially across application scenarios. General-purpose conversational agents often prioritize broad, high-level alignment with user values and interaction style, whereas domain-specific or high-stakes agents require precise identification of fine-grained attributes that directly affect outcomes. Future work should therefore focus on structured and adaptive preference representations that balance expressiveness with relevance, allowing agents to reason about which user characteristics matter when, rather than assuming a fixed or universally applicable user model.

9.2 Temporal Dynamics and Continual Personalization 

Personalization is inherently dynamic and unfolds through sustained interaction rather than being achieved instantaneously. User preferences are progressively revealed as agents observe behavior and feedback, and different aspects of user information evolve at different temporal scales. Short-term interests may shift rapidly across contexts, while long-term values, goals, or reasoning styles tend to remain relatively stable. This temporal heterogeneity poses a fundamental challenge for static personalization mechanisms. Promising directions include continual user modeling techniques that update representations over time without catastrophic forgetting, as well as interaction-aware mechanisms that leverage newly acquired signals to guide subsequent behavior. By closing the feedback loop between observation and action, agents can refine personalization progressively, maintaining alignment as user preferences evolve rather than allowing personalization quality to degrade. 

9.3 Generalization 

Personalized agents must operate under sparse and heterogeneous user data, raising the question of how personalization can generalize beyond observed interactions. In practice, user-specific signals are often unevenly distributed or unavailable for new users and tasks, making it difficult for agents to reliably align behavior beyond observed interactions. Generalization in personalized agents manifests along two dimensions. First, agents must generalize to unseen or low-interaction users, where limited evidence makes direct preference inference unreliable. Second, agents must generalize across domains and tasks, where preferences learned in one context may only partially apply to another. Even when user intent is stable, differences in task structure, action space, or decision criteria can cause misalignment if personaliza-tion mechanisms are overly context-specific. Promising directions include few-shot and meta-learning approaches that rapidly adapt to new users, uncertainty-aware personalization that avoids overconfident alignment when signals are weak, and trans-ferable preference abstractions that capture reusable decision principles rather than task-specific behaviors. Improving generalization is essential for scaling personalized agents beyond narrowly scoped or data-rich scenarios. 

9.4 Evaluation Protocols and Benchmarking 

Evaluating personalization poses a unique challenge because success is defined relative to individual users rather than objective task completion. Existing benchmarks often rely on synthetic user data generated by language models, which may fail to capture real human diversity and can suffer from homogenization effects. Additionally, com-mon evaluation protocols such as LLM-as-a-judge raise concerns about reliability and alignment with human satisfaction. Future evaluation frameworks should emphasize fine-grained, component-level analysis and distinguish between intra-user consistency and inter-user differentiation. Incorporating human-in-the-loop evaluation, longitudi-nal interaction studies, and user-centric satisfaction metrics may provide more faithful assessments of personalization quality.

9.5 Privacy and User Control 

Personalization inherently relies on collecting and retaining user data, making privacy a central concern for real-world deployment. Even when user data is stored separately, risks remain in the form of unauthorized access, unintended leakage, or misuse of personal information. Moreover, self-evolving agents that reuse historical data for learning may erode user trust if users perceive a loss of control over their data. Future systems must balance data utility with privacy preservation through careful system design. Promising directions include privacy-aware memory architectures, selective retention policies, on-device or federated personalization, and transparent user con-trols for inspecting and managing stored information. Privacy should be treated not only as a technical constraint but also as a core component of user experience. 

9.6 Efficiency and Deployment Cost 

A practical question concerns how personalization can be achieved efficiently under real-world constraints. In practice, personalization is often implemented via post-training adaptations or inference-time augmentations applied to a general-purpose agent. These mechanisms introduce additional costs, including memory storage, in-creased inference latency, and extra computation from re-planning or tool usage. Future work should explore workflow designs that explicitly trade off personalization depth against computational cost. Lightweight personalization mechanisms, adaptive memory usage, and budget-aware inference strategies may enable agents to deliver meaningful personalization without prohibitive overhead. 

10 Conclusion 

Personalization is a defining capability for next-generation LLM-powered agents, enabling them to move beyond generic task execution toward sustained, user-aligned collaboration. In this survey, we examine personalization from a system-level per-spective, organizing existing work around four core capabilities: profile modeling, memory management, planning, and action execution. This decomposition clarifies how user-specific signals are inferred, maintained, and operationalized throughout the agent pipeline, and highlights the inter-dependencies between different components. We further connect these capabilities to real-world deployment by surveying metrics and benchmarks, followed by representative applications. Looking ahead, while sub-stantial progress has been made, personalized agents continue to face fundamental challenges, including sparse and evolving user signals, generalization, evaluation, privacy preservation, and efficiency constraints. Addressing these challenges requires not only advances in individual components, but also holistic agent architectures that balance personalization, robustness, and safety. We hope this survey provides a coherent foundation for understanding the design space of personalized LLM-powered agents, bridges benchmark-driven research with real-world deployment, and inspires future work toward building agents that are capable and reliably aligned with the individuals they serve.
