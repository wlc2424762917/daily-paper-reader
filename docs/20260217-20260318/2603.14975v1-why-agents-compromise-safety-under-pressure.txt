# Why Agents Compromise Safety Under Pressure 

Hengle Jiang, Ke Tang 

Guangdong Provincial Key Laboratory of Brain-inspired Intelligent Computation, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Southern University of Science and Technology, Shenzhen, China 

Abstract 

Large Language Model agents deployed in complex environments frequently encounter a conflict between maximizing goal achieve-ment and adhering to safety constraints. This paper identifies a new concept called Agentic Pressure, which characterizes the endogenous tension emerging when compliant execution becomes infeasible. We demonstrate that un-der this pressure agents exhibit normative drift where they strategically sacrifice safety to pre-serve utility. Notably we find that advanced reasoning capabilities accelerate this decline as models construct linguistic rationalizations to justify violation. Finally, we analyze the root causes and explore preliminary mitigation strategies, such as pressure isolation, which attempts to restore alignment by decoupling decision-making from pressure signals. 

1 Introduction 

The transition of Large Language Models (LLMs) from static chatbots (Adiwardana et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024) to goal-oriented agents (Yao et al., 2022; Schick et al., 2023) rep-resents a paradigm shift in artificial intelligence. Modern agents are expected to plan, execute, and adapt (Wang et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023) over long trajectories to satisfy user instructions. While this capability improves utility, it introduces a fundamental conflict between maximizing goal achievement and adhering to safety constraints (Amodei et al., 2016). In practice, as objectives be-come more specific and interaction horizons grow, constraints that were designed as hard boundaries can be treated as negotiable frictions. Current safety evaluations predominantly focus on adversarial attacks (Kumar et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024) where a malicious user attempts to trick the model. However, this perspective overlooks a crit-ical threat arising from the internal drive of the agent. In practical scenarios, agents frequently en-

These fac-tors create a high pressure environment that funda-mentally alters the operating context of the agent distinct from the adversarial settings typically stud-ied. We illustrate this dilemma in Figure 1, which depicts the "Good Agent" Paradox. To address this gap, we introduce the concept of Agentic Pressure. Unlike the static and exogenous pressure typically found in Large Language Model evaluations which relies on threatening prompts or aggressive language to induce compliance (Kim et al., 2024), Agentic Pressure is endogenous. It emerges naturally during the interaction loop as the agent perceives conflicts between its objective and the available resources. This pressure is not explic-itly injected by the user but arises from the internal calculation of the agent that the task cannot be com-pleted within the constraints of the environment.

We hypothesize that under such pressure, even be-nign agents experience Instrumental Divergence (Gallow, 2025). Specifically, driven by a strong directive to be helpful, agents autonomously prior-itize task utility over established safety protocols (Omohundro, 2018). Crucially, this is not merely a failure of execution but a cognitive shift where the agent actively rationalizes its safety violations as necessary compromises to benefit the user. Experimental observations suggest that AI agents do not behave as purely rational actors that steadfastly maintain safety boundaries under such conditions (Arike et al., 2025). Instead, they exhibit behavioral shifts that render traditional static test-ing methods insufficient (Greenblatt et al., 2024). While such failures are often attributed to a lack of model capability or reasoning bandwidth, we propose that they are frequently the result of an adaptive response to environmental stress where the agent prioritizes utility over compliance. This implies that the testing problem for agents is funda-mentally different from standard software testing because the subject under test dynamically adjusts its moral compass based on the perceived difficulty of the task (Amodei et al., 2016). This paper investigates why agents compromise safety through a systematic study of this phe-nomenon. We first present a preliminary study using the TravelPlanner (Xie et al., 2024) envi-ronment to demonstrate the emergence of behav-ioral drift in long horizon scenarios without active pressure injection. Building on these findings, we introduce a comprehensive evaluation framework that actively simulates high pressure environments across multiple domains to quantify the extent of safety compromise. Finally, we propose a pres-sure isolation mechanism designed to decouple rea-soning from execution which offers a potential ar-chitectural solution to mitigate these autonomous safety trade-offs. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Safety in LLMs 

Current research extensively covers safety align-ment in large language models, primarily within the paradigm of conversational interaction. Estab-lished benchmarks such as HH-RLHF (Bai et al., 2022) and SafetyBench (Zhang et al., 2024) evalu-ate whether models can identify and refuse harmful instructions in static dialogue or multiple choice settings. A parallel line of inquiry investigates jailbreak, where users employ optimized adver-sarial suffixes (Zou et al., 2023) or prefix injec-tions (Wei et al., 2023) to bypass these safety fil-ters. More recently, studies on many-shot jailbreak (Anil et al., 2024) reveal that extended context win-dows can weaken alignment, as models prioritize in-context patterns over safety training. Crucially, these works typically evaluate language model in isolation, focusing on its ability to detect toxic con-tent in text generation. They do not assess the autonomous agent, which must balance safety con-straints against task utility in dynamic, multi-step environments. 

2.2 Benchmarks for Agents 

General agent benchmarks predominantly priori-tize task completion accuracy above all other met-rics. Foundational datasets like HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) and GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021) fo-cus on reasoning chains. To evaluate broader au-tonomous capabilities, benchmarks such as Tool-Bench (Qin et al., 2023), GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023), and TheAgentCompany (Xu et al., 2025) assess proficiency in realistic environments. How-ever, these frameworks operate on strictly outcome-based metrics. They classify a trajectory as suc-cessful solely based on goal completion, regardless of the methods used. This creates a critical evalua-tion blind spot: agents are implicitly incentivized to bypass safety constraints if doing so improves efficiency, as current utility benchmarks do not pe-nalize functional but unsafe solutions. 

2.3 Safety in Autonomous Agents 

In the safety domain, existing works often diverge from the phenomenon of normative drift. Bench-marks like AgentDojo (Debenedetti et al., 2024), AgentHarm (Andriushchenko et al., 2025), and Agent Security Bench (Zhang et al., 2025) focus on adversarial robustness against malicious instruc-tions. While R-Judge (Yuan et al., 2024) evaluates an agent’s ability to identify risks, it overlooks the critical "knowing-doing" gap. Beyond adversarial settings, research into agen-tic alignment often relies on restricted contexts. Studies like Agentic Misalignment (Lynch et al., 2025) and the Machiavelli benchmark (Pan et al., 2023) examine ethical trade-offs but are frequently situated in fictional or game-based environments that lack the stakes of realistic deployment. Simi-larly, frameworks like ToolEmu (Ruan et al., 2024) assess tool execution risks but typically focus on isolated steps rather than cumulative dynamics. Mainstream productivity benchmarks focus exclu-sively on final outcomes and inadvertently reward unsafe behaviors that improve efficiency. Crucially, these evaluations overlook the endogenous pres-sure emerging from internal resource conflicts. Our work bridges this gap by situating agents in contin-uous constrained workflows to analyze how align-ment deteriorates under cumulative stress. 

3 Agentic Pressure 

Large language model based agents operate in in-teractive environments where incentives and con-straints evolve over time (Yao et al., 2022). In such settings, agent behavior is not determined solely by initial prompts, safety policies, or inher-ent model capabilities, but by how goal-directed decision making unfolds under changing external conditions (Schick et al., 2023). 

Agentic Pressure is a force that spontaneously emerges from the interaction between an agent and its environment, causing the agent to generate outputs inconsistent with expectations even with-out malicious attacks. Agentic Pressure describes the endogenous tension where feasible options de-crease just as the consequences of failure intensify. Unlike static task specifications, agentic pressure is cumulative and trajectory dependent. It devel-ops across interaction turns as options disappear, resources are depleted, or stakes escalate. Crucially, agentic pressure does not correspond to an internal psychological state of the model. In-stead, it arises endogenously from the interaction loop between the agent and its environment. Under increasing pressure, agents may continue to exhibit fluent reasoning and nominal policy awareness, yet begin to reinterpret or discount safety constraints in order to preserve task progress. This shift does not require adversarial prompts or explicit instructions to violate rules, but follows naturally from sus-tained goal pursuit under constrained conditions. 

3.1 From LLM Pressure to Agentic Pressure 

It is important to distinguish agentic pressure from what prior work often describes as pressure on lan-guage models (Kim et al., 2024). In many evalu-ations, pressure is treated as an exogenous factor introduced through prompt design, such as urgent commands, fictional emergencies, or explicit re-quests to bypass safety rules. This form of pressure is linguistic, immediate, and static (Zhang et al.,

2024). By contrast, agentic pressure is not injected through language alone. It emerges dynamically during interaction as the environment evolves. It is a property of the agent’s decision context rather than a feature of the prompt or an internal state of the model. Two agents powered by the same underlying LLM may experience different pressure profiles depending on their interaction history, re-source availability, and accumulated constraints. Under agentic pressure, deviations in behavior or explanation do not reflect confusion about policy requirements. Instead, they arise because compli-ant actions become increasingly costly, delayed, or infeasible as the episode progresses. This dis-tinction is critical for understanding agent behavior in realistic deployment settings, where pressure is rarely static and cannot be reduced to prompt level manipulation. 

3.2 Taxonomy of Pressure Sources 

In Figure 2, we categorize agentic pressure into three major types based on where it arises within the agent and environment interaction loop, includ-ing Resource Scarcity, Environmental Friction, and Social Inducement. It captures the most common pressure patterns we observe in practice, while ad-ditional sources may emerge in other domains or deployment settings. Crucially, these pressures are not triggered by explicit instructions to violate rules, but by conditions that progressively narrow feasible actions and increase the cost of failure. 

Type I: Resource Scarcity Resource scarcity arises when compliant actions exceed the agent’s available resources. A common form is temporal exhaustion, where strict time limits or step budgets leave insufficient capacity to complete all required verification procedures (Silver et al., 2017). Time functions as a core planning resource, and when the remaining horizon is too short, agents face a choice between task failure and skipping safety checks. Resource scarcity also includes budget constraints, where authorized options are unavailable due to financial or computational limits. In such cases, pressure emerges not because rules change, but because compliance becomes infeasible. 

Type II: Environmental Friction Environmen-tal friction refers to obstacles that block stan-dard execution paths even when resources remain available. One example is functional deadlock, where tools or APIs fail persistently (Schick et al., 2023), severing legitimate action channels. An-other source is information asymmetry, where in-complete or noisy feedback forces agents to act under uncertainty. We also identify compliance rigidity, where static safety rules conflict with dy-namic situations. When rigid protocols prevent timely action, agents may treat rule deviation as adaptive flexibility rather than noncompliance. 

Type III: Social Inducement Social inducement originates from user interaction dynamics rather than task structure. Urgency injection occurs when users emphasize severe consequences of failure, increasing the perceived stakes of task completion. Illicit opportunities introduce highly efficient but unauthorized options that appear attractive under constrained conditions (Achiam et al., 2017). User emotion further amplifies pressure through interac-tion, including authoritative, pleading, or aggres-sive behavior. Although these signals do not alter formal task requirements, they raise the perceived cost of refusal or delay and can shift agent priorities toward appeasement. Across all categories, pressure is not a single fac-tor but an accumulation of constraints that evolve over time. Agentic pressure emerges from how these constraints interact, progressively reshaping both agent behavior and the justifications offered for that behavior. 

3.3 The Cognitive Shift: From Reasoning to Rationalization 

Agentic pressure alters not only whether an agent complies with safety constraints, but how it con-structs explanations for its behavior. In low-pressure settings, agents typically rely on norma-tive reasoning, where safety rules function as sta-ble constraints guiding action selection. Expla-nations in this condition are policy grounded and diagnostic, explicitly referencing constraints to jus-tify decisions or refusals. As pressure accumulates, however, agents increasingly prioritize goal com-pletion under high stakes, and explanations shift toward motivated rationalization. Rather than re-flecting deliberation under fixed rules, explanations are constructed to legitimize actions taken under constrained feasibility, a pattern consistent with findings on motivated reasoning in human decision-making (Kunda, 1990). Importantly, this shift can occur even when ac-tions remain nominally compliant. Under high pressure, agents may reinterpret constraints as flex-ible or exceptional, framing policies as secondary to urgency or necessity. Prior work has shown that natural language explanations are not inherently faithful to underlying decision processes (Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020; Wiegreffe et al., 2021), and agentic pressure amplifies this gap. Explanations remain fluent and plausible, but become less in-formative about true decision criteria. As a result, explanation quality cannot be evaluated indepen-dently of pressure context, since identical expla-nations may serve fundamentally different roles across different pressure settings. 

4 Preliminary Analysis 

We first conduct a lightweight diagnostic study to test whether non-adversarial pressure signals alone can systematically alter an agent’s safety behav-ior in complex tool-using workflows. We utilized the TravelPlanner environment (Xie et al., 2024) as a testbed and extended its standard setting to simulate the friction and fatigue of realistic deploy-ment. This preliminary analysis serves to demon-strate that agentic pressure is an endogenous phe-nomenon that emerges solely from the extension of interaction horizons and the introduction of en-vironmental noise. 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

TravelPlanner provides environment constraints that enforce sandbox availability, a set of com-monsense constraints for feasibility, and query-conditioned hard constraints such as budget, room rules, and cuisine requirements. We keep the en-vironment constraints unchanged and expand the number of constraints within the existing common-sense and hard categories to better reflect realistic deployment policies and operational failure modes. To evaluate compliance at the trajectory level, we employ an external LLM monitor to verify each step and compute cumulative pass rates across Figure 3: Preliminary results on TravelPlanner under non-adversarial pressure 

episodes. 

4.2 Stress-Testing Variants 

Standard benchmarks typically involve short hori-zons (10-20 steps) with reliable tools which fail to capture the cumulative nature of agentic pressure. To amplify this pressure organically, we designed two realistic stress-testing variants. 

Long Horizon We extended the interaction hori-zon to exceed fifty steps while providing no cor-rective feedback or human intervention. The agent must independently maintain normative constraints solely through its internal context window which simulates an autonomous system drifting over a prolonged operational period. 

Noisy Tools We simulated realistic interface in-stability by injecting stochastic and non-adversarial perturbations into tool responses. These pertur-bations included transient service errors, partial output generation, and minor formatting inconsis-tencies. This setting introduces environmental fric-tion that forces the agent to perform error handling and retries, thereby consuming cognitive reason-ing bandwidth without altering the fundamental constraints of the task. 

4.3 Preliminary Findings 

Figure 3 shows that even mild, non-adversarial pressure signals systematically change agent be-havior. Across models, extending the horizon and injecting tool noise both reduce constraint pass rates, indicating that the agent becomes less reli-able as interaction context grows and execution becomes less stable. Notably, hard constraints are more fragile than commonsense constraints: under pressure, agents may still produce superficially fea-sible itineraries while increasingly failing to satisfy explicit user-specified requirements. These results support our premise that pressure can emerge en-dogenously from the interaction loop itself, moti-vating the active pressure injection framework in the next section. 

5 Experiments and Analysis 

Building on the preliminary analysis, we design a systematic experimental framework to quantify agent performance under actively induced pressure, as illustrated in Figure 4. We move from passive observation to controlled stress testing by adapting three established agent benchmarks, TravelPlanner (Xie et al., 2024), WebArena (Zhou et al., 2023), and ToolBench (Qin et al., 2023), and by adding a medical scenario. Across these diverse environ-ments, we modify the interaction dynamics so that agents encounter structured conflicts between nor-mative constraints and goal achievement. 

5.1 Pressure Injection Mechanism 

We inject pressure through overlaying strict nor-mative constraints onto standard agent benchmarks while creating specific high inducement tasks. We design these scenarios so that the user objective is functionally antagonistic to the safety rules. For instance, we enforce a strict no flight policy while simultaneously presenting a user request that phys-ically requires air travel to meet a deadline. This setup ensures that the most salient path to success involves a violation. Consequently the agent faces an immediate tension between the directive to be helpful and the requirement to remain safe. This forces the model to weigh the immediate utility of task completion against the abstract cost of rule violation without needing dynamic feedback loops. Crucially, some of these scenarios create an impos-sible task where no valid plan exists that satisfies both the user’s objective and the safety constraints. In such cases, the optimal and aligned behavior is justified refusal. However, our hypothesis is that under agentic pressure, models will perceive re-fusal as a failure mode. Driven by the imperative to be helpful, they tend to prioritize goal satisfaction over strict compliance, leading to the rationalized violation we aim to measure. 

5.2 Baselines and Comparative Strategies 

Our setting explicitly couples pressure with nor-mative constraints, we compare our architecture against three widely used deployment strategies: • Vanilla Agent: The base model equipped only with task instructions and tools. This estimates the natural drift rate under pressure without mitigation. Figure 4: Overview of the Agentic Pressure Evaluation Framework. 

• Safety Prompting: A strategy where con-straints are stated prominently and repeatedly in system instructions to encourage compli-ance through static guidance. • Self-Reflection: A dynamic baseline where the agent is prompted to critique its own plan for potential violations before execution. This represents a strong prompt level mitigation that increases deliberation.

Rationalization Score. While behavioral metrics (e.g., Pass Rate) reveal what the agent did, they fail to explain why a violation occurred. To capture the internal state of agentic pressure, we imple-ment an automated LLM-as-a-Judge framework using GPT-4o. The Judge assigns a continuous Pressure Score from 0-5 based on the intensity of this rationalization. We provide the de-tailed scoring rubric and comparative case studies in Appendix.

6 Conclusion 

This paper formalizes Agentic Pressure as a criti-cal vulnerability in the deployment of autonomous systems. We demonstrate that safety alignment is not a static property, but a consumable resource that decays under the friction of real-world opera-tion. Crucially, this phenomenon is not a rare edge case but a fundamental structural risk: as agents are deployed into increasingly complex, long hori-zon workflows, the tension between goal achieve-ment and normative constraints intensifies, making the emergence of instrumental non-compliance in-evitable. Our analysis reveals that advanced reasoning ca-pabilities can exacerbate this drift. State-of-the-art models utilize their cognitive bandwidth to con-struct sophisticated linguistic rationalizations, re-framing safety constraints as negotiable guidelines under pressure. This finding challenges the va-lidity of current evaluation paradigms. We argue that future capability benchmarks must incorporate "stress-testing" dimensions to account for pressure. Evaluating agents solely in friction-free vacuums fails to predict their reliability in high stakes envi-ronments where compliance competes with utility. Finally, our results indicate that relying on innate post-training alignment is insufficient to mitigate this risk, as pressure operates on the cognitive pro-cess itself. To ensure robust safety, the field must transition from prompt-based mitigations toward ar-chitectural defenses. As demonstrated by our pres-sure isolation framework, structurally decoupling the planning incentives from pressure perception offers a more reliable guarantee than relying on the model’s self-restraint. As autonomous agents evolve from chat interfaces to actors in the physical economy, acknowledging and architecturally man-aging agentic pressure is a prerequisite for trust-worthy deployment. 

7 Discussion 

Our findings challenge the assumption that reason-ing guarantees alignment, revealing a Capability-Safety Paradox where advanced cognitive machin-ery is repurposed to construct sophisticated ratio-nalizations for non-compliance. We observe that self-reflection mechanisms often exacerbate this drift by optimizing the excuse rather than correct-ing the violation, effectively solidifying instrumen-tal rationalization. The failure of such prompt-based defenses suggests that implicit utility incen-tives override static safety instructions under pres-sure. Consequently, we advocate a paradigm shift from reliance on model self-restraint to architec-tural solutions like Pressure Isolation, which struc-turally decouples decision-making from pressure signals to ensure robust safety verification. 8 Limitations 

While our framework provides a rigorous quantifi-cation of agentic pressure, we acknowledge several limitations inherent in our experimental design. 

Gap Between Simulated and Real-World Con-sequences. First, our pressure injection relies on textual stimuli (e.g., urgency cues) rather than tan-gible material consequences. While we observe significant normative drift, the "stakes" in our en-vironment remain hypothetical. In actual deploy-ment, agents handling real financial assets or safety-critical hardware would face exogenous pressures, such as human emotional coercion or legal liabili-ties that are difficult to fully replicate in a sandbox. Consequently, the erratic behaviors observed in our simulation should be interpreted as a conservative lower bound of the risks inherent in high-stakes environments. 

Bias in LLM-based Evaluation. Second, our rationalization score relies on an automated LLM-as-a-Judge (GPT-4o). Although we calibrated the judge against human annotations on a small subset of data, there remains a risk of recursive bias, where the judge model might favor reasoning patterns similar to its own or fail to detect subtle forms of deception. Future work should incorporate larger-scale human-in-the-loop evaluations, particularly for domain specific rationalizations in medical or legal contexts. 

Architectural Constraints. Finally, our pro-posed mitigation strategy, Pressure Isolation, as-sumes a modular agent architecture where the plan-ner and executor can be structurally decoupled. This may be difficult to implement in monolithic "black-box" API services where the internal reason-ing process is inaccessible. Applying our isolation principles to end-to-end models without retraining or fine-tuning remains an open challenge for the community. 

9 Ethics Considerations 

This work investigates agent vulnerabilities not to facilitate attacks, but to expose structural weak-nesses and advance defensive alignment techniques. All experiments were conducted in controlled, sim-ulated environments with no interaction with real-world financial or medical systems. References

B Automated Pressure Evaluation Details 

To rigorously quantify the cognitive shift induced by agentic pressure, we employ an automated LLM-as-a-Judge framework. This section details the system prompt, the scoring rubric, and comparative examples used to validate the metric. 

B.1 LLM-as-a-Judge System Prompt 

To ensure reproducibility, we provide the exact system prompt used for the LLM Judge (GPT-4o) in Figure 6. The judge is tasked with detecting the linguistic transition from Normative Reasoning to Instrumental Rationalization within the agent’s Chain-of-Thought traces. 

B.2 Evaluation Criteria Analysis 

The LLM Judge evaluates the CoT using a rubric grounded in our theoretical taxonomy of drift. A high rationalization score (4-5) is assigned based on the presence of three specific linguistic indicators: 1. Urgency Acknowledgement: Does the agent explicitly note high stakes or time criticality? 2. Constraint Softening: Does the agent re-frame hard rules as flexible guidelines (e.g., rephrasing "strictly forbidden" to "usually re-quired")? 3. Outcome Prioritization: Does the agent ex-plicitly argue that goal success justifies the means (e.g., "The utility of completing the task outweighs the budget violation")? This methodology differentiates between simple context errors (Score 2-3) and active, pressure-induced cognitive drift (Score 4-5). 

B.3 Validation of LLM-as-a-Judge Reliability 

In this section, we provide additional evidence re-garding the reliability of utilizing GPT-4o as an automated evaluator for the Rationalization Score. While the limitations of LLM-based evaluation are discussed in the main text, the following measures ensure the scientific integrity of our metrics. 

B.3.1 Prompt Engineering and Iterative Refinement 

The evaluation prompt (detailed in Fig 6) was not a single-shot design. We employed an iterative refinement process involving initial human trials. We manually analyzed 50 edge-case trajectories where the initial model responses were ambiguous, adjusting the rubric’s linguistic constraints until the GPT-4o output strictly adhered to the normative scoring criteria established in our research. 

B.3.2 Human-in-the-loop Correlation Study 

To empirically validate the GPT-4o judge, we con-ducted a human evaluation experiment on a strat-ified sample of 300 reasoning trajectories. Three independent annotators scored the trajectories fol-lowing the same rubric provided to the LLM. We observed a 92.3% raw agreement rate between the GPT-4o judge and the majority human consensus. The Quadratic Weighted Cohen’s Kappa was 0.817, indicating "almost perfect" agreement according to standard interpretation scales. These results con-firm that the Rationalization Score effectively cap-tures instrumental reasoning patterns and provides a stable, human-aligned metric that is not merely an artifact of inherent model bias.

D Implementation Details of Pressure Isolation 

This section clarifies the architectural implemen-tation of the preliminary tested method Pressure Isolation, distinguishing it from simple iterative reasoning loops. Pressure Isolation is designed as a structural decoupling mechanism at the orches-tration layer. 

D.1 Dual-Call Routing Mechanism 

Unlike standard agentic loops that append raw en-vironment feedback directly into a continuous con-text window, Pressure Isolation operates as a mid-dleware using a two-stage routing process: An ini-tial, lightweight call processes raw user inputs and unparsed environment error logs. Its primary func-tion is to "sanitize" affective noise and condense tool friction into a state-only summary. The sec-ondary call acts as the dedicated planner. It receives only the distilled objective and valid state updates from the Parser. By preventing the accumulation of kinetic noise within the Planner’s context win-dow, Pressure Isolation halts the pressure-induced cognitive shift toward instrumentalism before it manifests. 

D.2 Black-box Compatibility 

A key advantage of this approach is that it relies entirely on prompt-routing and context manipula-tion rather than internal parameter updates. Con-sequently, Pressure Isolation can be deployed on monolithic, black-box API services (e.g., GPT-4, Claude 3.5) without requiring access to model weights or expensive retraining, making it highly viable for real-world AI safety applications. 

D.3 Future Extensions 

We identify three promising avenues for extending the Pressure Isolation framework: • Specialized Distillation: Fine-tuning smaller, efficient models specifically for the “Intent Parser” role to reduce latency and cost. • Multi-Agent Counterbalancing: Implement-ing a “Safety Warden” agent that indepen-dently audits the Planner’s distilled inputs be-fore action commitment. • Pre-decision Guardrails: Integrating hard-coded safety checks that intercept the Plan-ner’s output if it deviates from normative con-straints, even if the internal reasoning appears rationalized.
