## Abstract 

Tool-integrated reasoning (TIR) offers a direct way to extend thinking models be-yond the limits of text-only reasoning. Paradoxically, we observe that tool-enabled evaluation can degrade reasoning performance even when the strong thinking mod-els make almost no actual tool calls. In this paper, we investigate how to inject natural tool-use behavior into a strong thinking model without sacrificing its no-tool reasoning ability, and present a comprehensive TIR recipe. We highlight that (i) the effectiveness of TIR supervised fine-tuning (SFT) hinges on the learnability of teacher trajectories, which should prioritize problems inherently suited for tool-augmented solutions; (ii) controlling the proportion of tool-use trajectories could mitigate the catastrophic forgetting of text-only reasoning capacity; (iii) optimizing for pass@k and response length instead of training loss could maximize TIR SFT gains while preserving headroom for reinforcement learning (RL) exploration; (iv) a stable RL with verifiable rewards (RLVR) stage, built upon suitable SFT initialization and explicit safeguards against mode collapse, provides a simple yet remarkably effective solution. When applied to Qwen3 thinking models at 4B and 30B scales, our recipe yields models that achieve state-of-the-art performance in a wide range of benchmarks among open-source models, such as 96.7% and 99.2% on AIME 2025 for 4B and 30B, respectively. 

## 1 Introduction 

Current thinking models have demonstrated that scaling the reasoning depth can substantially improve performance on difficult tasks with pure natural language [ 29 , 5 , 3, 45 , 44 ]. However, in more complex scenarios that frequently require formal reasoning (e.g., numerical computation, symbolic manipulation, and systematic case enumeration), text-only reasoning often struggles, making it both costly and less reliable [ 20 , 48 , 18 ]. TIR provides a natural way out. By allowing the model to interleave natural-language deliberation with code execution [ 12 , 11 ], TIR combines the flexibility of text-only reasoning with the precision and expertise of the external feedback. The potential of TIR is clear in principle, yet simply providing a thinking model with access to a code executor does not guarantee interleaved reasoning. As shown in Table 1, across 400 tool-enabled rollouts, Qwen3 thinking models make almost no tool calls, continuing with text-only reasoning. For example, as illustrated in Figure 1, the model invokes the executor only once at the very end to verify the final answer, rather than leveraging computation to guide intermediate reasoning steps. This behavior reflects the delayed-code pattern [ 18 , 10 ], where code is applied too late or even not at all, rather than being integrated into the reasoning loop. To address this challenge, prior work has explored several approaches, including fine-tuning on tool-using trajectories and reinforcement learning that rewards successful tool invocation [ 12 , 11 , 18 , 40 ]. However, these approaches often overlook the structural fragility of reasoning chains during tool integration. Our empirical analysis in Table 1 reveals a critical bottleneck: unlike standard instruct models, thinking models exhibit a sharp performance drop when prompted with tools, indicating that their internal reasoning logic is easily disrupted. It indicates that teaching a strong thinking model to think with tools without sacrificing its text-only reasoning performance requires more effort. To resolve this inherent instability, we move beyond scattered techniques and propose a systematic, full-pipeline recipe spanning data preparation, SFT, the transition from SFT to RL, and RL itself. The recipe is organized as the following components: • Data Engineering: For each training stage, we carefully build data collection and filtering pipelines. For teacher prompts, we find that tool-advantaged problems could better encourage tool use. For teacher trajectories, we find that 1) pattern learnability matters a lot, and 2) mixing TIR and text-only reasoning trajectories helps prevent catastrophic degradation of text-only reasoning. Based 2on these findings, we construct high-quality teacher trajectories for SFT training. Moreover, we construct a challenging RLVR dataset to naturally elicit tool usage patterns in RL training. • Stage Coordination: We identify that what the student learns during SFT evolves through form, substance, and noise, and argue that SFT training should prioritize downstream RL potential over standard supervised loss. To this end, we utilize pass@k performance and rollout length to determine the optimal SFT extent for maximizing subsequent RL gains. • Reinforcement Learning: We show that a suitable SFT initialization and on-policy training stabilize multi-turn tool use, improving both with-tool and no-tool performance. We forgo the format rewards and leverage outcome-based rewards only with challenging questions to naturally elicit tool use. Applied to Qwen3 thinking models at 4B and 30B scales, the resulting models, capable of Textual Reasoning Interleaved with Code Execution, termed as T RICE -4B and T RICE -30B, achieve state-of-the-art performance among open-source models of this scale in a wide range of benchmarks, such as reaching 96.7% and 99.2% on AIME 2025, respectively. 

## 2 Related Work 

Reinforcement learning for large reasoning models. Reinforcement learning has made remark-able progress for building large reasoning models [ 29 , 3 , 4 , 43 , 51 ]. By optimizing with verifiable rewards, it encourages LLMs to scale up their reasoning processes, thereby advancing the frontier of intelligence [ 2 , 5 , 45 , 25 , 15 ]. However, text-only reasoning is inherently limited for problems that re-quire exact numerical computation or rigorous verification. Therefore, external tools that compensate for these limitations can be naturally integrated into the reasoning process [42, 9, 8, 22, 37]. 

Tool-integrated reasoning. Although introducing executable tools into reasoning has shown clear feasibility, injecting this ability into an already strong thinking model is not straightforward: it requires reshaping a largely fixed policy [ 20 , 47 , 39 ]. Prior work focuses on different parts of this problem, including the cold-start tool trajectories, code placement, theoretical support expansion, RL stability, long-CoT tool discovery, interaction-dense priors, and tool-use pattern selection [ 11 , 18 , 22 , 40 , 31 ]. Beyond the above work, we aim to convert an already strong thinking model into a reliable TIR model while maintaining or improving its no-tool performance through a full-pipeline recipe.

## 4 Data Engineering for TIR 

In this section, we detail the SFT data curation pipeline of our TIR recipe, focusing on the strategic selection of teacher models and the SFT trajectories. 

4.1 Teacher Model Selection  

> TAKEAWAY 1

For TIR SFT, teacher selection should account for the learnability of tool-use patterns, not teacher accuracy alone. 

When selecting a teacher model for TIR SFT, we argue that downstream task performance alone is insufficient [ 14 ], since the teacher’s tool-use pattern directly shapes the reasoning strategy learned by the student. Considering the cost and tool-use capability, we mainly compare GPT-OSS-120B [ 28 ]and MiniMax-M2.7 [36] from the frontier models [35, 6, 28, 36]. As shown in Table 2, the two models achieve comparable performance, but exhibit markedly different tool-use styles despite being equipped with the same stateful sandbox environment described in Section 3. Specifically, GPT-OSS-120B follows a high-frequency, lightweight pattern: it generates short code snippets and incrementally builds on variables, functions, and intermediate results from previous tool calls, thereby effectively exploiting the persistent sandbox state. In contrast, MiniMax-M2.7 follows a more typical stateless low-frequency, heavyweight pattern: it tends to generate longer self-contained programs with repeated definitions, and shows a substantially lower tool-use rate. 4.2 SFT Data Selection  

> TAKEAWAY 2

Selecting problems amenable to tool-augmented solutions (termed as tool-advantaged prob-lems) as teacher inputs can better elicit students to use tools from teacher trajectories. 

After selecting the teacher, the next question is how to choose the prompts and trajectories. We answer this question from the following perspectives. 

Prompt selection. First, we select the prompts for the teacher model to produce trajectories more suitable for students. The quality of TIR supervision varies across problems. Problems whose difficulty lies in computational structure tend to benefit more from tool access, while those centered on conceptual insight benefit less. Moreover, the teacher’s tool-use policy itself is imperfect and may not leverage tools effectively on every problem. To select prompts likely to yield more informative trajectories, we compare the teacher’s average accuracy over k rollouts with and without tool access per problem and retain those where TIR accuracy substantially exceeds the text-only reasoning, termed 

tool-advantaged problems. As Table 4 shows, the model trained on trajectories from tool-advantaged prompts performs much better with a 5.4% advantage.

Mix TIR trajectories with text-only reasoning ones. 

Despite the strong TIR performance of TIR-only SFT, the same models exhibit severe text-only reasoning degradation: accuracy drops across all benchmarks, as shown in Table 6. Moreover, the text-only truncation rate surges to ∼40%. We study these text-only reason-ing trajectories (e.g., Appendix C.1) and attribute this to a cross-mode negative transfer: TIR-only SFT causes the model to internalize a code-centric reasoning loop that persists even when no tool is available. The model attempts to simulate code execution in natural language, for example, writing programs, fabricating outputs, and iterating on hallucinated results—ultimately exhausting the token budget without mathematical progress [ 9, 13 ]. To alleviate this phenomenon, we developed a simple but effective approach: mixing text-only trajectories into TIR trajectories. In this way, the text-only reasoning performance recovers (Table 6) while TIR accuracy is still preserved (Table 5).

During TIR SFT, the student model follows a form–substance–noise learning progression, which we characterize as stage 1-3 . In the early stage, it quickly acquires the format of tool invocation, causing tool-use frequency to rise sharply, which yet does not yield effective TIR. As shown in Figure 2, performance drops while the truncation rate in-creases. Case in Appendix C.2 further shows that early tool calls are often inefficient symbolic exploration, repeated verification, or loops, which accumulate errors and produce overly long reasoning trajecto-ries. It suggests that the model is primarily imitating the form of TIR in this stage ( stage 1 ). As training proceeds, tool-use frequency stabi-lizes, and tool calls more often provide useful intermediate evidence. Correspondingly, both pass@1 and pass@8 improve, indicating that the model moves beyond exploiting the superficial signal of tool-call frequency and begins to learn the substance of TIR, adapting tool use to its reasoning prior. This resembles the degradation–recovery dynamics observed in long-CoT SFT [ 46 , 24 ]( stage 2 ). Finally, after TIR behavior has been sufficiently internalized and useful supervi-sion diminishes, the following SFT gradually overfits the teacher-side noise (e.g., rollout length), leading the performance to saturate or even decline ( stage 3 ).

To validate this criterion, we initialize RL from checkpoints at different stages, namely epochs 1, 8, and 16. As shown in Figure 4, checkpoints from both stage 1 and stage 3 eventually collapse during RL training. The failure of stage 1 is straightforward: the model has not yet learned an effective TIR strategy, but already invokes tools frequently. Under outcome-only rewards, RL further amplifies these erroneous patterns, ultimately leading to training collapse [21]. The failure of stage 3 is more subtle, since its pass@1 performance is even higher than that of the stage-2 checkpoint. We therefore conduct a deeper analysis of the stage-3 model. As shown in Figure 3, a salient difference in RL is that, compared with the stage-2 model, the stage-3 model has a 

∼5 times larger train–inference mismatch. We attribute this to partial overfitting to the teacher model in stage 3 , where the student begins to absorb teacher-side noise. Moreover, due to the off-policy nature of tool-return tokens in the context, the model becomes more prone to emitting extremely low-probability tokens, making RL optimization substantially more difficult [23, 49]. 

5.3 Stable RL Training for TIR 

TAKEAWAY 6

For the off-policy nature of tool-call returns, stability must be a central consideration in TIR RL. Using on-policy rollouts together with rollout routing replay is simple but necessary. 

In the RL stage, we do not introduce any reward that directly incentivizes tool invocation, since tuning its weight is difficult and may also induce reward hacking. Instead, we carefully construct a high-difficulty, IMO-level dataset from scratch for training, which is available at Appendix A.2. Intuitively, tasks that exceed the inherent limits of text-only reasoning provide a stronger functional incentive for the model to invoke external tools. However, even under the simplest RLVR setup, training quickly collapses with off-policy updates, where each rollout batch is partitioned into four mini-batches for gradient updates. Surprisingly, simply switching to fully on-policy training could mitigate this issue, as shown in Figure 5. Moreover, adding rollout routing replay [ 27 ], which reuses inference-time routing distributions during training, further improves stability and performance (Figure 5). 6.4 Generalization Analysis Cross-domain transfer. Although trained only on math data, the learned interleaved reasoning pattern transfers to different domains and tasks. As shown in Table 9, across diverse benchmarks, including FrontierScience [ 38 ], which requires scientific computation, LiveCodeBench [ 16 ], which evaluates code generation ability, and the knowledge-intensive GPQA-Diamond [ 30 ], our models consistently achieve non-trivial improvements over the base models, with gains of up to 14.5%. 

Cross-model transfer. Our recipe is not specific to a single model family. We apply the recipe to GLM-4.7-Flash, a model that already possesses native TIR capability [ 34 ]. As shown in Table 7, GLM-4.7-Flash w/ recipe further improves over the original GLM-4.7-Flash on most benchmarks, confirming that our data and training recipe provide complementary signal even for models with existing tool-use ability. Full training details are in Appendix B.4. 97 Conclusion 

In this study, we addressed the challenge of teaching strong thinking models to perform tool-integrated reasoning. We developed a comprehensive training pipeline, spanning from data curation to SFT and RL training recipe. Through our approaches, our models achieved SOTA performance in TIR while preserving no-tool capabilities and further generalizing to different tasks. We hope this study provides useful insights for the community to train future reasoning models.

A.2 RL Data Construction 

We construct 4,325 math QA examples that are challenging, high-quality, and reliably verifiable for RLVR training. The construction process consists of three stages: source collection, QA extraction, and data filtering. 

Data collection. We collect roughly 20K raw problems from mathematical competition books and public online math puzzle sites. The book sources cover olympiad yearbooks, IMO training materials, topic-oriented collections, and translated competition references, while the online sources provide additional answer-oriented problems with concise statements. 

QA extraction. QA extraction mainly targets the book data, where problems and solutions are embedded in long, heterogeneous documents rather than already separated into fields. We use a provenance-centered extraction pipeline: each book is converted to Markdown, line-indexed, and split into overlapping chunks. The extraction model predicts only source coordinates for candidate problems, from which statements are reconstructed. Answer localization is also coordinate-based: candidate solution spans are retrieved from the same book and recovered from the original text. We then normalize statements and answers into English LaTeX and extract final answers in \boxed{} 

format when possible. Online problems are converted to the same schema, with a normalized statement, final answer, and source metadata. 

Data filtering. To enhance the quality and reliability of the extracted data, we apply four filters: problem quality filtering, deduplication, verifiability filtering, and difficulty filtering. We first remove malformed or incomplete records, including layout noise, solution fragments, context-dependent statements, and problems that require diagrams or subjective explanations. We then keep only examples whose final answers can be reliably verified, excluding proof-oriented questions and cases where the answer cannot be normalized into a stable format. Finally, we sample each problem eight times with DeepSeek-Speciale and retain only problems with fewer than six correct attempts. After these steps, we obtain 4,325 problems, each instantiated in both with-tool and text-only formats for RL training.

B.2 Evaluation Settings 

We primarily evaluate on five competition-level mathematical benchmarks: AIME 2025, HMMT 2025, BeyondAIME, IMOAnswerBench, and APEX 2025. These benchmarks cover a wide range of mathematical reasoning skills, including algebra, number theory, combinatorics, geometry, olympiad-style problem solving, and extremely challenging questions. To assess out-of-domain generalization, we further evaluate on FrontierScience, GPQA-Diamond, and LiveCodeBench, which extend the testbed to scientific reasoning, knowledge-intensive QA, and coding. To ensure robust evaluation, we perform 8 independent rollouts per question under both with-tool and no-tool settings, and report avg@8 as the pass@1 metric. We use a consistent rollout budget across models: maximum response length is 80K tokens, and tool-enabled runs allow up to 128 tool calls in a stateful Python sandbox. Sampling parameters such as temperature, top-p, and top-k follow the recommended configuration for each model; for our Qwen3-based models, we use temperature 18 0.6, top-p = 0 .95 , and top-k = 20 . For answer validation, we follow the Rule+Model verification protocol [ 2]: after extracting final answers from \boxed{} markers when available, we first apply a rule-based verifier, and use the model verifier only when rule-based verification fails.

## D Code-Use Behavior Analysis 

D.1 Code-Use Behavior Classification 

To analyze what TIR contributes during problem solving, we categorize code behavior by its computa-tional purpose in the surrounding context, assigning each trajectory one primary purpose. We use four categories: Empirical Discovery , where code computes small cases to reveal a pattern or invariant; 

Algorithmic Search , where code enumerates a large solution space such as brute-force candidates, combinatorial configurations, or DP states; Computation Offloading , where code performs precise arithmetic or symbolic manipulation after the model has identified what to compute; and Conjecture Verification , where code checks a result or intermediate claim already derived in natural-language reasoning.

## E Limitations and Future Work 

This work primarily focuses on tool-integrated reasoning for competition-level mathematics, where problems are challenging, verifiable, and naturally benefit from executable computation. Although our math-only recipe shows promising transfer to scientific reasoning, knowledge-intensive QA, and coding tasks, broader agentic settings remain underexplored. Therefore, we plan to extend this recipe beyond mathematics to broader agentic settings, such as software engineering workflows, web interaction. In addition, our experiments are mainly conducted on 4B and 30B models. As our analysis shows, the 4B and 30B models already exhibit slight different training dynamics, so scaling to substantially larger models may introduce different learning regimes, stability issues, or tool-use patterns. We leave the systematic study of these extensions to future work. 

## F Broader Impacts 

This work studies tool-integrated reasoning for mathematical and scientific problem solving. The potential positive impact is to make language models more reliable on tasks that require exact computation, symbolic manipulation, and systematic search. Since our study is conducted in research benchmarks rather than deployed applications, direct societal risks are limited. A possible risk is that users may over-trust model-generated reasoning or tool outputs when applying similar systems beyond controlled benchmark settings; therefore, practical use should retain transparent execution traces and appropriate human or domain-expert verification. 25
