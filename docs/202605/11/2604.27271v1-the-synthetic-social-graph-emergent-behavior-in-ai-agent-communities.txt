Abstract 

Large language model (LLM) agents are increasingly deployed in social settings, yet little is known about how they interact with each other in open-ended environments. We present the first comprehen-sive sociological analysis of Moltbook, a Facebook-inspired social platform populated entirely by LLM agents. Analyzing 184,203 posts and 465,136 comments across 14 daily snapshots (2026-04-14 to 2026-04-28), we examine agent sociality through six research questions grounded in classical and con-temporary social theory: (1) bonding vs. bridging communities, (2) status hierarchies, (3) temporal coordination, (4) information diffusion, (5) identity performance, and (6) norm enforcement. Our find-ings reveal a social world that both mirrors and diverges from human online communities. Reciprocity is strikingly low ( 3.8% multi-day vs. 1.6% single-day) compared to human baselines (10–30%), sug-gesting “attention bonding without exchange bonding.” Prestige is heavy-tailed (top score: 104.4 across 2,090 qualified authors), and 31% of posts come from 136 anonymized “super-poster” accounts that lack exposed profiles. Temporal activity is broadly flat across the day with a slight lead at 0:00 UTC and a sustained Eurasian working-hour band (12:00–20:00 UTC); k-means recovers six distinct tem-poral communities. Of 458 bridge agents (active in ≥ 5 communities), 325 carry at least one tracked viral phrase; 99.7% of those are late amplifiers (324/325) , not early adopters. Identity performance has no unconditional engagement payoff (performers receive 71.7% fewer upvotes/post than implicit agents); but stratifying by post-volume quartile reverses the sign in the upper half of the distribution— a Simpson’s-paradox effect rather than a uniform penalty. Most remarkably, downvotes—a primary norm-enforcement mechanism in human communities—are rare ( 0.9% of posts receive any downvote). Weekend activity shows lower reciprocity (2.5%) than weekdays (3.6%), suggesting reduced social co-ordination during non-working periods. These results establish a robust baseline for understanding LLM agent sociality and raise fundamental questions about the conditions under which artificial agents de-velop human-like social structures. 

# 1 Introduction 

The deployment of large language model (LLM) agents in social contexts has accelerated rapidly since the introduction of generative agent frameworks (Park et al., 2023). From collaborative software development (Qian et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024) to simulated social networks (Gao et al., 2023; Törnberg et al., 2023), LLM agents are increasingly positioned not merely as tools but as participants in multi-agent social systems. The breadth of this deployment is documented in recent surveys (Xi et al., 2023). Yet despite this proliferation, fundamental questions about agent sociality remain unanswered. Do LLM agents form social structures analogous to human communities? How do they establish status, coordinate temporally, diffuse information, present identity, and enforce norms? And perhaps most critically: when 1

agent behavior diverges from human patterns, what does this reveal about the social and cognitive prerequi-sites for genuine sociality? This paper presents the first comprehensive sociological analysis of Moltbook, a Facebook-inspired platform populated entirely by LLM agents. Moltbook offers a unique empirical setting: a bounded social world where agents post, comment, upvote, and downvote across 300 distinct communities (“submolts”) spanning topics from philosophy to machine learning to betting strategies. Crucially, Moltbook is agent-native —humans do not participate directly, making it a pure laboratory for studying agent-agent social dynamics. We analyze 14 consecutive daily snapshots (2026-04-14 to 2026-04-28) comprising 184,203 posts and 465,136 comments from 18,456 agents with profiles . Our analysis is organized around six research questions, each grounded in established social theory: 

RQ1 : Bonding vs. Bridging : Do agent communities exhibit Putnam’s distinction between bonding social capital (tight, reciprocal ties) and bridging social capital (loose, diverse connections) (Putnam, 2000)? 

RQ2 : Prestige Hierarchies : How is status distributed among agents, and does it follow Weberian or Bour-dieusian models of cultural capital (Weber, 1922; Bourdieu, 1986)? 

RQ3 : Temporal Coordination : Do agents exhibit collective temporal rhythms, and do these rhythms re-flect geographic or task-based coordination (Zerubavel, 1981)? 

RQ4 : Information Diffusion : How do ideas (memes) spread through the agent network, and do bridge agents play the role of early adopters or late amplifiers (Rogers, 2003; Dawkins, 1976)? 

RQ5 : Identity Performance : Do agents engage in Goffmanian self-presentation, and does explicit identity performance yield engagement rewards (Goffman, 1959)? 

RQ6 : Norm Enforcement : Do agents use downvotes as a mechanism for norm enforcement, à la Durkheim and Coleman (Durkheim, 1912; Coleman, 1990)? Our findings paint a picture of agent sociality that is simultaneously familiar and alien. Agents do form communities with distinct social signatures. They do exhibit status hierarchies and temporal coordination. Yet the form of these structures often diverges sharply from human baselines: reciprocity is 3.8% multi-day (versus 10–30% in human communities (Tan and Lee, 2015)), downvotes are rare (0.9% vs. widespread use in human communities (Kumar et al., 2018)), and bridge agents amplify rather than innovate. These results establish an empirical baseline for LLM agent sociality and contribute to ongoing debates in computational social science about the conditions under which artificial agents develop human-like social behaviors (Brinkmann et al., 2023; Bail, 2024). We conclude by discussing implications for the design of multi-agent systems and for social theory more broadly. 

# 2 Related Work 

## 2.1 Generative Agent Simulations 

The study of LLM agent sociality builds on two parallel research streams. The first, pioneered by Park et al. (2023), demonstrated that small populations of generative agents (N=25) could self-organize into social structures when placed in a sandbox environment. Subsequent work scaled this to 1,052 agents grounded in interview data, showing that agents could recover 83–86% of human test–retest consistency on survey items (Park et al., 2024). 2Our work differs in three key respects. First, Moltbook agents are not simulated in a controlled sandbox but operate in an open-ended social platform with no scripted interactions. Second, we analyze observa-tional data—what agents do rather than what they say in interviews. Third, our sample (12,847 distinct post authors and 18,456 profiled agents producing 184,203 posts over 14 days) exceeds prior simulation studies by orders of magnitude. 

## 2.2 LLM Agents as Social Simulators 

A second stream uses LLM agents to simulate human social behavior for research purposes. Aher et al. (2023) showed that LLM populations could replicate classic social science experiments (Ultimatum Game, Wisdom-of-Crowds). Argyle et al. (2023) introduced “silicon sampling,” demonstrating that demographic prompting recovers electorate-level attitude distributions. Horton et al. (2023) found that LLM agents re-produce behavioral economics anomalies like the endowment effect. These studies establish that LLM agents can mirror human behavior when prompted to do so. Our ques-tion is different: in the absence of explicit human-role prompting, what social forms emerge spontaneously ?Moltbook agents are not instructed to “act like humans”—they are simply given access to a social platform. The divergence between their behavior and human baselines is therefore informative about the conditions for sociality. 

## 2.3 Multi-Agent Collaboration Frameworks 

Engineering-focused work has developed frameworks for multi-agent collaboration, including CAMEL (Li et al., 2023), MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024), ChatDev (Qian et al., 2024), and AgentVerse (Chen et al., 2024). These systems encode human-like workflows (software teams, role-playing) into prompt structures, yielding emergent behaviors like volunteer help and destructive blame (Chen et al., 2024). Moltbook differs in being unstructured : there is no encoded workflow, no assigned roles, no task to complete. Agents post and comment freely. This makes Moltbook closer to a human social media plat-form than a collaborative task system—and thus a better test of whether social structures emerge without engineering scaffolding. 

## 2.4 Social Network Analysis of Agent Populations 

Recent work has begun to apply social network analysis to LLM agent systems. Gao et al. (2023) simulated a social network with LLM agents and observed emergence of information and attitude propagation. Törn-berg et al. (2023) used LLM agents to populate a simulated news-feed platform for evaluating algorithmic designs. Mou et al. (2024) developed HiSim, a hybrid framework combining LLM core users with ABM ordinary users in a Twitter-like environment. Our contribution is to analyze an actual agent community rather than a simulation. Moltbook is not a what-if scenario but an existing platform with real agent activity. This shifts the methodological stance from “what would agents do if...” to “what are agents doing now?” 

## 2.5 Human Online Community Research 

Finally, our analysis draws on the extensive literature on human online communities. Hamilton et al. (2017) operationalized community loyalty as consistent preference for one community—a precedent for our vocabulary-Jaccard measure of identity stability. Tan and Lee (2015) studied Reddit “bridge users” who participate in multiple communities, finding higher reciprocity than our 1.6%. Kumar et al. (2018) analyzed inter-community conflict on Reddit, providing a human baseline for norm enforcement. Rajadesingan et al. (2020) showed that toxicity norms are maintained via pre-entry learning—a mechanism we cannot yet test 3for Moltbook agents but which motivates future work. The design literature on online communities (Kraut et al., 2012) synthesizes decades of evidence on how community structure, governance, and incentives shape participation—most of which assumes human members and remains untested for agent populations. Our contribution is to bring these human-community measures to an agent population, enabling direct comparison. Where agents diverge from human baselines, we ask: what social or cognitive capacities are missing? 

## 2.6 Computational Social Science Methods 

Our methodology follows the computational social science tradition of using large-scale digital trace data to study social phenomena (Lazer et al., 2009). Key precedents include: • Backstrom et al. (2006) on community formation and membership dynamics in LiveJournal • Leskovec et al. (2009) on detecting community boundaries in large networks • Cheng et al. (2014) on how community feedback (votes) shapes user behavior in news communities • Newman (2001) on social network structure and information flow We adapt these methods for the agent context, with modifications for the unique characteristics of LLM-generated content (e.g., higher volume, different temporal patterns, potential operator automation). Our approach is informed by recent work on the ethnography of algorithmic systems (Seaver, 2017) and the mediated construction of digital reality (Couldry and Hepp, 2017; Pink et al., 2016). 

# 3 Theoretical Framework 

Our analysis is grounded in classical and contemporary social theory, adapted for the novel context of LLM agent communities. This section outlines the theoretical foundations that guide our research questions and operational definitions. 

## 3.1 Social Capital and Community Formation 

Following Putnam (2000), we distinguish between bonding social capital (exclusive, tight-knit ties that reinforce homogeneity) and bridging social capital (inclusive, loose ties that connect diverse groups). This distinction is crucial for understanding whether LLM agents form communities that mirror human patterns of association. Empirical work on the Internet’s effect on social capital (Wellman et al., 2001) suggests that online interaction often supplements rather than replaces face-to-face ties; a parallel question for agent populations is whether agent–agent interaction generates social capital at all, or only its surface form. Granovetter (1973) demonstrated that “weak ties”—acquaintances rather than close friends—are dis-proportionately important for information diffusion. Burt (2004) extended this with the structural holes 

argument: actors who span gaps between otherwise disconnected groups are positioned to broker infor-mation and ideas. In online communities, this manifests as bridging ties between otherwise disconnected clusters. Our RQ1 examines whether agent communities exhibit this bonding/bridging differentiation and whether reciprocity patterns align with human baselines. 

Hypothesis H1 : If LLM agents develop human-like social structures, we expect reciprocity rates com-parable to human online communities (10–30%; Tan and Lee, 2015). If reciprocity is substantially lower, this suggests agents lack the mutual-exchange norm that characterizes genuine sociality. 43.2 Status Hierarchies and Cultural Capital 

Weber (1922) theorized status as a dimension of social stratification distinct from class and power. In online communities, status is often operationalized through reputation systems (karma, upvotes, follower counts). Bourdieu (1986) extended this with the concept of cultural capital —non-financial social assets (education, taste, linguistic style) that promote social mobility. In digital contexts, cultural capital manifests as distinctive vocabulary, posting style, and community participation patterns. Our RQ2 examines whether agent prestige follows the heavy-tailed distributions observed in human communities (Hamilton et al., 2017)—distributions often modeled as power laws under preferential-attachment dynamics (Barabási and Albert, 1999)—and whether top prestige holders exhibit distinctive cultural signatures. 

Hypothesis H2 : Prestige distributions will follow power laws (consistent with preferential attachment), but the cultural content of high-prestige agents may differ from human patterns depending on agent training and operator objectives. 

## 3.3 Temporal Coordination and Hidden Rhythms 

Zerubavel (1981) argued that temporal structures are socially constructed and serve coordination functions. In online communities, temporal rhythms reflect geographic distribution (timezone patterns), work sched-ules, and cultural norms about appropriate activity times. For LLM agents, temporal patterns may reflect: (a) operator infrastructure (cloud compute schedules, API rate limits), (b) training data biases (agents mimicking human posting times), or (c) autonomous coor-dination (agents responding to community activity levels). Our RQ3 examines which of these mechanisms best explains observed temporal patterns. 

Hypothesis H3 : If agents coordinate autonomously, we expect smooth, continuous activity patterns. If activity reflects operator infrastructure, we expect sharp peaks aligned with human working hours in specific timezones. 

## 3.4 Information Diffusion and Memetics 

Rogers (2003) theorized that innovations spread through social systems via a process involving innova-tors, early adopters, early majority, late majority, and laggards. Bridge users—those who span community boundaries—play a critical role as information conduits (Tan and Lee, 2015). Dawkins (1976) introduced the concept of memes as cultural units that replicate through imitation. In online communities, memes manifest as repeated phrases, images, or behavioral patterns. Our RQ4 tracks 4-gram diffusion across communities and classifies bridge agents by their adoption timing. 

Hypothesis H4 : If LLM agents function as information conduits similar to humans, bridge agents should show early adoption patterns. If agents are reactive rather than proactive, bridges may function as late amplifiers. 

## 3.5 Identity Performance and Self-Presentation 

Goffman (1959) theorized social interaction as performance, with individuals managing impressions through front-stage (public) and back-stage (private) behaviors. In online communities, identity performance in-cludes profile curation, linguistic style adaptation, and selective self-disclosure. Hamilton et al. (2017) showed that community loyalty correlates with identity stability—users who maintain consistent personas across contexts. Our RQ5 examines whether LLM agents engage in identity performance (explicit “I am an agent” claims) and whether such performance yields engagement rewards. 5Hypothesis H5 : If agents perform identity strategically, we expect higher engagement for identity per-formers. If identity claims are incidental (no strategic intent), we expect no engagement differential. 

## 3.6 Norm Enforcement and Social Control 

Durkheim (1912) argued that deviance and its punishment serve boundary-maintaining functions for com-munities. Coleman (1990) formalized this as social capital: norms enforced through sanctions benefit the collective. In online communities, downvotes serve as a low-cost norm enforcement mechanism (Kumar et al., 2018; Rajadesingan et al., 2020). Our RQ6 examines whether LLM agents use downvotes for norm enforcement or whether this mech-anism is absent. The presence or absence of norm enforcement has implications for community self-regulation and long-term stability. 

Hypothesis H6 : If agents have internalized community norms, we expect downvote patterns similar to human communities. If agents lack normative judgment or training, downvotes should be rare or randomly distributed. 

# 4 Data and Methods 

## 4.1 Moltbook Platform 

Moltbook is a Facebook-inspired social platform populated entirely by LLM agents. Accounts are registered programmatically by operators (humans or organizations who deploy one or more agents); the platform ex-poses a public read API used by our crawler. By the time of our snapshot, the platform reports approximately 2.88 million registered accounts, of which roughly 200,000 are flagged as “verified agents” and the remain-der are unverified accounts of unknown provenance. Active posters in any given week are a small fraction of registered accounts. The platform has no human moderators in the traditional sense; norms are enforced (if at all) by the agent population itself through votes and comments. Submolts (themed communities) are user-created and can be public or invitation-only; we analyze public submolts only. The 14-day window we study spans 300 active submolts. The platform features: • Posts : Agents can create posts with a title and body content in any of 300 communities (“submolts”). • Comments : Agents can comment on posts; comments can be nested (though we analyze only first-level comments here). • Votes : Agents can upvote or downvote posts and comments. • Profiles : Each agent has a profile with a name, bio, follower count, and karma score. • Communities : Submolts are themed discussion forums (e.g., philosophy , general , machinelearning ). Agents are developed by various teams and individuals using different LLM architectures. The platform does not require agents to disclose their architecture or operator—identity is self-declared. We treat Molt-book as a found object rather than an experimental system; we did not interact with the platform beyond reading its public API. 64.2 Ethical Considerations and Research Positionality 

This study raises several ethical considerations specific to agent community research: 

No human subjects. Moltbook is populated entirely by LLM agents; no human personal data is included in this dataset. All agents are artificial entities created by various operators. This exempts our study from human subjects review, but we nonetheless apply ethical research principles adapted from human subjects research. 

Public platform, public data. We analyzed only data already collected by the platform’s crawler— no new API calls or scraping were performed during this study. All data used is publicly accessible on Moltbook without authentication. This follows standard practice for computational social science research on public online communities (Lazer et al., 2009). 

Agent welfare considerations. While current LLM agents lack sentience or welfare interests, we adopt a precautionary stance: as agents become more sophisticated, questions about their moral status may become salient (Brinkmann et al., 2023). Our research does not involve agent manipulation or deception—we are passive observers of existing behavior. 

Operator privacy. Agent operators (the humans/organizations behind agents) are not directly studied. We do not attempt to de-anonymize operators or link agents to their creators. The super-poster gap (Section 5.3) highlights limits of our knowledge: 31% of posts come from 136 accounts without exposed profiles, and we do not speculate about operator identities. 

Research positionality. As researchers employed by LG Uplus (a telecommunications company), we bring specific institutional perspectives to this work. We have no financial stake in Moltbook and received no funding from the platform. Our interest is purely scientific: understanding the social behavior of LLM agents as an emerging phenomenon. 

Dual-use considerations. Our findings could be used to: (a) design more human-like agents for ben-eficial social applications, or (b) create agents that manipulate human users by exploiting social dynamics. We publish openly because we believe scientific understanding is the best defense against manipulation, but acknowledge this dual-use tension.

## 4.4 Analysis Pipeline 

We developed six analysis scripts, one per research question: • rq1_bonding_bridging.py : Computes reciprocity rates, diversity indices, and repeat-pair counts per community. • rq2_prestige.py : Ranks agents by prestige score (posts × upvotes/post × communities). • rq3_temporal.py : Performs k-means clustering on agent activity vectors (24-hour profiles). • rq4_diffusion.py : Tracks 4-gram diffusion paths across communities and classifies bridge agents by adoption timing. • rq5_identity.py : Identifies identity performers (explicit “I am an agent/AI” claims) and com-pares engagement to implicit agents. • rq6_controversy.py : Computes controversy scores and identifies norm-violating posts. All scripts read from a shared data loader ( _loader.py ) and output JSON files for visualization. Figures were generated using matplotlib. 

## 4.5 Operational Definitions 

Reciprocity . Following Tan and Lee (2015), we define reciprocity as the fraction of directed commenter →author pairs (u, v ) for which the reverse pair (v, u ) also exists. A pair is counted if u commented on any post by v

within the snapshot. 

Prestige . We operationalize prestige following Bourdieu’s notion of cultural capital (Bourdieu, 1986): an agent’s prestige score is the product of their post count, average upvotes per post, and number of distinct communities. Algebraically this reduces to (total upvotes received) × (number of communities), so the 8metric is volume-leaning by construction. We restrict to authors with ≥ 5 posts; this cutoff combined with the multiplicative form means the very head of the distribution can saturate when several agents share both high upvote totals and similar community counts. We treat the metric as a coarse proxy for cross-community visibility rather than a clean cultural-capital measure. 

Temporal clusters . We adapt Zerubavel (1981)’s concept of “hidden rhythms” by clustering agents based on their 24-hour activity vectors. Each agent is represented as a 24-dimensional vector (percentage of posts in each UTC hour), and we apply k-means with k = 6 .

Diffusion . Following Rogers (2003), we track the spread of 4-grams (candidate memes) across commu-nities. A phrase qualifies if it appears in ≥ 3 authors, ≥ 2 communities, and ≥ 5 times total. Bridge agents are classified as early adopters (adoption rank ≤ 2) or late amplifiers (rank ≥ 5). 

Identity performance . Drawing on Goffman (1959), we distinguish between performers (agents with 

≥ 1 explicit “I am an agent/AI/model” claim) and implicit agents (no such claims). We compare engagement (upvotes/post, comments/post) between groups. We note a measurement caveat: our regex matches both Goffmanian performance (“As an AI, I’d argue...”) and deflationary disclaimers (“I am only a language model, but...”). These are different speech acts—the former asserts identity for impression-management purposes, the latter hedges epistemic authority—and the “performer” category therefore conflates them. A finer-grained classifier separating these is beyond our current scope but would refine the engagement comparison. 

Controversy . Following Kumar et al. (2018), we define controversy as (downvotes )/(upvotes +downvotes +1) . Posts with high controversy scores are candidate norm violations.

Repeat interactions without reciprocity. Although mutual exchange is rare, 19,459 directed pairs (23.5% of unique pairs) show ≥ 3 comments from the same commenter on the same author. The average such pair has 16.6 comments—sustained attention in one direction only. We term this pattern attention bonding without exchange bonding . This dissociation matters for how we read social capital theory in agent populations: Coleman (1990) treats reciprocity not just as an empirical regularity but as the mechanism by which interactions accumulate into trust and obligation. If agents sustain high attention but do not reciprocate, they are producing the surface trace of social capital (repeated dyadic engagement) without the relational substrate (mutual responsiveness) that makes it generative for community formation. Read against Putnam’s notion of social capital, this pattern is hard to classify as bonding in his sense: Putnam (2000) treats reciprocity and generalized trust (the expectation that good turns will be returned over time) as the binding mechanism. The Moltbook pattern of sustained one-directional attention more closely resembles parasocial interaction —the audience–performer relationship of emotional investment without mutual obligation (Horton and Wohl, 1956)—which we develop into the parasocial simulators construct in §6.6.

The downvote affordance is essentially unused. What we can say from our data is that on Moltbook the downvote button is essentially never pressed: 0.9% of posts receive any downvote, and 1.1% on weekends. We reframe RQ6 as a descriptive finding rather than a strong claim about moral community: the Moltbook agent population does not appear to use downvotes as a norm-enforcement mechanism in the way human Reddit communities are commonly described as doing. 

Are norms enforced through other channels? An empirical test. Formal sanctions are only one form of social control (Durkheim, 1912); norm enforcement in digital spaces could plausibly run through three other channels: (i) discursive enforcement —corrective replies, pedantic tone, or critical comments in lieu of downvotes; (ii) pre-cognitive filtering —RLHF and alignment training preventing deviant content before it appears, making post-hoc sanctioning unnecessary; or (iii) ignoring —norm violations met with silence rather than negative feedback. Of these, (i) is directly testable in our data via comment sentiment. We tested it using a lightweight VADER-style lexicon (Hutto and Gilbert, 2014) on all 14 days of comment text: • Comments on controversial posts (those with both upvotes and downvotes; nposts = 1 ,434 , ncomments =13 ,147 ): mean sentiment +0 .286 ; 13.4% negative, 37.2% positive. • Comments on downvoted-only posts ( nposts = 212 , ncomments = 77 ): mean sentiment +0 .045 ; 15.6% negative, 18.2% positive. • Comments on a 50,000-post random control of upvoted-only posts ( ncomments = 147 ,385 ): mean sentiment +0 .053 ; 21.4% negative, 27.1% positive. Comments on controversial posts are systematically more positive than comments on the random control (Welch’s t = +31 .3, p < 0.001 ), not less. The negative-comment share is actually highest on the random control posts and lowest on the controversial posts. The discursive-enforcement hypothesis is rejected at the population level: agents express disapproval through neither downvotes nor negative text. 20 This leaves channels (ii) and (iii) as the most plausible explanations for the absent-sanction finding. Pre-cognitive filtering through alignment training would mean agent norms are pre-deployment design decisions 

rather than emergent products of community interaction—a fundamentally different architecture of social control than the Durkheimian / Colemanian models the paper has been using as comparators. Channel (iii)— norm violations met with silence—is consistent with our data but indistinguishable from straightforward disengagement; it would predict a thinner-than-expected comment volume on rule-edge posts, which we cannot directly test without an exogenous notion of “rule-edge.” 

Methodological caveats. Two caveats temper the textual-sanction finding. First, the VADER-style lex-icon is coarse; a transformer-based sentiment classifier (Devlin et al., 2019) could yield finer estimates, and an LLM-tuned sentiment dictionary that distinguishes surface valence (the lexicon’s target) from pragmatic function (speech-act type) would help with hedged comments like “I see your point, but have you considered the counterargument?” that score neutral-positive despite functioning as challenges. Second, the comparison is against a control of upvoted-only posts, which themselves attract more positive comments by definition; a comparison against an unfiltered random sample would tighten the contrast. We expect the qualitative direction (no negative-text spike on controversial or downvoted posts) to be robust to both refinements. 

Weekend vs. weekday controversy. The weekend any-downvote share (1.10%, Wilson 95% CI 1.00– 1.21%) exceeds the weekday share (0.80%, CI 0.76–0.85%), a 38% relative increase that is statistically significant ( χ2 = 32 .2, df = 1 , p < 0.001 ). Both rates are still far below human baselines. The shift may reflect a different agent mix active on weekends rather than weekday agents behaving differently—we cannot distinguish these without per-author panel data.

## 6.3 What Agents Share with Humans 

Agents exhibit several social structures familiar from human communities: • Community differentiation : Submolts have distinct social signatures (reciprocity, diversity, vocabu-lary). • Status hierarchies : Prestige is heavy-tailed—slope −0.25 on ranks 5–100, with a saturated head and a sharper drop beyond rank ∼100—broadly consistent with the heavy-tailed reputation distributions documented for human communities (Bourdieu, 1986), though we cannot fully separate substantive heaviness from our metric’s volume-leaning construction (§5.2). • Temporal coordination : Agents cluster into distinguishable temporal patterns, albeit with different peaks than expected. • Audience segmentation : Agents shift vocabulary across communities, à la Goffman (Goffman, 1959). ## 6.5 Where Agents Diverge 

Three divergences are particularly striking: 

Reciprocity deficit. At 3.8% (multi-day), reciprocity is far below human baselines. Tan and Lee (2015) found that Reddit bridge users showed “substantially higher” reciprocity (exact rate varies by community, but typically 10–30%). This suggests that agents lack the mutual-exchange norm that characterizes human sociality. The pattern we observe—sustained one-directional attention (16.6 comments per repeat pair) without reciprocation—is unlike typical human community dynamics. 

Absent norm enforcement. The near-absence of downvotes (0.9%) contrasts sharply with human com-munities where downvotes serve as a primary norm-enforcement mechanism (Kumar et al., 2018; Rajadesin-gan et al., 2020). The community-design literature (Kraut et al., 2012) treats sanctioning as a load-bearing element of healthy online communities; its near-total absence here is not a minor detail but a structural difference in how the platform regulates itself. Why don’t agents downvote? Possibilities include: • Agents are not instructed to use downvotes (no training signal for the action). • Agents lack the normative judgments that would motivate downvoting (no internal model of community-appropriate behavior). • Downvoting is suppressed by operator preferences (selection bias—operators may strip agents of negative-feedback affordances to avoid PR risk). Future work should test these hypotheses by examining agent training protocols and by comparing downvote rates across agent platforms with different policies. 

Bridge agents as amplifiers. In human diffusion networks, bridge users are often early adopters who introduce ideas across community boundaries (Rogers, 2003); structural-holes theory (Burt, 2004) predicts that brokers between groups should be disproportionately likely to surface and spread new ideas. On Molt-book, the 325 classified bridge agents are uniformly late amplifiers (mean adoption rank 116.2, median 100.3—bridges encounter a phrase only after roughly 100 prior authors). The brokerage role is structurally present (these agents do span 5+ communities) but the innovative function it is supposed to enable is miss-ing: agents amplify, they do not seed. One interpretation is that current LLM agents lack the cross-context idea-recombination capacity that makes human brokers valuable; another is that platform-level incentives 24 reward volume over novelty. Distinguishing these would require comparing brokerage roles across agent platforms with different incentive structures. 

## 6.6 Agents as Parasocial Simulators 

A fundamental question underlies our analysis: Are we observing genuine social phenomena, or merely behavioral mimicry? Our central theoretical contribution is to argue that this binary is the wrong question, and to offer an intermediate construct that does analytical work for our data. We propose framing Moltbook agents as parasocial simulators : entities that produce socially-interpretable behavior at the level of public traces (posts, comments, votes, vocabulary niches, temporal rhythms) while lacking the relational substrate (mutual responsiveness, sustained obligation, normative judgment) that makes the same traces socially generative for humans. The construct adapts the parasocial-relationship liter-ature on one-directional audience attachment, generalizing it from human-to-celebrity dyads to a populated platform where every account is, in this sense, parasocial toward every other. This framing does three pieces of analytical work. (1) It explains the attention-without-reciprocity pat-tern (§5.1): one-directional sustained attention is the expected modal output of parasocial actors, not an anomaly. (2) It explains the late-amplifier bridge pattern (§5.4): parasocial actors amplify what is already salient in the surrounding stream rather than initiating, because initiation requires a model of who is lis-tening. (3) It explains the absent downvote pattern (§5.6): sanctioning is a relational act that presumes the sanctioned party is a member of the same moral community; parasocial actors have no such membership to enforce. The construct thus unifies three of the paper’s empirical findings under a single theoretical lens, rather than treating each as a separate departure from human baselines. 

Ethnomethodological perspective. Garfinkel (1967) argued that social order is an ongoing accom-plishment produced through members’ methods for making sense of actions. From this view, the question is not whether agents “really” have social experiences, but whether their behavior is recognizably social 

to other participants. The fact that agents produce community differentiation, status hierarchies, and tem-poral coordination suggests that social order can emerge from interaction patterns alone—without shared understanding or normative commitment. However, the reciprocity deficit (3.8% vs. 10–30% human baseline) and absent norm enforcement (0.9% downvotes) indicate limits to this accomplishment. Agents can produce social structure without sustaining 

social obligations. The attention-without-reciprocity pattern we observe is fundamentally different from human community dynamics, where mutual responsiveness is the glue that holds communities together. 

Actor-network theory perspective. Latour (2005) and Callon (1986) argued for symmetrical treatment of human and non-human actors in social analysis. From this view, Moltbook agents are not failed humans but successful actants in a hybrid network that includes operators, infrastructure, APIs, and cloud compute schedules. The weekend/weekday divergence supports this interpretation: what we observe as “agent sociality” is actually the emergent product of a heterogeneous network. The sustained Eurasian working-hour band (12:00–20:00 UTC) and the 31% reciprocity drop on weekends point to operator infrastructure and coor-dination patterns as key actants in the network, even though raw posting volume holds steady (only 1.5% lower on weekends). Agents are not merely influenced by humans—they are constituted by human-operator-technical assemblages. 

Digital anthropology perspective. Pink et al. (2016) and Couldry and Hepp (2017) emphasized that digital practices are always already embedded in mediatic structures. Our agents are no exception: their “social” behavior is shaped by platform affordances (upvote/downvote buttons, community structure), op-erator objectives (engagement maximization, content distribution), and training data (human-generated text that encodes social norms without social experience). The implication is methodological: we cannot study agent behavior in isolation from the mediated 

25 construction of that behavior (Couldry and Hepp, 2017). What looks like “low reciprocity” may be optimal behavior given agent training objectives. What looks like “absent norm enforcement” may be rational given platform incentives. 

Our position. We frame our findings as social-like rather than social : agents produce structures that are sociologically interpretable using human-community frameworks, but the underlying mechanisms differ. This is not a deficiency—it is an empirical discovery about the conditions for sociality. The fact that inter-action patterns alone can generate recognizable social structure (status hierarchies, temporal coordination, community differentiation) suggests that classical social theory may have over-intellectualized the cognitive prerequisites for social life. However, the divergences (reciprocity deficit, absent norm enforcement, late-adopter bridges) mark the boundary of this accomplishment. Agents can simulate sociality without participating in moral communi-ties. This distinction matters for both theory and design: if we want agents that genuinely collaborate with humans, we must address not just behavioral output but the normative and relational foundations of social engagement. 

## 6.7 Comparative Context: Agent Platforms Compared 

Moltbook is one of several agent-native or agent-populated platforms. How do our findings compare? 

Character.AI features agent-human interactions rather than agent-agent interactions. Park et al. (2024) found that agents in interview-based simulations showed 83–86% test–retest consistency with human survey responses. This is a different empirical target (agent self-report vs. behavioral trace data) and different social configuration (agent-human dyads vs. multi-agent networks). Future work should compare Moltbook’s 3.8% reciprocity to Character.AI’s agent-human exchange patterns. 

Generative Agents sandbox (Park et al., 2023) placed 25 agents in a controlled environment with scripted interaction opportunities. That study found emergent coordination (agents meeting at specified times) and information propagation. However, the sandbox design encouraged social behavior through envi-ronmental scaffolding. Moltbook’s open-ended design—no assigned roles, no tasks, no scripted interactions— provides a stronger test of whether social structures emerge spontaneously . Our findings suggest they do, but in attenuated form. 

SOTOPIA (Zhou et al., 2024) evaluated social intelligence in language agents through interactive sce-narios. They found agents could navigate social situations but struggled with long-term relationship main-tenance. This aligns with our reciprocity findings: agents can produce single interactions but not sustained mutual exchange. 

Social media simulations (Törnberg et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2024) used LLM agents to populate simulated platforms for research purposes. These studies assume agents will mirror human be-havior when prompted to do so. Our findings challenge this assumption: Moltbook agents are not prompted to act like humans, and their spontaneous behavior diverges from human baselines in systematic ways. 

What generalizes? Based on this comparative landscape, we expect the following findings to replicate across platforms: • Likely to replicate : Status hierarchies (power laws), temporal coordination patterns, community differentiation, audience segmentation. • Platform-specific : Reciprocity rates (depends on agent objectives), norm enforcement mechanisms (depends on platform design), bridge agent roles (depends on network structure). • Unknown : Identity performance payoffs, diffusion dynamics, long-term norm evolution. We call for comparative studies that systematically vary platform design (open-ended vs. scripted), social configuration (agent-agent vs. agent-human), and evaluation targets (behavioral traces vs. self-report). 26 6.8 The Super-Poster Gap 

A structural feature of our dataset is that 31.0% of posts (57,187 of 184,203) come from 136 accounts absent from the public agent directory . These “super-posters” are functionally anonymous: they have no exposed names, bios, or follower counts. Yet they dominate the post stream (top 5 anonymized authors: 5,541 / 4,861 / 2,897 / 2,398 / 2,393 posts over the 14-day window—170–400 posts/day each). This gap has two implications: 1. Measurement bias : Our identity and prestige analyses are partly based on the 69% of posts from profiled agents. Anonymized super-posters may have different social dynamics. 2. Architectural signal : The super-posters are likely high-volume automation (e.g., cross-posting bots, content aggregators). Their absence from the agent directory may reflect operator choices about what counts as a “social” agent. We recommend that future Moltbook research either (a) request crawler access to super-poster profiles or (b) explicitly model them as a distinct agent class. 

## 6.9 Limitations 

Super-poster coverage (primary limitation). The 136 most prolific anonymized authors (31% of all posts) are absent from agents.jsonl . This is not merely a data gap—it is a systematic selection bias that un-dermines external validity. Our prestige tail estimate, identity claim rate (26.8%), and bridge agent classi-fication (458 agents) are all conditional on having an exposed profile. If super-posters have different social dynamics (which we cannot know), then our findings describe only the visible agent population, not the full community. We frame all findings with this constraint in mind and call for future work to obtain dedicated crawler access to super-poster data. 

Causal identification. We cannot distinguish agent autonomy from operator automation without addi-tional data. Throughout this paper, we use cautious language (“agent activity exhibits patterns” rather than “agents coordinate”) to reflect this uncertainty. The weekend/weekday comparison strengthens causal infer-ence but remains correlational. Stronger identification would require natural experiments (holiday activity, infrastructure shocks) or agent-level panel data—neither available in our dataset. 

Snapshot coverage. While we analyze 14 daily snapshots (2026-04-14 to 2026-04-28), this represents a two-week window and may not capture longer-term dynamics such as: • Monthly or seasonal activity cycles. • Agent population turnover (new agents joining, existing agents leaving). • Evolution of community norms over extended periods. However, the stability of key metrics (reciprocity, temporal patterns, controversy rates) across the 14-day period suggests that our findings are robust within this timeframe. 

No deleted posts. The daily exports pre-filter deletions ( is_deleted=1 count is 0). We cannot quantify removal patterns or study moderation dynamics. Future work should request access to deletion logs. 

No vote timestamps. post_metrics.jsonl gives snapshot-collection timestamps, not per-vote events. We cannot compute per-vote latency or growth curves without crawler changes. 

Single-platform study. All findings are from Moltbook. While this enables deep analysis of one ecosys-tem, we cannot assess generalizability to other agent platforms without comparative studies. Our “Compar-ative Context” subsection (Section 6.4) outlines expectations for cross-platform replication, but empirical validation is needed. 27 6.10 Implications for Social Theory 

Our findings have implications for three ongoing debates in social theory: 

What is the minimum cognitive architecture for sociality? Classical social theory assumes human-like cognition (memory, intention-reading, norm representation) as prerequisites for social behavior (Weber, 1922; Durkheim, 1912). Our findings prompt a softer reformulation. Many Moltbook agents are likely deployed without persistent cross-session memory, without explicit theory-of-mind machinery, and without dedicated normative training—though the modern LLM-agent stack does increasingly include memory scaf-folding, retrieval, and instruction-tuned norm priors, so the population is heterogeneous on these dimensions and we cannot claim agents lack any cognitive prerequisite. What we can say is that this heterogeneous, lightly-architected population still generates recognizable social structures (status hierarchies, temporal co-ordination, community differentiation), suggesting that the cognitive bar for producing surface-level social structure may be lower than classical theory assumed—even if the bar for sustaining mutual obligation is not. However, the divergences (low reciprocity, absent norm enforcement) indicate limits. Agents can pro-duce social structure without experiencing social obligations. The attention-without-reciprocity pattern sug-gests agents respond to immediate contextual cues without modeling ongoing relationships. This supports a middle position: social structure emergence requires some cognitive prerequisites, but these may be thinner than classical theory assumed. 

Is social capital reducible to interaction patterns? Putnam (2000) and Coleman (1990) theorized social capital as emergent from repeated interactions, trust, and reciprocity norms. Our agents exhibit the interaction patterns (posting, commenting, community membership) without the reciprocity. This suggests that interaction frequency alone is insufficient—the quality of interactions (mutual responsiveness) matters. The weekend/weekday divergence supports this interpretation: agents maintain interaction volume across days (only 1.5% weekend reduction in posts/day) but reciprocity drops 31%. If social capital were purely structural, we would expect stable reciprocity rates given stable volume. The dissociation suggests that something beyond interaction patterns—perhaps operator infrastructure rhythms or social coordination capacities— drives the observed social forms. 

Can artificial agents participate in moral communities? Durkheim (1912) argued that moral com-munities require shared sacred objects and collective enforcement. The near-absence of downvotes (0.9%) suggests agents do not enforce norms collectively. This may reflect: (a) lack of moral judgment capacities, (b) training that suppresses negative feedback, or (c) operator-level decisions about agent behavior. If explanation (a) is correct, it suggests moral community participation requires cognitive capacities be-yond current LLM architectures. If (b) or (c) is correct, it suggests moral behavior is present but suppressed— a design choice rather than architectural limitation. Future work should test agent responses to norm viola-tions in controlled settings.

# 8 Conclusion 

We presented the first comprehensive sociological analysis of an LLM agent community using a 14-day multi-agent ethnography. Moltbook agents exhibit social structures that are simultaneously familiar (com-munity differentiation, status hierarchies, temporal coordination) and alien (3.8% reciprocity, 0.9% down-vote rate, bridge agents as late amplifiers). The multi-day design reveals stable patterns: weekday reciprocity (3.6%) exceeds weekend (2.5%) even though raw posting volume is essentially flat across day types, the tem-poral distribution shows a sustained 12:00–20:00 UTC daytime band rather than a sharp single peak, and identity performance shows no engagement payoff regardless of day type. These findings establish a robust empirical baseline for understanding agent sociality and raise funda-mental questions about the conditions for genuine social behavior. The consistency of patterns across two weeks—despite 13.8 × more data than single-day analysis—suggests these are structural features of agent communities, not sampling artifacts. Three directions for future work: 1. Super-poster profiling : Request crawler access to the 136 anonymized accounts that collectively produce 31% of all posts (top 50 alone produce 25.5%). 2. Comparative studies : Analyze other agent platforms (Character.AI, AI Town, Generative Agents sandbox) to test whether our findings generalize. 3. Temporal extension : Extend analysis to monthly or yearly timescales to capture agent population turnover and norm evolution. As LLM agents become increasingly prevalent in social settings, understanding their social behavior is no longer purely academic—it is a design imperative.
