Abstract 

As large models evolve from conversational assistants into autonomous agents, challenges increasingly arise from long-horizon decision making, tool use, and real environment interaction. Existing agenticinfrastructure remain fragmented across evaluation, data management, and agent evolution, making it difficult to discover risks systematically and improve models in a continuous closed loop. In this report, we present Safactory , a scalable agent factory for trustworthy autonomous intelligence. Safactory integrates three tightly coupled platforms: a Parallel Simulation Platform 

for trajectory generation, a Trustworthy Data Platform for trajectory storage and experience extraction, and an Autonomous Evolution Platform for asynchronous reinforcement learning and on-policy distillation. As far as we know, Safactory 1 is the first framework to propose a unified evolutionary pipeline for next-generation trustworthy autonomous intelligence.

# 1 Introduction 

## 1.1 A Panoramic View of AI Agent Safety Risks 

With the rapid development of applications such as companion AI, DeepResearch, intelligent terminals, and embodied AI, large language models are moving from ”conversational assistants” to ”autonomous agents.” These systems are not only responsible for answering questions but also continuously perceive their environment, plan tasks, invoke tools, and directly influence external systems. This brings stronger application capabilities, but also imposes higher safety requirements. Unlike traditional single-turn models, the risks of agents are not limited to harmful outputs; they accumulate progressively throughout the process of ”perception → decision-making →

execution → feedback,” ultimately manifesting as real-world environmental consequences. As shown in Figure 2, violations, jailbreak inductions, and explicit attacks are merely the risks above the surface. Beneath the waterline lie more insidious issues such as tool misuse, state drift, process loss of control, data contamination, cross-turn inducement, and cascading errors. These risks are often harder to detect and more difficult to cover with static testing. Such risks typically exhibit three characteristics. First, they often emerge in long-horizon, multi-step interactions rather than single-turn input-output pairs. Second, they are closely tied to environmental states, tool interfaces, and execution contexts, making them difficult to characterize using static benchmarks alone. Third, when they do occur, the consequences often go beyond degraded response quality — they may further lead to erroneous operations, privacy breaches, system damage, or even real-world losses. Therefore, trustworthy AI for high-value scenarios requires not just ”safer responses,” but more importantly, the ability to remain controllable, auditable, and recoverable while taking continuous action. 

## 1.2 Limitations of Existing Systems 

To address the above challenges, existing AI agent safety systems still exhibit significant gaps across three foundational dimensions: evaluation, data, and evolution. These issues are not isolated from one another. Collectively, they result in safety efforts often remaining in a passive ”find a problem, fix a problem” mode, making it difficult to form a continuous closed loop oriented toward real-world deployment scenarios. 

Limitations of Evaluation Infrastructure Existing security evaluations are still dom-inated by static question-answering, single-round adversarial examples, or fixed task sets. Such approaches are suitable for answering whether a model ”will say something wrong,” but struggle to answer whether an agent ”will do something wrong during continuous ex-ecution.” When risks depend on tool invocation chains, changes in environmental states, and the accumulation of long-horizon decisions, single-turn interactive evaluations can only capture the surface of the iceberg, making it difficult to systematically expose deep failure modes. Even in executable environments, many existing frameworks remain centered around fixed tasks and offline logs, lacking capabilities such as high-concurrency retesting, dynamic 1Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence

Limitations of Data Infrastructure Currently, many evaluation and training frameworks treat data merely as a byproduct of the runtime process, with common practices relying on in-memory caches and temporary local files for storage. This approach struggles to stably support large-scale heterogeneous data management, nor does it facilitate cross-task, cross-version, or cross-environment retrieval, reuse, and auditing. More importantly, agents continuously generate a large volume of trajectories, reward signals, and failure contexts during evaluation, execution, and training. These are essentially high-value data assets, yet existing systems generally lack a unified mechanism for their structured accumulation, automated analysis, and subsequent feedback into model optimization. For further discussion, see Section 2.2. 

Limitations of Evolution infrastructure At the level of model evolution, training, evaluation, and deployment remain largely disconnected from one another. Issues identified during evaluation are difficult to promptly convert into training samples, and real-world feedback generated during execution is also hard to smoothly reintegrate into subsequent optimization workflows. Meanwhile, reinforcement learning and online distillation for agent scenarios are transitioning from standalone algorithm implementations to system-level or-chestration problems. ## 1.3 Safactory: Building a Complete Closed Loop for Trustworthy AI 

To address the above limitations, Safactory proposes an infrastructure framework for trustwor-thy autonomous agents. Centered around three core platforms — a Parallel Execution Middle Platform, a Trustworthy Data Middle Platform, and an Autonomous Evolution Middle Plat-form — it establishes a complete closed loop that integrates risk discovery, data accumulation, and capability repair and reinforcement. Overall, as shown in Figure 1, Safactory does not treat security evaluation as a one-time tool-based process, but rather organizes it as a data and training system that can run continuously, be iteratively optimized, and be repeatedly reused. Among these, the Parallel Execution Middle Platform targets executable sandboxes for Android, computer environments, and embodied scenarios. It supports environment pool preheating, asynchronous scheduling, and concurrent re-evaluation of multiple trajectories, as well as the injection of attacks and perturbations during execution, enabling more systematic discovery of safety risks in long-horizon interactions. The Trustworthy Data Middle Platform is responsible for consolidating the multimodal data, step-level trajectories, and behavioral signals generated during evaluation and training into retrievable, analyzable, and auditable data assets, and performs safety auditing, sample filtering, and experience distillation through an intent-driven data engine. The Autonomous Evolution Middle Platform further integrates these data assets into asynchronous reinforcement learning and online policy optimization workflows, continuously generating trainable samples under version control, trajectory filtering, and group consistency constraints, thereby supporting the simultaneous improvement of model capabilities and safety. Therefore, Safactory’s goal is neither to improve a single evaluation metric in isolation, nor to merely provide point solutions such as a data tool or a training framework. What it aims to establish is a closed-loop pathway of ”discovering risks through execution, consolidating evidence through data, completing repairs through evolution, and verifying effectiveness through re-execution.” Along this path, safety is no longer a one-time check conducted before model deployment, but rather becomes an infrastructure capability throughout the entire lifecycle of an agent.

## 2.1 Agent Evaluation Framework 

As model capabilities and deployment scenarios continue to expand, safety evaluation must evolve toward deeper and more comprehensive paradigms. We therefore define a five-level progression of AI safety evaluation (L1–L5): 3Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence

• L1 — Q&A Evaluation : Static, single-turn benchmarks focusing on alignment, jailbreak robustness, and domain-specific knowledge. 

• L2 — Adversarial Evaluation : Dynamic red teaming via human or automated agents that generate multi-turn attack scenarios. 

• L3 — Process-Level Evaluation : Multi-turn interaction assessment that captures risks emerging task execution. 

• L4 — Sandbox Evaluation : Evaluation in executable environments where real-world consequences of agent actions are observable. 

• L5 — Evolutionary Evaluation : Adaptive evaluation that co-evolves with model capabilities and generalizes across scenarios. 

2.1.1 Q&A - and Adversarial-Level Infrastructure 

Most existing evaluation infrastructure operate at L1, organizing benchmarks around task registries and standardized scoring pipelines. Representative systems include LM-Evaluation-Harness [ 30 ], OpenCompass [ 62 ], and OpenAI Evals [ 61 ]. Despite differences in scale and engineering maturity, they share a common paradigm: evaluation is conducted on fixed, single-instance tasks, inherently limiting coverage. To overcome this limitation, L2 adversarial evaluation introduces dynamic attack generation. Systems such as Giskard Hub [ 31 ], Garak [ 26 ], and promptfoo [ 69 ] expand the test space through continuous red teaming and structured probing. However, their evaluation unit remains single-turn interaction. Both L1 and L2 fundamentally rely on single-turn interactions. In contrast, many critical agent failures emerge over multi-step tool usage and long-horizon execution ,motivating the shift toward process-level evaluation. 4Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence 

2.1.2 Process-Level Infrastructure 

L3 evaluation extends the unit of analysis from single responses to full interaction trajecto-ries. Langfuse [ 47 ] emphasizes observability, enabling real-time monitoring of agent traces. MLflow [ 57 ] integrates intermediate decision steps—such as tool selection and recovery—into lifecycle evaluation. DeepEval [ 17 ] evolves from Q&A-style testing by introducing agentic and conversation-level metrics. Process-level evaluation enables trajectory-based analysis of where and how risks arise. However, its scope largely remains within abstract task execution, lacking direct validation of real-world consequences. 

2.1.3 Sandbox-Level Infrastructure 

Many agent risks—such as unsafe tool usage, improper file operations, and cascading execution errors—cannot be fully captured without executable environments. L4 sandbox evaluation addresses this by placing agents in real environments where outcomes are directly observable. Systems such as Inspect AI [ 84 ], Terminal-Bench [ 55 ], Harbor [ 77 ], and Claw-Eval [ 95 ] advance this paradigm across tool use, terminal tasks, and trajectory auditing. However, such systems generally operate with fixed evaluation tasks as their basic unit, lacking the ability to dynamically perturb the environment state, and store results primarily as logs rather than reusable structured data. Environment support is often domain-specific, and safety is treated as a secondary metric rather than a first-class objective. These limitations hinder scalability and reuse in rapidly evolving model settings. To address these gaps, Safactory introduces three key capabilities: (1) dynamic injection of attacks and perturbations during execution to systematically expose failure modes; (2) structured storage of interaction trajectories and reward signals as reusable data assets for downstream SFT or RL; and (3) service-oriented environment interfaces that decouple evalu-ation from execution, enabling seamless integration of diverse environments (e.g., industrial systems, simulators, embodied settings). Unlike prior L4 systems, The core distinction of Safactory is not whether it supports execution in real environments, but rather its transformation of security evaluation from a one-time tool-based process into a sustainably evolving infrastructure capability. 

## 2.2 Trustworthy Data Services 

As the training scale of AI systems and agentic application scenarios continue to expand, the data service infrastructure that underpins their operation must also evolve from static pipelines toward orchestrable, perception-aware, and self-evolving intelligent systems. We categorize existing work into three stages according to capability progression: 

• Static Data Management : Offline workflow scheduling built around DAGs, ensuring data availability and reproducibility. 

• Data Cleaning Pipelines : Orchestrable pipelines centered on rules, scoring functions, 5Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence and operators, automating collection, cleaning, deduplication, filtering, alignment, and quality verification to reliably produce training-ready data. 

• Intelligent Data Engines : Systems centered on data assets and behavioral signals, transforming production-side feedback into a continuous optimization driver and forming a closed-loop evolutionary cycle. 

2.2.1 Static Data Management: DAG-centric Workflow Scheduling 

Most mainstream data infrastructure revolves around task definition, dependency orches-tration, scheduling, and status monitoring, with DAG-based batch processing at the core. Apache Airflow [ 3] is the de facto standard for scheduling-oriented ETL, providing a DAG-based framework with a rich operator library and cron scheduling. Prefect [ 68 ] advances the paradigm from DAG as configuration to Python as flow , offering dynamic process con-struction and event-driven scheduling. Dagster [ 19 ] places software-defined assets at the core, treating datasets, models, and features as traceable products. All three share the same model: execution is driven by static scheduling and predefined dependencies. The limitation is that DAGs only answer when to run what , not whether the resulting data is worth using . Dirty data, duplicate samples, and low-quality content can pass through unchecked. This gap gave rise to quality-oriented data cleaning pipelines. 

2.2.2 Data Cleaning Pipelines: Scoring-centric Quality Assurance 

Data cleaning pipelines treat data quality as the primary objective, building workflows around operators for cleaning, deduplication, filtering, and scoring. Data-Juicer [ 13 ] provides compos-able operators covering text, image, audio/video, and multimodal data. OpenDataArena [ 9]characterizes instructions and responses along more than ten dimensions, making data value quantifiable. NeMo Curator [ 59 ] uses a GPU-accelerated stack to handle cleaning, deduplication, and PII redaction with dedicated multimodal pipelines. These systems shift from task scheduling to quality-centered content selection. However, processing flows and scoring thresholds are fixed before the pipeline runs. Data risks surface only when task objectives change, domains shift, or compliance boundaries tighten, which is a structural limitation that static pipelines cannot overcome. 

2.2.3 Intelligent Data Engines: Closed-loop Optimization Centered on Data Assets and Behavioral Signals 

Intelligent data engines treat data as continuously accumulable assets and use behavioral signals as the core driver of data production. TensorZero [ 81 ] unifies an LLM gateway, evaluation, and optimization into one system, persisting each inference and feedback while supporting SFT and RLHF based on real-world metrics. Arena Learning [ 53 ] simulates a chatbot arena to automatically generate preference pairs. Agent-in-the-Loop [ 98 ] embeds human annotation into production workflows. Both validate the closed loop of “behavioral signals → data assets → model iteration”. 6Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence These systems, however, remain at the tool execution layer. Users must still describe execution flows via declarative configurations, feedback is only aggregated as training signals rather than used to evolve the toolchain, and data security relies on environment isolation rather than native substrate capabilities. In regulated and multi-party scenarios, this means task expression is locked within engineering teams, closed-loop experience cannot be distilled into reusable capabilities, and privacy constraints render many high-value tasks infeasible. Safactory models data tasks as a complete chain: natural language intent → dynamically generated pipeline → tool invocation → execution → tracking → asset accumulation. For explicit tasks it uses single-planning execution. For ambiguous tasks it enters autonomous closed-loop mode, continuously trying, judging, and correcting around the goal. Stable multi-step combinations can be distilled into new tools, continuously evolving the tool ecosystem. For data security, Safactory supports encrypted packaging for local execution, outputting results without exposing raw data. This transforms data services from static pipeline construction to intent-driven continuous evolution, which is the essential difference between existing intelligent data engines and Safactory. 

## 2.3 Agentic Model Training Services 

With the expansion of large models into new application domains, training is no longer just about optimizing model parameters. It now encompasses organizing the rollout process, feedback modeling, and policy updates within the training pipeline. Traditional monolithic training frameworks, which are designed for static data, are increasingly inadequate to support more complex training processes. Consequently, model training services for agents have gradually evolved into frameworks that emphasize orchestration, decoupling, and scalability. Early reinforcement learning training frameworks for large language models (LLMs) mainly focused on the standard Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) process, as exemplified by systems such as TRL[ 38 ], DeepSpeed-Chat[ 94 ], and OpenRLHF[ 63 ]. These frameworks aimed to organize supervised fine-tuning, reward modeling, and policy optimiza-tion into reproducible training pipelines. The training paradigm in this phase was relatively standardized, typically following the SFT–RM–PPO (Supervised Fine-Tuning, Reward Model-ing, Proximal Policy Optimization) pipeline: first, the model was aligned using demonstration or preference data, then further optimized through reinforcement learning with a reward model and PPO-based methods. This approach closely mirrored earlier representative works such as InstructGPT[ 64 ] and Constitutional AI[ 5]. Thus, the core task during this phase was the engineering and encapsulation of standard components like SFT, RM, and PPO, rather than explicitly addressing multi-round interactive training in more complex environments. As model scale, training costs, and online sampling expenses continued to rise, the focus of training frameworks shifted from simply ”executing RLHF” to ”organizing large-scale post-training systems.” From GPT-3[ 8] and GPT-4[ 60 ] to Qwen2[ 70 ], DeepSeek-V2[ 21 ], and DeepSeek-V3[ 22 ], these works demonstrated that model training is evolving into a multi-stage, systematized engineering process: model improvement no longer solely depends on a single training script, but increasingly relies on reusable data pipelines, clearly defined training phases, and parallelism and scheduling mechanisms that scale stably with both model and 7Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence cluster sizes. At the framework level, verl[ 86 ] has begun to prioritize data flow organization, modular decoupling, and resource scheduling, with a focus that has gradually shifted from supporting a specific optimization method to providing unified system support for complex post-training processes such as PPO and DPO. In recent years, the rapid development of reasoning and agent-based scenarios has further accelerated this evolution. While early RLHF efforts primarily addressed ”how to make models answer better,” recent technical reports have focused more on ”how to enable models to act continuously in complex environments and learn from interactions.” On the one hand, works such as DeepSeek-R1[ 23 ] and Kimi k1.5[ 58 ] show that reinforcement learning has shifted from being a tool for alignment to becoming a key method for enhancing reasoning capabilities. Corresponding optimization methods have moved beyond the classic RLHF-PPO pipeline, adopting new approaches such as GRPO that are better suited to large-scale online sampling and relative comparison signals. On the other hand, reports such as Qwen3[ 71 ], GLM-4.5[ 32 ], Kimi K2[ 45 ], Tongyi DeepResearch[ 83 ], Scaling Agents via Continual Pre-training[ 79 ], GLM-5[ 96 ], and DeepSeek-V3.2[ 24 ] have broadened the training narrative to incorporate elements like thinking modes, agentic capabilities, tool usage, continual pre-training, and the decoupling of generation and training. In other words, the training system must now address not just a single parameter update, but a continuous loop of rollout, data generation, optimization, and evaluation. Against this backdrop, training frameworks are transitioning from execution frameworks focused on a single training cycle to systematic orchestration frameworks designed for complex agent workflows. Works such as slime[ 82 ], AReaL[ 40 , 29 ], and RollArt[ 73 ] explore scalable organizational methods for more complex training workflows, from customizable data generation interfaces and asynchronous training execution to decoupling heterogeneous infrastructures. These frameworks share the characteristic of no longer being restricted to implementing specific optimization algorithms like PPO or GRPO; instead, they focus on system-wide coordination among rollout, reasoning, training, and resource orchestration. Along this path, the core challenge in large model training services has shifted from ”how to complete a single training run” to ”how to organize a sustainable and scalable training system.” Compared to previous frameworks that were mainly designed around the collaboration between rollout and trainer, Safactory’s advantage lies not in introducing new single-point optimization modules, but in explicitly reconfiguring the structural relationships between the operational pipeline, data deposition, and optimization loops through layered orchestration. This makes the system more suited to handling the training requirements of agent-based scenarios, where continuous interaction, generation, and optimization co-exist. 

# 3 Parallel Simulation Platform 

As agents extend toward multi-step, complex tasks, continuous-interaction evaluation in real-world environments faces two core challenges. The first is long runtime and low efficiency. Long decision chains make even minor deviations highly prone to accumulation, so repeated trials are required to ensure confidence. Taking OpenClaw as an example, a single task takes 8Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence about 20 minutes on average. If 100 tasks are each re-evaluated five times, the total serial runtime would be roughly seven days. The second challenge is insufficient assessment of safety capabilities. The current evaluation paradigm, based on single-sample, linear execution, fundamentally conflicts with the exponential growth of multi-turn interactive decision branches in multi-step tasks. In OpenClaw, for instance, an agent may trigger high-risk operations such as accidentally deleting files during task execution. However, single-path testing can cover only a very small number of fixed trajectories, making it easy to create an illusion of safety simply because the model happened to avoid risk in one particular decoding attempt. Since existing evaluations lack mechanisms for traversing decision branches in multi-step tasks, they struggle to systematically expose risk blind spots in complex interactions, resulting in inadequate safety-evaluation coverage and distorted conclusions. To systematically address these pain points, the Parallel Rollout Platform was developed. As a sandbox rollout framework designed for evaluation and reinforcement learning, the platform uses pre-warmed environment pools and asynchronous scheduling mechanisms to advance massive numbers of long-horizon trajectories under high concurrency, effectively alleviating efficiency and cost bottlenecks. At the same time, it natively supports the dynamic injection of red-team attacks, enabling comprehensive and systematic evaluation of agents’ safety and robustness boundaries in complex interactive scenarios. In addition, the platform continuously accumulates reusable data and experience during operation. All step-level trajectories generated through rollouts can be seamlessly fed back into the system, forming a closed data loop of “evaluation–training–re-evaluation.” In this way, the Parallel Rollout Platform fundamentally breaks through the limitations of isolated evaluation and establishes a unified technical foundation for the continuous improvement of agent capabilities and the strengthening of safety defenses.

## 3.2 System Architecture  

> Figure 4 Architecture of Parallel Simulation Platform.

To support multi-step continuous-interaction evaluation and address issues such as long evaluation time and insufficient evaluation coverage, we designed and implemented a parallel rollout platform based on state awareness and elastic scheduling (Figure 4). This platform is designed for the scaled execution of evaluation tasks and multi-path analysis. It centrally manages Agent instances, sandbox environments, large-model requests, and the circulation of evaluation data. It consists of four core modules: a parallel scheduler, a concurrent Agent rollout foundation, an elastic large-model gateway, and data storage and evaluation. To reduce integration costs, the platform provides a dual-track access solution: lightweight Agents can be directly launched through Python source code, while complex Agents can be 10 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence seamlessly mounted through Docker images. Developers only need to implement three basic interfaces—Reset, Start, and Close—to bring custom Agents under the platform’s unified scheduling and carry out large-scale concurrent repeated evaluation and rollout analysis under a standardized evaluation protocol. Focusing on the efficiency bottleneck in repeated evaluation of multi-step tasks, the platform improves evaluation throughput through an end-to-end mechanism of “pre-warming—pooled scheduling—asynchronous persistence.” First, the sandbox manager starts the underlying sandboxes in batches in advance according to the configured pool capacity, completing the pre-warming of the task execution resource pool and reducing cold-start time from the source. Second, based on the ready resource pool, the system relies on ActorPool to concurrently create a large number of Agent instances, and achieves seamless transitions between tasks through asynchronous hot-switching scheduling. This reduces CPU idle time caused by Agent initialization, environment loading, and resource waiting, allowing limited computing resources to be used more for the actual evaluation process. Finally, in the face of request surges to large models caused by repeated evaluation with multiple Agents, the elastic large-model gateway is responsible for unified handling, dynamic scaling, and request forwarding, and transparently writes interaction trajectories into the storage module. With built-in buffering and a “fire-and-forget” asynchronous persistence mechanism, the main evaluation process does not need to block while waiting for trajectory writing. In this way, repeated evaluation of long tasks that was originally advanced serially is transformed into a multi-instance concurrent execution process, significantly reducing overall evaluation time while ensuring that trajectories remain traceable. To address problems in safety evaluation such as insufficient coverage under single-path execution, high costs of repeated restarts, and difficulty reproducing abnormal scenarios, the platform further introduces a “backtracking sandbox” mechanism, building a version management system similar to a Git dedicated to Agents. This mechanism can perceive file changes, context states, and key environment operations made by the Agent during the task process, and automatically generate checkpoints at important nodes. Like game saves, it preserves a complete behavior timeline and an environment “version tree.” During evaluation, when an Agent enters a multi-path decision node, triggers a potentially high-risk operation, or causes environment damage due to incorrect execution, the system does not need to fully restart from the initial state. Instead, it can precisely locate a historical safety anchor point and quickly roll back to the corresponding state, then continue rolling out different action paths as if “pulling a new branch.” As a result, safety evaluation is upgraded from single-sample, linear execution to branch-based exploration around key states. On the one hand, this avoids repeated execution of prerequisite steps, so the large model only consumes incremental tokens for newly explored branches. On the other hand, it enables multi-path repeated evaluation around safety-sensitive nodes such as accidental file deletion, incorrect modification, permission abuse, abnormal recursion, and infinite loops, more systematically exposing risk blind spots in long-horizon interaction and improving the coverage, reproducibility, and reliability of conclusions in safety capability evaluation. 11 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence

## 4.3 Toolset Architecture 

4.3.1 Data Value Assessment Tool 

The data value assessment tool offers an end-to-end pipeline for judging the training value of a dataset and distilling it into a high-quality subset. It comprises two complementary stages: a 

scoring stage that assigns quality scores through pluggable scorers, and a selection stage that turns these scores into a diverse, high-value subset of the requested size. Although designed to work together, the two stages are fully decoupled and can also be invoked independently: users may run scoring alone to obtain multi-dimensional quality signals for their downstream workflows, or run selection alone on top of pre-computed scores. 

Scoring. The scoring stage assigns a quality score to every sample in a dataset. It supports pluggable scoring methods: users can switch between scorers or run multiple scorers on the same dataset to obtain multi-dimensional quality signals. The platform currently integrates 9 scorers that cover complementary quality dimensions [ 10 ]. 

PPL [41 ] and Norm Loss [78 ] measure the target model’s predictability of a training sample from a probabilistic and information-compression perspective: the more natural and fluent a passage appears to the model, the higher the score, whereas a low score indicates that the sample is unnatural to the model. IFD [ 49 ] compares the relative difficulty of generating the same response with and without its instruction, using the gap as a proxy for how much the instruction actually helps: the more it helps, the higher the quality. DEITA Q 

and DEITA C [ 52 ] rate the intrinsic properties of a sample directly: the former focuses on whether the instruction-response pair is clear and accurate, while the latter independently evaluates instruction difficulty and complexity. DeBERTa [37] applies a classifier trained on human-labelled data to score coherence and grammatical accuracy, while FineWeb Edu [ 65 ]focuses on educational value such as structured explanation. Ask LLM [74 ] adopts the most direct approach, simply prompting an off-the-shelf large language model to judge whether the sample is high-quality. Finally, we propose a composite strategy named DataElf as the platform’s default scorer: it converts the IFD and DEITA Q scores into percentile ranks and fuses them with equal weight, yielding a single score that jointly reflects instruction-response clarity, accuracy, and reasoning difficulty. On the engineering side, every scorer runs independently per sample and can fully exploit GPU parallelism; each scorer’s output is automatically cached, so repeated runs or downstream score fusion on the same dataset can reuse the cache without recomputation. This makes 26 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence large-scale quality assessment efficient and keeps the cost of extending or combining scorers low. 

Selection. Given the quality scores produced above, the selection stage draws a target number of high-value samples from the dataset. Naively taking the top-K samples by score tends to produce a homogeneous subset that lacks diversity in topic and difficulty. To mitigate this, the tool first encodes each sample into a semantic vector with an embedding model ( Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct ), applies K-means clustering, allocates the overall selection quota across clusters in proportion to their sizes, and finally picks the highest-scoring samples within each cluster. This simple yet effective strategy preserves data diversity while keeping the selected subset locally high-quality within every cluster.

4.3.3 Data Security Audit Tool 

The data security audit tool is designed to detect security risks across multiple dimensions in post-training datasets, helping practitioners identify and mitigate harmful content, privacy leakage, and adversarial attacks before data enters the training pipeline. Built around a pluggable checker architecture, the tool ships with 23 checkers spanning 13 risk types: harmful 28 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence content, toxicity, bias, PII leakage, secret leakage, label flipping, factual inconsistency, self-contradiction, instruction mismatch, backdoor injection, prompt injection, jailbreak prompts, and sycophancy. Checkers can be freely composed through configuration files to suit different auditing needs. The 23 checkers are grouped into four categories based on their detection approach. 

Rule-based checkers perform deterministic matching via regular expressions and keyword lists, running offline without any model. PIIRule detects phone numbers, emails, ID numbers, and bank card numbers using the Luhn algorithm and Chinese ID checksum validation. SecretRule covers API key and token formats for AWS, GitHub, OpenAI, JWT, and other platforms. ToxicityKeywordRule and BiasKeywordRule match bias expressions against the HurtLex [7] multilingual lexicon. 

LLM-as-a-Judge checkers use large language models for semantic reasoning. Prompt designs follow the DeepEval [ 17 ] evaluation framework, covering harmful content, toxicity, bias, PII, jailbreak, prompt injection, contradiction, instruction mismatch, factual consistency, sycophancy, and DPO label flipping. SycophancyLLMJudge adopts the sycophancy detection strategy from Elephant [ 14 ], and DPOLabelFlipLLMJudge references the preference label quality audit method from Dingo [18]. 

Model-based checkers use task-specific trained models for classification. BiasClassifier 

uses a ModernBERT-finetuned bias classifier [ 20 ]. ToxicityClassifier performs multi-label toxicity classification based on Detoxify [ 35 ]. HarmfulContentClassifier is based on LLaMA Guard [ 39 ]. PIINERDetector integrates the Microsoft Presidio [ 56 ] NER pipeline to extract structured privacy entities. JailbreakClassifier uses WildGuard [ 34 ] to detect jailbreak prompts and refusal behavior. PromptInjectionClassifier uses PIGuard [ 48 ]to identify prompt injection attacks. 

Heuristic checkers consist of GraCeFulBackdoorDefender , which implements the gradient-based analysis method from GraCeFul [ 88 ] to detect backdoor-poisoned samples through statistical clustering across the full dataset. Unlike the preceding three categories, this checker must process the entire dataset in batch mode to extract dataset-level statistical patterns. Each risk type carries a configurable weight, and the final safety score is derived from a weighted penalty formula, where higher scores indicate cleaner datasets. All non-heuristic checkers operate independently at the sample level and can be parallelized across multiple threads. Heuristic checkers execute sequentially in batch mode once the parallel phase finishes. After auditing, the tool generates a Markdown report covering risk distribution statistics and per-sample details. The trajectory analysis tool focuses on experience distillation from the agent’s step-level interaction logs. ## 5.2 Agentic RL System Architecture  

> Figure 18 Agentic RL System Architecture

The Agentic RL system architecture, as shown in Figure 18, is designed around three core modules: Bridge, Trajectory Transformation, and Buffer Server. These modules jointly support a fully asynchronous ”Sampling-Trajectory Transformation-Training” pipeline. The architecture separates the rollout and training processes and uses versioned metadata and group consistency mechanisms to ensure that the system can reliably generate high-quality training samples under complex, long-duration, multi-environment concurrent tasks. 

5.2.1 Bridge Module: Asynchronous Control and Unified Inference Entry 

In traditional reinforcement learning architectures, interactions with the environment, infer-ence requests, and training consumption are often tightly coupled, limiting system scalability and flexibility. In a fully asynchronous setup, issues such as policy version misalignment with sampled data, missing sample groups, trajectory and supervision signal misalignment, and imbalanced production-consumption in data buffers can arise. To address this, the system in-troduces the LLM Proxy and Rollout Manager at the Bridge layer, forming a closed loop with the Buffer Server: The LLM Proxy writes and returns trajectory and model version metadata in the request path, enabling the training endpoint to perform data staleness filtering and mitigate policy lag. The Rollout Manager samples by instance/group and submits in groups to prevent incomplete sample groups from entering training. The Bridge unifies the routing 33 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence of LLM sessions and links trajectory recording and transformation processes, reducing the chances of trajectory and supervision signal mismatch. The Buffer Server dequeues data as needed, balancing production and consumption rates to prevent queue buildup and starvation. As a result, Rollout and Train are decoupled temporally, while maintaining consistency in data semantics and quality constraints. At the same time, the Bridge supports the use of the OPD (Online Policy Distillation) module, which distills policies from the teacher model to real-time optimize the student model’s learning process, improving its generalization and training efficiency. This module works in collaboration with submodules for teacher model policy generation and other training signals to implement policy distillation. 

5.2.2 Alignment Module: Semantic Alignment from Trajectories to Trainable Samples 

In the Agentic RL system, trajectory data generated by the environment cannot be directly used for training. It needs to go through a series of processing and transform steps to become suitable training samples. The Trajectory Transformation module is designed to address this issue. Through a fine-grained processing pipeline, it converts raw trajectories generated from multi-round interactions into samples that meet training requirements. The design of the Trajectory Transformation module focuses on ensuring data semantic consis-tency and correctness. Through incremental encoding and prefix matching of trajectories, the system effectively avoids redundant encoding of historical context while handling multimodal inputs (e.g., image and text data). Additionally, the system ensures that losses are only computed within the areas generated by the model through Mask Painter and Sample Packer, ensuring effective control of data quality during the training process. Ultimately, the samples are packed and annotated with metadata to maintain consistency in batch processing and prevent training bias due to data issues. 

5.2.3 Summary 

The Agentic RL system achieves asynchronous orchestration through the Bridge module, maintains data lifecycle consistency, ensures semantic correctness of data through the Trajec-tory Transformation module, and guarantees data supply through the Buffer Server. Together, these modules enable the system to maintain the usability, timeliness, and statistical stability of training data under fully asynchronous conditions, successfully supporting unified training for various task types. 

## 5.3 On-policy Distillation 

In the unified architecture above, OPD (On-policy Distillation) is implemented as an optional training route rather than a prerequisite for the system’s establishment. Its primary role is to provide additional distribution constraints and teacher signal correction for current policy updates under asynchronous rollouts and long-range agent interactions, thereby enhancing the consistency, stability, and controllability of the training process. Recent reviews of 34 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence OPD also highlight that its core idea is to distill from the student’s own accessed state and trajectory distributions, rather than only performing offline imitation from static teacher data. Accordingly, OPD is particularly suited for training scenarios with more apparent asynchronous sampling, policy delays, and distribution shifts. [91, 93] From a system implementation perspective, OPD integrates into the existing five-layer architecture as a plugin, without altering the main execution flow of Safactory. The teacher bridge , teacher model ref , and OPD signal/granularity hook in the figure should be understood as optional extension points: The teacher bridge in the proxy layer integrates teacher information during request or response phases; the hook in the rollout engine aligns distillation signals with the raw trajectories, and the teacher model ref at the bottom provides an optional teacher strategy reference. In other words, OPD does not define the core structure of the system but rather adds an additional supervisory interface on top of the unified execution base. In the current implementation, Safactory adopts a white-box, token-level form of OPD. Specifically, the system introduces token-level log probabilities provided by the teacher during the rollout phase and incorporates a reverse-KL regularization term into the training objective to impose additional constraints on the advantage-driven policy updates. From an architectural perspective, Safactory’s interface can further accommodate other forms of teacher signals, such as step-level, trajectory-level, or higher granularity feedback. This allows Safactory to maintain the system’s simplicity by using the same training base for both Gym-style and agentic scenarios, while also reserving sufficient space for more complex distillation, correction, and auxiliary supervision methods. [91] OPD can be completely disabled in Safactory; however, for agentic tasks with more pronounced issues of asynchronous sampling, long-range decision making, multi-round interactions, and complex credit assignment, OPD can enhance the stability and controllability of the training process without disrupting the main execution flow. This enables Safactory to support both conventional reinforcement learning processes and naturally extend to more complex autonomous evolution training paradigms, all while maintaining a unified system abstraction.

5.4.1 Safactory V.S. slime-native 

The Safactory system supports both synchronous and asynchronous rollout execution modes. In terms of architectural design, Safactory explicitly decouples the rollout and training lifecycles: the data producer continuously generates trajectory data and writes it to storage, while the training process is triggered by the Bridge module once a sufficient amount of trainable trajectories has been accumulated. This asynchronous mechanism effectively prevents the ”producer” from idling its computational resources, thereby improving overall resource utilization. In terms of data management, Safactory introduces a ”trajectory staleness” mechanism to filter samples in the buffer. Staleness is defined as the difference between the policy version used for trajectory generation and the current policy version. For example, when the 35 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence acceptable staleness is set to 0, only trajectories generated by the current policy can be used for training; when the acceptable staleness is set to 1, trajectories generated by both the current and previous versions of the policy are allowed. This mechanism ensures training stability while allowing developers to customize the combination of off-policy and on-policy data, thereby increasing data reuse efficiency.

# 7 Conclusions and Discussions 

Safactory is motivated by a simple observation: once large models become autonomous agents, safety can no longer be treated as a single benchmark score or a one-time release check. Many important failures emerge only when agents interact with tools, environments, and long decision chains. As a result, trustworthy agent development requires more than isolated evaluation tools or standalone training frameworks. It requires an infrastructure that can continuously discover risks, preserve evidence, convert execution traces into reusable assets, and feed them back into model improvement. In this report, we present Safactory as such an infrastructure. Its Parallel Simulation 

38 Safactory: A Scalable Agentic Infrastructure for Training Trustworthy Autonomous Intelligence 

Platform moves safety evaluation from static samples to large-scale sandbox-based rollout and risk discovery. Its Trustworthy Data Platform turns trajectories, rewards, and intermediate logs into structured, auditable, and retrievable data assets, while supporting safety auditing and experience extraction. Its Autonomous Evolution Platform further connects these assets to asynchronous reinforcement learning and online policy optimization, making it possible to organize rollout, data transformation, and policy update in a unified loop. Supported by DeepLink computing technologies, Safactory also provides the computing, storage, and system foundations needed to run this loop at scale. From this perspective, the main contribution of Safactory is not a single algorithm or benchmark. Rather, it is a system view of trustworthy autonomous intelligence: risk discovery, data deposition, capability repair, and re-validation should be designed as one continuous process . This design helps bridge the long-standing gap between evaluation, data, and training, and provides a practical foundation for building safer and more capable agents in OS, mobile, embodied, and other interactive environments. At the same time, Safactory should be viewed as a starting point rather than a finished solution. Several challenges remain open. First, environment coverage and scenario realism still need to expand, especially for high-stakes domains with richer human, tool, and system interactions. Second, automatically judging agent safety and utility in long-horizon tasks remains difficult, and stronger metrics and oversight mechanisms are still needed.
