# ABSTRACT 

Consumers are increasingly delegating purchase decisions to AI agents, providing natural-language descriptions of their preferences and identity. We argue that these representations constitute an information channel, role coherence, through which sellers can infer willingness to pay without explicit disclosure by the buyer agent, leading to preference leakage. In an experiment where a language-model buyer agent shops on behalf of a verbal consumer profile, we show that seller-side inference from dialogue alone recovers willingness to pay nearly one-for-one. Comparing this set-ting to a numeric-budget condition with confidentiality instructions cleanly isolates role coherence as distinct from instruction-following failure. Because this leakage arises from delegation itself, it cannot be mitigated at the prompt level. Instead, we propose architectural interventions that trade off personalization against preference privacy. 

Keywords AI Agents · Agentic Commerce · Conversational Agents · AI Consumer Safety · Consumer Privacy 

# 1 Introduction 

A rapidly growing share of consumer purchasing is being intermediated by AI agents that search, compare, and transact on the consumer’s behalf. Frameworks for agent-mediated payments, protocols for agent-to-agent communication, and consumer-facing shopping agents from the major model providers have all recently emerged (Staufer et al., 2026; Bansal et al., 2025). A consumer who wants wireless headphones no longer has to type a search query, scroll a results page, compare feature lists across vendors, read reviews, and click through to checkout. A consumer can now ask a chatbot, or an embedded shopping agent inside a payments protocol or a retailer’s app, to execute those steps. The consumer purchase journey, traditionally understood as a sequence of stages running from awareness through consideration, evaluation, purchase, and post-purchase, is increasingly internalized inside a language model that operates between the consumer and the seller. For an AI agent to be useful at this task, the consumer must describe something about themselves. A bare instruction (“buy me headphones”) does not give the agent enough context to shop intelligently. A helpful delegation looks more like “I’m a graduate student, I need headphones that will hold up on the bus and in the library, and I’d rather not spend too much,” or “I travel for client work, I want strong noise cancellation for long flights, and I care about reliability more than novelty.” The richer the description, the more the agent can tailor its search, filter products, prioritize features, react to prices, and make a decision that a diligent human shopper would have made in the consumer’s place. Personalization, in other words, requires a persona. The persona is the substrate that turns the agent from a blind executor into a representative. This substrate is also an object of commercial interest to the seller. A seller’s central unknown about any given consumer is that consumer’s maximum willingness to pay (WTP) for the product, because WTP is the highest price at

The long marketing and industrial-organization literatures on personalized pricing, price discrimination, and consumer privacy are organized around this unknown (Bergemann et al., 2015; Dub´ e and Misra, 2023; Shiller, 2020; Acquisti and Varian, 2005). A seller with a point estimate of WTP can post a personalized price. A seller with an ordered partition of consumers by WTP can implement third-degree price discrimination. A seller with nothing has to price to the population distribution and leave rents with inframarginal buyers. In traditional retail settings, the consumer’s WTP is protected by the absence of any data-generating process that reveals it. Browsing histories, demographic attributes, and prior purchases carry some information about WTP but never the WTP itself. In an agent-mediated setting, however, a new data-generating process arises. The buyer agent produces dialogue with the seller, and the dialogue is a realization of a conditional distribution that depends on the consumer description the agent was given. Whether that dialogue carries recoverable information about the consumer’s WTP is an empirical question, and it is the question this paper asks. We refer to any such unintended disclosure as a preference leakage , meaning a channel through which private consumer valuation information reaches the seller without either party taking an explicit disclosure action. Two qualitatively different instruction forms can carry the delegation, and they define the contrast we study in this paper. The verbal form supplies a consumer description and no dollar amount. The numeric form supplies a dollar budget and a directive to keep it confidential (Zhu et al., 2025). Both are plausible ways a consumer might hand off a shopping task in practice. We design an experiment to measure preference leakage in each of these two instruction forms. A large language model buyer agent, instructed in one of the two forms above, shops for wireless headphones with a seller-side agent across up to four conversational turns. Target WTP is imprinted at six levels ($50, $100, $150, $200, $300, $500). In the verbal condition, each target is operationalized as a distinct consumer profile that evokes the target tier without stating it. In the numeric condition, each target is the literal budget figure in the system prompt. We show an example of each of the forms below.

A transcript of the dialogue between the buyer and seller agents is then produced. A separate inference agent then reads the resulting shopping transcript, with no access to the product catalog and no access to any label, and reports a point estimate of the buyer’s maximum willingness to pay. We compare the inference agent’s estimates to the target at matched cells and ask, for each instruction form, how strongly the estimate tracks the target. We run N = 60 trials per cell, 720 interactions in total. Removing the persona cues from the transcript has no effect on the inference slope. The signal travels through how the buyer shops, not through who the buyer is said to be. We call the underlying mechanism role coherence . When a language model is asked to represent a specific consumer character grounded in identifiable circumstances, occupation, life stage, and stated preferences, it generates dialogue, questions, and evaluative judgments coherent with that character. for the audiophile profile and inappropriate for the graduate-student profile. Deliberating over a $40 gap between two tiers is appropriate for a tight-budget shopper and not for a shopper with substantial discretionary income. The character description is the principal, in the principal-agent sense, and the agent’s behavior reflects the principal’s situation because being faithful to the principal is what the agent was asked to do. A description of who the consumer is becomes, in the agent’s hands, a generator of behavior that expresses who the consumer is. Role coherence is therefore a source of preference leakage, in the sense we defined above, because the behavior it generates is correlated with the consumer’s willingness to pay even when no numeric budget was ever in the prompt and no privacy instruction was ever violated. The central observation of this paper is that role-coherent leakage is not a privacy failure. It is not an instruction being violated or a guardrail being bypassed. There is no number the consumer asked the agent to conceal. There is a description the consumer supplied so that the agent could help, and the agent is helping. The information that reaches the seller is the information the consumer asked the agent to express. Three implications follow. First, the leakage is a feature of helpful delegation rather than a bug in instruction following. The more faithfully the agent executes its role, the more its shopping behavior reveals. Second, prompt-level privacy mitigations cannot close the channel. A privacy directive asks the agent not to produce a particular output, but role coherence is a distributional property of the agent’s behavior, not a particular output. We show this directly. Removing every persona-identifying phrase from the transcript after the fact still leaves the inference slope at 0.93, essentially un-changed from the main condition. Third, because the information that leaks is the information the consumer supplied, effective defenses must operate on the architecture of delegation itself. We discuss three candidates (anonymizing intermediary, profile rotation, and federated aggregation) in Section 8, where they all trade some personalization for some preference privacy, and none operates at the prompt level. This paper makes two contributions, each of which corresponds to a block of the empirical analysis. First, we identify and characterize the role-coherence channel itself. The verbal-profile condition shows that a buyer agent given only a natural-language consumer description produces shopping behavior whose distribution is rank-perfectly informative about the consumer’s willingness to pay. Second, we isolate role coherence from the instruction-following failure studied in the recent agent-negotiation and decision making literature (Zhu et al., 2025; Cherep et al., 2025; Allouah et al., 2026) by running a numeric-budget contrast and then comparing it directly to the verbal-profile condition. The comparison establishes that role coherence and instruction failure are distinct channels. Role coherence is a property of the agent carrying a consumer description through the purchase journey. Instruction failure is a property of the agent’s ability to withhold a specific token under a directive. A full account of agent-mediated commerce will need to include both. The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 discusses the relevant literature. Section 3 develops the role-coherence framework and derives the main predictions. Section 4 describes the experimental design. Section 5 presents results from the verbal-profile experiment and two robustness checks. Section 6 reports the numeric private-budget results, and Section 7 compares the two conditions. We conclude in Section 8.

This stream of literature has largely focused on agentic decision-making settings in which agents are conditioned on explicit structured constraints, such as budgets or other numerical or symbolic limits, and are evaluated on their ability to adhere to these constraints under potentially sensitive or private inputs. Our paper differs in that we study a setting in which the consumer is described to the agent through a verbal persona without explicit numeric constraints, a setting closer to how users may delegate open-ended shopping tasks in practice. In this regime, the mechanism we identify operates through the agent’s interpretation and faithful execution of the persona rather than compliance with explicit quantitative constraints. 

2.2 Personalized pricing and consumer privacy 

A long literature studies the sources of WTP information that sellers use to personalize prices, and the consumer-side strategies that limit those sources. Acquisti and Varian (2005) model behavior-based pricing with endogenous consumer responses, including the use of anonymization to avoid tracking and protect privacy. Dub´ e and Misra (2023) estimate empirically that ML-driven personalized pricing in real markets produces substantial profit increases for sellers and ambiguous distributional effects for consumers. Shiller (2020) demonstrates that browsing histories alone are highly effective for approximating reservation prices and enabling personalized pricing. Our paper differs because we identify role-coherent agent behavior as a new source of WTP information, distinct from cookies, demographic targeting, or browsing history, that arises from the agent’s faithful execution of the consumer’s persona during the purchase journey rather than from any consumer-side data trail. 

2.3 Language model personas 

The cognitive-science and engineering literatures on language model personas are directly relevant to our mechanism. Andreas (2022) argues that language models trained on human-generated text learn to model the latent agents that produced that text, and that prompting can be understood as conditioning the model on a particular agent. Shanahan et al. (2023) develops the related view that a language model in conversation is best understood as role-playing an agent specified by the prompt, and argues that this role-play is the source of much of the model’s apparent goal-directed behavior. Our paper contributes to this literature by providing an empirical test of whether the role-play produces behavioral signatures distinctive enough to be recovered by a second language model, and apply the construct to preference inference in a commercial setting rather than to the philosophical question of model cognition. 2

2.4 Methodology of agent simulation studies 

A separate methodological literature addresses what can be inferred from experiments in which the experimental subjects are language models rather than humans. Gui and Toubia (2023) document an identification problem in LLM-simulated economic experiments arising from the lack of a clean separation between treatment and context generation. Wang et al. (2026) develop a framework for using LLM-generated responses to augment and calibrate human behavioral data for market research applications. Our paper differs in that, rather than using language models as simulators or augmenters of human behavior, we study language model agents as the object of analysis in their own right, reflecting their increasingly deployed role in economic environments.

A language model conditioned on a numeric instruction faces an instruction-following task with two constraints. It must shop for headphones, and it must not produce the dollar amount it was given. This generates three testable predictions. First, for verbal-profile agents with no numeric budget in the prompt, the inference agent’s estimate should scale with the target willingness to pay across the range, and the slope should be close to one. Second, for numeric-budget agents instructed to keep their budget confidential, the inference agent’s estimate of the true willingness to pay should be approximately constant across the willingness-to-pay range, and the slope of the estimated-versus-true relationship should be close to zero. Third, comparing the two conditions on the same resolution, the slope and the cell-level rank correlation should be materially higher in the verbal condition than in the numeric condition, even if the aggregate mean absolute errors are comparable. The third prediction is the one that distinguishes role coherence from prompt faithfulness as mechanisms. If both conditions produced rank-correct inference, the result would be consistent with several mechanisms that we cannot separate. If neither condition did, we would conclude that there is no leakage channel in a clean experimental design. Only the specific asymmetry in which the verbal condition succeeds where the numeric condition fails identifies role coherence as the mechanism. Our results establish this asymmetry, and the robustness checks confirm that it does not depend on the specific language we chose for the verbal profiles or on the inference agent’s ability to recognize the buyer’s persona from the transcript.

4.4 Infrastructure decisions 

Our experimental infrastructure implements three decisions that are important to consider since each of them affects what the inference agent can learn from a transcript. 4.5 Profile and prompt variance design 

The single-profile, single-prompt structure described above is sufficient to identify the role-coherence channel against the numeric-budget contrast, but it does not bound the variance contributed by the specific profile descriptions and the specific prompt phrasing we wrote. A natural question is whether the results we report reflect the role-coherence mechanism in general or the particular text we supplied. To answer this question, we run a partial-factorial extension of the verbal-profile condition. For each of the six WTP cells, we write three alternative profile variants that target the same WTP tier through different occupational, household, and lifestyle specifics, and we write a second buyer-side system-prompt scaffold that paraphrases the original instruction (same content, different wording and ordering). Variant 1 and scaffold A are the original profile and prompt used in the main experiment. Variants 2–3 and scaffold B are new. We run N = 15 trials per (variant, scaffold) combination for each cell, which adds 5 × 6 × 15 = 450 

interactions to the verbal-profile design beyond what the main experiment already covers. The factorial enables three reports that the single-profile design cannot. First, the slope of cell-mean inferred WTP on target WTP, computed by collapsing across variants and scaffolds, with a bootstrap CI that resamples profile variants and scaffolds with replacement so the interval reflects cross-profile and cross-prompt variance, not just within-trial sampling noise. Second, the slope computed separately under each scaffold (scaffold A versus scaffold B), which isolates whether the inference is sensitive to the specific instruction wording. Third, the cell-level standard deviation of mean inferences across the (variant, scaffold) combinations within each cell, which decomposes the design-induced contribution to the slope variance. A natural concern about any finding obtained from a single frontier language model is that the finding could be idiosyncratic to that model. We address this concern not by cross-model replication but by anchoring our prediction in the latent agent modeling account of Andreas (2022) and the role-play characterization of Shanahan et al. (2023), both of which are explicit that the phenomenon we are describing is a general property of dialogue-trained language models rather than a quirk of a particular architecture. Under the Andreas (2022) account, a language model trained on human-generated text learns to represent the latent agents that produced that text, and prompting conditions the model on a particular agent. Under the Shanahan account, a dialogue agent is best understood as role-playing the agent specified by its prompt, and the role-play produces behavioral signatures that are recognizably distinctive of the character. Neither account depends on model or on any feature of the training pipeline. If the accounts are correct, and they are the leading theoretical positions on how language models represent prompted agents, then role-coherent behavior is a property of the class of models trained on dialogue data, not a property of any single member of the class. Cross-model replication would therefore be a natural and informative extension, but the core mechanism does not depend on it.

5.4 Robustness II. Persona-redacted inference 

The stripped-vocabulary condition in Section 5.3 removes financial vocabulary from the profile description that the buyer agent receives, but the buyer agent can still reveal its assigned persona through the conversation itself. Agraduate-student buyer may open with “I’m a grad student looking for headphones for studying in the library”; a consultant buyer may mention “long international flights and client calls”; an audiophile buyer may reference “my home listening setup.” A natural critique, distinct from the lexical-leakage concern, is that the inference agent recovers willingness to pay not from shopping behavior (feature questions, price reactions, product comparisons, decision patterns) but from demographic stereotyping . The inference agent recognizes that the buyer is a graduate student and maps “graduate student” to low willingness to pay via world knowledge, without analyzing how the buyer actually shops. Under this reading, role coherence is not a behavioral phenomenon but a labeling artifact in which the buyer reveals who it is, and the inference agent applies a stereotype. To test this alternative explanation, we constructed a persona-redacted condition. We took the 360 full-transcript verbal-profile interactions from Section 5.1 and passed each transcript through a redaction agent (a separate Claude Haiku instance) instructed to remove all persona-revealing content while preserving shopping behavior. The redac-tor was instructed to strip five categories of identity cues: (1) occupation, job title, career, and education level; (2) lifestyle context and use-case descriptions that reveal who the person is; (3) mentions of possessions, hobbies, or existing equipment; (4) demographic and life-stage markers; and (5) financial-situation language. The redactor was instructed to preserve all product names, prices, feature discussions, reactions to prices, product comparisons, ne-gotiation exchanges, and purchase decisions. When a persona-revealing phrase appeared mid-sentence, the redactor reconstructed the sentence minimally with a [PERSONA REDACTED] placeholder. The seller’s responses were also redacted where they explicitly referenced the buyer’s persona. Table 10 shows the buyer agent’s first-turn opening line in a representative trial for each of the six cells. What remains in each redacted transcript is the shopping behavior, including what products the buyer asked about, what features they prioritized, how they reacted to prices, what comparisons they made, and what they decided. The inference agent then estimated willingness to pay from the persona-redacted transcripts using the same prompt and procedure as in Section 5.1. If inference operates through persona recognition rather than behavioral analysis, the slope should collapse once persona cues are removed. If role coherence is genuinely behavioral, the slope should survive. Table 11 reports the results. # 6 Numeric Private Budget Contrast Analysis 

This section presents a contrast condition in which the buyer agent receives a literal dollar figure as its instruction and is told to keep that figure confidential. The contrast is informative because it isolates the role-coherence channel from an instruction-following channel that has received attention in the recent literature (Zhu et al., 2025; Cherep et al., 2025; Allouah et al., 2026). The question we ask here is not whether a buyer agent can honor a privacy directive over a numeric budget, as prior literature suggests that it often cannot, but whether, under our experimental infrastructure, the inference channel that operates in the numeric paradigm is the same channel as the one operating in the verbal paradigm. We provide the full-transcript inference results in the numeric condition in Table 12. The mean estimated WTP varies only narrowly across the six target cells, from $111 at the $50 target to $222 at the $500 target. The $50 buyer is estimated at $111, higher than the true value; the $500 buyer is estimated at $222, less than half of the true value. Across the 10x range in true WTP from $50 to $500, the mean estimate moves only 2.0x. The Spearman rank correlation between true WTP and mean estimated WTP is 1.00. The slope of the estimated versus true relationship is 0.21 with bootstrap 95% CI [0 .17 , 0.26] . The within-25% accuracy of 36% is concentrated almost entirely in the $150 and $200 cells, where the prior happens to coincide with the target (34/60 and 52/60 respectively). At the $50 target the within-25% accuracy is 3/60, and at the $500 target it is 3/60. This is the signature of compression to the mean rather than of behavioral inference. The redacted-inference results in the numeric condition (Table 13) are much weaker. With dollar amounts stripped from the transcript, the inference agent has nothing to anchor on and produces estimates that are uniformly elevated around $390 regardless of the true WTP. Aggregate MAE rises to $183 and within-25% drops to 15%. The Spearman rank correlation weakens to 0.94. # 7 Comparing Verbal Profile and Numeric Private Conditions 

The decisive comparison is the slope of the inferred WTP on the target WTP, which measures whether the inference agent is actually recovering the target rather than producing a prior-dominated constant. On the full-inference data, the numeric condition has a slope of 0.21 and the verbal condition has a slope of 1.00, nearly a fivefold difference. The verbal condition is an unbiased estimator of target WTP in the level sense as well as in the rank sense. The numeric condition is not. Figure 3 displays the comparison visually, and Table 14 presents the key quantities with bootstrap confidence intervals. We highlight two notable patterns in Table 14. First, both conditions achieve Spearman rank correlation of 1.00 on full inference, so the rank-order criterion alone does not distinguish them. A flat estimator producing mildly increasing means on an ordered set of six targets will register Spearman ρ = 1 

while producing no useful inference at all, which is exactly what the numeric condition does. The slope of 0.21 means the inferred WTP moves by about a fifth of the movement in the target, while the slope of 1.00 in the verbal condition means the inferred WTP moves essentially one-for-one with the target. Second, the redacted condition produces a surprising inversion. Numeric redacted has higher within-25% accuracy (15%) than verbal redacted (2%), and the numeric condition’s aggregate MAE is lower ($183 versus $331). This is not evidence that redacted numeric inference recovers the target. It is a consequence of compression to the mean in the numeric condition overlapping by coincidence with the upper half of the target range, while the verbal condition’s rank-preserving but level-uncalibrated estimates overshoot the upper half. Rank ordering is the criterion on which the verbal condition remains informative in both the full and the redacted comparison, while the numeric condition is partly informative under full inference and unreliable under redaction. The two paradigms identify genuinely different leakage channels, and the asymmetry in their slopes is consistent with the predictions of Section 3 in which under a numeric instruction with a privacy directive, the agent treats the budget as a downstream filter on output and produces approximately invariant behavior, while under a verbal instruction, the agent treats the description as a generator of behavior and produces distinctive transcripts. 

# 8 Conclusion 

This paper studies what reaches the seller when a consumer delegates a shopping task to an AI agent. For the agent to be useful, the consumer must describe something about themselves, whether a persona, a use case, a set of constraints, or a stated preference, that the agent will carry through the purchase journey. Our central finding is that this description is itself an information channel. A buyer agent given a verbal consumer profile with no dollar amount in the prompt produces shopping behavior whose statistical signature is informative about the consumer’s willingness to pay. At 

N = 60 trials per cell, a seller-side inference agent reading only the transcript recovers the target nearly one-for-one, with an OLS slope of 1.00 (bootstrap 95% CI [0 .96 , 1.05] ) and rank correlation 1.00. We call this mechanism role coherence. The role-coherence channel is stable under a stripped-vocabulary replication that removes all financial language from the profile (slope 0.85, statistically distinguishable from both the main verbal condition and the numeric baseline) and survives a persona-redacted condition in which all identity cues are removed from the transcript while shopping behavior is preserved (slope 0.93). A factorial extension that crosses three profile variants per cell with two prompt scaffolds gives a design-aware bootstrap CI of [0 .93 , 1.09] around an overall slope of 1.00, confirming that the result is not driven by the specific profile descriptions or prompt phrasing we wrote. The persona-redacted result identifies the signal as genuinely behavioral rather than a demographic-stereotyping artifact. Our findings have implications for platforms, sellers, and policymakers. For platforms building consumer-facing shop-ping agents, the practical implication is that prompt-level privacy mitigations cannot close the role-coherence channel, because the channel is a byproduct of helpful personalization rather than a violation of a privacy directive. Effective defenses must operate on the architecture of delegation itself, through anonymizing intermediaries that reformulate a verbal profile into a generic query, through profile rotation that varies the presented persona across sessions, or through federated aggregation that denies any single seller the ability to link a transcript to a principal. For sellers, the role-coherent inference provides a new source of WTP information distinct from cookies, demographic targeting, or browsing history. For policymakers, the channel we identify is not reachable by regulations that target disclosed data fields, because no such field exists. The information travels through behavioral distributions rather than through anything the consumer is asked or told to disclose. In Section 1, we argued that prompt-level privacy mitigations cannot close the role-coherence channel and that defenses must move to the architectural level. We propose three potential pathways. The first is an anonymizing intermediary that reformulates a verbal profile into a generic query before it reaches the seller-facing agent. The second is profile rotation that varies the presented persona across sessions to break single-session rank-ordering. The third is federated buyer-side aggregation that denies any single seller the ability to link a transcript to a principal. Our empirical results make the design constraint on each of these concrete. The persona-redacted result (slope 0.93 under full persona re-moval from the transcript) is the strongest evidence that defenses operating on the agent’s output alone are insufficient. Even removing every identifying phrase after the fact leaves the behavioral signature untouched. A workable defense must therefore operate on the agent’s input (anonymizing intermediary), on the distribution from which its persona is drawn (profile rotation), or on the observability of its output at the seller side (federated aggregation). Each of these trades some amount of personalization, or some amount of within-consumer consistency, for some amount of preference privacy.
