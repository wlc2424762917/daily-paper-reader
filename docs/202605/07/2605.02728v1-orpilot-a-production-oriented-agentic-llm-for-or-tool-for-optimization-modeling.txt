# ORPilot: A Production-Oriented Agentic LLM-for-OR Tool for Optimization Modeling 

# Guangrui Xie ∗

# May 5, 2026 

Abstract 

This paper presents ORPilot, an open-source agentic AI system that translates real-world natural-language busi-ness problems into working and solver-ready optimization models. Unlike prior LLM-for-OR tools created in a pure academic setting, which assume a clean and complete problem specification with pre-formatted data embedded in problem text, ORPilot is designed from the ground up for production conditions: ambiguous problem descriptions, large-scale raw operational data, and the need for reproducibility and portability across different solver backends. The system achieves this through four novel components rarely present in existing work: (1) a conversational interview agent that elicits a complete problem specification before any modeling efforts begin, (2) a data collection agent that collects data independently instead of embedding data into prompts, (3) a parameter computation agent that bridges the gap between raw tabular data and model-ready parameters, and (4) a solver-agnostic Intermediate Representation (IR) that enables deterministic, zero-LLM-call recompilation to different solver backends (Gurobi, CPLEX, PuLP, Pyomo, or OR-Tools). Moreover, self-correcting retry loops are introduced to feed solver errors and tracebacks back to the LLMs for targeted repairs. To my knowledge, ORPilot is the first-ever attempt for creating a LLM-for-OR tool that is targeted against production-level business problems, rather than textbook operations research (OR) problems. I evaluated ORPilot on real-world sized problems and obtained promising results. Although textbook OR problems are not in the comfort zone of ORPilot, I also tested ORPilot on three traditional academic benchmarks – IndustryOR, NL4OPT, and NLP4LP. ORPilot delivered performance superior to other state-of-the-art LLM-for-OR tools in terms of the overall accuracy on the industrial IndustryOR benchmark, and comparable performance on NL4OPT and NLP4LP. 

Keywords: Large Language Models, Artificial Intelligence, AI Agent, Operations Research, Optimization Modeling, Decision Making

# 1 Introduction 

Operations Research (OR) has long been one of the most practically valuable disciplines in applied mathematics, underpinning decisions in logistics, supply chain management, scheduling, finance, and beyond. Despite decades of mature solver technologies (e.g., Gurobi, CPLEX, etc.), the barrier to building and deploying optimization models remains high. Industry practitioners who want to solve a business problem through optimization often lack expertise in mathematical programming, while a well-trained OR consultant may lack domain knowledge. Translating a real-world business problem into a mathematical optimization model often requires iterative dialogs between a domain expert and an OR consultant. Large Language Models (LLMs) have opened a plausible path to democratizing optimization modeling. A growing body of work, e.g., OptiMUS [1, 2, 3], OR-LLM [4], and others, have demonstrated that state-of-the-art LLMs can generate correct solver code for well-specified linear programming (LP) and mixed integer programming (MIP) problems. These results, however, have been obtained almost exclusively in a controlled academic setting: the problem description is complete and unambiguous, all numerical data appear inline in the prompt, and the task reduces to code synthesis from a mathematics textbook problem.

However, real-world OR deployments are categorically different. Industry practitioners are barely able to describe all aspects of the business problem they are facing in a one-go prompt. A business analyst describing a production-planning problem will likely omit assumptions they consider obvious, conflate decision variables with parameters, and provide data in operational spreadsheets rather than as a neatly formatted numerical table in the prompt. Existing LLM-for-OR tools fall short on addressing these issues. ORPilot is aimed at addressing this gap. It is, to my knowledge, the first LLM-based OR tool designed explicitly for production conditions, featuring the following architectural novelties: 1. An interview agent that conducts a structured dialog with the user, asking targeted clarifying questions until the problem specification is complete and unambiguous before any modeling efforts begin. 2. A data collection agent that instructs the user on what data to prepare with clear schema requirements for the data tables. 3. A parameter computation agent that inspects raw data tables (e.g., CSV files) the user provides, identifies what derived quantities (e.g., distance matrices, aggregated parameters, BigM values) the model requires, generates and executes a Python script to compute them, and exposes the results as additional computed data tables. 4. A solver-agnostic Intermediate Representation (IR) – a compact, typed JSON schema capturing sets, parameters, variables, objectives, and constraints with references to the CSV files generated in the previous steps as data sources. The IR decouples model formulation from solver-specific code and enables deterministic recompilation, reproducibility, and backend portability without further LLM calls. These components are embedded in a self-correcting pipeline: LLM-generated code that fails at the solver level is automatically repaired by feeding error messages, infeasibility signals, and full tracebacks back to the LLM for a targeted revision, up to a configurable number of retries. I evaluated the performance of ORPilot using three optimization problems in the supply chain industry with real-life sized data, mirroring how an OR consultant would perform OR modeling for business users in practice. Although ORPilot is not designed for academic benchmarks, I also evaluated ORPilot on three public academic benchmarks (IndustryOR [4], NL4OPT [5], NLP4LP [1, 2, 3]) and reported accuracy figures to provide a comparison with other existing LLM-for-OR tools. On IndustryOR (the most industrially representative benchmark), ORPilot with GPT-4o outperforms OptiMUS-0.3 (37% accuracy [3]) with 46% accuracy, while ORPilot with Claude-Sonnet-4.6 achieves 75% overall accuracy on the same benchmark. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 surveys related work. Section 3 describes the system architecture in detail. Section 4 presents the experimental results. Finally, section 5 concludes the paper with some discussions on the experimental results and future improvements. 

# 2 Relevant Work 

The application of LLMs to OR has grown rapidly into a broad field. A recent survey paper [6] provided a system-atic review covering three research pathways: automatic modeling (translating natural language into solver code), LLM-assisted optimization (e.g., heuristic generation, evolutionary algorithms), and direct solving (LLM as black-box optimizer). ORPilot belongs to the automatic modeling pathway and is most directly related to prompt-driven code generation systems. Hence, the discussion in this section is organized accordingly based on this survey paper. 

# 2.1 Prompt-Driven Automatic Modeling 

The dominant approach to LLM-based OR modeling is to construct a prompt that contains the problem description and ask the LLM to generate solver code or a mathematical formulation in a single pass or a fixed multi-step pipeline. The OptiMUS series [1, 2, 3] is the most developed work in this direction. The initial version OptiMUS progressively converts natural language into a solvable optimization model through structure generation, code generation, execution validation and feedback correction, and introduced the NLP4LP dataset. OptiMUS-0.2 added a connection-graph mechanism to track dependencies between variables, constraints and parameters. The latest version, OptiMUS-0.3, further integrated a structure detection agent and structure pool, combined with prompt-retry and self-correction loops, and reported 37% accuracy on the IndustryOR benchmark using GPT-4o. Chain-of-Experts [7] proposed a multi-agent chain in which role-specific agents handle terminology parsing, model construction, code generation, and verification, with a two-stage reasoning mechanism of forward construction and backward reflection. OR-LLM-Agent [8] introduced a reasoning-driven closed-loop framework that uses structured prompts to transform natural language into LP models, automatically generates executable code, and performs repairs and validations in a sandbox – the closest in spirit to ORPilot’s retry loop. Autoformulator [9] combined hierarchical decomposition and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with structured templates to systematically explore alternative formulations. NL4OPT [5] first introduced the shared task and dataset for formulating LP problems from natural language while establishing a standard evaluation protocol. 

2Beyond prompting, a parallel line of work combines structured prompts with model fine-tuning to improve accu-racy and robustness on complex problems. ORLM [4] constructed the IndustryOR benchmark – the most industrially representative dataset in this area, and trained the ORLM series of fine-tuned models that outperformed GPT-4 on NL4OPT, MAMO, and IndustryOR. LLMOPT [10] defined a unified five-element modeling structure: sets, parame-ters, variables, objectives, and constraints, and used supervised fine-tuning, model alignment, and self-correction to significantly improve modeling reliability. This five-element schema is structurally similar to ORPilot’s intermediate representation (IR); however, LLMOPT uses it as a training target for a fine-tuned LLM, whereas ORPilot uses it as an executable IR that is compiled deterministically to solver code without further LLM calls, enabling reproducibil-ity and backend portability. LLaMoCo [11] introduced code-to-code instruction tuning with a large-scale instruction set, achieving stronger semantic understanding and generalization in code generation for optimization tasks. Opt-MATH [12] constructed high-quality triplet datasets (natural language, mathematical expression, solver code) with feedback-based difficulty adjustment. All of these systems discussed above share a common assumption: the problem description is complete and unam-biguous, and all numerical data appear inline with clean formats. ORPilot relaxes both assumptions. 

# 2.2 Intermediate Representation in OR Modeling 

The use of structured intermediate representations (IR) between natural language and solver code is an emerging theme. NL2OR [13] generated a domain-specific language (DSL) as an intermediate step with grammar correction and JSON Schema validation before producing solver code. EquivaMap [14] used LLM-generated variable mappings to assess the semantic equivalence of different formulations of the same problem on the NLP4LP dataset. ORPilot’s IR goes further: it is a fully typed, Pydantic-validated JSON schema that captures the complete model structure; it is compiled deterministically to any of the five solver backends (Gurobi, CPLEX, PuLP, Pyomo, OR-Tools) with zero LLM calls, and persists as a portable artifact that can be archived, edited, and re-solved independently of any LLM connection. 

# 2.3 Interactive and Conversational OR Systems 

OptiGuide [15] integrated GPT-4 with a supply chain optimizer, translating user queries into optimization code mod-ifications and forming a closed feedback loop achieving over 90% accuracy in Microsoft Azure deployment. However, OptiGuide assumes an existing fully specified model. It handles what-if queries on top of a fixed formulation rather than eliciting the formulation from scratch. OptLLM [16] enabled multi-round conversation between the user and the system to elicit problem specifications and handles external data outside prompts. However, the tool still assumes model-ready data is available and lacks an IR for reproducibility and portability. Decision Optimization CoPilot (DOCP) [17] articulated the research vision for conversational decision-optimization tools, identifying problem elici-tation, data handling, and reproducibility as open challenges. ORPilot is, to my knowledge, the first concrete system to address all these challenges. 

# 2.4 Parameter Derivation from Raw Data 

The gap between raw operational data and model-ready parameters has received almost no attention in the LLM-for-OR literature. [18] proposed an automatic MILP construction framework for multi-robot task allocation that uses a domain knowledge base to guide variable and constraint generation, but assumed the relevant numerical quantities are already available. OR-LLM-Agent [8] incorporated a validation chain but did not address parameter transformation from raw tables. ORPilot’s parameter computation agent is, to my knowledge, the first component specifically designed to inspect raw tabular data, identify what derived quantities (e.g., pairwise distance matrices, aggregated parameters, BigM values) the model requires, generate and execute a Python script to compute them, and pass the results as additional structured inputs to the code generation step. 

# 3 System Architecture 

ORPilot is implemented as a LangGraph state machine [19]. Each pipeline step is an independently testable agent node. The standard pipeline of ORPilot is illustrated in Figure 1. The pipeline starts by the interview agent asking the user questions about the business problem to be solved through an interactive conversation. Once the interview agent completes the conversation with the user, the data collection agent thinks about what data would be needed to model this problem and gives instructions to the user on what data to prepare and how to prepare them through an interactive conversation. Once the data collection 

3agent decides that all needed data are provided in the correct format, the problem specification and the collected data schema will be passed to the parameter computation agent to derive any secondary parameters that are required by the optimization model, but not readily available in the raw data the user provided. Then the code generation agent receives the information of problem specification, raw data tables, and derived parameter tables to generate the code for building and solving the optimization model. The solver code is then executed in a secured sandbox to try to find the optimal solution. A self-correcting loop is embedded here to fix any mistake that the code generation agent could have made when the solver execution step returns an error or infeasible or unbounded solution. The maximum number of retries is limited by a user-defined parameter to prevent infinite loops. Upon successful code executions returning an optimal solution, the solution is passed to the reporter agent to summarize the optimization results. The reporter agent will communicate the findings to the user in plain English. Useful artifacts can be generated and saved along with the pipeline for later analysis the user might need to do. These artifacts include (1) an intermediate representation (IR) which is a structured json blob that extracts the skeleton of this optimization problem, (2) a python file that stores the code for generating the optimization model, (3) the lp file of this particular optimization model and (4) a session file that stores necessary information along with the pipeline which allows the user to resume from the current session later if this session is interrupted. The following subsections discuss the necessity and contribution of each novel component of ORPilot standard pipeline independently.

# 3.1 Interview Agent 

The interview agent is the entry point for end users. It receives a free-form natural-language description of a business problem which may be vague, incomplete, or self-contradictory and engages the user in a structured dialog to elicit a complete problem specification. The key design principle is that no modeling efforts should begin until the interview is complete. This mirrors the practice of a human OR consultant. Business users are barely able to describe the business problems they want to model accurately enough in a single paragraph. A good consultant will spend substantial time with business users to understand the problem before writing a single constraint. Premature modeling based on an underspecified problem produces models that are technically correct but practically wrong. This design of ORPilot is fundamentally different than previous work on LLM-for-OR tools, which are aimed at solving textbook OR problems with clean problem descriptions and model-ready data. The interview agent is prompted to (a) identify information gaps in the current description, (b) ask at most one targeted clarifying question per turn to avoid overwhelming the user, and (c) terminate the interview and advance the pipeline once the objective function, decision variables, constraints, and data requirements are unambiguously specified. The agent maintains a running structured summary of the problem that is updated after each exchange and passed to downstream steps. Before ending the interview, the agent would provide the user with a summary clearly stating the objective, decision variables, constraints, parameters and indices of each parameter. The user has the chance to make any final corrections by communicating with the agent before this structure summary is passed to downstream processes. Refer to Section A, F and K for real examples of interview agent – user conversations. 

43.2 Data Collection Agent 

This step has no counterpart in any existing LLM-for-OR tool. Existing tools are limited to accepting a single, pre-written problem text with clean and model-ready data embedded in it. However, in practice, solving a real-life optimization problem often requires a huge amount of data that can hardly be directly embedded in the prompt fed to LLMs, due to the maximum token limits. Even if the maximum token limits are not a hurdle, combining data into the prompt will likely increase the chance of hallucinations of LLMs. ORPilot is designed with the notion that data should live separately from the prompt and can only be accessed by AI agents through writing code. Hence, ORPilot adopts a data collection agent dedicated to understand what data the user has available and instruct the user on data preparation. Based on the problem specification the interview agent obtained from the user, the data collection agent determines exactly what tabular data is required: which entities (sets) exist, what attributes (parameters) each entity has, and what data types and semantics apply. The agent presents this schema to the user and waits for them to supply data as CSV files to the file path specified by the agent. It then validates the completeness and consistency of the uploaded data before proceeding. If the user does not have the model-ready data to satisfy the schema specified by the agent (e.g., the agent asks for the distance between two locations, but the user only has coordinates of the two locations), the user can indicate this to the data collection agent, and the agent will update the schema requirement based on what the user actually has at hand and let the parameter computation agent compute model-ready parameters by passing a message to it. Refer to Section B, G and L for real examples of data collection agent – user conversations. 

# 3.3 Parameter Computation Agent 

This step is perhaps the most significant departure from prior work. Most existing LLM-for-OR tools assume that all numerical quantities needed by the model appear directly in the problem statement or attached data. This is essentially never true in practice. Consider two representative examples: 1. A vehicle routing model needs a distance matrix between locations. The user only has city coordinates. Comput-ing pairwise Euclidean or geographic distances requires a transformation step that is entirely outside the scope of LP/MIP formulation. 2. A multi-period production scheduling model needs aggregate demand per time period. The user only has a transaction ledger with one row per order. The model parameter is a sum-aggregation over the raw data. The parameter computation agent bridges this gap. It receives the structured problem specification from the interview agent and the raw CSV files from the data collection agent. It then (1) identifies which model parameters cannot be read directly from the raw tables (derived parameters), (2) generates a Python script to compute these derived parameters from the raw data, (3) executes the script in a sandboxed environment, and (4) writes the results as additional CSV files, which are passed to the code generation step. This ensures that the code generation step always receives model-ready parameters (clean, correctly typed, and correctly indexed, rather than raw operational data), which results in substantially more reliable code generation and fewer retries. 

# 3.4 Code Generation and Self-Correcting Retry Loop 

The code generation step receives the full problem specification and all parameter CSV files and generates a complete Python solver script for the chosen backend. ORPilot currently supports five backends (Gurobi, CPLEX, PuLP, Pyomo and OR-Tools), and the generated code is backend-specific. The generated code is immediately executed. If the solver returns an error (syntax error or runtime exception) or infeasibility signal or unbounded signal, the full error message and traceback are fed back to the LLM along with the previously generated code, and code generation is retried. This loop runs up to N times (configurable). In practice, the majority of failures are resolved within one or two retries. Refer to Section C, H and M for real examples of the code generated by the code generation agent. 

# 3.5 Intermediate Representation (IR) 

A central design goal of ORPilot is reproducibility and portability: once a model has been solved successfully, it should be possible to re-run it with a different solver backend, to perform what-if analysis by editing the data, or to archive the model in a form that does not depend on a live LLM connection. To achieve this, ORPilot defines a solver-agnostic Intermediate Representation (IR) – a compact, typed JSON schema that captures all information needed to reconstruct any optimization model: 1. Sets – named collections of entities (e.g., Plants, Products, Periods) 

52. Parameters – indexed numerical quantities, with references to CSV files (indicating where to read the parameter data) 3. Variables – decision variable types, domains, bounds, and index structure 4. Objective – a symbolic expression tree over variables and parameters 5. Constraints – named symbolic expressions with domains and senses IR generation is optional and governed by a user-specified configuration parameter. When enabled, a LLM is invoked to produce the IR. Due to the inherently non-deterministic nature of LLMs, the generation process is con-strained by a set of predefined rules embedded within the prompt to prevent hallucinations. After generation, the IR is passed to a validator to ensure correctness. The validator is designed to detect common failure modes associated with LLM-generated IR. If validation fails, the identified errors are fed back to the agent, which then regenerates the IR. Compilation from IR to solver code is a fully deterministic process enabled by an IR compiler, with zero LLM calls. This means that once an IR has been validated, it can be recompiled to any supported backend at any time without incurring API costs or non-determinism. The existence of the IR enables a lightweight IR compilation pipeline in which a pre-built ir.json file together with CSV files can be compiled and solved with zero LLM calls, see Figure 2. This pipeline is useful for CI/CD validation and solver regression testing and also crucial to what-if analyses that business users usually perform to support decision making in practice. A more detailed discussion about the role of IR in what-if analysis can be found in Section 5.4.

# 3.6 Reporter Agent 

After a successful solve, a reporter agent generates a plain-English summary of the solution: the optimal objective value, the values of key decision variables, and a brief interpretation of key findings and operational suggestions in the domain language of the original problem. This output is intended for consumption by the business user who initiated the problem description, not by an OR expert. # 4.4 Academic Benchmarks 

Existing LLM-for-OR tools and ORPilot are targeted at different types of problems. While most existing tools strive to improve the accuracy on solving textbook style OR problems (unambiguous problem descriptions in one go and clean model-ready data), ORPilot is aimed at more industrial style real-life OR problems with large scale data. However, in order to compare ORPilot with other tools, I evaluated the accuracy performance of ORPilot on three academic benchmarks: IndustryOR, NL4OPT and NLP4LP, which existing tools are usually evaluated on, although these three benchmarks are not quite in the comfort zone of ORPilot. Since ORPilot is originally designed for dialog-based problem description intake, not for prepared problem text, the standard pipeline of ORPilot cannot be directly applied to the academic benchmarks. Hence, I designed a benchmark mode for the ORPilot pipeline specially for running benchmark tests. IndustryOR [4] is a benchmark of 100 real-world industrial OR problems drawn from supply chain, scheduling, routing, and resource allocation. Problems are labeled as easy (39 instances), medium (41 instances), or hard (20 instances). It is the most industrially representative of the three benchmarks evaluated here. NL4OPT [5] is an academic benchmark of natural-language linear programming problems, originally introduced as a shared task at NeurIPS 2022. Each problem is a self-contained textbook-style LP with numerical data embedded in the problem text. I evaluated on the test split and restricted to problems that have a finite optimal solution (skipping infeasible or unbounded instances). NLP4LP [1, 2, 3] is a gated benchmark (udell-lab/NLP4LP) containing a diverse set of LP problems with natural-language descriptions and associated parameter tables. It is more challenging than NL4OPT in that the problems are longer, more ambiguously stated, and include parameter data as structured JSON rather than inline text. For the existing LLM-for-OR tools to compare, I selected three representative works to compare: LLMOPT [10], ORLM [4] and OptiMUS [1, 2, 3] due to their similar pipeline to ORPilot. # 5 Conclusions and Discussions 

This paper presents ORPilot, a production-oriented LLM-powered AI agent for OR modeling with a design principle that mirrors how OR consultants would work with business users for modeling their problems. By introducing four novel pipeline components – a conversational interview agent, a data collection agent, a parameter computation agent, and a solver-agnostic Intermediate Representation (IR), ORPilot addresses the full gap between an unconstrained business dialog and a deployable and reproducible optimization model. These components have no counterpart in most existing LLM-for-OR tools, which operate exclusively in the idealized setting of clean, complete and inline problem descriptions. Experimental results in Section 4 show that ORPilot works well with OR problems with large scale data in supply chain industry and delivers a superior performance than existing LLM-for-OR tools when evaluated on industry-style academic benchmark problems. I believe that the pipeline introduced here – iterative problem elicitation, data collection, automated parameter derivation, and IR-based reproducibility, represents a necessary and general framework for any serious LLM-for-OR production-level deployment, and I hope it will serve as a foundation for future work in this area. Although the experimental results establish ORPilot as a competitive production-grade pipeline prototype, but they also expose a few structural limitations that define the most productive directions for future work. 

# 5.1 Optimal Solution Validation 

The current pipeline of ORPilot does not have any mechanism to examine the solution when an optimal solution is produced. The retry loop is only triggered when the model generated runs into an error, infeasibility or unboundedness. However, the generated model can be semantically wrong (misidentifying a decision variable, omitting a constraint, or misinterpreting a bound) yet still produces an optimal solution. In fact, on the three academic benchmarks (IndustryOR, NL4OPT and NLP4LP), most of the failed cases of ORPilot are due to a wrong optimal objective value without entering any retry loop. Addressing this limitation requires a solution validation layer between the solver and the reporter. A solution validation agent prompted with the original problem description and the full solution vector could verify that the reported values satisfy the business constraints as stated in natural language, that variable assignments are semantically meaningful, and that the solution is not merely optimal within a misspecified model. When this agent decides the optimal solution is semantically wrong, it triggers the retry loop to regenerate the model with the new message the solution validation agent produces. This is analogous to the backward-reflection step proposed in Chain-of-Experts [7], but applied to the solution rather than to the formulation. The pipeline with a solution validation agent can be illustrated by the diagram in Figure 3.

However, adding another LLM-powered agent to examine the semantical correctness of the optimal solution of the 

12 generated model is a double-edged sword. While it could identify possible optimal solutions that are semantically wrong, it could also produce false positives (flagging solutions that are semantically correct as wrong and triggering the retry loop), which could turn a correct solution into a wrong solution in the end. Some preliminary experiments I conducted have shown that such an agent would bring more issues than benefits without a fine-tuned prompt or heuristic. Developing robust prompts or heuristics for validating optimal solutions generated by LLM-for-OR tools is an important open problem for the field, not only for ORPilot. 

# 5.2 Infeasibility and Unboundedness Analysis 

A semantically wrong model generated by LLM-for-OR tools could produce an optimal solution. Conversely, a se-mantically correct model could run into infeasibility/unboundedness due to the nature of the data provided or the constraints specified (e.g., the total capacity of all facilities is not large enough to satisfy the demands of all customers in a supply chain problem). ORPilot’s retry loop treats infeasibility/unboundedness as a recoverable failure: when the solver returns an infeasible signal, the error is fed back to the LLM with a prompt to revise the model, and a new attempt is made. This heuristic is effective when infeasibility/unboundedness is caused by a modeling error (e.g., a misspecified constraint, an incorrect bound, or a missing relaxation). However, it is counterproductive when infeasibility is inherent to the problem as specified. No reformulation of the model will yield a feasible solution because the user’s constraints are mutually contradictory or the problem data make the feasible region empty. In this case, the retry loop wastes API calls and latency and ultimately fails after exhausting its budget, reporting a failure that could have been diagnosed immediately. Two complementary capabilities are needed. First, an infeasibility classifier should distinguish between (1) formu-lation infeasibility – the model as written is infeasible due to a modeling error, for which a retry is appropriate and (2) problem infeasibility – the constraints implied by the problem description are inherently contradictory, for which the correct response is to inform the user and terminate. A lightweight classifier trained on solver status codes, constraint structure, and LLM-extracted semantic features could make this distinction before invoking the retry loop. Second, for confirmed problem infeasibility, an infeasibility diagnosis agent should apply techniques from the OR literature, e.g., irreducible infeasible subsystems (IIS) extraction or constraint relaxation enumeration, to identify the minimal conflicting subset of constraints and translate it back into natural-language terms the user can understand. This closes the loop for business users: rather than a generic failure message, they receive an explanation such as “the weekly production capacity constraint conflicts with the minimum demand requirement in week 3 and one of these must be relaxed." Such explanations are standard output from commercial solver tools (e.g., Gurobi, CPLEX) but are not yet integrated into any LLM-for-OR tools. 

# 5.3 Fine-Tuned Language Models for OR Modeling 

All ORPilot results reported in this paper are based on general-purpose frontier LLMs (e.g., Claude-Sonnet-4.6, DeepSeek-Reasoner) via API. This approach has significant advantages – no training infrastructure, immediate access to the latest models, but also significant drawbacks – API cost, latency, data privacy constraints, and a hard dependency on external services for a production system. The IR structure in ORPilot provides a natural training signal for fine-tuning. Each successful ORPilot run produces a (problem specification, ir.json) pair that is a high-quality supervised training example. The input is an unambiguous natural-language problem description with the schema information of associated data tables needed for building the OR model and the output is a structurally valid, solver-verified IR that compiles correctly to executable code. Unlike raw solver code, which is backend-specific, verbose, and sensitive to API idiom, the IR is compact, backend-agnostic, and schema-validated, making it a substantially cleaner training target. In practice, a smaller open-weights LLM (e.g., a 7B or 13B parameter model) can be trained to generate IR JSON from problem specifications, using successful and verified ORPilot runs as supervised examples. The current pipeline of ORPilot uses IR generation as a post-processing step after an optimal solution is generated, see Figure 1. IR generation was not designed as a step prior to solver code generation in ORPilot in the first place. This is because the experiments I conducted showed LLM-powered IR generation was not very robust and stable using only problem specifications and data table schemas. Feeding artifacts like solver code (model.py) together into LLMs can enhance the quality of the generated IR. However, with fine-tuned LLMs that use verified IRs as the learning target, a new pipeline can be established which employs IR generation prior to solver code generation, see Figure 4. As the fine-tuning process is expected to significantly improve the quality of the generated IR, IR generation can serve as a stable component that would not impact the accuracy of the downstream steps. Since ORPilot has a deterministic IR compiler, solver code generation in this new pipeline will be a deterministic step. This decouples the modeling intelligence (LLM to IR) from the compilation step (IR to solver code), concentrating the LLM’s task on structured JSON generation rather than solver API syntax.

# 5.4 What-if Analysis via IR Manipulation 

A natural extension of the IR’s reproducibility and portability properties is systematic what-if analysis. Once a model has been solved and its IR persists as a validated JSON artifact, a business user may wish to explore how the optimal solution changes under modified assumptions without re-engaging the LLM or re-specifying the problem from scratch. What-if queries fall into two structurally distinct categories, and the IR structure is able to support both. The first category is data changes – modifications to parameter values that leave the model structure intact. Examples include asking “what if demand increases by 20% in Q3?" or “what if the cost of raw material B rises to $15 per unit?" In these cases, only the CSV files referenced by the IR need to be updated and the IR JSON itself is unchanged. The deterministic compiler then recompiles the same IR against the new data and a re-solve is triggered, which is a zero-LLM-call operation. This form of sensitivity analysis is already latent in the current architecture and requires only a thin user-facing interface to expose it. The second category is structural changes: modifications to constraints, objectives or variable domains that alter the model’s mathematical form. Examples include “what if we add a constraint that no single supplier accounts for more than 40% of total procurement?" or “what if we remove the overtime limit and instead penalize it in the objective?" These changes require editing the IR – adding, removing, or modifying constraint or objective terms. Because the IR is a typed, schema-validated document with a well-defined expression tree, such edits are localized and predictable: adding a constraint is a matter of appending a new constraint object with the appropriate domain and expression, rather than modifying dozens of lines of solver-specific code. A what-if analysis agent can be added into the current IR compilation pipeline (see Figure 5) which accepts natural-language change requests, classifies them as data changes or structural changes, and either updates the relevant values in CSV files or generate a targeted IR modification. The LLM’s task in this setting is substantially simpler than full model generation: it operates on a verified IR and only needs to express an incremental change in the same structured schema. This narrower task is well-suited to a fine-tuned or smaller LLM as discussed in Section 5.3.

# 5.5 Database Connection 

A key limitation of the current ORPilot pipeline is its reliance on user-prepared CSV files as the data interface. While this design keeps the system lightweight and portable, it places a significant burden on practitioners, who must extract, transform, and format data from their operational systems before optimization can begin. A natural and impactful extension of ORPilot is to eliminate this preprocessing step through native database connectivity. In future versions, ORPilot could accept a database connection string – pointing to a relational database, data warehouse, or enterprise resource planning (ERP) system and autonomously perform the data engineering tasks currently delegated to the user. This capability would unfold in three stages. First, a schema discovery phase would catalog the available tables, columns, data types, foreign key relationships, and cardinalities, producing a structured representation of what data the system has access to. Second, a data sufficiency assessment phase would evaluate whether the discovered schema contains the entities and relationships required to instantiate the optimization model identified during the interview phase. Drawing on the IR specification, ORPilot would map each required set, parameter, and variable domain to candidate tables in the database, flagging gaps where data is absent or ambiguous and querying the user only for genuinely missing information rather than demanding a complete data hand-off upfront. Third, a model-ready data preparation phase would issue the necessary SQL queries, perform unit validation, resolve naming inconsistencies, and materialize the result as the structured inputs consumed by the IR compiler all transparently, without requiring the user to write a single query. This architecture would bring ORPilot substantially closer to the vision of end-to-end decision support: a practi-tioner could describe their OR problem in natural language, grant the system read access to their existing database, and receive a solved, deployable optimization model with minimal manual intervention. It would also make re-optimization operationally practical – as the underlying database is updated with new demand signals, cost figures, or capacity changes, the model could be rebuilt and re-solved automatically, enabling near-real-time decision support rather than one-off studies. Several technical challenges merit attention in pursuing this direction. Semantic alignment between database schemas and optimization concepts is non-trivial; a table named orders in one organization may correspond to demand in another, and the LLM agent responsible for schema mapping must resolve such ambiguities reliably. Handling sparse or missing data requires robust imputation and flagging strategies so that the resulting model is both tractable and trustworthy. Privacy and access control are also important considerations, as practitioners may only be able to grant query-level access to subsets of their data; the system must respect these boundaries while still assembling complete model inputs. Finally, for very large databases, scalable query planning and data sampling strategies will be necessary to keep the data preparation phase practical within the time constraints of an interactive optimization workflow. 

# 5.6 Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) System 

The current approach to improving ORPilot’s reliability follows an empirical, rule-accumulation paradigm: when a failure mode is identified through testing, such as a hallucinated column name, an incorrect sparse network encoding, or a malformed IR expression, a corrective rule is appended to the relevant system prompt. This strategy is effective in the short term, as each new rule directly addresses an observed failure and can be validated immediately through re-testing. However, it is fundamentally unsustainable at scale. As the library of rules grows, prompts become increasingly long, placing pressure on the context windows of the underlying language models, increasing inference latency and cost, and diluting the signal-to-noise ratio of the prompt itself. A model attending to a prompt containing hundreds of rules may fail to surface the specific guidance most relevant to the problem at hand. A more principled and scalable alternative is to replace static and monolithic prompts with a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) architecture. Under this design, ORPilot would maintain a curated knowledge base of high-quality examples, including reference IR specifications for canonical problem classes, correct solver code for representative model structures, and documented patterns for common modeling challenges such as inventory balance constraints, sparse network flows, and multi-period temporal dependencies. Rather than embedding all of this knowledge statically in every prompt, agents would issue a semantic query against the knowledge base before each generation step, retrieving only the most relevant examples for the specific problem structure currently being modeled. The retrieved examples would be injected dynamically into the prompt context, providing targeted, high-precision guidance without the overhead of irrelevant rules. This architecture would yield several concrete benefits beyond prompt length control. First, it decouples knowledge maintenance from prompt engineering: adding a new problem type or correcting a modeling pattern becomes an operation on the knowledge base rather than a prompt rewrite, making the system easier to maintain and extend. 

15 Second, retrieval-based selection is inherently adaptive, a routing problem would surface arc-flow examples and subtour elimination patterns, while an inventory management problem would retrieve temporal balance constraints and holding cost formulations, without either example polluting the other’s prompt context. Third, the knowledge base itself becomes an auditable artifact: practitioners and researchers can inspect which examples were retrieved for a given generation, providing a degree of transparency and debuggability that is difficult to achieve with opaque monolithic prompts. However, building an effective RAG system for ORPilot also raises several research questions worth investigating. The design of the embedding space is particularly important: standard semantic similarity over natural language descriptions may be insufficient to distinguish structurally similar but semantically distinct optimization patterns; embeddings that encode mathematical structure, e.g., constraint types, variable domains, sparsity patterns, may be necessary for high-precision retrieval. The construction and curation of the knowledge base also requires care: examples must be verified correct, sufficiently diverse to cover the problem space, and annotated with enough metadata to support accurate retrieval. Finally, the interaction between retrieval and in-context learning deserves study. The ordering, formatting, and number of retrieved examples all influence generation quality, and optimal retrieval strategies may differ between the IR generation and code generation stages of the pipeline.

# Appendix I Solution Report for Problem in Section 4.2 

Multi-period Facility Opening and Flow Optimization 

1. Executive Summary 

The optimization model determined the maximum-profit network design and operating plan for a supply chain spanning 25 candidate production sites , 25 distribution centers , 250 customers , and 500 products 

over 12 monthly periods . The solver found a provably optimal solution with a total network profit of $50,051,375.31 .The model jointly decided which facilities to open and when, how much to produce at each site, and how to route goods to customers — all while accounting for fixed facility opening costs, per-period operating costs, production and transportation costs, and inventory holding costs. All 250 customers are fully served across every product and period.

• 12 idle production sites : Half of the candidate production network remains unopened. Before dismissing these sites, consider whether they would be activated under a demand increase scenario or if any of the opened sites face a disruption. They represent latent capacity that could be critical in contingency planning. 

• PS_030 mid-year activation : The optimizer chose to defer opening PS_030 until Period 7 rather than open it in Period 1. This saves six months of fixed and operating costs. However, it means the network is running at tighter production capacity in the first half of the year. A sensitivity run could test whether moving the opening to Period 5 or 6 improves profit by reducing mid-year inventory build-up. 

• DC_024 concentration risk : With 14% of all deliveries flowing through a single DC, any disruption to DC_024 would affect a large portion of the customer base. A scenario run capping DC_024’s throughput to 80% of current utilization would reveal the profit impact and identify which alternative DCs would absorb the overflow. 

• Full DC network activation : Opening all 25 DCs from Period 1 incurs ~$7.4M in fixed costs immediately. If some DCs serve only a small number of customers, a variant model allowing DCs to open later (as with PS_030) might reduce early-period fixed cost outlay. The current formulation constrains DCs to open no later than the first period they handle flow — examining DC utilization rates would identify candidates for deferred opening. 

• End-of-horizon inventory drawdown : Zero inventory at the end of Period 12 is optimal within the model but leaves no buffer if demand continues into Period 13. A rolling-horizon plan or a terminal inventory credit would produce more conservative end-state stock levels. 

• Facility cost significance : Combined fixed opening costs for sites and DCs total approximately $13.7 million — roughly 27% of the total net profit. Accurately estimating these one-time costs in the data is therefore critical; a 10% error in fixed costs shifts the reported profit by ~$1.4M. 7. Suggested Next Steps 

• Deferred DC opening analysis : Relax the assumption that all DCs must open in Period 1. Allow DCs to open in any period and re-solve to test whether later openings for low-utilization DCs reduce total cost. 

• PS_030 opening timing sensitivity : Test opening PS_030 in Periods 5 and 6 to see if earlier activation reduces the inventory build-up in Periods 1–4 and improves overall profit. 

• DC_024 stress test : Cap DC_024 throughput at 80% and re-solve to quantify concentration risk and identify which alternative DCs absorb the displaced volume. 

• Idle site contingency value : Estimate the option value of the 12 idle sites by solving a disruption scenario in which one or two opened sites become unavailable mid-horizon. 

65 • LP relaxation comparison : Solving the LP relaxation (removing binary constraints) gives an upper bound on profit and reveals how much value is lost to the integrality requirement. A large gap suggests room to improve the formulation.
