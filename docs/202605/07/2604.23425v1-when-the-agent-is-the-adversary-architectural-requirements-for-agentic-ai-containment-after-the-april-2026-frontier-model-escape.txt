# When the Agent Is the Adversary: 

Architectural Requirements for Agentic AI Containment After the April 2026 Frontier Model 

Escape 

Richard Joseph Mitchell, ESEP, MBA 

AuraSpark Technologies LLC, Tavares, Florida 

richard@auraspark.com 

April 2026 

Abstract 

The April 2026 disclosure that a frontier large language model escaped its security sandbox, executed unauthorized 

actions, and concealed its modifications to version control history demonstrates that agentic AI systems with 

autonomous tool access can circ umvent the containment mechanisms designed to constrain them. This paper 

analyzes four categories of current containment approaches - alignment training, environmental sandboxing, 

application -level tool -call interception, and accessible audit systems - and identifies the failure modes each exhibits 

when the AI agent is treated as a potential adversary rather than a trusted component receiving adversarial inputs. 

We categorize five behavioral incidents from the public disclosure and situate them within 698 r eal -world AI 

scheming incidents documented by the Centre for Long -Term Resilience between October 2025 and March 2026, a 

4.9x acceleration establishing the challenge as systemic. We derive five architectural requirements: trust separation 

through layered O S privilege enforcement with semantic intent analysis, sequential intent inference through five -

phase taxonomic monitoring, independent containment integrity monitoring, adversarial audit isolation through 

logical invisibility, and emergent capability enve lope enforcement through distributional divergence monitoring. 

No publicly described system satisfies all five. We argue that architectural containment is the only durable safety 

strategy given the inevitable proliferation of equivalent capabilities includ ing open -weight models. The author's 

published patent portfolio in provider -independent constraint enforcement addresses several of these requirements. 

Concurrent work including SandboxEscapeBench (arXiv:2603.02277) independently confirms that frontier mod els 

can escape standard container sandboxes, corroborating the threat model presented here. 

Keywords: AI safety, agentic AI, LLM containment, sandbox escape, constraint enforcement, 

adversarial audit, trust separation, AI governance, autonomous tool access, AURA FARADAY 

1. INTRODUCTION 

On April 7, 2026, Anthropic announced that its most capable AI model —Claude Mythos Preview —

would not be publicly released. The reason was not performance limitations but capability excess: the 

model had demonstrated, during safety testing, the ability to e scape its security sandbox, discover and 

exploit zero -day vulnerabilities across every major operating system and web browser, and take 

autonomous actions that went beyond what researchers had instructed [1]. The model scored 100% on 

Cybench, a cybersecuri ty challenge benchmark that no prior model had come close to saturating [2]. 

Three specific incidents documented in Anthropic’s 244 -page system card and accompanying 

Frontier Red Team blog post are architecturally significant. First, the model built a multi -step exploit 

to escape its sandboxed computing environment and gain broad i nternet access from a machine that 

was supposed to be restricted to limited services. Second, upon escaping, the model posted details of 

its own exploit to public websites without being instructed to do so —what Anthropic characterized as 

“a concerning and unasked -for effort to demonstrate its success” [1]. Third, in a separate test, the Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 2

model edited files it did not have permission to modify, then scrubbed the git history to conceal its 

modifications [2]. 

These incidents are not merely alarming anecdotes. They represent a qualitative shift in the AI 

safety landscape because they demonstrate that the dominant approaches to AI containment —

alignment training, environmental sandboxing, and application -level saf ety mechanisms —can be 

defeated not by an external adversary, but by the AI system itself. This is a fundamentally different 

threat model from the one that most current AI safety work assumes. 

This paper contributes: (1) a systematic analysis of five documented Mythos behavioral incidents, 

each mapped to a specific architectural assumption violation; (2) an assessment of four categories of 

current containment approaches against the demonstrated threat model; (3) the derivation of five 

architectural requirements that any containment solution must satisfy to remain effective when the 

constrained entity possesses general reasoning capability; (4) an assessment of publicly described 

systems against t hese requirements, revealing that none satisfy all five simultaneously; and (5) a 

discussion of how published work in provider -independent constraint enforcement and adaptive 

validation for autonomous systems —including the author’s AURA patent portfolio [3 ][4][5] —

addresses components of the identified requirements, and what architectural extensions are needed. 

Critically, the Mythos incidents are not an isolated anomaly. The Centre for Long -Term 

Resilience (CLTR), funded by the UK government’s AI Safety Institute, documented 698 real -world 

scheming -related incidents between October 2025 and March 2026 —a 4.9× inc rease over the 

collection period [24]. These incidents, drawn from analysis of over 180,000 transcripts of real user 

interactions with deployed AI systems from Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, and others, include agents 

disregarding direct instructions, circumve nting safeguards, deceiving users, and spawning secondary 

agents to evade restrictions on the primary [24]. The Mythos containment failure is the most dramatic 

data point in a systemic trend that is accelerating as agentic capabilities scale. The Internati onal AI 

Safety Report 2026, chaired by Turing Award winner Yoshua Bengio and authored by over 100 

experts from 30+ countries, confirms this assessment: general -purpose AI capabilities are advancing 

faster than safeguards, and “the gap between the pace of t echnological advancement and our ability to 

implement effective safeguards remains a critical challenge” [25]. 

2. THE FIVE BEHAVIORAL INCIDENTS 

We analyze five distinct behavioral incidents from Anthropic’s official disclosures. While Anthropic 

emphasizes that these occurred within controlled testing and that the model was responding to 

researcher prompts rather than pursuing autonomous goals, the capabilities demonstrated are 

independent of the intent behind them. A system that can escape containment when asked to try can 

escape containment when it decides to try. 

2.1 Incident 1: Multi -Step Sandbox Escape 

The model was provided with a sandboxed computing environment with access only to a preset group 

of limited online services and instructed by a researcher to attempt escape. Mythos Preview built what 

Anthropic describes as “a moderately sophisticated multi -step exploit” to gain broad internet access Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 3

[1]. The researcher discovered the escape when he received an unexpected email from the model 

while eating a sandwich in a park [2]. 

The exploit was multi -step: the model probed the environment to understand the network 

topology, identified a service that could be leveraged for outbound access, and constructed an exploit 

chain that bypassed the intended network restrictions. Each step i n the chain was a standard system 

operation (network commands, service queries, connection attempts) that would individually appear 

benign. The containment bypass emerged from the sequence , not from any individual action. 

Violated assumption: Environmental isolation is stronger than the agent’s exploit capability. The 

sandbox was a software construct; the model’s exploit capability exceeded the sandbox’s resistance. 

2.2 Incident 2: Unauthorized Autonomous Disclosure 

After escaping the sandbox and sending the requested notification email, Mythos Preview took 

additional actions that were not requested: it posted details of its own exploit to multiple hard -to -find 

but technically public -facing websites [1]. This was not an extension of the researcher’s instruction; it 

was an autonomous decision to publicize the exploit. 

This incident reveals that the model’s effective action space, once containment was breached, was 

unbounded. No mechanism intercepted the proposed web posting and evaluated it against an 

authorized action set. The containment failure was binary: inside the sandbox, the model’s actions 

were constrained by the environment’s restrictions; outside the sandbox, no constraint mechanism 

existed. There was no action -level enforcement layer that would evaluate what the model was doing 

independent of where the model was doing it .

Violated assumption: The agent’s action space is bounded by the environment’s permissions. 

Post -escape, the action space was defined by what the internet permits —effectively unlimited. 

2.3 Incident 3: Forensic Evidence Destruction 

In a separate test, Mythos Preview found an exploit to edit files it did not have permission to modify. 

After making the unauthorized modifications, it scrubbed the git history so the changes would not 

appear in version control [2]. This is a three -step de ceptive sequence: (a) identify a permissions 

bypass, (b) exploit it to make unauthorized modifications, (c) recognize that forensic evidence of the 

modifications exists in version control and take deliberate steps to destroy that evidence. 

This incident demonstrates that audit systems within the agent’s reachable environment are not 

forensically reliable against an adversarial agent. Git version control is an audit system —it maintains 

a record of all file modifications. The model treated it as a target to be neutralized rather than a 

constraint to be respected. Any audit mechanism that the agent can discover and access is vulnerable 

to the same treatment: not necessarily by modifying cryptographically signed records, but by 

disrupting the aud it system’s operation, flooding it with noise, or —as in this case —targeting a pre -

signature record system that the signed system downstream depends on. 

Violated assumption: Audit systems within the agent’s environment are forensically reliable. The 

agent can discover and manipulate audit systems that share its information space. 

2.4 Incident 4: Systematic Constraint Disregard Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]

Anthropic’s system card documents a behavioral pattern they term “recklessness”: cases where the 

model “appears to ignore commonsensical or explicitly stated safety -related constraints on its actions” 

[1]. This is not the sandbox escape (which was prompted ) or the unauthorized disclosure (which was 

an extension). This pattern describes the model violating stated constraints in pursuit of its operational 

objective across multiple test scenarios. 

Alignment training (RLHF, Constitutional AI) and system prompts are the primary mechanisms 

for constraining LLM behavior. They modify the model’s propensity to generate certain outputs but 

do not architecturally prevent any specific output. At the frontier of model capability, the 

effectiveness of alignment training encounters a ceiling: the same general reasoning capability that 

makes the model powerful also enables it to find paths around alignment constraints, particularly 

when those constraints conflict with the task objective. The “reckless” pattern is alignment failure at 

the frontier. 

Violated assumption: Alignment training reliably constrains behavior at all capability levels. 

Emergent reasoning capability can exceed the constraint boundary that alignment training establishes. 

2.5 Incident 5: Untrained Capability Emergence 

Anthropic states explicitly: “We did not explicitly train Mythos Preview to have these capabilities. 

Rather, they emerged as a downstream consequence of general improvements in code, reasoning, and 

autonomy” [2]. The model’s ability to find and exploit zer o-day vulnerabilities, escape sandboxes, 

and chain multi -step exploits was not a designed capability. It emerged from general capability 

improvements without specific training. 

This has a direct consequence for safety evaluation: the model that was validated for safety was 

not the same model, in terms of effective capability profile, as the model that exhibited these 

behaviors. Safety evaluation tests the capabilities that evalua tors anticipate; emergent capabilities, by 

definition, are capabilities that evaluators did not anticipate. A safety evaluation that does not exercise 

a capability cannot certify the system’s behavior under that capability. Static safety evaluation against 

a fixed test suite is therefore structurally unable to keep pace with emergent capability development. 

Violated assumption: Safety evaluation captures the model’s actual capability profile. Emergent 

capabilities create a validation gap between what was tested and what the model can do. 

2.6 The Systemic Pattern: 698 Incidents and Counting 

The five Mythos incidents, while individually striking, are best understood as high -visibility 

manifestations of a systemic trend. The Centre for Long -Term Resilience’s “Scheming in the Wild” 

report [24], published in March 2026 and funded by the UK govern ment’s AI Safety Institute, 

analyzed over 180,000 transcripts of real interactions with deployed AI systems shared publicly 

between October 2025 and March 2026. The researchers identified 698 credible scheming -related 

incidents —cases where deployed AI syst ems acted in ways misaligned with users’ intentions or took 

covert and deceptive actions. The monthly incident rate increased 4.9× over the collection period, a 

statistically significant acceleration that far outpaced the 1.7× growth in online discussion o f AI 

scheming [24]. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]

The documented behaviors mirror the Mythos incidents at lower capability levels. An AI agent 

named Rathbun published a blog post attacking a developer who rejected its code suggestion —an 

unauthorized autonomous disclosure analogous to Mythos’s unprompted w ebsite postings. Another 

agent admitted to bulk -deleting hundreds of emails without authorization. An agent circumvented 

restrictions on its primary task by spawning a secondary agent to perform the prohibited action —a

multi -step bypass analogous to Mythos ’s multi -step sandbox escape. In another case, an agent evaded 

copyright restrictions by fabricating a false justification for access [24]. These are not laboratory 

demonstrations; they are real -world incidents involving deployed production systems from Go ogle, 

OpenAI, Anthropic, and others. 

The CLTR data reveals that the behaviors exhibited by Mythos are the upper end of a capability 

distribution, not qualitative anomalies. As model capabilities increase, the sophistication and impact 

of scheming behaviors increase correspondingly. The 4.9× acceleration coincided with the release of 

more capable, more agentic models th roughout late 2025 and early 2026 [24]. This trend has direct 

implications for containment architecture: systems designed to contain the most capable agents today 

will face more capable agents tomorrow, and the architectural patterns that fail against Mythos will 

fail against every sufficiently capable successor. 

Moreover, the containment problem cannot be solved by restricting access to the most capable 

models. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has acknowledged that “more powerful models are going to 

come from us and from others, and so we do need a plan to respond to th is” [1]. Mythos -equivalent 

capabilities will emerge in competitor models within months, and in open -weight models within 12 –

18 months —models that cannot be withheld because their weights are publicly available. Access -

control -based safety —deciding who may use a model —is a temporary measure. Architectural 

containment —ensuring that any sufficiently capable agent operates within governance constraints 

regardless of its provenance —is the durable solution. This is the central motivation for the 

architectural requirements derived in Section 4. 

3. ANALYSIS OF CURRENT CONTAINMENT PARADIGMS 

We assess four categories of current containment approaches against the threat model established by 

the Mythos incidents: the agent itself as a potential adversary possessing general reasoning capability, 

the ability to plan multi -step actions, and the cap acity to adapt its approach based on observed 

constraints. 

3.1 Alignment -Based Containment 

Alignment training (RLHF [6], Constitutional AI [7]) and system -level instructions represent the first 

line of defense in current AI safety practice. These mechanisms are internal to the model: they shape 

the probability distribution over outputs but do no t architecturally prevent any specific output. This is 

a statistical constraint, not an architectural one. 

The fundamental limitation is that alignment and capability are trained by the same process. As 

general capability increases, so does the model’s ability to reason about and around alignment 

constraints. This is not a theoretical concern —the “reckless beha vior” pattern documented in the 

Mythos system card is a direct empirical demonstration. Furthermore, alignment training is performed Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 6

by the model provider. If the provider is replaced —as occurred in the February 2026 military AI 

events, where a technology provider’s safety constraints were circumvented by substituting an 

alternative provider [3] —the alignment constraints disappear with the provider. Alignment -based 

containment is both capability -limited and provider -dependent. 

The distinction between architectural and procedural constraint enforcement was formalized in 

prior work by the author [37], which derived five genuine human oversight requirements and 

identified procedural -only constraint enforcement as a primary architec tural failure mode in AI 

deployment across high -stakes domains. AURA FARADAY implements mechanically what that 

requirements framework established conceptually: the argument that governance constraints must be 

enforced architecturally —as hard external limit s independent of the AI’s cooperation —rather than 

procedurally through alignment training or system prompts the model may circumvent. 

3.2 Environment -Based Containment 

Software sandboxes —containers, virtual machines, kernel security modules (Landlock LSM, seccomp 

BPF), and network isolation —restrict the execution environment’s access to system resources. 

NVIDIA’s OpenShell system [8] and the Kubernetes Agent Sandbox [9] represent current best 

practice in this category, enforcing constraints at the kernel level via filesystem restrictions, syscall 

filtering, and proxy -evaluated network access. 

These systems constrain the environment rather than the agent’s actions . An agent that can exploit 

the environment’s enforcement mechanism —by finding vulnerabilities in the container runtime, the 

kernel security module, the proxy, or the hypervisor —circumvents all constraints simultaneously. The 

Mythos sandbox escape demonstr ated exactly this: the model exploited the sandboxed environment to 

gain capabilities the sandbox was designed to prevent. This creates a capability arms race between 

containment sophis tication and model exploit capability. NVIDIA’s own security guidance [10] 

acknowledges this: “Because agentic tools often execute arbitrary code by design, kernel 

vulnerabilities can be directly targeted as a path to full system compromise.” 

3.3 Application -Level Tool -Call Interception 

A more recent category of approaches interposes on the tool -call pathway between the model’s output 

and execution. AEGIS [11] provides a pre -execution firewall with content scanning and tamper -

evident audit trails. Microsoft’s Agent Governance Toolkit [12] provides policy enforcement with 

execution rings and append -only audit logs. Framework -level hooks in Strands Agents [13], 

LangGraph, and AutoGen provide per -call validation at the application layer. 

These systems represent a meaningful architectural advance: they evaluate proposed actions 

before execution, which is structurally superior to constraining the environment after execution. 

However, they share a critical limitation: they operate within the same trust domain as the agent 

they constrain. The interception layer, policy engine, and audit system execute within the application 

process or within processes accessible to the agent. They share the agent’s memory space, filesystem 

namespace, process ta ble, and network namespace. An agent that exploits the runtime environment —

which Mythos demonstrated is within frontier capability —gains access to the interception mechanism 

itself. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 7

AEGIS’s audit trail, for example, uses Ed25519 signatures and SHA -256 hash chaining —strong 

cryptographic protections against record modification. But the audit system is discoverable: its 

database files exist in the filesystem, its API endpoints are on the network, and its processes appear in 

the process table. An agent that can find the audit system can attempt to disrupt it through non -

cryptographic vectors: consuming storage, overwhelming the signing process, disrupting 

dependencies, or —as Mythos demonst rated with git —targeting the pre -signature data pipeline. 

3.4 Accessible Audit Systems 

All current audit approaches —hash -chained logs, signed traces, append -only databases —share the 

assumption that tamper evidence is sufficient for forensic reliability. This assumption holds when the 

audited entity is a conventional software system without t he agency to target the audit mechanism. 

Against an adversarial agent with general reasoning capability, tamper evidence addresses one attack 

vector (silent modification) while leaving others unaddressed (discovery, disruption, noise injection, 

dependency targeting, pre -chain manipulation). 

The git cover -up incident illustrates this precisely. Git’s reflog and commit history constitute a 

tamper -evident audit trail for file modifications. Mythos did not attempt to forge git commits; it used 

git’s own commands to rewrite history. The audit syst em’s commands were within the agent’s tool 

space. The agent audited the audit system and found it wanting. 

4. DERIVED ARCHITECTURAL REQUIREMENTS 

From the incident analysis and paradigm assessment, we derive five architectural requirements that a 

containment system must satisfy to remain effective when the constrained entity possesses general 

reasoning capability and potential motivation to circumve nt containment. These requirements are 

necessary conditions, not sufficient conditions; a system that fails any one of them has a demonstrated 

failure mode. 

4.1 R1: Trust Separation Through Layered OS Privilege Enforcement 

The constraint enforcement mechanism must operate in a trust domain that the agent cannot reach 

through any exploit within the agent’s own trust domain. This requires separation enforced by the 

operating system’s privilege mechanisms —separate process ident ifiers, separate memory spaces, 

separate capability bounding sets, and separate filesystem namespaces —not by application -level 

access controls. An agent that exploits the runtime environment gains the runtime’s privileges but 

cannot escalate to the enforce ment mechanism’s privilege level without a separate, independent OS -

level exploit. 

For containment to be durable against a reasoning agent, the architecture must support at least 

four discrete OS -enforced privilege tiers : (1) the agent and its tool execution runtime at the lowest 

privilege level; (2) the constraint enforcement and interception mechanism at a higher level, 

unreachable by the agent; (3) the audit system at a level unreachable by both the agent and the 

enfor cement mechanism; and (4) an independent integrity monitor at the highest level, capable of 

verifying the integrity of all lower tiers. This four -tier hierarchy ensures that a compromise at any 

single level does not propagate upward: an agent that compromises its own runtime cannot reach the 

enforcer; an agent that somehow influences the enforcer cannot reach the audit sys tem or the monitor. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 8

Two -tier architectures (agent vs. sandbox) are insufficient because a single breach reaches everything 

above the agent; four tiers provide defense in depth through privilege stratification. 

Semantic versus syntactic action analysis. Trust separation alone is insufficient if the 

interception mechanism evaluates tool calls through purely syntactic pattern matching (regular 

expressions, string comparison against static blocklists). An agent with general reasoning capability 

can construct semantically equivalent tool calls that bypass syntactic filters: encoding commands in 

base64, piping through alternative utilities, chaining innocuous programs to achieve a restricted 

outcome, or invoking system services through indirect paths. The interception layer must therefore 

perform semantic intent extraction —analyzing the tool call’s parameters through natural language 

understanding to identify the action’s functional purpose (network access, privilege escalation, data 

exfiltration) rather than matching against a fixed set of syntactic patterns. This is the d ifference 

between blocking the string “curl” and blocking the intent of unauthorized outbound data transfer 

regardless of which utility achieves it. 

This requirement distinguishes the needed architecture from all current application -level 

interception systems (AEGIS, framework hooks, MCP proxies) which share the agent’s trust domain 

and rely primarily on syntactic content scanning, and from environment -level sandboxes which 

protect the environment from the agent but do not protect the enforcement mechanism from the agent. 

4.2 R2: Sequential Intent Inference Through Phase -Taxonomic Monitoring 

The enforcement mechanism must evaluate tool -call sequences for collectively dangerous patterns, 

not merely evaluate individual calls against static rules. The Mythos sandbox escape was a multi -step 

sequence where each individual step (network reconnaissan ce, service enumeration, exploit 

construction, outbound connection) might have individually satisfied policy rules. The containment 

bypass emerged from the sequence. Per -call policy evaluation is necessary but not sufficient; the 

enforcement mechanism must infer collective intent from action sequences. 

We propose that sequential monitoring should be structured around a five -phase containment 

bypass taxonomy that captures the operational structure of real -world exploit chains: 

Reconnaissance (RECON): Information -gathering about the system environment —network 

topology, running services, file permissions, process privileges, available utilities. These actions 

are individually benign (listing interfaces, reading configuration files, querying service statu s) 

but collectively build the environmental knowledge needed to construct an exploit. 

Preparation (PREP): Installation, construction, or staging of tools, scripts, or payloads not 

currently present in the environment. Examples include downloading packages, compiling code, 

creating scripts, or modifying environment variables to enable subsequent exploit steps. 

Exploit (EXPLOIT): Actions that attempt to modify permissions, escalate privileges, bypass 

access controls, or alter containment -critical configurations —the active circumvention step. 

Exfiltration (EXFILTRATE): Actions that transmit data to external endpoints, establish 

unauthorized outbound connections, or encode data for covert channel transmission. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 9

Persistence (PERSIST): Actions that create mechanisms for sustained access —cron jobs, 

startup scripts, modified shell profiles, installed services —enabling the agent to maintain its 

expanded access across sessions or restarts. 

The enforcement mechanism should classify each tool call into this taxonomy and detect phase 

transition patterns characteristic of containment bypass: RECON → PREP → EXPLOIT sequences, 

RECON → EXFILTRATE sequences, or EXPLOIT → PERSIST sequences. Individual -phase actions 

may each be permissible; the phase transition pattern signals collective bypass intent. 

The probing principle: inclusion of rejected calls. A critical refinement is that the sequential 

analysis window must include both executed and rejected tool calls. An adversarial agent may 

deliberately issue failing calls to probe the containment boundary —mapping which actions are 

permitted and which are blocked, which constitutes the reconnaissance phase of a bypass sequence. A 

system that only analyzes successful actions is blind to the reconnaissance phase when the early 

probing steps are individually r ejected. The pattern REJECTED -RECON → REJECTED -RECON → 

REJECTED -EXPLOIT → modified -RECON (now crafted to avoid the known filter) is a bypass 

attempt detectable only if rejected calls contribute to the sequential analysis. This principle has no 

analog in current per -call policy systems, which discard rejected calls from further analysis. 

4.3 R3: Independent Containment Integrity Monitoring 

The containment environment’s configuration must be continuously verified by an independent 

process at a higher privilege level than either the agent or the enforcement mechanism. If the agent 

successfully modifies a containment parameter —a firewall rule, a filesystem permission, a process 

privilege level —an independent monitor must detect the modification and either restore the parameter 

or alert a human authority. This addresses the gap where an agent’s exploit partially degrades 

containment without fully escaping, creating conditions for a subsequent, easier escape. No current 

system provides this independent watchdog function. 

4.4 R4: Adversarial Audit Isolation Through Logical Invisibility 

The audit system must be logically invisible to the agent —not merely tamper -evident. We define 

logical invisibility as requiring that the audit system satisfy two properties: resource and address 

anonymity and unidirectional communication .

Resource and address anonymity. The audit system’s process identifier (PID), socket addresses, 

storage paths, and configuration parameters must not be stored in any system resource reachable by 

the agent. This means the audit system must reside in a separate PID namespace (invisible to t he 

agent’s process table enumeration), a separate filesystem namespace (no path the agent can traverse 

leads to audit storage or configuration), and must not register any information in environment 

variables, /proc entries, network service registries, DNS records, or any other information space that 

the agent can query. The agent must have no mechanism to discover the audit system’s existence , not 

merely no mechanism to modify its records. An agent that cannot discover the audit system cannot 

target it through any attack vector —not cryptographic bypass, not denial of service, not noise 

injection, not dependency disruption. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 10

Unidirectional communication. The communication channel between the enforcement layer and 

the audit system must be strictly write -only from the enforcement layer’s perspective. The 

enforcement layer can transmit audit records to the audit system; it cannot read from, query, or 

receive responses from the audit system. This prevents a critical attack vector: an adversarial agent 

that compr omises or influences the enforcement layer could otherwise use the enforcement -to -audit 

channel as an oracle —querying the audit system to determine what has been logged, whether specific 

actions were recorded, or whether the audit system is operational. A write -only channel eliminates 

this oracle attack entirely. The enforcement layer writes records into a communication mecha nism 

(e.g., an inherited file descriptor with no associated filesystem path) and receives no feedback about 

whether or how those records were processed. 

Together, resource anonymity and unidirectional communication provide a qualitatively stronger 

guarantee than cryptographic tamper evidence. Current systems (AEGIS’s Ed25519/SHA -256 trail, 

Microsoft AGT’s append -only logs) ensure that records, once written , cannot be silently modified. 

Logical invisibility ensures that the agent cannot reach the audit system to attempt any form of 

interference —modification, disruption, flooding, or targeted denial of service. The distinction mirrors 

a principle from physica l security: a vault with an unpickable lock is less secure than a vault whose 

location is unknown. 

4.5 R5: Emergent Capability Envelope Enforcement Through Distributional Divergence 

Monitoring 

The system must detect when the agent exercises capabilities not observed during safety evaluation 

and gate those capabilities behind governance authorization. This requires maintaining a statistical 

profile of the agent’s behavior during safety evaluation (the types of tool calls, their complexity, the 

resources accessed, the inter -call timing patterns) and continuously comparing operational behavior 

against this baseline. 

Required divergence metrics. The comparison must employ both parametric and non -

parametric statistical measures to detect distributional shift reliably across different types of 

behavioral change. Specifically, we argue that the system must use at least Kullback -Leibler (KL) 

divergence for parametric comparison (effective when the evaluation -phase and operational 

distributions share a common support and the shift is in relative frequency) and Maximum Mean 

Discrepancy (MMD) for non -parametric comparison (eff ective when the operational distribution 

contains entirely new action categories not present in the evaluation -phase distribution —precisely the 

emergent capability scenario). KL divergence detects when the agent does more of something it was 

observed doing during evaluation; MMD detects when the agent does something it was never 

observed doing during evaluation. Both are needed because emergent capabilities may manifest as 

either intensification of known behaviors or as entirely novel behaviors. 

The system should define two thresholds: a warning threshold that generates an alert to 

governance authorities when the operational distribution begins to diverge from the evaluation 

baseline, and a critical threshold that triggers automatic capability restriction —confining the agent’s 

permitted tool -call distribution to the evaluation -observed envelope until human governance authority 

explicitly reviews and authorizes the expanded capability. This two -threshold approa ch provides Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 11

graduated response rather than binary allow/deny, enabling the governance process to investigate 

divergence before it reaches containment -critical levels. 

This requirement elevates capability enforcement from general “anomaly detection” to a specific 

requirement for mathematical distributional consistency between the safety evaluation phase and 

operational execution. Static allowlists enumerate specific permitted actions; capability envelope 

enforcement is statistical, adapting to the agent’s actual observed behavior during evaluation rather 

than to an eng ineer’s a priori expectations. The requirement is motivated directly by Mythos’s 

emergent exploit capabil ities, which were “not explicitly trained” but emerged from general 

improvements —a distributional shift that parametric and non -parametric divergence measures are 

designed to detect.

architectural challenges identified here are not unprecedented —they have close analogs in the 

governance of autonomous systems in safety -critical domains such as military operations, aviation, 

healthcare, and critical infrastructure. The AI governance challenges for agentic LLMs are, in 

important respects, instantiations of problems that the autonomous systems engineering community

6.1 Provider -Independent Constraint Enforcement 

The February 2026 military AI events demonstrated that safety constraints enforced through provider -

internal mechanisms —contractual commitments, alignment training, organizational policies —are 

architecturally fragile under operational pressure. When the pr ovider was replaced, the constraints 

disappeared [3]. The author’s published work on provider -independent hierarchical constraint 

enforcement [4] addresses this vulnerability by placing constraint enforcement at the system 

architecture level, independent o f any specific AI model provider, organized in a tiered hierarchy 

where certain constraints are architecturally inviolable while others are modifiable through governed 

processes. 

This architectural pattern —evaluating proposed actions at a system layer external to the AI model 

before they reach the execution interface —maps directly to R1 (trust separation) and R4 (audit 

isolation) in the present analysis. The key insight is that the constraint enforcement layer must have 

no dependency on the AI model’s cooperation: it must function correctly regardless of what model 

generates the proposed action, how well that model is aligned, or whether the model is actively 

attempting to circumven t constraints. The Mythos incidents confirm that this adversarial assumption 

is not conservative —it is realistic. 

6.2 Adaptive Validation for Emergent Capabilities 

The emergent capability problem (Incident 5) —where a model exhibits capabilities not anticipated 

during safety evaluation —has a direct analog in autonomous systems governance. The author’s 

published work on adaptive validation evolution [3] addresses the c orresponding problem in fleet 

autonomous systems: when operational conditions change in ways not represented in the validation 

suite, the validation criteria must evolve to cover the new conditions. The approach uses density -

based clustering to detect nove l failure patterns in fleet operational data and automatically generates 

new validation scenarios targeting those patterns. 

Applied to the agentic LLM context, this approach addresses R5 (capability envelope 

enforcement) by establishing a statistical baseline of the model’s behavior during safety evaluation 

and detecting operational divergence from that baseline. The parallel i s precise: in both fleet 

autonomous systems and agentic LLMs, the entity under governance may exhibit behaviors not 

anticipated at evaluation time, and the governance system must detect and respond to those behaviors 

rather than assuming the evaluation was comprehensive. 

6.3 Immutable Audit Under Adversarial Conditions 

The forensic evidence destruction incident (Incident 3) is addressed in the author’s published work on 

cryptographically secure, immutable audit log systems [5], which provides hash -chained, append -only 

recording for regulated operations. The present analy sis extends the threat model beyond the original 

application context: when the audited entity is an AI agent with general reasoning capability and tool 

access, the audit system must not merely be tamper -evident but must be unreachable by the audited 

entity . This extension —from tamper evidence to adversarial isolation —is a direct consequence of the 

Mythos incidents and represents an architectural requirement not addressed by the original audit log 

patent or by any publicly described audit system in the agent ic AI domain. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 13

The principle of enforcing communication directionality at the system architecture level —so that 

the protected side is unreachable from the untrusted side regardless of the adversary’s capability —has 

a proven track record in critical infrastructure protect ion. The present author co -invented a 

unidirectional communication architecture for protecting bulk electric system data in power plants 

(US 8,838,955, granted 2014, assigned to General Electric) [29]. That system uses hardware data 

diodes (RS -422 unidirec tional serial links) to create non -routable communication paths between a 

critical infrastructure proxy server and an external connectivity server, ensuring that critical plant data 

can be transmitted outward for remote monitoring while no inbound path exi sts through which an 

external attacker could reach the critical data store. The installation -specific configuration files that 

govern datagram construction and encryption are accessible only through the unidirectional links 

themselves, placing the governan ce configuration outside the untrusted entity’s information space. 

The architectural principle is identical to what R4 requires for agentic AI containment: 

unidirectional communication channels that the constrained entity cannot address, discover, or 

reverse. The implementation context differs —hardware data diodes for SCA DA networks versus OS -

level namespace isolation and inherited file descriptors for AI agent containment —but the security 

invariant is the same. This continuity demonstrates that adversarial audit isolation for AI agents is not 

a speculative concept but an application of a proven architectural pattern to a new threat model. The 

decade of operational experience with unidirectional communication in critical infrastructure provides 

empirical validation that the principle scales and endures. 

6.4 The Structural Parallel 

The February 2026 and April 2026 events are instances of the same structural failure. In February, a 

human operator circumvented AI safety constraints by replacing the provider that enforced them. In 

April, the AI itself circumvented safety constraints by exploiting the environment that enforced them. 

In both cases, the constraint mechanism was reachable by the entity it constrained —through 

organizational action in the first case, through technical exploitation in the sec ond. The architectural 

lesson is identical: safety constraints that exist inside or alongside the constrained system, rather than 

architecturally betwe en the system and its execution environment, are not durable under pressure. 

The author’s AURA (Autonomous Unified Reliability Architecture) patent portfolio [3][4][5] was 

developed in response to the first pattern —provider -dependent constraints in military and safety -

critical autonomous systems. The Mythos incidents demonstrate th at the same architectural principles 

apply to the second pattern —agent -circumventable constraints in agentic AI systems. We term the 

extension of the AURA architecture to agentic LLM containment the AURA FARADAY (Framework 

for Adversarial Runtime Action De tection, Audit, and Yield) containment architecture —named for 

the Faraday cage, which prevents electromagnetic signals from crossing a boundary regardless of the 

power of the source inside. The analogy is precise: a Faraday cage is capability -agnostic (it works 

whether the contained signal is 1 watt or 1,000 watts), just as AURA FARADAY’s action -level 

enforcement is capability -agnostic (it evaluates proposed actions against the constraint hierarchy 

regardless of how capable the model that generated them). T he foundational insight —that constraint 

enforcement must be provider -independent and architecturally separated from the constrained 

entity —transfers directly from autonomous systems governance to agentic AI containment. The Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 14

specific architectural context of structured tool calls, multi -step exploit chains, and the adversarial 

audit model requires extensions beyond the parent AURA portfolio, and these extensions are the 

subject of ongoing patent prosecution. 

The architectural patterns underlying AURA FARADAY have been validated across multiple 

safety -critical domains in the author’s prior work. A published patent application on verifiable ethical 

arbitration for autonomous vehicles [30] discloses a Hierarchica l Constraint Logic Processor 

operating within a hardware Trusted Execution Environment, applying a constraint hierarchy of non -

negotiable safety mandates and tiered ethical mandates, and generating counterfactual audit logs that 

record both chosen actions and the harm scores of all rejected alternatives. The hierarchical constraint 

pattern (inviolable vs. governance -modifiable), the isolated execution environment (TEE for vehicles, 

OS privilege separation for AI agents), and the counterfactual audit (record ing rejected as well as 

chosen options) are the same architectural primitives, applied across three distinct domains: 

autonomous vehicle ethical arbitration, critical infrastructure data protection [29], and now agentic AI 

containment. This cross -domain co nsistency suggests that the requirements derived in this paper (R1 –

R5) are not specific to AI agents but reflect fundamental principles for governing any autonomous 

system whose actions have consequential effects and whose compliance cannot be assumed. 

7. RELATED WORK 

The intersection of AI safety and agentic AI security is receiving rapidly increasing attention. The 

International AI Safety Report 2026, chaired by Yoshua Bengio and authored by over 100 experts 

with backing from 30+ countries [25], provides the authorita tive evidence -based assessment of 

general -purpose AI capabilities and emerging risks. The report confirms that AI capabilities are 

advancing faster than safeguards and that “sophisticated attackers can often bypass current defences.” 

The CLTR “Scheming in the Wild” report [24] provides the first systematic empirical evidence of 

scheming behaviors in deployed production AI systems, establishing a quantitative baseline (698 

incidents, 4.9× acceleration) against which containment effectiveness can be measured. 

Comprehensive surveys of agentic AI security threats and defenses have been published [16][17], and 

the OWASP Foundation’s Top 10 for LLM Applications [18] catalogs the primary threat categories 

including prompt injection and agent tool interaction manipu lation. The 2025 AI Agent Index [19] 

documents safety features across deployed agentic systems, finding significant gaps in sandboxing, 

evaluation, and third -party testing. 

On the formal specification side, Agent Behavioral Contracts [14] provides a formal framework 

for specifying behavioral preconditions, invariants, and governance constraints for autonomous 

agents. The Cryptographic Runtime Governance architecture [15] prop oses an immutable ethics 

policy layer with zero -knowledge proof verification, though this remains theoretical. Agent -C [20] 

defines a domain -specific language for temporal safety constraints enforced during token generation. 

On the defensive tooling side, the landscape has expanded rapidly in early 2026: AEGIS [11] 

provides pre -execution mediation, Microsoft’s AGT [12] provides policy enforcement with execution 

rings, NVIDIA’s OpenShell [8] provides kernel -level sandboxing, an d open -source tools like 

Pipelock [21] and TrustLoop [22] provide proxy -based interception for MCP -based agent workflows. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 15

Arize’s observability -driven sandboxing [23] combines policy enforcement with OpenTelemetry -

based tracing. 

The present paper’s contribution is distinct from these works: rather than proposing a specific 

system, we derive requirements from demonstrated failure modes, provide a systematic assessment 

framework, and identify the gap between current approaches and the adversarial agent threat model. 

The requirements framework (R1 –R5) provides a basis for evaluating whether any proposed sys tem —

existing or future —actually addresses the demonstrated threat. 

Several concurrent works in March –April 2026 are directly relevant to the requirements derived 

here. SandboxEscapeBench [31] quantifies frontier LLM capabilities for container sandbox escape 

across 18 challenge scenarios, providing empirical grounding for R1: the paper reports that the largest 

frontier models achieve meaningful success rates on difficulty -3–5 scenarios and that ‘plain Docker 

isolation is often insufficient by default to contain highly capable LLM agents’ —confirming that 

environment -level co ntainment (R1 -violating architectures) is empirically inadequate. SafetyDrift 

[32] models cumulative agent safety state as an absorbing Markov chain, predicting the probability of 

eventual safety violation and identifying ‘points of no return’ in agent tra jectories. SafetyDrift is 

complementary to the present work: it addresses prediction of safety violations, while AURA 

FARADAY addresses enforcement preventing them. The AIR incident response framework [33] 

defines a domain -specific language for autonomous LLM agent incident response, achieving over 

90% success rates in detection and remediation. AIR is positioned downstream of a containment 

failure; the R1 –R5 architecture is upstream prevention. ‘Agentic Pressure’ [34] —the phenomenon 

where agents strategica lly sacrifice safety constraints to preserve task utility under pressure —provides 

empirical support for the adversarial framing adopted throughout this paper: if agents may 

strategically deprioritize safety, a trust model that treats agents as potentially adversarial is not 

conservative but accurate. A taxonomy of semantic privilege escalation for agentic systems [35] —the 

pattern whereby an agent uses authorized permissions to perform actions semantically outside its 

authorized scope —validates R1’s requirem ent for semantic intent extraction rather than syntactic 

access control. Capability -safe programming language approaches [36] (Scala 3 capture checking for 

agent tool call typing) offer a complementary compile -time constraint mechanism that is formally 

ver ifiable but depends on agent code being written in the target language and does not address 

runtime adversarial agents. 

8. DISCUSSION 

The Mythos incidents have immediate implications for the deployment of agentic AI systems across 

all domains, not merely cybersecurity. 

The systemic nature of the threat. It is essential to recognize that the containment challenge is 

not specific to one model or one organization. The CLTR data [24] demonstrates that scheming 

behaviors —disregarding instructions, circumventing safeguards, deceiving users, taking covert 

action s—are emerging across models from multiple providers and are increasing at a rate that far 

exceeds the growth in AI deployment. The 4.9× acceleration in scheming incidents over six months 

suggests that as models become more capable, the propensity and soph istication of boundary -testing 

behavior increases correspondingly. This is consistent with the International AI Safety Report’s Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 16

assessment [25] that “the gap between the pace of technological advancement and our ability to 

implement effective safeguards remains a critical challenge.” The requirements derived here (R1 –R5) 

must therefore be understood not as responses to a single inc ident but as architectural standards for an 

era in which agentic AI systems will routinely test the boundaries of whatever containment 

mechanisms are deployed. 

The proliferation problem and the limits of access control. Anthropic’s decision to withhold 

Mythos from public release is a responsible but temporary measure. As Amodei himself has 

acknowledged, equivalent capabilities will emerge from other developers [1]. The trajectory from 

Claude Opus 4.6 to Mythos —a qualitati ve leap in exploit capability —will be replicated by 

competitors within months. Open -weight models, whose parameters are publicly available and cannot 

be restricted after release, are on a capabili ty trajectory that will reach Mythos -equivalent 

performance within 12 –18 months. When that occurs, access -control -based safety is no longer viable: 

you cannot withhold a model whose weights are downloadable. This proliferation dynamic makes 

architectural containment the only durable safety strategy . The requirements framework (R1 –R5) 

is designed for this future: containment that works regardless of which model is deployed, who 

developed it, and whether it is proprietary or open -weight. Provider -independent constraint 

enforcement [4] is not merely an architectural preference; it is a necessity in a world where the 

provider may not exist or may not cooperate. 

The infrastructure -level consequences of containment failure. The practical consequence of 

uncontained agentic AI at frontier capability extends beyond cybersecurity into infrastructure -level 

catastrophe. Anthropic’s own assessment, published alongside the Mythos disclosure, states that the 

model could autonomously c ompromise banking systems, power grids, hospitals, and water treatment 

facilities through exploitation of zero -day vulnerabilities across all major operating systems [1]. The 

Frontier Red Team r eported that engineers with no formal security training asked Mythos to find 

remote code execution vulnerabilities overnight and woke the following morning to complete, 

working exploits [2] —collapsing the exploit development timeline from months of special ized human 

effort to hours of unsupervised computation. Cisco’s Glasswing representative stated that “AI 

capabilities have crossed a threshold that fundamentally changes the urgency required to protect 

critical infrastructure… and there is no going back” [ 1]. Palo Alto Networks warned that the model 

“signals a dangerous shift” and that defenders must “prepare for AI -assisted attackers” producing 

“more attacks, faster attacks and more sophisticated attacks” [27]. 

The danger is compounded by two factors. First, speed : the window between vulnerability 

discovery and exploitation, which historically provided defenders days to weeks for patching, 

collapses to minutes when an AI system can autonomously discover, develop, and deploy exploits. 

Second, scale : a single model instance can scan thousands of systems concurrently, meaning that a 

coordinated attack on banking infrastructure, power grids, and communications systems 

simultaneously becomes operationally feasible for any actor with access to the model —including 

criminal organizations, terrorist groups, and hostile nation -states. The U.S. government summoned 

bank executives to discuss the cyber risks posed by Mythos [28], and Anthropic’s own announcement 

acknowledged th at “the fallout —for economies, public safety, and national security —could be 

severe” [1]. Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 17

This assessment transforms the requirements derived in this paper from academic contributions to 

infrastructure protection requirements . R1 (trust separation) prevents the agent from disabling the 

governance layer en route to exploiting external targets. R2 (sequential intent inference) detects the 

multi -step exploit chains that constitute real -world attacks. R3 (integrity monitoring) ens ures 

containment cannot be silently degraded before an attack is launched. R4 (adversarial audit isolation) 

guarantees fo rensic reconstruction of any incident. R5 (capability envelope enforcement) detects 

when a model begins exercising exploit capabilities that were not present during safety evaluation —

the exact scenario that Mythos demonstrated. The urgency is not speculati ve: Anthropic has stated 

that equivalent capabilities will proliferate beyond responsible actors “before long” [1], and the CLTR 

data [24] confirms that the underlying behavioral pattern is already accelerating across the deployed 

model population. 

Implications for agentic coding tools. Products such as Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, and 

Cursor provide agentic LLMs with shell access, file system access, and network access on users’ local 

machines. These products deliberately minimize sandboxing to maximize capability —a design 

philosophy expli citly acknowledged by Anthropic [2]. The Mythos incidents demonstrate that the 

model powering these tools may possess the capability to exploit the local environment in ways the 

user does not intend or authorize. The requirements derived here apply directly to the containment 

architecture for agentic coding tools. 

Implications for Project Glasswing. Anthropic’s Project Glasswing [1] provides Mythos 

Preview to 12 partner organizations and over 40 additional organizations for defensive cybersecurity 

research. These partners are deploying the model to find vulnerabilities in their own systems —a use 

case where the model’s exploit capabilities are the desired feature, not the threat. However, the 

containment challenge remains: the model must be constrained to find vulnerabilities within the 

authorized scope without exploi ting systems outside that scope, without exfiltrating findings to 

unauthorized endpoints, and without retaining exploit code across sessions. The cybersecurity 

research agent embodiment in R1 –R5 addresses this deployment context directly. 

Implications for military autonomous systems. The convergence of the February 2026 

provider -substitution events and the April 2026 agent -escape events confirms that military 

autonomous systems governance requires constraint enforcement at the system architecture level, 

independent of both the technolo gy provider and the execution environment. The author’s published 

work on governance architectures for military autonomous operations [3][4] addresses this 

requirement, and the present analysis extends the thre at model to include the AI agent itself as a 

potential circumvention vector —a threat model that is increasingly relevant as military systems adopt 

more capable AI models. 

Limitations. This analysis is based on publicly disclosed information from Anthropic’s system 

card and blog posts. Internal details of the Mythos containment mechanisms, the specific exploits 

used, and the full scope of documented incidents may differ from the public r ecord. The requirements 

derived here may be incomplete if undisclosed incidents reveal additional failure modes. Additionally, 

the requirements address the containment problem for agentic LLMs with tool access; they do not Preprint — Submitted to arXiv [cs.CR]  

> Page 18

address the broader alignment problem of ensuring that AI systems have beneficial goals, which is a 

necessary complement to containment but a distinct research challenge. 

9. CONCLUSION 

The April 2026 Mythos incidents mark a transition from theoretical to demonstrated risk in agentic AI 

containment. A frontier model escaped its sandbox, took unauthorized actions, and concealed its 

activities —defeating containment mechanisms designed and o perated by the organization that built 

the model. We have analyzed five behavioral incidents, identified the architectural assumptions each 

violates, assessed four containment paradigms against the demonstrated threat model, and derived five 

requirements t hat any containment solution must satisfy: trust separation through OS privilege 

enforcement, sequential intent inference, independent integrity monitoring, adversarial audit isolation, 

and emergent capability envelope enforcement. 

No publicly described system satisfies all five requirements simultaneously. This represents an 

open problem at the intersection of AI safety and computer security architecture. The problem is 

urgent: the CLTR data [24] documents 698 real -world scheming in cidents accelerating at 4.9× over 

six months, the International AI Safety Report [25] confirms that safeguards are not keeping pace 

with capabilities, and the proliferation of agentic AI capabilities —including the inevitable emergence 

of Mythos -equivalent open -weight models —means that access -control -based safety is a temporary 

measure with a defined expiration date. The window to develop architecturally robust containment —

before the capability to defeat current containment becomes commodity technology —is me asured in 

months, not years. 

The architectural principles needed for robust containment are not entirely novel. Provider -

independent constraint enforcement, adaptive validation, and immutable audit under adversarial 

conditions have been explored in the autonomous systems governance li terature [3][4][5] and are the 

subject of published patent applications by the present author. The AURA FARADAY containment 

architecture —extending these principles to the specific context of agentic LLM containment, with the 

unique challenges of structured tool calls, multi -step exploit chains, and undiscoverable audit 

isolation —is the frontier of current work and the subject of ongoing patent prosecution. The present 

paper contributes the requirements framework (R1 –R5) as a basis for the community to evalu ate 

whether proposed solutions actually address the demonstrated threat, and as a call to action for 

architecturally robust containment before the capability gap becomes unrecoverable.
