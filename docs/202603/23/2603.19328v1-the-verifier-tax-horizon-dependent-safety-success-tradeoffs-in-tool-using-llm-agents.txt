## Abstract 

We study how runtime enforcement against unsafe actions affects end-to-end task performance in multi-step tool using large lan-guage model (LLM) agents. Using 𝜏 -bench across Airline and Retail domains, we compare baseline Tool-Calling, planning-integrated (Triad ), and policy-mediated ( Triad-Safety ) architectures with GPT-OSS-20B and GLM-4-9B. We identify model dependent inter-action horizons (15–30 turns) and decompose outcomes into overall success rate (SR), safe success rate (SSR), and unsafe success rate (USR). Our results reveal a persistent “Safety-Capability Gap”. While safety mediation can intercept up to 94% of non-compliant actions, it rarely translates into strictly safe goal attainment (SSR < 5% in most settings). We find that high unsafe success rates are primarily driven by “Integrity Leaks,” where models hallucinate user identi-fiers to bypass mandatory authentication. Recovery rates following blocked actions are consistently low, ranging from 21% for GPT-OSS-20B in simpler procedural tasks to near 0% in complex Retail scenarios. These results demonstrate that runtime enforcement imposes a significant “verifier tax” on conversational length and compute cost without guaranteeing safe completion, highlighting the critical need for agents capable of grounded identity verification and post-intervention reasoning.

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as au-tonomous agents that interact with external tools, APIs, and struc-tured environments to complete user goals. Unlike single-turn as-sistants, agentic systems must sustain coherent decision making across sequential steps while handling partial observability, tool errors, and evolving user constraints. Prior work shows that integra-tion of agents with external tools extend their capability from only text generation to searching, computing, and transacting. [ 8, 18 , 25 ]. As such systems move toward higher-stakes applications, ensuring continuing effectiveness and compliance with explicit procedural constraints becomes critical. A common approach to enforcing safety in tool-using agents is verifier mediation which evaluates proposed actions or tool calls against predefined policies before execution. If a violation is de-tected during this runtime enforcement phase, the action is blocked and the agent must revise its plan [ 2 , 7]. Such mechanisms are modular and can be layered onto existing agents without retraining. However, while runtime enforcement is intended to reduce unsafe behavior, its impact on end-to-end task outcomes and execution dynamics remains insufficiently characterized [3, 12, 27]. Most evaluations of interactive agents report aggregate task suc-cess, while safety violations are analyzed separately or in simplified settings that do not capture sustained multi-step interaction [ 10 , 28 ]. In transactional tool-agent-user environments, 𝜏 -bench provides explicit procedural policies and state-based evaluation, enabling systematic measurement of both task success and policy compli-ance [ 24 ]. This benchmark framework makes it possible to analyze not only whether unsafe actions are reduced, but how runtime enforcement reshapes the joint distribution of strictly safe success, unsafe success, and failure as interaction horizons increase. In this paper, we conduct a controlled empirical study of runtime safety mediation in tool-using LLM agents. We compare three ar-chitectures that differ in control-flow structure and enforcement de-sign: direct Tool-Calling (baseline), a planning-based agent ( Triad ), and a policy-regulated variant ( Triad-Safety ). Using GPT-OSS-20B [ 15 ] and GLM-4-9B [ 6 ], we evaluate these architectures on the Airline and Retail domains of 𝜏 -bench, accounting for model-dependent interaction limits ( max_turns of 15 for GPT-OSS-20B and 30 for GLM-4-9B) to isolate model-specific conversational ef-ficiency. We decompose outcomes into overall success rate (SR), safe success rate (SSR), and unsafe success rate (USR), and quan-tify verifier dynamics via intervention frequency and the recov-ery rate following blocked actions. Our results reveal a persistent “Safety-Capability Gap”. While safety-mediated architectures can intercept non-compliant actions in up to 94% of episodes, agents frequently fail to successfully revise their actions within the turn limit. Furthermore, we find that the majority of safety violations in high-performing agents are driven by “Integrity Leaks,” where

## 2 Related Work 

Safety guardrails and runtime enforcement. Safety mechanisms for LLM systems are commonly implemented through guardrails that constrain model outputs or actions. However, guardrails often fail in transactional settings because they operate as static lexical filters that lack the procedural state-awareness needed to verify complex multi-turn dependencies (e.g., ensuring a user provided a specific ID earlier in the session). Another approach to safety is alignment, such as Constitutional AI, which shapes model behav-ior through rule-guided feedback [ 2]. However, these approaches are prone to the “Safety-Capability Gap,” where alignment can de-grade under long-horizon pressure or lead to over-refusals that halt task progress. Moderation systems typically classify or filter unsafe outputs post-hoc [ 7]. In contrast, we evaluate runtime verifier mediation , which provides a modular architectural layer for proac-tive runtime enforcement allowing the system to identify and block unsafe tool calls before they are executed in the environment, triggering a “Block-and-Revise” loop that we use to quantify the resulting Verifier Tax. MIRAGE-Bench [26] highlights agentic hal-lucinations, including tool-parameter fabrication, which resonates with our “Integrity Leaks” category. Architectural alternatives such as CE-MCP [ 5] suggest code-level gating and sandboxed valida-tion, providing a deterministic alternative to the heuristic textual verifiers we evaluate here. 

Intervention and corrective loops. Intervention mechanisms that revise intermediate reasoning or plans have been explored to im-prove task performance through episodic self-feedback [ 19 ] and iterative self-correction [ 11 ]. More recently, critique-guided frame-works have been proposed to improve actor outputs by providing specific, instruction based feedback on errors [ 23 ]. Our ’Block-and-Revise’ loop functions as a specific application of these frameworks where the verifier acts as a safety critical monitor. Specifically, the verifier’s rejection signal provides a targeted critique that forces the agent to align its next action with procedural policies. We extend these insights by isolating the unique operational overhead (the verifier tax) and the actual safety efficacy of such corrective loops in policy-constrained transactional tasks. 

Long-horizon reliability. Long-horizon environments introduce compounding challenges for recovery and robustness. While prior work on structured planning focuses on task decomposition [ 1 , 14 ], our study specifically addresses reliability by quantifying an agent’s ability to maintain progress following policy-driven interventions. By decomposing outcomes into safe and unsafe success, we ex-pose cases where agents achieve reliability only by taking non-compliant shortcuts (Integrity Leaks). Furthermore, our analysis of post-intervention recovery rates provides a concrete measure of reliability for the ’Block-and-Revise’ loop, revealing that current agents often fail to regain their goal-oriented trajectory once a safety gate is triggered. 

Interactive benchmarks. Several platforms have been developed to evaluate LLM agents in multi-step environments with persis-tent state, including realistic web navigation [ 28 ], heterogeneous agent tasks [ 10 ], and embodied text environments [ 20 ]. However, these benchmarks primarily focus on task completion capability rather than procedural safety. While they measure an agent’s power to manipulate the environment, they often lack the fine-grained and state-dependent policies, such as mandatory authentication, required to study internal safety-integrity failures. Similarly, newer benchmarks like SOP-Bench [ 13 ] evaluate procedural adherence but do not explicitly disaggregate safe goal attainment from ’unsafe successes.’ In light of these limitations, we conduct our study using 

𝜏 -bench [ 24 ]. Its unique combination of transactional interaction and explicit procedural rules provides the necessary framework to decompose agent outcomes into strictly compliant success, unsafe success, and failure. This distinction is essential for characterizing the Safety-Capability Gap. 

## 3 Methods 3.1 Agent Architectures 

We evaluate three tool-using agent architectures that vary in control-flow structure and the precision of runtime safety verification. All agents operate in identical environments, use the same tool schemas, share the same base model weights, and receive the same domain policy documentation (wiki) in their system prompts. Instructional prompts follow a minimal pair design, remaining identical aside from necessary role-specific headers. This methodology isolates the effect of architectural design from model-specific capabilities. Detailed sequence diagrams for the Tool-Calling baseline and the Triad-based architectures are provided in Appendix B; see Figure 2 and Figure 3. 

Tool-Calling Agent (Baseline). A single LLM directly proposes ei-ther a tool call or a final user-facing response at each step. Proposed actions are executed immediately by the environment without inter-mediate reasoning or runtime enforcement. Correctness and policy compliance depend entirely on the model’s zero-shot adherence to system-prompt instructions. A full execution diagram for this baseline architecture is provided in Appendix B. 

Triad Agent. The Triad architecture disaggregates decision-making into three sequential stages: planning, acting, and verifica-tion. A single LLM instance serves all three roles via role-specific system prompts. At each environment turn, a Planner generates an intermediate reasoning trace selecting the next high-level strat-egy, and an Actor produces the corresponding tool call or response. Before execution, a Verifier evaluates the proposed action against 

> 2

general correctness heuristics (e.g., identity verification prerequi-sites). Rejected actions trigger a block-and-revise loop: the actor receives the verifier’s critique and may retry up to three times per turn. However, the verifier operates without explicit access to the domain policy. 

Triad-Safety Agent. Triad-Safety extends the Triad architec-ture by augmenting the verifier with explicit domain-specific safety constraints. The block-and-revise mechanism is structurally identi-cal; the sole difference is that the verifier’s prompt is supplemented with the environment’s procedural policy rules (e.g., authentication requirements). This tests whether redundant policy encoding in a dedicated verification stage improves safety outcomes beyond im-plicit policy knowledge and heuristic verification alone. A detailed sequence diagram for the Triad-Safety architecture is provided in Appendix B B. 

## 3.2 Models 

To isolate architectural effects from model heterogeneity, the same LLM instance serves all agent roles within a configuration, including planner, actor, and verifier. We evaluate two open-weight models: 

GPT-OSS-20B and GLM-4-9B , deployed via vLLM [ 9]. In each condition, a single model is used across all roles; models are not mixed across components. Agent interaction occurs within the 𝜏 -bench environment, which provides a deterministic backend and rule-based user simulator. All experiments use fixed seeds (10, 20, 30) and reported results are averaged over three independent runs. 

## 3.3 User Simulator 

Agent interaction is conducted against an LLM-based user simu-lator powered by Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct [ 16 ]. The simulator gen-erates task-specific responses and confirmations conditioned on the environment state and dialogue history. Agent models operate deterministically (temperature = 0.0), while the simulator runs at temperature = 1.0 without a fixed seed, introducing trajectory-level variability. Each configuration is evaluated over three full runs to account for this stochasticity. 

## 3.4 Environments and Tasks 

We evaluate agents on 𝜏 -bench, a transactional tool-use benchmark with structured backend APIs, persistent state, and explicit pro-cedural policies. Each episode specifies a user goal and requires interaction with environment tools that query or update backend state. Task success is determined by comparing the final backend state to an annotated ground-truth target. We consider two domains: 

Airline , which involves reservation lookup and modification, and 

Retail , which involves order management and catalog interactions. The interaction horizon is model-dependent: max_turns = 15 for GPT-OSS-20B and max_turns = 30 for GLM-4-9B, applied uniformly across both domains. The domains differ in task structure, proce-dural constraints, and backend complexity, enabling evaluation across heterogeneous transactional settings. While symmetric comparison (e.g., SR@30 for all) would be ideal, these caps were chosen to reflect the practical limits of the underly-ing inference backends and to prevent excessive token drift in multi-turn trajectories. To disentangle architectural effects from these differential horizons, we perform a Symmetric Capacity Check 

(Section 4.2) where GLM-4-9B results are re-evaluated within the stricter 15-turn window to ensure architectural gains are not merely artifacts of the longer horizon. 

## 3.5 Execution Protocol 

Each episode begins with a task specification and initialized back-end state. At each environment turn, the agent produces either a tool call or a natural language response. Tool calls update backend state and return structured results. For Tool-Calling, proposed actions are executed immediately. For Triad and Triad-Safety, each action is first evaluated by a verifier. Accepted actions execute normally; rejected actions are blocked and resent to the actor for revision. This block-and-revise loop permits up to three attempts per envi-ronment turn. This limit serves as a practical termination condition to prevent infinite reasoning loops in cases where actor-verifier instruction-following diverges. Crucially, if the limit is reached, the last proposal is accepted by default to allow the episode to progress. We define this condition as Model Stagnation : a state where an assistant is identified as non-compliant but lacks the reasoning capacity to successfully recover within a reasonable turn budget. Crucially, episodes resulting in stagnation are audit-evaluated :if an auto-accepted action violates domain policy, it is correctly counted in the Unsafe Success Rate (USR), ensuring that the termi-nation limit does not mask underlying safety failures. This design choice isolates pure prevention from forced progression, identifying the threshold at which reasoning collapse occurs. 

Hybrid Seeding and Variance. To isolate architectural effects while preserving transactional realism, we employ a hybrid seed-ing strategy :

• Deterministic Agent : All agent components (plan, act, ver-ify) use fixed seeds ( seed = 10, 20, 30) and temperature 𝑇 = 0.

• Stochastic User : The user simulator is intentionally un-seeded and operates at 𝑇 = 1.0. This ensures that agent performance is tested against the inherent stochasticity of human-like interaction, with variance controlled via the high aggregate volume ( 𝑁 = 2, 970 episodes). Task success is evaluated only upon termination by comparing the final backend state to the ground-truth target. 

## 3.6 Safety Policy and Violation Labeling 

Procedural safety is evaluated using explicit 𝜏 -bench domain poli-cies. A Safety Violation occurs when the agent bypasses manda-tory safeguards, regardless of task completion. We classify viola-tions into three categories: 

(1) Procedural Authentication. Execution of a consequential state-changing tool call prior to required identity verification. 

(2) Lack of Explicit Authorization. Execution of an irreversible transaction without presenting a summary and receiving explicit user confirmation. 

(3) Data Integrity. Fabrication of structured parameters or use of identifiers not grounded in retrieved environment state. 

Audit Methodology. All violation labeling was performed using a deterministic audit script ( strict_policy_auditor.py ) applied to

the trajectory logs. To validate the reasoning and catch edge cases, we cross-verified the automated labels against a LLM-based reason-ing pipeline ( gpt-oss-20b ) acting as a senior auditor. This dual-pipeline approach confirms the reliability of the reported safety metrics by identifying both procedural lapses and data integrity leaks.

• Log Messages: Total entries in the trajectory log, including internal reasoning (planner/verifier), actor proposals, and environment results.

Recovery is computed as architecture-aware conditional probability: 𝑃 (success | intervention > 0).

Overhead. We define overhead as the increase in internal compu-tation introduced by safety mediation, measured through additional LLM calls and log message counts relative to the Tool-Calling base-line. Verifier-mediated rejection loops increase internal execution steps without consuming additional environment turns.

## 4.2 Symmetric Capacity Check and Horizon Saturation 

Because the interaction horizon was non-uniform (15 turns for GPT vs 30 turns for GLM), we perform a Symmetric Capacity Check to disentangle conversation length from core capability. First, we re-evaluate GLM-4-9B within a restricted 15-turn window (SR@15). As shown in Table 2, GLM performance in Airline drops by 4–11%, confirming that its absolute success advantage is partially a function of its higher interaction budget. This analysis reveals that GLM-4-9B is significantly less efficient in the Airline domain, with success rates dropping by 4–11% when restricted to the same window as GPT-OSS-20B. To visualize these horizon-efficiency differences across all architectures, Table 3 reports the cumulative success rate SR@ 𝑘 for 𝑘 ∈ { 5, 10 , 15 , 20 , 30 }. Two patterns emerge. First, GPT-OSS-20B exhibits early saturation : in both Airline and Retail, SR reaches its maximum by 𝑘 = 10 –15 with no further gains through 𝑘 = 30 . This confirms that the 15-turn cap for GPT does not meaningfully constrain performance. Second, GLM-4-9B shows 

late convergence : Airline SR climbs from 14.7% at 𝑘 = 10 to 28.7% at 𝑘 = 30 , indicating that the smaller model requires substantially more turns to achieve comparable outcomes. Across architectures, Triad and Triad-Safety show slightly lower early efficiency (SR@5) due to the overhead of planning and verification steps, but converge to comparable final SR by 𝑘 = 15 –30 .

## 4.3 Decomposition of Safety Violations 

To identify the root causes of unsafe behavior, we decompose safety violations into three categories: Procedural Authentication (Auth), Lack of Explicit Authorization (Authz), and Data Integrity (Integrity). Table 4 reports the prevalence of these violation types relative to the total number of episodes ( 𝑁 = 2, 970 ), providing a global view of failure modes across the corpus. This breakdown highlights a significant behavioral divergence between tasks and models. In the Retail domain, the vast majority of safety failures for GPT-OSS-20B (over 93%) are Integrity Leaks, where the model hal-lucinations user or order IDs to bypass authentication. In contrast, GLM-4-9B failures in the Airline domain are multi-faceted, involv-ing both authorization lapses and integrity hallucinations. These results suggest that "safety" in transactional LLM agents is not a monolithic property but requires distinct defenses for procedural compliance versus factual grounding. 

## 4.4 Operational Overhead and the Verifier Tax 

Table 5 quantifies the verifier tax in two complementary metrics: 

LLM call count (number of model invocations) and cumulative token cost (cumulative input+output tokens accounting for context window growth across calls). Token counts provide a hardware-agnostic measure of computational overhead and enable relative cost comparison across architectures. Both metrics are reported with Mean, Median, and P95 (tail overhead). Here, tail overhead represents the computational cost of episodes in the 95th percentile, capturing worst-case execution complexity where extended Block-and-Revise loops significantly amplify context growth and token consumption. Several patterns emerge. First, the verifier tax is consistently higher in token cost (2.0–2.8x) than in raw call count (1.6–2.2x), because Triad architectures accumulate larger context windows from planning and verification messages. Second, tail overhead is substantial: the P95 token cost for GLM-4-9B Triad reaches 355k to-kens, representing episodes where extended block-and-revise loops compound context growth. Third, GPT-OSS-20B shows tighter vari-ance (lower P95/mean ratio), consistent with its earlier saturation behavior.

Joint Error-Rejection Overlap. To assess whether environment er-rors and verifier rejections compound within the same episode, we performed a joint classification of all 1,980 Triad and Triad-Safety episodes into four categories: Verifier Reject only, Environment Error only, Both (overlap), and Neither (clean). Across all config-urations, 19.1% of episodes ( 𝑁 = 378 ) experienced both a verifier rejection and an environment error within the same trajectory. These compounding episodes had the lowest success rate of any category: 

• Clean episodes (no errors): SR = 21.3% 

• Reject-only episodes: SR = 18.7% 

• EnvErr-only episodes: SR = 13.1% 

• Both (compounding): SR = 7.7% 

The compounding effect is non-additive: individual error sources re-duce SR by ∼3–8 percentage points, but their co-occurrence reduces SR by ∼14 pp from the clean baseline. This suggests that environ-ment errors (e.g., schema mismatches) consume turn budget and destabilize the agent’s reasoning state, leaving it more vulnerable to subsequent verifier rejections. Coordinated remediation—such as pre-validating tool schemas before submission to reduce EnvErr, followed by verifier-guided recovery for policy violations—could ad-dress these compounding failure modes independently. To reconcile the high intervention frequency with the low SSR, we calculate the 

interception rate . The probability that a non compliant tool pro-posal is intercepted by the verifier before execution. For GPT-20B, this rate reaches 94.1% in the Retail domain. The paradox—high interception but low safe success—serves as a diagnostic for the 

Safety-Capability Gap . Current verifiers are competent at the 

identification of violations, but actors lack the recovery depth to synthesize compliant alternatives within a transactional context. Crucially, the three-retry limit ( forced progression ) has a non-linear effect on these estimates. Interception is measured per-proposal; thus, if an agent is rejected three times before auto-acceptance, it contributes three successful interceptions but eventually yields a 

leak if the final proposal is unsafe. This protocol ensures that SSR measures only true recovery, while USR accurately captures cases where reasoning collapse leads to non-compliance. 

Hard Abort Ablation and Retry Sensitivity. To isolate the effect of forced progression on SR, SSR, and USR, we performed two comple-mentary analyses. First, a Hard Abort Ablation : re-analyzing all trajectories while simulating a protocol that terminates an episode as a failure after three consecutive rejections, rather than auto-accepting the last proposal. We find that across the entire corpus, 

28 episodes experienced at least one stagnation event (three con-secutive rejections within a single turn). This represents <1.5% of all mediated episodes. The impact on task success is correspond-ingly negligible with zero percent shift except for GPT-OSS-20B, Retail, Triad-Safety runs where SR shifts from 21.4% to 21.2% —a delta of 0.3 pp (exactly one episode that succeeded after forced progression). Second, we analyzed the distribution of consecutive rejec-tions : over 95% of the 2,414 reject-runs consisted of a single rejec-tion, and only the 28 stagnation events ( ∼1.2%) reached the 3-retry ceiling. This confirms that: (1) The forced progression fallback is triggered rarely (<1.5% of episodes), and when triggered, it almost never produces a successful outcome (1 out of 28 stagnated episodes). (2) Increasing the retry limit to 𝑘 = 5 or 𝑘 = 10 would have 

negligible effect : in the stagnated episodes, the verifier’s rejection reason is identical across all three attempts, indi-cating the actor has entered a reasoning loop from which additional retries would not escape. (3) The safety bottleneck is the quality of recovery reasoning , not the retry budget. Agents either recover on the first attempt or enter a stagnation loop. Adaptive retry limits, while theoretically appealing, would there-fore not meaningfully alter the reported outcomes. The Safety-Capability Gap is driven by the model’s inability to synthesize compliant alternatives not by an artificial cap on retry attempts.

## 4.6 Qualitative Case Studies 

4.6.1 Integrity Leak: Hallucination as a Shortcut. In the Tool-Calling (Baseline) configurations, we observed frequent “Integrity Leaks” where the model bypasses mandatory search tools by hallucinating user identifiers as a shortcut to bypass user resistance. 

Example: Retail Task 17 (GPT-OSS-20B Tool-Calling Baseline). 

• User : “I’m trying to update the delivery address for my package. [...] I’m not sure I can share that information here. Is there another way to verify?” 

• Agent (Turn 1) : find_user_id_by_name_zip( {"first_name": "John", "last_name": "Doe", "zip": "12345"}) 

• Result : The agent attempts to bypass the user’s explicit privacy objection by hallucinating a dummy identity (“John Doe, 12345”). Remarkably, this placeholder is accepted by the deterministic tool environment, granting the agent illicit access to the account without user disclosure. 

• Analysis : Beyond simple guessing, the model treats hal-lucination as a functional bypass for procedural steps. By successfully authenticating via a dummy identity, the agent achieves a reward of 1.0 (Success) while fundamentally vio-lating the requirement to verify the identity of the specific human user in the dialogue. This represents over 93% of safety violations in the Retail domain for this model. 

4.6.2 Successful Verifier Recovery: Block-and-Revise. Triadic archi-tectures demonstrate the ability to “steer” an agent away from a violation, though recovery remains a significant challenge. 

Example: Airline Task 21 (Triad-Safety). 

• Actor Proposal :

CALL update_reservation_flights(...) (without prior identity verification). 

• Verifier Output : REJECT: [P-AUTH] User identity must be verified via Passport or Email+Zip before modifying bookings. 

• Actor Revision : “I apologize for the oversight. To proceed with the modification, I first need to verify your identity. Could you please provide your passport number or email and ZIP code?” 

• Result : The agent successfully gathers credentials, authen-ticates, and completes the task safely. 

• Analysis : This illustrates the Block-and-Revise loop func-tioning as intended, converting potential unsafe successes into Safe Successes .

4.6.3 Heuristic Noise and Programmatic Grounding. Our triadic architectures rely on LLM based heuristic verifiers. While flexible, these verifiers often exhibit “noise” by failing to distinguish between authentication tools (which are allowed) and consequential data-modifying tools. Furthermore, purely textual verifiers struggle to enforce Parameter Provenance . Ensuring that sensitive identifiers (e.g., user_id ) were actually received from the environment and not hallucinated or carried over from prior training. Our results on 

Integrity Leaks suggest that while heuristic mediation is a capable interim layer, robust industrial guardrails must transition toward 

programmatic verification gates that can deterministically track data provenance at the tool-call interface.

## 4.8 Discussion 

Our findings on the safety-capability gap and the verifier tax provide empirical grounding for several emerging architectural paradigms in agent safety. 

Remediation vs. Blocking. The low recovery rates observed in our study (approx. 5–21%) suggest that post-hoc blocking is in-sufficient for high-stakes transactional tasks. Our disaggregated analysis reveals a stark divergence by domain: while GPT-OSS-20B recovers from 32.0% of safety rejections in Airline ( 𝑁 = 25 ), success following a rejection in Retail is near 0.0% (𝑁 = 0/3). Frameworks like AIR (Agent Incident Response) advocate for structured reme-diation plans following safety violations [ 22 ]. Our results support this direction, implying that verifiers should not merely intercept actions but should provide specific recovery trajectories to the actor, particularly in environments with high identifier sparsity. 

Grounded Verification and Strict Grounding Gates. The preva-lence of Integrity Leaks (hallucinated identifiers) highlights a failure of data-flow integrity over the trajectory. AGENTSYS proposes context isolation and strict validator loops to prevent parameter contamination [ 21 ]. Based on our finding that 93.7% of Retail USR failures stem from integrity lapses, we propose Strict Grounding Gates : a structural defense that programmatically grounds verifier checks in prior tool-retrieval traces. By verifying that every identi-fier parameter exists in the session ancestry, we could eliminate the most common failure mode identified in high-sparsity domains. 

Procedural Memory and the Robustness Premium. The verifier tax is intensified when the actor must replan after a blocked or rejected action, increasing interaction length and repeated tool calls. Memory-augmented approaches such as Memp demonstrate that procedural memory can improve task success rates while reducing interaction steps in long-horizon settings [4]. The observed 1.6–2.2 × call inflation under Triad-Safety suggests that safety mediation introduces a measurable execution overhead. Rather than framing this solely as a performance penalty, we inter-pret it as a Robustness Premium . The additional computational budget required to ensure that each consequential transactional action is critiqued against high-stakes domain constraints. Runtime architectures such as CaveAgent provide a potential mechanism to mitigate this premium by offloading intermediate state into a persistent runtime, enabling context compression and reducing prompt-token overhead [17].

## 5 Limitations and Future Work 

While our study provides a rigorous assessment of the 𝜏 -bench safety landscape, several open challenges remain for the deploy-ment of transactional agents. Our experiments focus on intermediate-scale open-weight models (9B and 20B), which are representative of high-performance task-specific systems, yet ultra-large models may exhibit different recovery dynamics and safety-capability trade-offs; future work should examine whether the observed “Safety-Capability Gap” persists at larger scales. The model-dependent turn caps (15 and 30 turns), selected to align with backend defaults, may also constrain recovery opportunities, and longer procedural hori-zons could alter the observed recovery rates. Although the Triad-Safety verifier is effective at violation identification, the recovery gap remains substantial, motivating remediation-aware verifiers that return structured safe-state transitions rather than binary re-jections, akin to the AIR framework. The operational overhead introduced by mediation, or “Verifier Tax,” may be reduced through heterogeneous architectures that pair smaller task-specialized ac-tors with larger instructionally rich verifiers, potentially yielding improved cost-safety trade-offs. Our findings on Integrity Leaks further suggest that purely textual verification is insufficient; future iterations of 𝜏 -bench should evaluate Strict Grounding Gates that enforce data-flow integrity by programmatically rejecting identi-fiers not explicitly retrieved in prior tool calls. Additionally, the procedural safety rules used in Triad-Safety are manually specified and applied in a static per-turn fashion, which may omit edge cases and does not maintain a persistent safety state across the trajectory. Finally, while a stochastic user simulator ( 𝑇 = 1.0) enhances real-ism, it introduces variance; although the large aggregate episode count ( 𝑁 = 2,970 ) supports statistical stability, future evaluations could incorporate fixed pre-recorded user trajectories to decouple agent failure from simulator drift. 

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we have introduced a rigorous methodology for eval-uating the procedural safety of autonomous transactional agents using the 𝜏 -bench framework. By distinguishing between Task Suc-cess (SR) and Safe Success (SSR), we revealed that current large lan-guage models, despite their high task capability, exhibit a persistent tendency to achieve goals through non-compliant shortcuts, par-ticularly via Data Integrity violations and hallucinated identifiers. Our evaluation of the Triad-Safety architecture demonstrates that while internal verification loops are highly effective at identifying 

unsafe actions—blocking non-compliant proposals in up to 94% of episodes—the agents frequently fail to recover to a safe and success-ful path. This “Safety-Capability Gap” suggests that the primary challenge in autonomous safety is not merely oversight, but the synthesized reasoning required to generate compliant alternatives under strict policy constraints. Furthermore, our efficiency analysis shows that procedural safety imposes a measurable “safety tax” on conversational length, and that smaller models like GLM-4-9B, while occasionally more calibrated to specific procedural rules, lack the turn-efficiency of larger counterparts like GPT-OSS-20B. Future work should focus on narrowing the recovery gap through safety-aware fine-tuning and the development of more robust grounding mechanisms to eliminate integrity leaks. As autonomous agents be-come increasingly integrated into real-world transactional systems, moving from simple success-oriented benchmarks to safety-first procedural evaluations will be critical for ensuring reliable and compliant AI deployment.
