Empowering large language models with long-term memory is crucial for building agents that adapt to users’ evolving needs. However, prior evaluations typically interleave preference-related dialogues with irrelevant conversations, reducing the task to needle-in-a-haystack retrieval while ignoring relationships between events that drive the evolution of user preferences. Such settings overlook a fundamental characteristic of real-world personalization: preferences emerge gradually and accumulate across interactions within noisy contexts. To bridge this gap, we introduce PERMA , a benchmark designed to evaluate persona consistency over time beyond static preference recall. Additionally, we incorporate (1) text variability and (2) linguistic alignment to simulate erratic user inputs and individual idiolects in real-world data. PERMA consists of temporally ordered interaction events spanning multiple sessions and domains, with preference-related queries inserted over time. We design both multiple-choice and interactive tasks to probe the model’s understanding of persona along the interaction timeline. Experiments demonstrate that by linking related interactions, advanced memory systems can extract more precise preferences and reduce token consumption, outperforming traditional semantic retrieval of raw dialogues. Nevertheless, they still struggle to maintain a coherent persona across temporal depth and cross-domain interference, highlighting the need for more robust personalized memory management in agents. Our code and data are open-sourced at https://github.com/PolarisLiu1/PERMA.

1 Introduction 

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) [ 15 , 22 , 71 ] has catalyzed a shift from static knowledge access paradigms to interactive agents capable of sustained engagement [ 83 ]. In this new paradigm, long-term memory is no longer merely a tool for knowledge retrieval, but a prerequisite for maintaining coherent user representations across interactions. A reliable memory-equipped agent not only stores past information, but continuously constructs and updates a consistent persona state that reflects users’ constraints and behavioral tendencies over time [20]. Despite these requirements, inherent limitations in context window size and token costs present significant bottle-necks [ 47 , 51 , 76 ]. To mitigate these challenges, recent progress in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [ 12 , 32 , 33 , 75 ]has bolstered LLMs’ capacity to extract relevant factual knowledge [ 5 , 31 , 64 , 84 ]. To further refine retrieval quality, several approaches have introduced structured memory indices [ 63 , 65 , 66 ], such as tree- [ 44 , 46 ] and graph-based [ 6, 16 , 34 , 56 ]representations. Complementing these retrieval techniques, reinforcement learning (RL) has been incorporated to optimize memory utilization in long-context settings [ 37 , 69 , 73 ]. Beyond generic storage mechanisms, personalization-oriented agents leverage modular memory architectures and context-aware memory structures. These agents emphasize capturing user profiles and preferences to inform downstream conditioning for long-horizon planning and lightweight deploy-ment [ 10 , 29 ]. While these architectural innovations endow agents with the potential for long-range memory, the method for quantifying their personalized performance within complex, noisy real-world scenarios remains an open challenge. Along this line, empirical studies [ 23 , 80 ] have underscored the inherent difficulties in verifying user-centric personal-ization, a task typically benchmarked by preference recall fidelity and robustness to token-level noise across extended interactions [ 36 ]. While current LLMs excel at factual retention [ 55 ], effective personalization necessitates a paradigm shift [ 2, 17 ]: treating user preferences not as static attributes, but as evolving behavioral patterns that must be contextually inferred and extracted during ongoing task interactions [ 24 , 68 ]. Thus, the primary bottleneck is not simple memorization, but the interpretation , integration , and retrieval of preference signals that are often latent, incrementally expressed, or obscured by conversational noise. Unfortunately, as illustrated in Figure 1 (a), existing evaluation frameworks remain insufficient for assessing the longitudinal consistency of persona understanding, due to three limitations: (1) Preference-Centric Framing : Existing protocols [ 80 ] construct the origins of preferences using static statements from a user perspective, rather than capturing the adaptive nature of preferences through iterative feedback. Such a formulation turns the conversation into a preference briefing, thereby overlooking how preferences manifest through event-driven dialogues. Consequently, the resulting evaluation ignores the interactive nuances of real-world deployment, failing to represent noise-rich scenarios such as ambiguous requests [ 27 ]. (2) Static User Modeling : As preferences are predefined prior to dialogue, models are evaluated based on isolated user preferences [ 11 , 80 ], treating them as static snapshots and neglecting cross-session dependencies that are essential for assessing agents’ ability to reason over continuous interactions. It remains difficult to verify whether a model can achieve the cross-domain synthesis and retrieval required for complex queries [ 23 ], often leading retrieval-based approaches to appear effective under recall-oriented settings while their performance is overestimated [ 62 ]. (3) Outcome Assessment : Ideally, a memory-augmented agent should progressively integrate interaction histories and user preferences to enhance the efficiency of subsequent sessions, thereby

Current evaluation paradigms primarily focus on the capabilities of long-context models and assess performance mainly based on the final LLM outputs, conflating memory retrieval with the generation process in memory systems rather than evaluating them separately. As a result, the quality and efficiency of the underlying memory systems, as well as memory utility in interactive scenarios, remain unexamined. These limitations highlight the need for alignment between preference benchmark construction and real-world interaction scenarios. To this end, we introduce PERMA , a benchmark designed to evaluate the maintenance of persona states, defined as a combination of dynamic user preferences and episodic memory. PERMA models personalization as a temporally evolving state derived from interaction events, where each session addresses specific task requirements within a targeted topic. Through this process, fine-grained preference details are not explicitly given as standalone statements, but are inferred from how users articulate requests, provide feedback, and refine constraints across turns. These extracted preference signals, together with interaction history, are integrated into the evolving persona state, progressively enriching the user profile. As illustrated in Figure 1 (b), we construct the interaction histories of representative users across 10 countries and diverse age demographics, spanning over 20 distinct topics and extending the scope to include 2,166 preference details. As shown in Table 1, the corpus contains upwards of 800 events, with style-aligned contexts totaling 1.8M tokens. To ensure both structural coherence and linguistic realism, we adopt a two-stage generation process as illustrated in Figure 2. A timeline generation agent first transforms these events into detailed descriptions and objectives necessary for dialogue reconstruction. Events are categorized into “emergence” and “supplement” types to distinguish between the emergence of new preferences and the refinement of existing ones across different topics. We benchmark a diverse set of standalone LLMs and agents equipped with memory systems, analyzing their effectiveness in contextualizing user preferences and retrieving relevant memory segments. Furthermore, we conduct probing evaluations at various temporal intervals along the dialogue timeline to examine how performance evolves as persona states accumulate and potentially drift. This comprehensive analysis offers critical insights into the underlying mechanics of memory systems and their capacity to sustain coherent persona states under realistic, long-term interaction conditions. Our contributions are summarized below: (1) We introduce a novel benchmark for evaluating LLM agents that shifts the paradigm from static preference matching to event-driven dialogue scenarios. (2) We formulate a dynamic user modeling evaluation protocol that tests an agent’s ability to maintain and update persona states. 2 Related Work 2.1 Evolution of Personalization Memory Benchmarks 

2.1.1 Evaluating Long-Context Memory Capabilities of LLMs. The evaluation of long-context capabilities in LLMs [ 58 , 70 , 72 ] has historically relied on “Needle-in-a-Haystack” style tests that measure a model’s ability to retrieve facts embedded within lengthy contexts [ 30 , 40 ]. While early benchmarks were limited to 10k-token interactions, recent efforts [ 36 , 49 , 51 ], such as LongMemEval [ 61 ] extend evaluation to ultra-long multi-session dialogues ranging from 115k

2.1.2 Towards Personalization in Memory. At the core of personalized interaction and lifelong digital compan-ions [ 62 ] lies the concept of user preferences [ 9 , 43 , 77 ], defined as the longitudinal collection of a user’s stable interests, stylistic inclinations, and task-specific constraints [ 18 ]. For a personalized agent [ 21 , 26 , 68 ], these preferences constitute a latent state that must be captured from historical interactions to guide subsequent response generation. This ensures that agent behavior aligns with individual expectations without necessitating repetitive, explicit instructions. The fundamental necessity of preference modeling is to alleviate the “interaction burden” — empowering agents to proactively anticipate user needs from historical context, thereby enhancing efficiency and user satisfaction in real-world applications. Broadly, personalization benchmarks have evolved from (i) static preference conditioning to (ii) multi-session pref-erence retention, yet few explicitly evaluate whether models can maintain a coherent persona state under evolving, information-dense interactions. Early frameworks, such as PrefEval [ 80 ] and PerLTQA [ 9], characterized personalization as conditional response generation, focusing on an LLM’s capacity for long-context understanding, user modeling and adherence to a constrained set of stated preferences. These studies revealed that contemporary LLMs struggle to infer implicit preferences accurately and apply them consistently across conversational turns, particularly when intervening dialogues introduce unrelated topics as “contextual token noise” [ 48 ]. Despite these advancements, it remains designed for “Needle-in-a-Haystack” tasks, in which isolated preferences are retrieved from vast volumes of unrelated context. To enhance the recognition of personalized intent, UserBench [ 41 ] and PersonaLens [ 82 ] introduced interactive, gym-like environments. These benchmarks advanced the field toward simulating real-world agent dynamics; however, they treat user profiles as fixed inputs rather than evolving constructs. A pivotal shift toward memory retention occurred with PersonaMem [ 23 ], which incorporated multi-session interaction histories. PersonaMem centers on preference-driven dialogue construction; consequently, its dialogues resemble subjective opinion sharing rather than objective problem solving. Subsequently, PersonaMem-v2 [ 24 ] further advances this paradigm by focusing on implicitly expressed prefer-ences and requiring LLMs to infer user traits from subtle conversational nuances. It assumes that implicit behavioral cues can be consistently abstracted into stable preference variables. However, in realistic interactions, many implicit signals are situational and do not necessarily correspond to enduring persona states. These frameworks still evaluate preference recognition and recall within constructed interaction traces, without explicitly probing the stability, cross-domain syn-thesis, and temporal consistency of the underlying persona state. Recent efforts, such as the KnowMe-Bench [ 62 ], use long-form narratives as a data substrate to evaluate evidence-grounded inference about motivation, identity development, and psychological states. Yet KnowMe-Bench is better characterized as a narrative-reasoning benchmark rather than as an assessment of personalized conversational user-assistant interaction or dialogue management. To address these limitations, PERMA introduces a dynamic evaluation paradigm centered on active dialogue man-agement. We shift the evaluative focus from single-point preference recall to composite synthesis, requiring memory systems to integrate interdependent traits across information-dense sessions to resolve complex queries. By employing an event-driven methodology, our framework captures the evolutionary nature of user profiles; specifically, the interplay between newly emerged and updated preferences, while moving beyond “black-box” long-context reasoning evaluation through a decoupled assessment of retrieved memory quality and generative reasoning. As a result, PERMA reframes personalization evaluation from preference identification to persona state maintenance across temporally ordered events

2.2 From Static Retrieval to Persistent Memory 

Personalization in LLMs has undergone a fundamental architectural shift driven by the growing mismatch between transient context conditioning and the requirements of long-term user–assistant relationships [ 28 , 68 ]. Early approaches implicitly equated personalization with prompt augmentation, assuming that user history could be sufficiently represented within a fixed or expanded context window. However, this assumption fails at scale: attention-based architectures exhibit context dilution and the “lost-in-the-middle” phenomenon, rendering long-term user modeling inefficient [30]. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) [ 12 , 17 , 33 ] reframed this problem by externalizing memory into non-parametric storage. By enabling semantic access to large repositories of user-related information, RAG alleviated context window constraints and improved factual grounding. Yet this architectural move introduced a new limitation: memory was reduced to static, document-like artifacts retrieved reactively based on semantic similarity [ 27 , 42 ]. In doing so, RAG systems remain inherently stateless, primarily serving as indexing mechanisms for user-assistant dialogue rather than modeling the temporal evolution and coherence of user personas [38]. Unlike passive retrieval in vanilla RAG, agentic frameworks utilize LLMs as reasoning engines to orchestrate tool use [ 59 , 74 ] and multi-step planning [ 78 ]. However, for an agent to exhibit consistent personality and long-term adaptation, it requires a persistent memory system, which functions as the agent’s long-term storage. Recent work [ 8, 57 , 67 , 79 ]has begun to formalize this transition by treating memory as a structured, lifecycle-aware subsystem. Rather than storing immutable embeddings, memory architectures introduce mechanisms for consolidation, reinforcement, decay, and reorganization [ 20 , 45 ]. This marks a shift from storage-centric memory to process-oriented memory, in which the

Memory systems that support fine-grained updates recognize that personalization must operate at multiple levels, including episodic experience, user preferences, and behavioral patterns. MemOS [ 29 ] was among the first to formalize memory as a core system component, introducing a lifecycle management mechanism (MemCube) that enables dynamic memory scheduling, aging out obsolete facts while reinforcing relevant ones over long horizons. Similarly, Mem0 [ 6] is a scalable memory -centric architecture that dynamically extracts, consolidates, and retrieves salient conversational information from ongoing interactions, supporting persistent, structured memory beyond raw document retrieval. Memobase focuses on extracting and maintaining structured user profiles and tagged memory records from chat interactions, serving as a scalable backend for personalized applications. Supermemory provides a unified memory API that builds structured relationships between facts to support persistent recall and relational context beyond simple vector proximity. Compared to existing benchmarks (Table 3), the differentiation of PERMA lies in the multi-dimensional decoupling of its evaluation. While LLMs and memory systems have improved in memory capacity, they still lack a fine-grained characterization of event-driven persona state evolution, which is essential for reflecting the mastery of user preferences. Furthermore, by integrating context noise and temporal probing, PERMA enables us to verify the coherence persona state within complex event streams, independent of mere interaction quality.

4 Methodology 

In this section, we introduce PERMA . Rather than treating personalization as static preference recall, our framework models personalization as evolving preferences through an event-driven reconstruction of dialogue histories (Section 4.1). To better reflect real-world user-assistant interactions, Section 4.2 introduces two interaction variants by (1) injecting text variability based on common user prompt biases [ 53 ], (2) aligning query linguistic styles with real-world data. We evaluate downstream queries that do not explicitly trigger preference retrieval, thereby testing whether agents can proactively leverage stored information to integrate and enforce composite, cross-domain preferences (Section 4.3).

4.2 Alignment with Real-World Interactions 

In event-driven persona state modeling, the inherent noise and disfluencies of real-world interactions present significant hurdles. For example, user utterances can be vague, and preferences are often fragmented across multiple messages. While prior research [ 23 , 80 ] has introduced preference updates across multiple sessions, we aim to incorporate a broader spectrum of text variability observed in human prompts for LLM [53], as well as individual idiolects. 

4.2.1 Text Variability Injection. In natural interactions, user inputs frequently contain vague expressions, inconsistent preferences, or context switch, as illustrated in Table 2. Such phenomena challenge personalized memory agents not only in retrieving relevant information but also in integrating histories and composing accurate persona-state signals. Specifically, we perturb user turns with contextually grounded in-session noise that preserves conversational plausibility. 4.2.2 Linguistic Alignment. Real-world user inputs exhibit individual idiolects. In contrast, synthetic conversations often follow standardized templates, which simplifies retrieval and artificially inflates evaluation scores, especially when the generator and the agent share the same underlying model. To prevent models from exploiting overt lexical cues, we construct user prompts to mimic the authentic linguistic patterns observed in real-world interaction corpora. Specifically, we randomly sample real-world conversations from WildChat [ 81 ] for each user prompt in the Clean 

setting, and instruct an LLM to align the linguistic style of the synthetic user prompt with the real data, while preserving the underlying user intent. In addition, we interleave WildChat data as irrelevant distractors within the global timeline, extending the total context up to 128k tokens. This design challenges models to infer persona states from diverse, individualized linguistic patterns across long-context sessions, thereby increasing the tasks’ complexity. Together, text variability and linguistic alignment ensure that our evaluation extends beyond idealized preference recall. Instead, PERMA probes the model’s capacity to maintain coherent persona-state tracking even when confronted with pervasive ambiguity and authentic linguistic variability. 

4.3 Evaluation Protocol 

To systematically evaluate persona state tracking across temporal depths and contextual complexities, PERMA adopts a multi-dimensional evaluation protocol. This protocol integrates temporally controlled task insertion to evaluate cross-session evolution, positional probing within long contexts to assess retrieval robustness, and comprehensive metrics that capture both factual correctness and persona consistency. 

4.3.1 Evaluation Checkpoints (Type 1/2/3). Checkpoint tasks are strategically inserted into the global timeline to probe distinct stages of persona-state development. Type 1 tasks are positioned before any domain-relevant events occur, serving as a zero-memory baseline to disentangle the agent’s parametric bias from its persona-based responses. Type 2 tasks are introduced immediately following the emergence of all relevant domains to assess peak recall. Finally, Type 3 tasks are placed at the end of the global timeline, after multiple intervening sessions. At this stage, the persona state is fully developed but heavily occluded within a substantially longer and noisier history. 

4.3.2 Contextual Position Probing. In addition to temporal stage control, we probe evaluation tasks at varying positions within contexts (e.g., 10%–100% event segments of the histories). By measuring performance as a function of this relative distance, we analyze how different personalized memory agents balance retrieval efficiency and contextual 

> Manuscript submitted to ACM

PERMA : Benchmarking Personalized Memory Agents 13 scanning. This positional probing provides a fine-grained view of how persona state utilization degrades or stabilizes as context length increases. 

4.3.3 Metrics and Assessment Dimensions. Evaluation is conducted along three primary dimensions: task perfor-mance, memory fidelity and efficiency. 

Task Performance. We evaluate system performance in both one-shot and interactive settings. In the former, we employ Multiple-Choice Question (MCQ) to measure selection accuracy ( MCQ Acc. ). The MCQ options are generated by ablating three core aspects: (i) Task Completion, indicating the fulfillment of defined goals; (ii) Preference Consistency, ensuring responses are grounded in long-term preferences without hallucinating unsupported inferences; and (iii) Informational Confidence, identifying whether the model maintains a decisive stance without uncertainty (see Figure 18 for details). By enumerating all binary combinations of three aspects, we yield eight distinct evaluation categories. To better approximate real-world usage, we introduce an interactive evaluation using an LLM-based user simulator (see Figure 16 for the prompt). The simulator is granted access to the ground-truth dialogue history and relevant preference annotations. It terminates the session by outputting “TERMINATE” only when the task is successfully completed while satisfying all user preferences. Otherwise, it provides supplementary feedback, and the dialogue continues. We report the success rate at Turn=1 and Turn ≤2. Compared to the one-shot setting, the interactive evaluation not only assesses open-text responses but also distinguishes suboptimal memory (resolvable with a single feedback round) from low-quality memory (requiring extensive clarification). Interactions are capped at a maximum of 10 turns. We report task Completion representing the percentage of tasks successfully terminated within this interaction limit, serving as a measure of overall system reliability. 

Memory Fidelity. We calculate the BERT-f1 between the retrieved context and ground-truth dialogues, complemented by a Memory Score (see Figure 19) derived via an “LLM-as-a-judge” protocol. This protocol evaluates task coverage, accuracy, and preference consistency. Specifically, this scoring mechanism penalizes the omission of critical persona facets while rewarding concise, task-relevant retrievals that enhance downstream utility. 

Efficiency. We calculate Context Token to measure the average number of memory tokens consumed per query. To quantify memory retrieval efficiency within long-context environments, we also record the Search Duration . In the interactive setting, User Token tracks the total tokens generated by the user simulator, capturing the communicative overhead and degree of preference clarification required to resolve a task.

5.2.4 Performance Dynamics in Multi-Domain Noise Scenarios. In the multi-domain noise environment, the performance of standalone LLMs diverges significantly from memory systems-based agents, revealing critical insights into reasoning capacity and retrieval efficiency. (1) Performance of Standalone LLMs. As indicated in Table 7, standalone LLMs with enhanced reasoning capability, such as Kimi-K2.5, demonstrate substantial resilience to text variability and the complexity of multi-domain queries, maintaining a robust MCQ Acc. of 0.930 . It suggests that advanced reasoning is a pivotal driver in stable preference formation, especially navigating environments with high contextual entropy. In contrast, chat models such as Llama3.3-70B, suffer a marked performance degradation, with accuracy falling to 0.656 . highlighting a significant susceptibility to complex tasks with high contextual entropy. (2) Performance Changes in Interactive Scenarios. Figure 8 shows the performance fluctuations of memory system-based agents compared to standalone LLMs in the presence of noise. Notably, memory systems exhibit higher sensitivity, particularly during interactive tasks where response accuracy drops after noise is introduced. This degradation is more pronounced than that observed in the single-domain setting shown in Figure 4. These results suggest that while current memory systems decompose queries [ 35 ] and rerank retrieved memory, they still struggle to integrate and properly order information across domains when dealing with noisy environments. Finally, taking a holistic view of Figure 9, we observe that, when comparing performance between the Type 3 and Type 2 positions, memory systems generally maintain stable Turn=1 performance and remain largely robust to token-level noise introduced by irrelevant dialogue. Moreover, relative to the no-memory baseline (Type 1), they achieve consistent and steady improvements. However, compared to the single-domain tasks, the disparity among different memory systems becomes more pronounced in the multi-domain evaluation, particularly at the Type 2 position, suggesting that memory systems differ in their sensitivity to cross-domain complexity. (3) Efficiency Changes in Multi-domain Noisy Scenarios. Efficiency metrics further show that the introduction of noise leads to a substantial increase in computational overhead. As shown in Table 7, the Search Duration of EverMemOS rises to 26 s, significantly higher than in the clean multi-domain setting, suggesting that semantic noise increases the difficulty of retrieval and preference synthesis. In contrast, Lightmem maintains a consistently low latency of 9.1 ms, indicating better scalability under noisy conditions.

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduce a benchmark that shifts the evaluation of personalized memory agents from static preference recall to longitudinal persona consistency. By constructing temporally ordered interaction events, we propose an event-driven framework, PERMA, for assessing standalone LLMs and memory systems under text variability and real-world linguistic alignment. Our experiments in single- and multi-domain tasks, as well as long-context settings, reveal that most memory-based systems outperform RAG by linking related interactions into a persistent persona state. This advantage becomes more pronounced in high-entropy settings, where memory systems demonstrate greater robustness and achieve 50 × to 300 × higher token efficiency than vanilla long-context models. Notably, while standalone LLMs exhibit severe performance degradation when context exceeds their window sizes, memory systems remain stable by reducing long-range reasoning to structured persona state matching. Despite these gains, performance degradation under multi-domain transitions and conflicting signals reveals a sensitivity to context complexity, further highlighting the necessity of PERMA. Overall, our findings suggest that, while memory enhances robustness, achieving truly personalized agents requires moving beyond static retrieval toward task-aware, dynamic memory management capable of filtering noise and maintaining long-term coherence, ultimately enabling lifelong companionship.

A Prompt 

To establish a realistic foundation for evaluation, the framework utilizes data construction prompts that simulate the evolution of user preferences. By leveraging TIMELINE_GENERATION in Figure 13, the system generates interaction timelines, modeling how user interests emerge and deepen. These timelines are then transformed into naturalistic dialogues via the DIALOGUE_GENERATION prompts in Figure 15, which guide the LLMs to reveal preferences through feedback or corrections, thereby mirroring the ambiguity of real-world interactions. Building upon this data, the framework transitions to a dynamic interactive simulation driven by the User simulator. As the interaction unfolds, the USER_FEEDBACK_PROMPT in Figure 16 provides the user’s responses, enabling the agent to provide adaptive feedback. This mechanism allows the simulated user to clarify intent or correct the assistant based on history, creating a test of the assistant’s ability to maintain consistency and adapt to user guidance in real-time. Finally, to quantify agent performance, the module implements a standardized “LLM-as-a-Judge” evaluation. We score the assistant’s responses on quality and personalized memory (EVAL_MEMORY_SCORE in Figure 19). Crucially, the sys-tem assesses memory precision, verifying the retrieval of details against ground truth. OPTION_GENERATION_PROMPT in Figure 18 synthesizes challenging multiple-choice questions with options that are plausible but intentionally misaligned with user preferences or facts, thereby testing the agent’s discriminative ability in noisy environments. 

B Case Study 

In this section, we present a case study comparing the retrieved memory of different systems to provide a clearer horizontal evaluation. As illustrated in Table 11–12, we can intuitively observe the retrieved memory results of various systems for the same query: MemOS categorizes memory into three distinct dimensions—episodic facts, explicit preferences, and implicit preferences—utilizing a parallel retrieval mechanism that effectively addresses the three categories of challenges inherent in preference-driven scenarios; meanwhile, Mem0, Memobase, and EverMemOS prioritize the concatenation of episodic memories, which makes them better suited as personal assistants for querying historical records but leaves them relatively weak in preference extraction and processing; conversely, Supermemory leans toward highly abstracted preference extraction but fails to reconstruct the specific details of relevant events along the timeline.
