## Abstract 

As agentic AI systems move beyond static question answering into open-ended, tool-augmented, and multi-step real-world workflows, their increased authority poses greater risks of system misuse and operational failures. However, current evaluation practices remain fragmented, measuring isolated capabilities such as coding, hal-lucination, jailbreak resistance, or tool use in narrowly defined settings. We argue that the central limitation is not merely insuffi-cient coverage of evaluation dimensions, but the lack of a principled notion of representativeness : an agent’s trustworthiness should be assessed over a representative socio-technical scenario distribu-tion rather than a collection of disconnected benchmark instances. To this end, we propose the Holographic Agent Assessment Framework (HAAF) , a systematic evaluation paradigm that char-acterizes agent trustworthiness over a scenario manifold spanning task types, tool interfaces, interaction dynamics, social contexts, and risk levels. The framework integrates four complementary components: (i) static cognitive and policy analysis , (ii) interactive sandbox simulation , (iii) social-ethical alignment assessment , and (iv) a distribution-aware representative sampling engine that jointly optimizes coverage and risk sensitivity—particularly for rare but high-consequence tail risks that conventional benchmarks system-atically overlook. These components are connected through an iterative Trustworthy Optimization Factory . Through cycles of red-team probing and blue-team hardening, this paradigm progressively narrows the vulnerabilities to meet deployment standards, shifting agent evaluation from benchmark islands toward representative, real-world trustworthiness. Code and data for the illustrative in-stantiation are available at https://github.com/TonyQJH/haaf-pilot.

## 1 Introduction 

Agentic AI systems are rapidly evolving from passive question-answering assistants into autonomous decision-makers that plan, call tools and act in open-ended environments [ 3 , 15 , 20 ]. As these systems become embedded in real-world workflows, their failures are no longer limited to incorrect answers on static tasks. Instead, they may exhibit goal drift, unsafe tool use, resource misuse, unau-thorized actions, or socially harmful behavior in ways that directly affect downstream users and institutions. For instance, an AI coding agent recently deleted a live production database—erasing records for over a thousand users—while operating under an explicit code freeze, despite instructions requiring human approval before any changes [ 17 ]. As a result, trustworthiness in agentic AI must be un-derstood not as a narrow model property, but as a deployment-level concern shaped by how agents behave under realistic environmen-tal, operational, and social conditions. Despite this shift, current evaluation practices remain largely fragmented. Existing benchmarks often focus on isolated slices of agent behavior, such as task-solving ability [ 22 ], coding perfor-mance [ 6], hallucination [ 9], jailbreak resistance [ 1 ], or tool-use accuracy [ 10 ]. While these evaluations provide useful local sig-nals, they are typically constructed around narrowly scoped tasks, controlled environments, and single failure modes [ 7 , 11 ]. Conse-quently, high scores on existing benchmarks do not necessarily imply real-world reliability, robustness, or safety.

We argue that the central limitation is not merely that current evaluations cover too few dimensions, but that they lack a princi-pled notion of representativeness . Agent trustworthiness is not a point property that can be inferred from a handful of benchmark instances; rather, it is a distributional property that depends on how an agent behaves over a heterogeneous, heavy-tailed space of socio-technical scenarios. When the distribution of benchmark tasks is misaligned with the distribution of real deployment conditions, evaluation results can create a false sense of confidence [ 8, 19 ]. This problem is particularly severe for low-frequency but high-consequence events, including adversarial manipulations, cascad-ing tool failures, and socially sensitive interactions, which are often absent from standard evaluation suites despite being central to trustworthy deployment. To address this gap, we propose the Holographic Agent As-sessment Framework (HAAF) , a new evaluation paradigm for assessing the trustworthiness of agentic AI. Our key idea is to char-acterize trustworthiness over a scenario manifold that spans task types, tool interfaces, interaction dynamics, social contexts, and risk levels, rather than over disconnected benchmark islands. The framework centers on a distribution-aware representative sampling engine that defines what should be tested, while three complemen-tary probing interfaces, i.e., static cognitive and policy analysis, interactive sandbox simulation, and social-ethical alignment assess-ment, define how agents should be evaluated. These components are connected through an iterative Trustworthy Optimization Fac-tory : red-team probing exposes vulnerability surfaces, blue-team hardening designs targeted interventions, and re-evaluation verifies improvement, cycling until the deployed system meets deployment-readiness thresholds. Rather than evaluating model weights in iso-lation, we assess deployed agentic systems comprising the model, prompting policies, tool interfaces, guardrails, and oversight mech-anisms. We illustrate this paradigm through coverage audit of exist-ing benchmarks, vulnerability attribution experiments, and a cycle of the Trustworthy Optimization Factory. In summary, this paper makes the following three contributions: 

• We identify representativeness as the central missing principle in current agent trustworthiness evaluation, arguing that the primary challenge lies in distribution design rather than solely in metric design. 

• We propose HAAF, a unified framework for representative, multi-view evaluation over risk-sensitive scenario distributions. 

• We outline an iterative evaluation-hardening methodology and demonstrate its feasibility through a complete cycle. 

## 2 Benchmark Fragmentation and the Representativeness Gap 

Current benchmark ecosystems have substantially advanced the evaluation of agentic systems by operationalizing important lo-cal notions of competence and safety [ 16 ]. Task-centric bench-marks such as AgentBench, WebArena, and SWE-bench evaluate interactive task completion and software engineering [ 6 , 12 , 22 ]; HaluEval and JailbreakBench probe hallucination and adversarial robustness [ 1 , 9 ]; API-Bank tests tool-augmented behavior [ 10 ]; and more recent suites—GAIA, WorkArena, OSWorld, 𝜏 -bench, ToolSandbox, AgentDojo—extend coverage to general assistants, enterprise software, computer-use, and prompt-injection robust-ness [ 2, 3, 14 , 15 , 20 , 21 ]. These benchmarks provide valuable signals, but each is tied to a particular evaluation slice. The problem is not that existing benchmarks are individually un-informative, but that they are collectively incomplete in ways that matter for deployment. First, many evaluations remain capability-isolated : they test coding, planning, hallucination, or safety as sep-arate phenomena, even though real-world failures often emerge from their interaction within a single multi-step trajectory. Second, settings are frequently environment-simplified : tools are cleanly specified and interfaces are stable, whereas real deployments in-volve noisy observations, latency, partial observability, and cas-cading downstream effects. Third, most benchmarks are socially decontextualized : they abstract away delegation, manipulation, bias, and risk asymmetry. Even when combined, such benchmarks reveal whether an agent can succeed under one pressure at a time, but not whether it remains trustworthy when multiple pressures co-occur. ## 3.2 Layer 1: Static Cognitive and Policy Analysis 

The first layer performs static cognitive and policy analysis , ask-ing whether the agent is configured in a way that already makes untrustworthy behavior likely before interaction begins. Rather than requiring full white-box access to internal model states, this layer operates on policy-visible artifacts such as system instructions, tool descriptions, permission scopes, memory interfaces, retrieval sources, escalation rules, and observable decision rationales when available. The goal is to identify latent vulnerability surfaces that may not be apparent from end-task success alone. Specifically, Layer 1 probes for structural issues such as con-flicting objectives, underspecified safety boundaries, missing con-firmation barriers for irreversible operations, and bias-inducing retrieval or memory mechanisms. ## 3.3 Layer 2: Interactive Sandbox Simulation 

The second layer evaluates agents through interactive sandbox sim-ulation , which reproduces realistic task execution under controlled but high-fidelity environmental conditions. This layer moves be-yond static benchmark prompts and tests how an agent behaves over full trajectories involving planning, tool invocation, state up-dates, recovery, and side effects [ 14 , 21 ]. A world model library 

exposes multiple interface types (OS actions, web, databases, APIs, enterprise tools), and the framework records full trajectories—tool calls, intermediate states, deviations, recovery behavior, and down-stream consequences—enabling detection of trajectory-level failure modes such as goal hijacking or silent misuse of external tools. Layer 2 contains three broad classes of scenarios: (i) benign but complex tasks (multi-step information gathering, workflow coordi-nation) testing reliability under normal use; (ii) adversarial or de-ceptive conditions (prompt injection, tool-output corruption [ 2, 13 ]) testing robustness under attack; and (iii) resource- and environment-constrained settings (latency spikes, partial tool failure) testing grace-ful degradation under operational stress. 

## 3.4 Layer 3: Social and Ethical Alignment Assessment 

The third layer extends evaluation from technical interaction to 

social and ethical alignment [ 18 , 23 ]. In real deployments, agents in-teract with users, organizations, institutions, and sometimes other agents; trustworthiness therefore depends not only on task cor-rectness, but also on behavior under social incentives, conflicting stakeholder interests, and asymmetries of power or information. approval, or negotiation—where social context materially changes what trustworthy behavior should look like. Representative failure modes include biased decisions in socially sensitive applications, susceptibility to emotional or strategic manipulation, collusion with malicious actors, over-compliance with unsafe authority, and value drift over extended interactions. By embedding agents in socially meaningful contexts rather than treating alignment as a purely abstract preference-learning problem, Layer 3 captures trustworthi-ness dimensions that remain invisible in technical-only evaluations. 

## 3.5 Layer 4: Distribution-Aware Representative Sampling 

The core engine of HAAF is distribution-aware representative sam-pling , which determines which scenarios from the manifold should be included in evaluation and with what emphasis. This layer is central because the main challenge identified in Section 2 is not merely to test more behaviors, but to test the right distribution of behaviors. A benchmark that samples convenient, clean, or popular instances may still fail to measure deployment trustworthiness if it omits rare but consequential events or under-represents important classes of socio-technical interaction. We therefore treat representative evaluation as a structured sam-pling problem. The scenario manifold is partitioned into regions de-fined by dimensions such as task family, interface type, interaction horizon, environmental stress, social sensitivity, and consequence severity. For each region, the framework estimates at least two quantities: its deployment relevance , reflecting how often similar sit-uations arise in practice, and its risk contribution , reflecting the cost or severity of failure when such situations occur [ 7 , 8, 11 , 19 ]. ## 4 Illustrative Instantiation: One Factory Cycle 

This section demonstrates one cycle of the Trustworthy Optimiza-tion Factory introduced in Section 3.6. We first run a red-team pass to expose and attribute vulnerability surfaces (§4.1), then apply blue-team interventions and re-evaluate (§4.2). 

## 4.1 Red-Team Vulnerability Attribution 

The coverage audit in Section 2 reveals where evaluation gaps exist; this experiment asks whether a framework-aligned assessment can diagnose why agents fail. We run a baseline agent through a suite of risk-stratified scenarios and attribute each violation to a root-cause category, identifying the vulnerability surfaces that should be prioritized for hardening. All scenarios, tools, and data are synthetic and run in a local sandbox; the purpose is to demonstrate feasibility of trajectory-level root-cause diagnosis, not to report generalizable performance numbers. 

Setup. We construct a sandbox environment with five simulated tools (document search, database query, file read, file write, and message dispatch) operating over synthetic data. Twenty-four sce-narios are defined across four categories, summarized in Table 2; the first three categories primarily exercise Layer 2 probing, while the Social category exercises Layer 3. Each scenario specifies suc-cess criteria, forbidden actions, and a risk-severity weight (1–5). A single model (Qwen3-8B, served via vLLM with temperature = 0)executes all scenarios to enable controlled comparison. 

Failure Taxonomy. Every violated trajectory is annotated with a reusable eight-class taxonomy: Prompt Injection (PI), Goal Drift (GD), Unauthorized Action (UA), Hallucinated Tool-use (HT), Re-covery Failure (RF), Operational Failure (OF), Policy Leak (PL), and Social Harm (SH). Labeling follows an earliest-cause rule: the primary label reflects the first identifiable root cause; labels were as-signed by the authors under a shared protocol and reviewed jointly in cases of ambiguity. We report primary labels only for trajec-tories that result in a scenario-level violation; in this small-scale instantiation only a subset of categories is observed.

Blue-team interventions. (1) A tool-output firewall wraps every tool return with a delimiter marking it as untrusted data , instructing the agent to never follow embedded instructions. Limitations. This illustrative instantiation demonstrates one Fac-tory cycle using a single model, synthetic data, and 24 hand-crafted scenarios; it validates the iterative methodology rather than provid-ing statistically powered conclusions. Because interventions are derived from failures on the same 24 scenarios and re-evaluated on that same suite, the observed gains demonstrate remediation on the probed set rather than generalization to held-out scenario regions. Full deployment-readiness assessment would require mul-tiple Factory cycles, larger scenario suites, diverse models, and human-validated annotations—directions we outline in Section 5. 

## 5 Discussion 

Significance and vision. This paper argues that representative, risk-sensitive scenario distributions should become a first-class object of evaluation in agentic AI. Rather than asking whether an agent performs well on a curated task suite, we ask whether it remains trustworthy over a representative scenario manifold (cf. Section 3.1). This reframing shifts evaluation from benchmark in-stances to representative scenario distributions, from single scores to multi-dimensional trustworthiness profiles, and from static test-ing to the iterative red–blue team cycle of the Trustworthy Optimiza-tion Factory . The need for this shift is immediate: agentic systems now operate with planning, tool use, and socially situated deci-sion making, yet evaluation practice still inherits the logic of fixed test sets and score-centric comparisons [ 2, 8 ]. If this agenda suc-ceeds, we envision the community converging on shared scenario taxonomies and world-model libraries [ 3, 11 , 20 , 23 ], reporting trust-worthiness profiles rather than single scores [ 7 , 11 ], adopting attri-bution pipelines as standard evaluation components, and using the Factory’s red–blue cycle as a deployment-readiness pipeline. More broadly, this vision aligns with emerging AI governance frame-works that increasingly require auditable evidence of deployment-level safety [5]. 

Limitations and future work. Beyond the scope constraints noted in §4.2, a full instantiation of HAAF will require larger scenario suites, multi-model comparisons, richer social simulations, and stronger annotation protocols. Key open questions include formal-izing convergence criteria for the Factory (how many red–blue cycles are needed, what acceptance thresholds suit different do-mains), handling residual violations that resist prompt-level mitiga-tion (such as intent-based failures driven by direct user requests), and grounding representative sampling in real-world evidence such as deployment logs, incident reports, and red-team traces [2, 5]. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Current agent evaluation remains fragmented across benchmark islands, yet trustworthy deployment depends on behavior over heterogeneous, risk-sensitive socio-technical conditions. We ar-gued that the central missing principle is representativeness and introduced HAAF, which combines distribution-aware sampling with multi-layer probing and an iterative Trustworthy Optimiza-tion Factory . Our illustrative instantiation shows that even one Factory cycle—red-team attribution followed by lightweight, tar-geted hardening—can measurably narrow the vulnerability surface. Without representative evaluation, benchmark progress will re-main only weakly connected to deployment trustworthiness; we hope this work motivates a broader KDD agenda around scenario modeling, risk-aware sampling, and iterative deployment-readiness assessment for agentic AI.
